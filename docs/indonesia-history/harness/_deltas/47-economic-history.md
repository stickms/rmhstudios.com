## GLOSSARY

New terms to append to `glossary-ledger.md` (keep alphabetical):

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| agricultural involution | Clifford Geertz's concept (1963) for the process by which Javanese wet-rice agriculture absorbed a growing population through increasing social complexity and labour intensity rather than through technological change or capital accumulation, producing what Geertz called "shared poverty." The thesis has been substantially criticised and largely superseded empirically, but remains an influential analytical framework. |
| *batig slot* | (Dutch: "credit balance") The annual colonial surplus remitted from the Netherlands East Indies treasury to the Dutch state under the Cultivation System era. At its peak in the 1850s, the *batig slot* financed approximately 50 percent of Dutch government expenditure and funded the construction of Dutch railways. Symbolises the extractive logic of the Cultuurstelsel. |
| *Cultuurstelsel* | The "Cultivation System" imposed on Java from 1830 to c. 1870 by Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch. Javanese peasant households were required to cultivate export crops (sugar, coffee, indigo) on approximately one-fifth of their cultivated land or to perform sixty-six days of corvée labour annually. Phased out after the Agrarian Law of 1870. |
| dual economy | J.H. Boeke's concept (1910 dissertation; 1953 book) of colonial Indonesia as containing two mutually incompatible economic systems: a "western" capitalist sector (plantations, mining, trading houses) governed by classical economic laws, and a "pre-capitalistic" indigenous sector operating under different social logic. Critics including Benjamin Higgins (1956) argued the divide was structural/technological rather than cultural. |
| emporium (port polity) | A coastal state whose political power and revenue rested on brokering and taxing seaborne trade rather than on agricultural production or territorial control. The dominant political form in the Indonesian archipelago from Srivijaya (7th–11th centuries) through the sultanate era. |
| *peranakan* | Indonesian Chinese who had been born in the archipelago and adopted local cultural elements (language, dress, cuisine), distinguishing them from *totok* (recently arrived immigrants who maintained Chinese customs and language). The distinction shaped different roles in colonial and post-colonial commercial life. See Chapter 47 for fuller treatment. |
| *totok* | Recently arrived Chinese immigrants in the Dutch East Indies and post-independence Indonesia who retained Chinese language, customs, and cultural identity, as distinct from the *peranakan* (locally acculturated Chinese). *Totok* migrants dominated commercial life in many Outer Islands towns in the late colonial and early independence periods. |
