## GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| pemuda | Indonesian youth activists, particularly those who came of age under the Japanese occupation (1942–45); played a central role in the independence proclamation, social revolution, and armed struggle of the National Revolution. |
| NICA (Netherlands Indies Civil Administration) | The Dutch colonial administrative body, assembled in exile during the Japanese occupation, that followed Allied forces into Indonesia in 1945 intending to restore Netherlands sovereignty. |
| PPKI (Panitia Persiapan Kemerdekaan Indonesia) | Preparatory Committee for Indonesian Independence, established by the Japanese in August 1945 and repurposed by the nationalists as the founding body of the Republic; adopted UUD 1945 on 18 August 1945. |
| UUD 1945 | The 1945 Constitution (Undang-Undang Dasar 1945) of the Republic of Indonesia, adopted by the PPKI on 18 August 1945; a brief document of 37 articles establishing a strong presidency; remained the basic law (with interruptions) until its fundamental amendment in 1999–2002. |
| Jakarta Charter (Piagam Jakarta) | A draft preamble to the 1945 Constitution that included a clause requiring Indonesian Muslims to observe Islamic law; removed on 18 August 1945 before the constitution was formally adopted, in a compromise that defined Indonesia as a non-theocratic state. |
| Police Actions (politionele acties) | Dutch euphemism for two military offensives against the Indonesian Republic: Operatie Product (July–August 1947) and Operatie Kraai (December 1948–January 1949); termed "Dutch Military Aggressions" (Agresi Militer Belanda) in Indonesian historiography. |
| Van Mook line | The military demarcation line imposed after the first Dutch Police Action (1947), named after Lieutenant Governor-General Hubertus van Mook; left the Republic in control of only a portion of Java and parts of Sumatra. |
| Madiun Affair (Peristiwa Madiun) | The September–October 1948 uprising in which PKI-affiliated forces in Madiun clashed with the Republican army; suppressed by the Siliwangi Division; a pivotal turning point whose interpretation has been debated across multiple Indonesian political regimes. |
| Round Table Conference (Konferensi Meja Bundar) | Negotiations at The Hague, 23 August – 2 November 1949, producing the transfer of sovereignty from the Netherlands to the United States of Indonesia; also established the Netherlands-Indonesia Union and required the Republic to assume the Dutch colonial debt. |
| Hari Pahlawan | "Heroes' Day," the Indonesian national holiday observed on 10 November, commemorating the Battle of Surabaya (1945); a symbol of the revolution's capacity for mass armed resistance. |
| laskar | Irregular armed militias that proliferated during the National Revolution, ranging from pemuda units and ideologically motivated groups to neighbourhood defence forces and, in Jakarta, networks rooted in criminal and dockworker communities. |
| Peta (Pembela Tanah Air) | "Defenders of the Homeland," the Japanese-sponsored Indonesian volunteer militia established in 1943; provided military training to a generation of pemuda officers who became the core of the Republican army. |
| Perdjoeangan Kita (Our Struggle) | Sutan Sjahrir's 1945 political manifesto arguing that Indonesia's diversity required a secular, democratic national identity; published in Dutch as Indonesische overpeinzingen; influential in shaping the Republic's diplomatic strategy. |
| Serangan Umum (General Offensive) | The military operation of 1 March 1949 in which Republican forces under field command of Lt. Col. Suharto briefly recaptured Yogyakarta; demonstrated to the United Nations and the world that the Republic continued to exist as a fighting force despite the Dutch capture of the city. |
| TNI (Tentara Nasional Indonesia) | The Indonesian National Armed Forces, formally constituted during the National Revolution from the amalgamation of former Peta units, Republican military formations, and pemuda militias. |
| uleebalang | Territorial nobles of Aceh whose authority had rested on Dutch support; largely eliminated in the Acehnese social revolution of December 1945 – March 1946. |
