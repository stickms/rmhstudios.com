## GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| cabang atas | The hereditary peranakan Chinese elite appointed as Chinese officers (kapitein, luitenant, majoor der Chinezen) in the colonial Indies; they administered tax-farming contracts and brokered between Dutch capital and indigenous producers. |
| cultuurstelsel | The Cultivation System (1830–1915) introduced by Governor-General Van den Bosch, requiring Javanese villages to dedicate one-fifth of their cultivated land or sixty-six days of labor per year to growing government export crops (sugar, coffee, indigo, tea). |
| Inlanders | "Natives," the colonial legal designation for the indigenous majority of the Netherlands East Indies. Subject to adat (customary law) and native courts; could not hold freehold land. |
| involution | Clifford Geertz's concept (Agricultural Involution, 1963) for a pattern in which Javanese wet-rice agriculture absorbed a growing population through increasing social complexity and labor-intensity without technological change or rising per-capita productivity — a "shared poverty" condition attributed partly to colonial pressures. |
| lurah | A Javanese village headman who served as the lowest-level administrative intermediary between colonial authorities and the peasant population. |
| nyai | A Javanese and Malay term for an indigenous or Chinese woman who cohabited with a European man in the colonial Indies as housekeeper, domestic manager, and sexual partner. Tacitly sanctioned by colonial authorities; the woman had no formal legal standing and her children's status depended on the father's formal acknowledgment. |
| pangrèh praja | The colonial indigenous administrative corps staffed primarily by Javanese priyayi, who served as the intermediary bureaucracy between Dutch resident officers and village communities. |
| passenstelsel | The "pass system" in the colonial Netherlands East Indies, requiring Foreign Orientals and Inlanders to carry written authorization to leave their designated residential areas or travel between residencies. Formally abolished 1919. |
| pecinan | The designated Chinese residential and commercial quarter (Chinatown) in colonial Indies cities, created or formalized in the aftermath of the 1740 Batavia massacre and enforced by the wijkenstelsel until 1919. |
| peranakan | Indies-born Chinese, typically of long residence, who spoke Malay or Javanese, had often intermarried with local populations, and had adopted many local customs. Distinguished from totok (China-born) Chinese. |
| poenale sanctie | The "penal sanction" in the 1880 Coolie Ordinance of the Netherlands East Indies, imposing criminal penalties on contract laborers who breached their employment contracts by absconding, refusing work, or showing insubordination. Formally abolished 1931. |
| priyayi | The Javanese aristocratic-administrative class that staffed the colonial indigenous bureaucracy (pangrèh praja), mediating between Dutch authorities and the peasant majority; their children benefited from Dutch-language education, producing the nationalist intelligentsia of the next generation. |
| Regeeringsreglement | The 1854 Government Regulation of the Netherlands East Indies that formally established the tripartite legal hierarchy of Europeans, Vreemde Oosterlingen, and Inlanders. |
| rijsttafel | Literally "rice table"; an elaborate colonial-era feast incorporating dozens of Indonesian dishes served around a rice centerpiece, associated with the hybrid culinary culture of the nyai household. European women began replacing it with tinned European foods in the 1920s–1930s as a marker of racial distinction. |
| sarong-kebaya | A hybrid colonial dress — a tailored blouse (kebaya) worn with an Indonesian wrap skirt (sarong) — popularized by nyai women and worn by both European and Indonesian women in the Indies through the early twentieth century, before being consciously abandoned by European women as a marker of racial distinction. |
| tempo doeloe | Literally "the old time" in Malay; a nostalgic evocation of the colonial Indies elite lifestyle (bungalow life, clubs, rijsttafel, veranda) that acquired its specifically nostalgic charge in the memories of Dutch and Indo-European colonial residents after Indonesian independence. |
| totok | China-born Chinese migrants to the Netherlands East Indies who maintained stronger ties to China, spoke regional Chinese languages (Hokkien, Teochew, Cantonese), and often identified with Chinese nationalist politics. Distinguished from peranakan (Indies-born) Chinese. |
| Vreemde Oosterlingen | "Foreign Orientals," the colonial legal category in the Netherlands East Indies encompassing ethnic Chinese, Arabs, South Asians, and other non-indigenous, non-European Asians. Subject to a hybrid legal regime, positioned between Europeans and Inlanders in the tripartite hierarchy. |
| wijkenstelsel | The "quarter system" in the colonial Netherlands East Indies, requiring non-European inhabitants to reside within designated ethnic neighborhoods (Chinese pecinan, Arab kampung Arab). Formalized in the early nineteenth century; formally abolished 1919. |
