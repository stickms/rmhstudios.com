Learning Objectives

  • Explain how the New Order's technocratic economists — the "Berkeley Mafia" — stabilized Indonesia's economy after Sukarno-era hyperinflation and opened the country to foreign investment.
  • Analyze the oil boom of the 1970s: how petroleum revenues transformed state finances, what Pertamina's extraordinary rise and near-collapse in 1975 revealed about governance, and how a second boom renewed the dependency.
  • Describe the Green Revolution in Indonesia, including the BIMAS program, the role of BULOG, and the meaning and limits of rice self-sufficiency achieved in 1984.
  • Distinguish between import-substitution and export-oriented industrialization strategies, and trace the shift from one to the other in the mid-1980s in response to falling oil prices and deregulation.
  • Evaluate the World Bank's "East Asian Miracle" narrative as applied to Indonesia: what the poverty-reduction and growth figures show, how scholars have debated the role of state policy versus market forces, and what the distributional record actually looked like.
  • Identify the main discontents generated by New Order development: crony capitalism, ethnic disparities, labor repression, environmental degradation, the transmigration program, and regional inequality.
  • Explain the structural financial vulnerabilities — unhedged short-term debt, a lax post-1988 banking sector, crony lending, and a sustained currency peg — that positioned Indonesia for catastrophic exposure when the 1997 Asian financial crisis arrived.

In the thirty years between Suharto's seizure of power in 1966 and the eve of the Asian financial crisis in mid-1997, Indonesia underwent one of the fastest and most sweeping economic transformations of the twentieth century. A country that in 1966 was suffocating under more than six hundred percent annual inflation, an unserviceable foreign debt, and the near-total collapse of its productive infrastructure became, by the early 1990s, a World Bank showcase — a "tiger cub" whose annual growth rates regularly exceeded seven percent, whose poverty headcount had fallen from more than half the population to barely one in ten, and whose manufacturing sector had diversified far beyond the petroleum dependency of the 1970s. That transformation was real, and its material benefits — in nutrition, in infant mortality, in literacy, in physical infrastructure — were tangible and deep. Yet the same thirty years also produced a political economy riddled with cronyism and ethnic resentment, an environment scarred by unregulated logging and the early spread of industrial agriculture, a workforce kept deliberately cheap through the suppression of independent unions, a transmigration programme of enormous human and ecological cost, and a financial system whose extraordinary fragility was deliberately obscured by headline growth. Understanding New Order Indonesia requires holding both narratives simultaneously: the genuine achievement and the corrosive contradictions that would detonate it.

The Wreckage Inherited: Sukarno's Economic Crisis and the Technocrats' Response

To understand what the New Order built, it is necessary to grasp how comprehensively Sukarno's Guided Democracy had destroyed the economic framework it inherited. By 1966, Indonesia's annual inflation rate had reached approximately 635 percent — some estimates placed it above a thousand — driven by the government's practice of printing money to cover chronic budget deficits, fund grandiose prestige projects, and support a swelling military.1 The rupiah had depreciated to a fraction of its official value on the black market. Foreign exchange reserves were negligible. The country had defaulted on much of its external debt, estimated at around US$2.4 billion. Export revenues, particularly from rubber, tin, and copra, had stagnated as Sukarno's economic nationalism repelled foreign capital and left state enterprises chronically underfunded. The experience of mass poverty was not an abstraction: per capita income at the mid-1960s was estimated at around fifty US dollars a year, placing Indonesia among the world's poorest countries alongside sub-Saharan Africa.2

Suharto's immediate response was to delegate economic policy to a group of academic economists who would become famous — and controversial — as the "Berkeley Mafia": Widjojo Nitisastro, Ali Wardhana, Mohammad Sadli, Emil Salim, and J.B. Sumarlin, most of them graduates or doctoral students of the economics faculty at the University of California, Berkeley, supported by Ford Foundation scholarships in the 1950s and 1960s.3 Widjojo, who would serve as chief economic coordinator until 1983, was the intellectual anchor of the group. Their diagnoses and prescriptions were orthodox by the standards of the time: the root cause of Indonesia's crisis was fiscal indiscipline, and the cure was a balanced-budget law, monetary restraint, exchange-rate unification, and the removal of the controls and nationalizations that had driven away foreign capital and distorted resource allocation.

The October 1966 stabilization programme moved quickly. The government eliminated the multiple exchange rates that had allowed different transactions to take place at different official rates — a system that had enriched intermediaries while starving productive enterprises of hard currency. It adopted a balanced-budget principle that prohibited domestic financing of deficits: revenues had to cover expenditures, a rule that remained formally in force throughout the New Order. It restructured the foreign debt inherited from Sukarno through the newly created Inter-Governmental Group on Indonesia (IGGI), a consortium of Western donors and Japan, whose aid and debt relief was essential to preventing a complete external default. Inflation fell from 635 percent in 1966 to roughly 19 percent by 1969, an achievement that the technocrats' advocates called one of the most effective macroeconomic stabilizations of the twentieth century.4

Berkeley Mafia — the informal name for the group of U.S.-trained Indonesian economists — principally Widjojo Nitisastro, Ali Wardhana, Mohammad Sadli, Emil Salim, and J.B. Sumarlin — who served as Suharto's senior economic advisers from 1966. Most held doctorates from the University of California, Berkeley, financed by Ford Foundation scholarships. They pursued orthodox macroeconomic stabilization, open-capital policies, and later deregulation, in consistent tension with nationalist and military factions who favoured state-directed industrial policy.

The legal architecture of economic opening was laid in 1967–68. The Foreign Capital Investment Law of January 1967 offered tax holidays, guarantees against nationalization, and repatriation rights to foreign investors — provisions specifically designed to reassure the Western and Japanese capital that Sukarno had expelled.5 A complementary Domestic Capital Investment Law followed in 1968. These laws, combined with the political stability Suharto imposed, produced a rapid influx of foreign direct investment, concentrated initially in resource extraction — oil, timber, mining — and subsequently, from the early 1970s, in manufacturing. The IGGI donors, coordinated by the World Bank and International Monetary Fund, provided the external financial underpinning without which the stabilization could not have succeeded: between 1967 and 1970, aid disbursements ran at roughly US$350 million per year, covering the bulk of Indonesia's import needs during the adjustment period.6

Black Gold: The Oil Booms and the Pertamina Disaster

The technocrats' stabilization created the conditions for growth, but the first great engine of that growth was oil. Indonesia had been a significant oil producer since the late colonial period — Royal Dutch Shell had exploited Sumatran fields since the 1880s — but nationalization and mismanagement under Sukarno had badly damaged production. After 1966, the government reorganized the industry around Pertamina (Perusahaan Pertambangan Minyak dan Gas Bumi Negara), a state enterprise created in August 1968 from the merger of two predecessor companies, and around a system of production-sharing contracts that brought major international oil companies back into Indonesia under terms that gave the state a large share of the revenue while the foreign partner assumed the exploration risk.7

The quadrupling of global oil prices triggered by the OPEC embargo of 1973–74 was a windfall of extraordinary proportions. Indonesia's oil export revenues, already rising as production recovered, nearly trebled in a single year. Government revenues from petroleum rose from roughly thirty percent of total revenues in the early 1970s to an estimated seventy percent by 1980. The state suddenly had resources on a scale that permitted a genuine development programme: massive investment in roads, irrigation, schools, and health clinics, the subsidization of rice and fuel, and the funding of the first REPELITA five-year development plans. GDP growth accelerated; living standards improved across broad swaths of the population. To its beneficiaries, the oil boom felt like a permanent dividend of the New Order's political stability and prudent management.8

The man who embodied Pertamina's ambitions in these years was General Ibnu Sutowo, its president director from 1968 to 1976. Sutowo was a bold entrepreneur who used Pertamina's privileged access to oil revenues to build an industrial empire that had almost nothing to do with oil production: a fleet of supertankers, hotels, a domestic airline, a steel plant, construction companies, and agricultural projects. Pertamina was effectively operating as a parallel government with its own treasury, its own foreign borrowing, and its own development agenda, all beneath the formal oversight structures of the Indonesian state.9 The reckoning came in February 1975, when Pertamina announced it could no longer service its short-term debt obligations to American and Canadian creditors. The investigation that followed uncovered total debts officially placed at over US$10 billion — equivalent, as Peter McCawley calculated in his landmark 1978 study, to approximately one-third of Indonesia's gross national product at the time.10

REPELITA — Rencana Pembangunan Lima Tahun, meaning Five-Year Development Plan. Six successive REPELITA plans, covering the period 1969 to 1998, structured Indonesia's New Order development programme. REPELITA I (1969–74) prioritized agricultural rehabilitation and basic infrastructure; subsequent plans progressively emphasized industrialization, human development, and ultimately deregulation and export orientation. The plans were prepared by Bappenas, the National Development Planning Agency, under the intellectual leadership of Widjojo Nitisastro.

The Pertamina crisis was a defining moment in the history of Indonesian political economy. The government, embarrassed but unwilling to default on the foreign obligations of a state enterprise — the damage to Indonesia's international creditworthiness would have been severe — mounted a costly rescue, rescheduling the debts and ultimately covering them from oil revenues. Sutowo himself was "honourably discharged" in March 1976, but faced no criminal charges; he and his family remained among Indonesia's wealthiest for decades, a pattern of impunity that would recur throughout the New Order.11 McCawley's analysis argued that the crisis was not simply a tale of individual corruption but revealed a systemic failure: the lack of "institutional checks and balances" across state enterprises, the near-total absence of external audit, and the political protection that permitted Sutowo's ambitions to grow unchecked for almost a decade.12 These institutional weaknesses were patched but not repaired; they would resurface in more dangerous form after the banking deregulation of 1988.

A second oil boom, triggered by the Iranian Revolution's disruption of global supply in 1978–79, renewed Indonesia's petroleum windfall and, with it, the temptation to defer structural change. Oil revenues funded REPELITA III (1979–84) at an unprecedented scale, and the government used the windfall to subsidize food, fuel, and fertilizer at levels that were politically popular but fiscally unsustainable. The "Dutch Disease" risk — the tendency of a natural-resource boom to appreciate the exchange rate, pricing tradeable manufacturing out of competitiveness — was partially managed through periodic currency adjustments, but the manufacturing sector remained fragile behind its protection precisely because oil revenues removed the urgency of export diversification.

Fields of Green: The Agricultural Revolution and Rice Self-Sufficiency

If oil was the New Order's fiscal windfall, rice was its political foundation. For a government drawing its legitimacy from stability and development rather than popular mandate, the ability to feed the population — and particularly to provide affordable rice to Java's dense urban and rural poor — was not merely an economic objective but a political imperative. In 1966, Indonesia was the world's largest importer of rice. Sukarno-era agricultural neglect had left irrigation infrastructure deteriorated, extension services inadequate, and yields well below the potential of tropical rice cultivation. The Green Revolution technologies that were transforming Asian agriculture in the 1960s — high-yielding dwarf varieties developed at the International Rice Research Institute (IRRI) in the Philippines, combined with chemical fertilizers, pesticides, and systematic irrigation — offered a pathway to a different outcome.

The instrument was BIMAS (Bimbingan Massal, Mass Guidance), a national rice intensification programme that distributed improved seed varieties — particularly the IRRI variety IR-36, which could produce two or three crops per year where traditional varieties yielded one — alongside subsidized fertilizer, pesticide packages, and supervised credit through village cooperatives. BULOG (Badan Urusan Logistik), the state logistics agency, purchased rice from farmers at a government-set floor price and sold it to urban consumers at a subsidized ceiling price, functioning as a price-stabilization mechanism that protected both producer and consumer interest. Irrigation investment under the REPELITA plans expanded the command area by millions of hectares across Java, Bali, and Sumatra.13

The results were dramatic. Between 1969 and 1984, Indonesian rice production roughly doubled, from approximately 12 million to 25 million tonnes of milled rice per year. In 1984 Indonesia declared rice self-sufficiency — a symbolic triumph that earned Suharto an award from the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) in 1985 and was widely celebrated as evidence that the New Order could deliver the material improvements the population expected.14 The comparison with the mass hunger of the late Sukarno period was not lost on ordinary Indonesians.

The Green Revolution's achievements should not obscure its discontents. The high-yielding varieties and their associated input packages favoured farmers with sufficient land to absorb the investment and sufficient credit access to participate in the BIMAS programme. Smaller subsistence farmers and landless agricultural labourers benefited less directly, and mechanization — hand tractors and mechanical threshers displacing labour-intensive hand-harvesting — reduced employment opportunities for the rural landless poor, many of them women, particularly in Java. The subsidized pesticides bundled into BIMAS packages poisoned waterways, disrupted predator-prey balances in the paddy ecosystem, and ultimately triggered the brown planthopper infestations of the 1970s and 1980s, which devastated harvests in several seasons before the government belatedly moved toward integrated pest management in 1986.15 Self-sufficiency itself proved difficult to sustain: Indonesia would return to rice imports in the 1990s as population growth and urban expansion absorbed land that had previously been productive.

BULOG — Badan Urusan Logistik (State Logistics Agency), founded 1967. Under the New Order, BULOG managed the procurement, storage, and distribution of strategic commodities, above all rice. It purchased rice from farmers at a guaranteed floor price and sold it to urban consumers at a subsidized ceiling price, functioning simultaneously as a poverty-alleviation mechanism and as a vehicle for patronage and, frequently, corruption. BULOG's operations required continuous subsidy from the government budget, funded principally by oil revenues during the boom years.

Plans, Steel, and Planes: Industrialization Strategy and the Habibie Vision

The REPELITA plans provided a framework for the New Order's industrial ambitions, and between 1969 and the early 1980s those ambitions were pursued primarily through import-substitution industrialization (ISI): building domestic capacity in industries — cement, fertilizer, textiles, steel, chemicals — by protecting them behind tariff walls and import restrictions while channelling state investment into key enterprises. This approach was not unique to Indonesia; it reflected the dominant development economics of the 1960s and the political logic of a military government committed to national self-reliance. Krakatau Steel, established in 1970 in Cilegon, West Java, became the symbol of this ambition — a fully integrated steelworks whose costs, measured against imported steel, were deeply uneconomic but whose strategic significance was deemed to outweigh commercial logic.16

The most intellectually ambitious — and contested — version of Indonesian industrial strategy was associated with Bacharuddin Jusuf Habibie, the German-trained aerospace engineer whom Suharto appointed State Minister of Research and Technology in 1978. Habibie argued that Indonesia should not confine itself to the labour-intensive, resource-based industries the Berkeley Mafia technocrats preferred on grounds of comparative advantage; it should instead make a deliberate "technological leap" into capital-intensive, high-technology industries — aerospace, shipbuilding, armaments, telecommunications — that would create the domestic engineering capacity needed for long-run development. In 1976 he became chief executive of Industri Pesawat Terbang Nurtanio (IPTN), Indonesia's national aircraft manufacturer, and by 1991 he oversaw ten strategic state enterprises grouped under the BPIS (Badan Pengelola Industri Strategis), each receiving lavish implicit and explicit government subsidies.17

The tension between Habibie's vision and the technocrats' orthodoxy was one of the defining debates of New Order economic policy. Widjojo and his colleagues argued that protecting capital-intensive industries in a labour-abundant economy misallocated resources, suppressed employment, and perpetuated dependence on oil revenues to fund unviable enterprises. Habibie's counter was that comparative advantage was not static and that countries that did not invest in technological capability would remain permanently locked into commodity exports. The empirical verdict, at least in the medium term, tended to favour the technocrats: IPTN's N-250 turboprop regional aircraft, unveiled with great ceremony in 1995, failed to secure commercial orders, and BPIS enterprises absorbed subsidies that could have financed health, education, or infrastructure.18 Yet Habibie's critique of pure laissez-faire — that industrial policy was necessary to escape the resource curse — was not without foundation; the debate remained live in development economics long after the New Order's collapse.

After Oil: The Deregulation Turn and Export-Oriented Manufacturing

The illusion that Indonesia could indefinitely fund its development model on petroleum revenues dissolved in 1982–83, when global oil prices began a sustained decline that would reach a catastrophic nadir below ten US dollars per barrel in 1985–86. For a government that had allowed oil revenues to climb to perhaps seventy percent of its budget, this was a fiscal emergency of the first order. The rupiah was devalued in 1983 to reduce the current account deficit and boost the competitiveness of non-oil exports. A landmark tax reform in the same year, designed and implemented with World Bank assistance, shifted the revenue base from oil rents to a broader system including value-added tax and corporate income tax — a structural change that outlasted the New Order and proved to be one of the reforms with the most durable positive effects.19

The central deregulation package came in June 1983 (the so-called Pakjun 83): it liberalized commercial bank interest rates and credit allocation, removing government controls that had suppressed domestic financial intermediation for two decades. The financial sector had been so underdeveloped that state banks dominated credit allocation entirely, channelling funds to protected industries and state enterprises on criteria more political than commercial. Pakjun 83 began the process of building a market-oriented banking system — but, as events would show, without adequate prudential regulation.20

Subsequent years brought a cascade of further reforms. The PAKEM 1986 package reduced import duties and licensing restrictions on manufacturing inputs. PAKDES I and II (1987) streamlined investment licensing. Most consequential of all was the October 1988 banking deregulation package, Pakto 88, which dramatically relaxed the conditions for opening new banks, new branches, and new foreign-exchange businesses. In the five years following Pakto 88, the number of private commercial banks in Indonesia rose from roughly seventy to over two hundred, a proliferation that massively expanded credit availability but far outran the capacity of Bank Indonesia, the central bank, to supervise the system.21

For manufacturing, the deregulation packages were transformative. With import barriers lowered, investment licensing simplified, and the rupiah periodically devalued to maintain export competitiveness, Indonesia attracted waves of foreign direct investment — first from Japan, then from South Korea and Taiwan — in precisely the labour-intensive export industries where Indonesia's abundant low-wage workforce gave it genuine comparative advantage: textiles, garments, footwear, plywood, and by the early 1990s electronics. Manufacturing's share of GDP, which had stood at roughly eight percent in the late 1960s, climbed above twenty percent by the mid-1990s.22 Non-oil manufactured exports, negligible in the 1970s, reached US$17 billion by 1993, roughly equalling total oil and gas export revenues for the first time — a structural transformation that appeared to validate the deregulation strategy.

The Numbers of Growth: Poverty, the Miracle, and Its Discontents

The aggregate record of New Order economic growth is, on its own terms, remarkable. Between 1967 and 1996 — a period of nearly three decades — Indonesia's economy grew at an average annual rate of approximately seven percent, compounding to a seventeen-fold increase in total GDP. Per capita income rose from roughly US$50 in the mid-1960s to over US$1,100 by the mid-1990s, lifting Indonesia from the ranks of the very poorest countries into the lower-middle-income tier. Poverty, measured by the national poverty line, fell from an estimated sixty percent of the population in the late 1960s to slightly below eleven percent by 1996. Infant mortality dropped from 142 per thousand live births in 1967 to under 50 by the mid-1990s. Adult literacy, below fifty percent in 1961, exceeded eighty percent by 1990. These are not cosmetic improvements; they represent real and substantial changes in the lives of tens of millions of people.23

The World Bank gave this achievement its most famous theoretical framing in its 1993 report, The East Asian Miracle: Economic Growth and Public Policy, which grouped Indonesia alongside Japan, the four Asian Tigers (South Korea, Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Singapore), Malaysia, and Thailand as the "high-performing Asian economies" (HPAEs). The Miracle report argued that these countries had achieved exceptional growth through a combination of macroeconomic stability, high savings rates, investment in human capital (education and health), and, more controversially, selective industrial and trade policies. Indonesia's inclusion in this elite group was contested even at the time: it was the poorest of the HPAEs, its institutions were the weakest, and its growth was in significant part driven by extractable natural resources rather than the manufactured-export dynamism that defined the East Asian Tigers. Nevertheless, the "tiger cub" label — and the World Bank imprimatur — carried real political weight, reinforcing the New Order's narrative that its development model was vindicated by the evidence.24

Scholarly debate about the Miracle narrative as applied to Indonesia has been extensive. Hal Hill's authoritative survey, The Indonesian Economy, published in its first edition in 1996 and drawing on two decades of fieldwork and quantitative analysis, offered a broadly positive assessment of the growth record while drawing attention to the "rocky path towards economic reform, the large external debt, regional and ethnic disparities, and the need for a transparent and predictable policy environment" — what he characterized as unfinished business that the growth figures tended to obscure.25 Anne Booth's longer historical perspective, in The Indonesian Economy in the Nineteenth and Twentieth Centuries: A History of Missed Opportunities, located the New Order growth within a longer trajectory of unrealized potential, noting that the structural transformation remained incomplete, that the economy remained exposed to commodity price cycles, and that the institutional weaknesses that had constrained colonial and early post-independence growth had not been overcome — merely temporarily papered over by favourable external conditions.26

Spoils and Systems: Crony Capitalism and the Conglomerates

The headline growth statistics coexisted with a political economy of spectacular favouritism. Central to the New Order's system of resource allocation was the cukong relationship: an arrangement in which ethnic-Chinese business figures provided financial support, capital, and managerial capacity to politically connected military and government patrons, receiving in return privileged access to licenses, contracts, monopoly concessions, subsidized credit, and protection from competitors — including, critically, protection from the periodic eruptions of anti-Chinese popular sentiment that the New Order both suppressed and instrumentalized as needed. The term cukong, derived from a Hokkien word for "boss," carried a resonance in New Order Indonesia that blended commercial reality with ethnic politics and official condescension: it simultaneously described a business arrangement and stigmatized the ethnic group most visibly identified with it.27

The most prominent cukong was Liem Sioe Liong (Sudono Salim), a Fujian migrant who had arrived in Java in 1938 and had supplied young Suharto's Central Java military command during the revolutionary and early independence period. After 1966, Liem built the Salim Group into Indonesia's largest conglomerate, and in its heyday it controlled the country's largest private bank (Bank Central Asia), its dominant cement producer, its most important flour milling monopoly (Bogasari), and Indofood, which became the world's largest manufacturer of instant noodles. A 2014 study by Richard Borsuk and Nancy Chng, drawing on extensive interviews with surviving figures, described the Suharto-Liem relationship as a "symbiotic partnership" rooted in mutual obligation: Liem provided capital when the military needed it and built the enterprises Suharto wished to see built; in return, Suharto granted the concessions and protection without which those enterprises could not have flourished in a competitive market.28

Liem was the most visible but far from the only figure in this system. Bob Hasan (Mohammad "Bob" Hasan), Suharto's golfing companion, built a timber and plywood empire through concessions over millions of hectares of Outer Island forest. The Suharto family itself — above all the president's children Siti Hardijanti Rukmana ("Tutut"), Hutomo Mandala Putra ("Tommy"), and Bambang Trihatmodjo — accumulated business interests ranging from toll roads to petrochemicals to, in Tommy's notorious case, a state-protected monopoly on the domestically produced "national car" (Timor), shielded from international competition by tariff exemptions that eventually drew a World Trade Organization ruling against Indonesia.29 By the mid-1990s, estimates of the Suharto family's combined fortune ranged from US$10 billion to US$35 billion, a range that itself reflected the opacity of the arrangements involved.

The concentration of privilege in ethnic-Chinese hands — albeit in a small stratum of politically connected tycoons that represented a tiny fraction of the ethnic-Chinese community — generated resentment that the New Order periodically managed and occasionally stoked. The most dramatic early expression was the Malari incident of January 1974, when a student protest against Japanese investment and corruption — coinciding with Prime Minister Tanaka's state visit — escalated into riots that burned 144 buildings and 807 vehicles, with eleven people killed, before targeting Chinese-owned businesses in a pogrom dynamic that would recur, on a far larger scale, in May 1998.30 The Malari riots prompted Suharto to impose a series of reforms requiring greater formal Indonesian "partnership" in foreign-investment projects, but they did not dismantle the cukong system; they merely formalized its political risks.

cukong — from Hokkien for "boss": a term used in New Order Indonesia to describe the arrangement by which ethnic-Chinese businessmen provided capital and commercial expertise to politically connected military or government patrons, receiving in return licenses, concessions, and protection. The cukong relationship structured much of the New Order's private sector. Prominent cukong included Liem Sioe Liong (Salim Group), Bob Hasan (forestry), and William Soeryadjaya (Astra automotive group). The term carried pejorative ethnic and class connotations, obscuring the role of the non-Chinese patrons who made the arrangement possible.

Scholarship has debated whether the concentration of wealth in politically connected conglomerates meaningfully retarded Indonesia's growth or was, in a second-best institutional environment, a workable mechanism for mobilizing private capital in the absence of functioning capital markets and the rule of law. Hal Hill and others have argued that in the 1970s and early 1980s, the cukong conglomerates were genuinely productive: they built real factories, created real employment, generated real exports. The problem, in this reading, was less the initial bargain than its intensification in the 1990s, as the Suharto children's interests metastasized beyond the sector-specific concessions of the older generation into economy-wide monopolies and regulatory capture that genuinely distorted resource allocation and suppressed competitive entry. By the mid-1990s, cronyism had ceased to be an arrangement that the technocrats could corral; it was systematically undermining the deregulation agenda itself.

Workers, Forests, and Migrants: The Social Costs of Development

One of the New Order's most deliberately deployed development instruments was the suppression of labour costs. In a labour-abundant economy seeking to attract export manufacturing, low wages could be presented as a natural comparative advantage; in the New Order's political economy, they were also a policy choice, enforced through institutional means. The sole legally recognized trade union federation was the government-sponsored All-Indonesia Workers' Union (SPSI, Serikat Pekerja Seluruh Indonesia), whose leadership was integrated into the corporatist structures of the New Order state and whose primary function was to channel worker discontent into manageable channels rather than to advance wage claims. Independent unions were, until the very end of the New Order, systematically suppressed: the SBSI (Serikat Buruh Sejahtera Indonesia), founded in 1992 by Muchtar Pakpahan, was harassed, its leadership repeatedly arrested, and its right to organize denied on the grounds that only one union federation was legally permitted.31

The human cost of this system was made visible, with terrible clarity, by the murder of Marsinah in May 1993. Marsinah, a twenty-five-year-old factory worker and labour activist at a watch components factory in Porong, East Java, had led her co-workers in a strike demanding higher wages and the reinstatement of dismissed colleagues. Days later she disappeared; her body was found bearing evidence of torture. Nine factory managers and a military officer were initially convicted of involvement — but then acquitted on appeal after it emerged that all ten had been tortured into confessing during interrogation at a military intelligence facility. The case exemplified the pattern that Human Rights Watch documented in its 1994 report on Indonesia: a military apparatus that routinely intervened in labour disputes on the side of management, using extrajudicial coercion to deter organizing activity and maintain Indonesia's "competitive" wage levels.32

In 1994 the daily minimum wage in Medan, Sumatra was approximately US$1.50; strikers demanded US$3.50. When workers at dozens of factories responded to non-payment of even the minimum wage with demonstrations, the military broke up the protests by force. These were not aberrations; they were, as the APSN documented, the routine operation of a system that defined "industrial harmony" as the absence of collective worker action and treated any independent organizing as a subversive challenge to the state's development agenda.33

Environmental degradation proceeded alongside labour repression as a largely invisible cost of New Order growth. The 1967 Basic Forestry Law centralized control over approximately 144 million hectares of forest estate under the Ministry of Forestry — nearly three-quarters of Indonesia's land area — and enabled the government to allocate timber concessions (Hak Pengusahaan Hutan, HPH) across the outer islands to politically connected firms, including those of Suharto's children, military foundations, and cukong associates like Bob Hasan. Exports of tropical hardwood logs and then plywood became a major foreign-exchange earner: Indonesia in the early 1980s supplied roughly 80 percent of global tropical hardwood plywood exports, a share achieved by allowing harvesting far in excess of sustainable yields and by systematically underfunding enforcement of the nominal regulatory limits.34 The consequences were measured in the deforestation estimates: from roughly 300,000 hectares per year in 1970, the rate of forest loss climbed to approximately 900,000 hectares per year in the period 1980–86.35 By the time the New Order ended, some 16.6 million hectares of the 69.4 million hectares allocated to HPH concessions had been classified as degraded.

Transmigration — the Indonesian state's programme of relocating people from the densely populated inner islands of Java, Bali, and Lombok to the thinly settled outer islands of Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi, and Irian Jaya — was both one of the New Order's most ambitious social-engineering projects and one of its most contested. The programme had colonial roots, dating to Dutch efforts to relieve Javanese population pressure from 1905, but under Suharto's New Order it reached an entirely different scale: between 1979 and 1988, approximately two million transmigrants were relocated at a programme cost estimated at around US$7 billion, or roughly US$7,000 per migrant family — an extraordinary figure that, as critics noted, absorbed thirty to forty percent of development budgets allocated to the outer islands in some years.36 The New Order's motivations were a mix of the Malthusian (relieving Java's population pressure), the developmental (bringing labour to underused outer-island agricultural land), and the political (settling Javanese migrants in ethnically distinct regions, a nation-building strategy with destabilizing long-term consequences in places like Kalimantan and Irian Jaya). The outcomes were persistently disappointing: transplanting wet-rice agriculture to dryland environments that had not been adequately surveyed produced low yields, high settler attrition, and frequently the clearance of rainforest for agriculture that quickly proved unsuitable. The programme's environmental and social costs were high; its economic returns were poor; its contribution to Java's demographic pressure was negligible.

Regional disparities were the structural context within which all these costs accumulated. The New Order's political economy was effectively a system in which the natural-resource wealth of the outer islands — petroleum from Sumatra and Kalimantan, timber from Kalimantan and Sulawesi, copper and gold from Irian Jaya — was extracted and channelled through Jakarta, with the revenues redistributed in ways that consistently favoured Java. Military and fiscal centralization meant that regional governments had few independent revenues and little autonomy in allocating what revenues there were. The provinces of Aceh, Riau, and East Kalimantan, which generated the greatest petroleum revenues, received a share that their populations regarded as insultingly small given the environmental and social disruption that extraction brought to their territories — a resentment that fed directly into the separatist movements that would destabilize the country after 1998.

The Volcano Beneath: Structural Vulnerabilities and the Coming Crisis

In the mid-1990s, the surface of the Indonesian economy gleamed. GDP growth in 1996 reached 8.0 percent. Poverty had fallen below eleven percent. Indonesia was regularly cited alongside South Korea, Malaysia, and Thailand as proof that developing-country growth was a permanent feature of the new global economy. The World Bank's resident economists in Jakarta were broadly optimistic. And yet, beneath the surface, structural vulnerabilities had accumulated that would prove catastrophic when external pressure arrived in 1997.

The most dangerous was the private-sector short-term dollar debt. The banking deregulation of 1988 had been an instrument of growth: the more than two hundred private commercial banks that proliferated in its wake financed the construction boom, the consumer credit expansion, and the investment surge of the early 1990s. But the banks were inadequately supervised, their lending was deeply intertwined with the property and conglomerate interests of their owners, and their non-performing loan ratios — the share of outstanding credit that borrowers could not service — were by the mid-1990s far higher than the official figures acknowledged. Bank Indonesia, the central bank, lacked both the independence and the technical capacity to enforce prudential standards against institutions connected to politically powerful patrons.37

Meanwhile, Indonesian corporations — and the banks lending to them — had discovered that they could borrow US dollars on international markets at interest rates far below domestic rupiah rates, because the crawling-peg exchange rate policy maintained by Bank Indonesia created the impression of exchange-rate stability. This arbitrage was rational at the individual firm level and catastrophically dangerous at the systemic level: it created an enormous stock of unhedged, short-term, dollar-denominated liabilities matched against rupiah-denominated assets. When the Thai baht was floated on 2 July 1997 and contagion spread rapidly across Southeast Asian currency markets, investors who had been happy to assume that Indonesia's exchange-rate stability would continue suddenly reassessed the risk. Capital flowed out; the rupiah came under pressure; Bank Indonesia widened and then abandoned its crawling-peg band in August 1997. As the rupiah fell, the dollar debt burden of private corporations — valued in rupiah — soared, triggering a cascade of corporate bankruptcies, bank failures, and asset sales that fed back into further currency depreciation.38

The crony capitalism that had coexisted with growth in the 1970s and 1980s made the crisis dramatically worse. Banks owned by politically connected figures had lent to the business interests of their owners' patrons on terms that reflected political obligation rather than creditworthiness, creating concentrated portfolios of NPLs that were exposed as worthless once the boom ended. The IMF, which negotiated a US$43 billion emergency package with Indonesia in October 1997, made the closure of sixteen insolvent banks a condition of the first disbursement — but the selection of which banks to close, and the exemption of others connected to Suharto's family, exposed the government's inability to apply consistent rules in a crisis of its own making, undermining confidence further. By January 1998, the rupiah had lost more than seventy percent of its pre-crisis value. The GDP, which had grown 8.0 percent in 1996 and 4.7 percent in 1997, would contract by an estimated 13.6 percent in 1998.39

The crisis was not purely external, and it was not — as some New Order apologists subsequently argued — simply the product of irrational speculative contagion. It was the culmination of systemic choices. The banking deregulation of 1988 had been conducted without the regulatory infrastructure to manage the risks it unleashed. The exchange rate had been maintained at an overvalued level too long, subsidizing dollar borrowers at the expense of export competitiveness. The cronyism of the conglomerates had infected the banking system with loans that were never creditworthy. The political incapacity to reform any of these arrangements — because they were tied to the president's own patronage network and his family's economic interests — meant that each warning sign was ignored rather than addressed. The Asian financial crisis was the spark; the tinderbox had been constructed, methodically, over thirty years of the New Order's economic success.

Summary

Indonesia's New Order economic record is a study in genuine achievement and deep systemic failure existing simultaneously. The Berkeley Mafia technocrats achieved a remarkable macroeconomic stabilization after 1966, bringing inflation from over six hundred percent to manageable levels within three years and opening the economy to foreign investment through the 1967 investment laws. The oil booms of the 1970s funded massive development investments and the Green Revolution that made Indonesia a rice self-sufficient nation by 1984 — though the Pertamina crisis of 1975, with its US$10 billion debt, exposed the institutional fragility beneath the oil-fuelled facade. The deregulation turn of the 1983–88 period, forced by collapsing oil prices, produced a genuine structural transformation: export manufacturing grew from near-negligible to rival petroleum revenues by the early 1990s, and average GDP growth of seven percent over three decades reduced poverty from sixty percent to eleven percent of the population — a record the World Bank celebrated in its 1993 "East Asian Miracle" report.

The discontents were structural and deep. Crony capitalism organised around the cukong relationship between ethnic-Chinese tycoons and military patrons concentrated wealth in politically protected conglomerates rather than competitive markets. Labor repression kept wages artificially low and cost Marsinah her life in 1993. Environmental degradation accelerated as HPH forestry concessions handed out to Suharto cronies cleared millions of hectares of outer-island forest. The transmigration programme relocated two million people at enormous cost and with poor results. Regional disparities left resource-rich outer provinces chronically underfunded relative to their contributions to national revenues. And the banking deregulation of 1988, conducted without adequate supervision, created the short-term dollar debt exposure and non-performing-loan accumulation that would make Indonesia the hardest-hit economy in the entire 1997–98 Asian financial crisis.

Review & Discussion Questions

33.1 What were the core policy prescriptions of the Berkeley Mafia, and in what ways did they succeed in stabilizing the Indonesian economy after 1966? What were the political and social conditions that enabled their recommendations to be implemented?
33.2 The 1975 Pertamina crisis has been described as revealing structural rather than merely individual problems in Indonesian governance. What does Peter McCawley's analysis tell us about the institutional weaknesses the crisis exposed, and were those weaknesses ever genuinely repaired?
33.3 Compare the Green Revolution's social and environmental impacts in Indonesia with its aggregate production achievements. To what extent was the 1984 rice self-sufficiency a sustainable outcome, and who benefited most and least from the programme?
33.4 Evaluate the debate between the Berkeley Mafia technocrats and B.J. Habibie's strategic industries vision. What were the strongest arguments on each side, and what does Indonesia's subsequent economic history suggest about which approach was more correct?
33.5 The World Bank's 1993 "East Asian Miracle" report grouped Indonesia with far more advanced economies. How appropriate was that grouping? What evidence supports the comparison, and what evidence complicates or undermines it?
33.6 Analyse the cukong system as a feature of New Order political economy. Was it purely extractive, or did it serve developmental functions? What were the implications for ethnic relations between Chinese Indonesians and the pribumi majority?
33.7 What does the murder of Marsinah in 1993 reveal about the relationship between the New Order's labour policies and its economic development strategy? How did the government's treatment of labour activism compare with its treatment of other forms of political dissent?
33.8 To what extent was the 1997–98 financial crisis in Indonesia the product of external contagion, and to what extent was it the predictable consequence of internal structural choices? Which specific policy decisions made Indonesia most vulnerable?
