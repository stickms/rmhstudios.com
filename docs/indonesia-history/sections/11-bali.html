Learning Objectives

  • Explain why Bali is described as the great survival of the Hindu-Javanese world, and weigh that idea against its colonial origins.
  • Trace early Balinese history from its first inscriptions and the Warmadewa kings through the centuries of Javanese influence.
  • Assess the tradition that Majapahit elites fled to Bali after Java's Islamization, and the courts of Gelgel and Klungkung that claimed that inheritance.
  • Describe the distinctive features of Balinese Hinduism (Agama Hindu Dharma): its temple system, ritual calendar, caste, and the subak irrigation cooperatives.
  • Understand how wayang, dance, and a living manuscript tradition preserved and transformed the classical Javanese heritage into the colonial era.

When Islam spread across Java in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries and the great inland empire of Majapahit dissolved, the Hindu-Buddhist civilization that had built Borobudur, Prambanan, and the temples of Trowulan did not simply vanish. It migrated, adapted, and endured on a single small island east of Java. For five hundred years Bali has been the one place in the archipelago where the gods of the classical age were never displaced — where temples still smoke with offerings to Shiva, where priests still chant in Old Javanese, and where the rice terraces are managed by the same sacred logic that organized them a thousand years ago. This chapter follows that thread of continuity: how a Hindu-Javanese world washed up on Bali, took root, and became something unmistakably its own.

The last stronghold of an older world

Bali is, today, the only Hindu-majority province of the world's largest Muslim country: roughly 86 percent of its people profess Agama Hindu Dharma, the official name of the Balinese religion.1 That singular fact has made the island irresistible to a particular story — that Bali is a "living museum," a fragment of the medieval Hindu-Javanese past somehow preserved intact after the rest of the archipelago turned to Islam. The Indian historian R. C. Majumdar called Bali "the last stronghold of Indo-Javanese culture and civilisation," and the phrasing has stuck.2 There is real truth in it. But the metaphor of the museum is also a trap, and it is worth disarming at the outset.

The image of Bali as a timeless paradise was, in large part, deliberately manufactured. In the 1920s and 1930s the Dutch colonial government, having only just conquered the island through two notorious massacres, set out to present Bali to the world as "a kind of living museum of culture" — an idyllic, artistic Eden whose careful staging helped gloss over the bloody reality of subjugation.3 The anthropologist Michel Picard devoted a chapter of his study of Balinese tourism precisely to this construction, "A 'Living Museum' of Indo-Javanese Civilization," arguing that the islanders' own sense of having a "culture" was itself partly shaped by the colonial and touristic gaze.4 The historian Adrian Vickers made the same point in his aptly titled Bali: A Paradise Created: the island's reputation as an unspoiled relic was propagated as much by its visitors — scholars, painters, and tour operators — as by its own people.5

The honest version of the claim is therefore narrower and more interesting than the tourist brochure. Bali genuinely did preserve a Hindu-Buddhist civilization that was extinguished elsewhere in the archipelago; the Encyclopedia of Religion reasonably calls it the home of "the last surviving Hindu-Buddhist civilization of Indonesia."6 But that civilization was never frozen. The Balinese did not merely keep an inheritance — they actively rebuilt, recopied, reinterpreted, and reinvented it, century after century, so thoroughly that the "Hinduism" practised on Bali today would be barely recognizable to a fourteenth-century Majapahit courtier. Continuity, in Bali, was a kind of continuous work. That is the throughline of this chapter.

Agama Hindu Dharma — the formal name of the Balinese Hindu religion, adopted in the mid-twentieth century. It is best understood not as imported Indian Hinduism but as a locally evolved system that fuses Shaiva and Buddhist elements with ancestor veneration and indigenous belief, and that prizes correct ritual practice over fixed doctrine.

Early Bali: stupikas, the Belanjong pillar, and the Warmadewa kings

Long before any of this, Bali was an island of Austronesian rice farmers. The ancestors of today's Balinese arrived during the Neolithic, some time after roughly 3,000 BCE, bringing wet-rice cultivation and the irrigation knowledge that would later flower into the subak system; stone tools and earthenware excavated at Cekik, in the island's west, are about three thousand years old.7 Indian religious ideas reached Bali early. The first traces of writing on the island are Buddhist: small clay votive figurines called stupikas, stamped with Buddhist formulae and found at Pejeng, Tatiapi, and Blahbatuh in the Gianyar district, dated to about the eighth century CE — evidence that Buddhism was already established when the Chinese pilgrim Yijing noted a Buddhist presence in the southern seas in the seventh century.8

Bali enters recorded history with a king and a date. The Belanjong pillar (Prasasti Blanjong), a stone column at Sanur, is dated to 4 February 914 CE and was raised by a ruler named Sri Kesari Warmadewa. It is a remarkable object: inscribed in two languages, Sanskrit and Old Balinese, and in two scripts, an Indian Nagari and an Old Balinese hand, it commemorates military victories at places called Gurun and Suwal and styles its author a "universal sovereign" (samasta-samanta-adhipatih).9 Sri Kesari is the first king of Bali whose name survives in writing, and he gave his name — or at least his royal title, Warmadewa — to a loose line of monarchs who ruled over the following century and a half.

Historians are careful here. The "Warmadewa dynasty" is best defined not as a proven hereditary bloodline but as a group of rulers who shared the element Warmadewa in their titles. The sequence runs from Sri Kesari through Ugrasena, Tabanendra, Indrajayasingha, and Janasadhu to Udayana, who reigned until 1011, and his sons Marakata and Anak Wungsu.10 The Sembiran copper-plate inscriptions, a series issued between the tenth and thirteenth centuries, show a working society beneath the royal titles: they regulate the affairs of coastal trading communities and the busy port of Julah on the north coast, a reminder that early Bali was tied into the same maritime commercial world as Srivijaya and Java.11

Java comes to Bali: marriage, conquest, and Kawi

From the late tenth century, Bali's history becomes inseparable from Java's. The decisive moment was a marriage. King Udayana Warmadewa wed Mahendradatta, also titled Gunapriya Dharmapatni, a princess of the East Javanese Isyana dynasty and daughter of King Makutawangsawarddhana. The union is usually read as a deliberate political act, binding Bali into the orbit of the East Javanese Mataram realm; Mahendradatta evidently co-ruled, her name frequently placed before her husband's on joint inscriptions of the 990s.12 Their son Airlangga, born around 991, went on to become one of the great kings of East Java, ruling the realm of Kahuripan; through him Java and Bali were briefly joined in a personal union, a relationship documented in the Pucangan inscription of 1041.13 Meanwhile Airlangga's brother Anak Wungsu governed Bali, and it was in this period that Old Javanese — the literary language known as Kawi — became the language of the Balinese elite, an inheritance of incalculable importance for what followed.14

The political connection deepened, and at times turned coercive, over the next three centuries. Balinese kings of the twelfth century, such as Jayasakti and Jayapangus, traced their descent to Airlangga's line; a tradition holds that Jayapangus married a Chinese princess, a union still commemorated in the towering paired effigies of the Barong Landung.15 Then, in 1284, the ambitious Singhasari king Kertanagara — the first Javanese ruler whose designs reached beyond Java itself — sent an expedition that reduced Bali to vassalage, a campaign later recalled in the Nagarakretagama. Bali recovered its independence when Singhasari collapsed in 1292.16

The final and most consequential conquest came from Majapahit. According to Balinese and Javanese tradition, the great minister Gajah Mada launched an invasion in 1342, and after some seven months of fighting defeated the Balinese king at Bedulu (Bedahulu) in 1343. The conquerors installed a vassal court and distributed authority among Javanese nobles; Arya Kenceng, one of these, became the ancestor of the later royal houses of Tabanan and Badung.17 What crossed the strait in the fourteenth century was, in effect, an entire civilization in compressed form: the fourfold caste order, the Kawi script and its literature, wayang theatre, classical dance, and the architecture of split gates and tiered shrines. Whatever its violence, this is the conquest that made Bali culturally Javanese — and that prepared it to become the refuge of the Hindu-Javanese world when that world fell apart at home.

After Majapahit: the courts of Gelgel and Klungkung

The pivot of Bali's whole self-understanding is the fall of Majapahit. The chronology is genuinely debated. Dates for the empire's end range from 1478 — when an internal succession war, the Sudarma Wisuta, shattered the court at Trowulan — to 1527, when the rising Muslim sultanate of Demak finally extinguished the Majapahit remnant that had held on at Kediri.18 What matters for Bali is the human consequence. As the Hindu order on Java crumbled and Islam took the north-coast ports, "a large number of courtiers, artisans, priests, and members of the royalty moved east to the island of Bali."19 Balinese tradition, telescoping a decades-long trickle into a single dramatic event, holds that the entire court of Majapahit relocated to Bali — in effect transferring a whole civilization across the strait.

The emblem of that transfer is a priest. Dang Hyang Nirartha, a Shaiva Brahmin born on Java in Majapahit's twilight, crossed to Bali around 1492 during the reign of the Gelgel king Baturenggong. He never returned, the tradition explains, because Hinduism on Java had been suppressed by Islam.20 Nirartha reorganized the Shaivite priesthood, became the revered ancestor of all Balinese pedanda (high priests), founded clifftop temples such as Pura Luhur Uluwatu, and is credited with introducing the towering padmasana, the empty lotus-throne shrine that now crowns Balinese temples. In the figure of Nirartha, the migration of Java's priestly learning to Bali is condensed into a single charismatic life.

The court that received him was Gelgel, the political and ritual centre of Bali from roughly 1460 to 1686. Majapahit had installed a vassal dynasty, first at Samprangan and then at Gelgel under a line tracing to Sri Aji Kresna Kepakisan. Its golden age came under Dalem Baturenggong (who reigned in the first half of the sixteenth century), remembered as the most renowned of the Balinese kings: he unified the island, including the offshore Nusa Penida, and projected Balinese influence across the strait into the eastern Javanese districts of Pasuruan and Blambangan and eastward to Lombok and Sumbawa.21 Under Baturenggong's patronage, and with Nirartha as his spiritual partner, the distinctive Balinese synthesis took institutional shape. Gelgel's authority finally broke in the later seventeenth century, when a powerful minister, I Gusti Agung Maruti, usurped the throne; he was overthrown and killed in 1686.22

From the wreckage of Gelgel emerged the court that would carry the Majapahit mantle to the very end. Dewa Agung Jambe founded a new royal seat nearby at Klungkung (Semarapura) around 1686, its palace gate bearing a date corresponding to 1700.23 The old royal title "Dalem" gave way to Dewa Agung, "Great God." The Dewa Agung of Klungkung directly ruled only a small territory, yet he held a unique ritual primacy — a primus inter pares standing — over the nine kingdoms into which Bali had fragmented: Klungkung, Buleleng, Karangasem, Mengwi, Badung, Tabanan, Gianyar, Bangli, and Jembrana.24 His prestige rested less on soldiers than on sanctity, on his descent from the Hindu kings of Majapahit and on the sacred heirlooms (pusaka) believed to channel invisible power. The American anthropologist Margaret Wiener, studying the kingdom's fall, argued that Klungkung's authority must be understood in exactly these terms — as a power rooted in "magical weapons acquired from invisible agents" rather than in the material strength the Dutch expected to find.25

Agama Hindu Dharma: the religion of holy water

What kind of Hinduism did Bali preserve? Not, in any simple sense, the Hinduism of India. Balinese religion is a locally evolved system that places ritual practice — orthopraxy — above doctrinal authority, that stresses communal obligation and ancestor veneration, and that revolves around keeping the visible world (sekala) and the invisible world (niskala) in balance.26 Over the centuries between the fourteenth and the nineteenth, the competing sects brought from Java fused into a single tradition the Balinese themselves called Agama Tirtha, "the religion of holy water," after the consecrated water that is essential to nearly every rite.27

Two priestly orders embody the old Hindu-Buddhist synthesis. The pedanda Siwa (Shaivite high priests, descended from Nirartha) and the much rarer pedanda Boda (Buddhist high priests, traced to the priest Astapaka) cooperate in the greatest ceremonies. But the relationship is not one of equals. As one recent study stresses, the Shiva-Buddha cult in Bali maintains "a clear hierarchy ... with Saivism holding ritual and doctrinal primacy, and Buddhism functioning as its complementary and subordinate counterpart" — a pairing whose roots reach back to the Singhasari king Kertanagara in the thirteenth century.28 Bali thus preserves, in living institutional form, the very Shiva-Buddha amalgam that the Majapahit poet Mpu Tantular celebrated when he coined the phrase later adopted as Indonesia's national motto, Bhinneka Tunggal Ika.

The most visible expression of all this is the temple. Bali is often called the "island of a thousand temples," and the number is no exaggeration. The system is anchored in the Kahyangan Tiga, the three temples that every customary village is expected to maintain — an arrangement attributed to King Udayana and his eleventh-century priest Mpu Kuturan, who is said to have reconciled nine quarrelling sects at Pura Samuan Tiga.29 The three temples map onto the Hindu trinity and onto the island's sacred geography, which is oriented along an axis from the mountains (kaja, pure) to the sea (kelod):

The three village temples (Kahyangan Tiga)
  • Pura Puseh — the "temple of origin," dedicated to Vishnu the preserver, sited on the mountainward, upstream side of the village.
  • Pura Desa (or Bale Agung) — the village temple of communal assembly, dedicated to Brahma the creator, at the settlement's centre.
  • Pura Dalem — the "temple of the dead," dedicated to Shiva in his dissolving aspect and to the goddess Durga, sited on the seaward side, beside the cemetery.
Each temple follows the threefold trimandala plan, its courtyards graded from the most sacred inner enclosure (utama mandala) to the profane outer zone (nista mandala).

Above the village temples rise the great state and "directional" sanctuaries, and above them all stands Pura Besakih, the Mother Temple, on the slopes of the volcano Gunung Agung at about 900 metres. A complex of some eighty-six temples ascending six terraces of the mountainside, focused on the Pura Penataran Agung, Besakih takes its name from the Sanskrit wasuki, "salvation"; it is first mentioned in an inscription of 1007 CE and has served as the central temple of Balinese Hinduism since the fifteenth century.30 It is here that Bali stages its greatest purifications. The Panca Wali Krama is celebrated roughly every ten years to rebalance the cosmos; the Eka Dasa Rudra, the very greatest rite, falls only about once a century. The twentieth-century history of the latter is itself a parable of continuity under strain: an attempt to hold Eka Dasa Rudra in 1963 was overtaken by a catastrophic eruption of Gunung Agung that killed perhaps fifteen hundred people, and the ceremony was only successfully completed in 1979, over eleven weeks of some thirty rituals drawing around a hundred thousand participants.31

Balinese ritual life runs on two interlocking calendars, and their rhythm is itself a survival from the Indic past. The Pawukon is a 210-day calendar of ten concurrent week-cycles, from the one-day week to the ten-day week, including the five-day market week (Pancawara) and the familiar seven-day week; the island-wide festivals of Galungan and Kuningan recur with it every 210 days, as does the odalan, the anniversary celebration of each individual temple — so frequent across the island's thousands of temples that scarcely a day passes without one somewhere.32 Running alongside it is the lunar-solar Saka calendar, inherited from India, whose new year is marked by Nyepi, the extraordinary Day of Silence on which the entire island shuts down — no fires, no travel, no work — in a collective act of purification.33

Caste without untouchability: the Balinese wangsa

Among the institutions Bali inherited from Hindu-Javanese Majapahit, none is more distinctive — or more easily misunderstood — than its caste system, the wangsa or catur wangsa, the "four lineages." In descending order these are the Brahmana (priests and scholars, who bear the titles Ida Bagus and Ida Ayu), the Satria or Ksatria (royalty and nobility, with titles such as Anak Agung, Cokorda, and Dewa Agung), the Wesia (officials and merchants), and the great majority, the Sudra or jaba ("outsiders"), who make up around ninety-three percent of Balinese and whose familiar birth-order names — Wayan, Made, Nyoman, Ketut — recur across the island.34 The upper three groups together, the triwangsa, form a thin aristocratic stratum of perhaps seven percent.

The crucial point is how unlike the Indian model this is. In Bali, Sudra status carries no implication of ritual impurity, untouchability, or exclusion from religious life. On the contrary, it is ordinarily the Sudra majority who make the offerings to the gods, and participation in temple worship is governed primarily by membership in the village and its irrigation and neighbourhood associations — the banjar — rather than by caste rank.35 Caste in Bali has historically been encoded above all in language, in the choice between "high" and "low" registers of Balinese speech depending on the relative status of speaker and listener, though even this is eroding as Indonesian becomes the everyday tongue. The Dutch, in their Baliseering policy of 1929, codified and rigidified aristocratic titles for administrative convenience, freezing a system that had in practice always allowed some mobility.36 In recent decades the official Hindu council has adopted a more "democratic" interpretation, permitting non-Brahmins to be initiated as priests; the cultural commentator Jean Couteau observes that in modern Bali "a class system is slowly superseding the caste system."37

Subak: an engineered landscape of cooperation

If the temple expresses Balinese continuity vertically, toward the gods, the subak expresses it horizontally, across a thousand years of farming the same volcanic slopes. A subak is a self-governing cooperative of rice farmers who share the water of a single source. The word itself, derived through kasuwakara and suwak, carries the sense of cooperation, and the institution is documented from the ninth century.38 There are something like 1,200 to 1,500 subak on Bali, each comprising anywhere from fifty to several hundred farmers, each governed by its own written code (awig-awig) and led by an elected head, the pekaseh, who oversees the equitable division of water down a branching network of channels and tunnels.

What makes the subak extraordinary, and what earned it UNESCO recognition, is that it is not merely an engineering arrangement but a religious one. Water management is woven into a hierarchy of "water temples," from the supreme Pura Ulun Danu Batur beside the crater lake of Mount Batur — revered as the source of all the island's springs and rivers — down through regional temples to the small shrines at each subak's weir and in each farmer's field.39 Sixteen distinct ritual stages mark the rice cycle from planting to harvest. As one Balinese saying has it, "religion is like the blood running through the subak."

That this is not mere folklore was demonstrated, strikingly, by the anthropologist J. Stephen Lansing. Working with the ecologist James Kremer, Lansing built a computer model of the watershed and showed that the staggered, temple-coordinated planting schedules are not arbitrary tradition but an elegant solution to a real ecological problem: by synchronizing fallow periods across a region, farmers starve out rice pests, while staggering water use prevents shortages. When the Green Revolution of the 1970s pushed Balinese farmers to abandon the temple calendar and plant continuously, the result was a collapse — pest infestations destroyed up to half of some harvests — until the government reversed course in 1988 and restored the authority of the water temples.40 The subak, Lansing argued, is a self-organizing system in which a religious institution performs a sophisticated managerial function that no central bureaucracy had ever needed to design.

This fusion of the agricultural, the social, and the sacred is captured in the Balinese philosophy of Tri Hita Karana, the "three causes of well-being": harmony with the divine (parahyangan), harmony among people (pawongan), and harmony with nature (palemahan).41 It was precisely as a "manifestation of the Tri Hita Karana philosophy" that UNESCO inscribed the subak landscape on the World Heritage List on 6 July 2012, under the title "Cultural Landscape of Bali Province." The listed property — over 19,500 hectares across five components, including the Pura Ulun Danu Batur, Lake Batur, the ancient Pakerisan watershed with its rock-cut temples at Tirta Empul and Gunung Kawi, the broad terraces of Catur Angga Batukaru named in a tenth-century inscription, and the eighteenth-century royal water temple of Pura Taman Ayun — recognizes the subak as a continuously functioning democratic institution more than a thousand years old.42

Wayang, dance, and the survival of classical letters

Perhaps Bali's greatest act of cultural preservation was literary, and it was almost invisible. The classical literature of Java — the great Old Javanese poems composed at the Hindu courts between the ninth and sixteenth centuries — survives today overwhelmingly because the Balinese kept copying it. These kakawin, long narrative poems in Kawi using Sanskrit-derived metres, include the Kakawin Ramayana of around 870, the Arjunawiwaha of about 1030, the Bharatayuddha of 1157, and Mpu Prapanca's Nagarakretagama of 1365.43 On Java, after the Islamic transition, this corpus would very largely have been lost. It was, in the judgement of one reference work, only "the painstaking efforts of generations of Balinese literati," recopying the texts onto fresh palm-leaf roughly once a century, that saved the entire literature of Classical Javanese for the world.44 The single surviving manuscript of the Nagarakretagama itself, the indispensable source for the history of Majapahit, was recovered in 1894 not from Java at all but from the ruins of the Balinese-ruled royal palace of Cakranagara on Lombok.45

The medium of this survival was the lontar, the palm-leaf manuscript. The name comes from ron (leaf) and tal (the lontar palm). Leaves of the palm were cured for as long as two years, inscribed with a metal stylus (pangrupak), and then rubbed with the soot of burnt candlenut to bring out the incised letters; the finished manuscripts, covering everything from scripture and medicine to genealogies and tales, were and are venerated objects, honoured at the festival of the goddess of learning, Saraswati.46 The Gedung Kirtya library in Singaraja preserves thousands of them, and a digitization project completed in 2011 captured some three thousand manuscripts — around 130,000 leaves, perhaps ninety percent of Bali's surviving literature — for posterity.47 The custodians of this tradition were the Brahmana and aristocratic elites, for whom literacy in Kawi and Sanskrit was both a sacred duty and a mark of status.

The same stories the lontar preserved come alive in performance. Wayang kulit, the shadow theatre, projects intricately cut and painted leather puppets against a lit cloth screen to dramatize the Mahabharata and Ramayana. The puppeteer, the dalang, is no mere entertainer but a consecrated figure, an amangku dalang, who functions as a priest; the most sacred form, the wayang lemah, is performed in daylight, without screen or lamp, purely as a ritual of purification and exorcism.48 Balinese wayang differs from its Javanese parent in telling ways — more naturalistic puppets, a flickering coconut-oil lamp, the bright quartet of the gender wayang ensemble, and a dalang who sings the female parts himself rather than relying on a separate vocalist.

Accompanying the puppets and dancers is the gamelan, the bronze percussion orchestra whose ancestry on the islands reaches back at least to the carved reliefs of eighth-century Borobudur and which UNESCO inscribed on its list of intangible heritage in 2021.49 Balinese gamelan is the bright, sudden, dazzling cousin of the soft Javanese court style; its instruments are deliberately tuned in pairs slightly apart, so that the two pitches beat against each other to produce the shimmering acoustic effect the Balinese call ombak, "waves." Its most celebrated modern form, the gong kebyar — the name evokes a sudden bursting into flower — made its public debut in northern Bali around 1915, born partly as old ceremonial instruments were melted down and reforged in the upheaval of the Dutch conquest.50

Bali's dance is famous worldwide, and in 2015 UNESCO recognized three genres of it, graded by sacredness: the sacred wali dances performed only in the temple, the ceremonial bebali dances of the middle courtyard, and the balih-balihan dances meant for human entertainment.51 The Legong, an exquisitely refined court dance of darting eyes and fingers traditionally performed by young girls, belongs to the third category. The Barong drama stages the eternal, unresolved struggle between the lion-like Barong, protective king of good spirits, and the terrifying witch-queen Rangda — a figure whose mythology Balinese tradition links all the way back to Mahendradatta, the Javanese princess of the tenth century. In its climactic Keris Dance, entranced men turn their daggers against their own chests yet are miraculously unharmed, and the duel typically ends with no victor at all, dramatizing the Balinese conviction that good and evil are not to be conquered but held in perpetual balance.52

The most internationally recognizable of all, the Kecak or "monkey chant," in which scores of bare-chested men sit in concentric rings chanting an insistent cak-cak-cak in place of any orchestra while a Ramayana drama unfolds at the centre, is in fact a modern creation. It was assembled in the 1930s from the older sanghyang trance ritual by the German painter Walter Spies, working with the Balinese dancer Wayan Limbak — a vivid illustration of how Balinese "tradition" has always been a living, inventive thing, capable of producing something wholly new out of inherited materials.53

Into the colonial era: the puputan and the "living museum"

Bali's independence from European rule lasted far longer than Java's, but it ended in two of the most harrowing episodes in the archipelago's colonial history. The Balinese term for them is puputan, from puput, "to finish" or "to end": a mass fight to the death, undertaken in preference to the dishonour of surrender, in which warriors and their families dressed in white cremation garments, received the rites of the dead, and advanced on the enemy carrying blessed kris daggers.54

The first great puputan came in 1906. The pretext was a dispute over the wreck of a Chinese schooner, the Sri Kumala, plundered near Sanur under the Balinese salvage custom of tawan karang when the raja of Badung refused Dutch demands for compensation. On 14 September 1906 a Dutch expedition landed at Sanur, and on 20 September a silent procession, led by the raja of Badung borne on a palanquin and all clad in white, emerged from the Denpasar palace. As they neared the Dutch line the raja signalled a priest to stab him, and the puputan began; the Dutch opened fire with rifles and artillery, while court women flung their gold jewellery at the troops. At least a thousand Balinese died that day, in this and a second puputan at Pemecutan the same afternoon.55 (Who fired the first shot remains contested: Dutch accounts spoke of a stray gunshot or a lance attack, Balinese memory of Dutch volleys into an unarmed procession.)

The end came two years later. In April 1908, resistance to a Dutch opium monopoly brought matters to a head at Klungkung, the seat of the Dewa Agung and the apex of the entire Balinese ceremonial order. The Dewa Agung Jambe led some two hundred white-clad followers out against the Dutch, armed with a legendary kris believed capable of destroying the enemy; the sortie failed, the raja was shot down, and his wives and retainers killed themselves around him.56 With Klungkung's fall, the last independent Balinese kingdom — and the living heir of Hindu Majapahit — passed under direct Dutch rule. This is a story we will tell in full in a later chapter; here it serves to mark the moment when Bali's long political continuity with the classical age was finally severed.

Yet the cultural continuity was not. Indeed, the colonial conquest set in motion a strange epilogue. The bloodshed of the puputans, widely reported in the European press, embarrassed a Dutch government that had just proclaimed a high-minded "Ethical Policy" of guardianship over its colonial subjects.57 In partial atonement and partial calculation, the Dutch reinvented their relationship to Bali through the policy known as Baliseering, "Balinization" — preserving and codifying Balinese custom, restraining missionary activity, and presenting the island to the world as a precious "living museum" of the Hindu-Majapahit past. The politics of conquest gave way to a politics of cultural display, and Bali was put on show, from the opening of tourism around 1914 to the Paris Colonial Exposition of 1931.58 The image of timeless Bali that still draws millions of visitors was, in this sense, forged in the immediate aftermath of the very massacres that destroyed Balinese sovereignty — the final irony in the long history of an island that has always preserved its inheritance by remaking it.

A note on the "living museum." When you encounter the familiar claim that Bali is an unchanging relic of medieval Hindu Java, treat it as a historical artefact in its own right. The phrase was popularized under Dutch colonial cultural policy and amplified by the tourism industry. The defensible truth is that Bali preserved a Hindu-Buddhist civilization extinguished elsewhere — but it did so dynamically, by continuous reinvention, not by standing still.

Summary

Bali is the great survival of the archipelago's Hindu-Buddhist age — the only place where the religion, letters, and arts of the classical Hindu-Javanese world were carried unbroken into modern times. Its recorded history begins with Buddhist stupikas and the bilingual Belanjong pillar of 914 CE and the Warmadewa kings, deepens through a tenth-century royal marriage that tied it to Java, and is reshaped by Singhasari and then Majapahit conquest. When Java turned to Islam and Majapahit fell, courtiers, priests, and the priest Nirartha are remembered as carrying the Hindu-Javanese inheritance east to the Gelgel court and, after 1686, to Klungkung under the Dewa Agung. That inheritance lives on in Agama Hindu Dharma — its three-temple villages, its Pawukon and Saka calendars, its great rites at Pura Besakih, its caste system shorn of untouchability, and the millennium-old subak irrigation cooperatives recognized by UNESCO. It lives on too in wayang, gamelan, dance, and above all in the palm-leaf manuscripts through which Bali preserved the entire literature of Classical Java. Bali's independence ended in the puputans of 1906 and 1908, after which the Dutch reinvented the island as a "living museum" — a label that captures a real survival but obscures the constant reinvention that made it possible.

10.3 The fall of Majapahit is dated anywhere from 1478 to 1527, and Balinese tradition compresses a long process into the dramatic flight of an entire court. How should a historian handle the gap between traditional memory and documentary chronology?
10.4 In what concrete ways does the Balinese caste system differ from the Indian one? What does the absence of untouchability suggest about how an imported institution was adapted?
10.5 Explain how the subak system functions simultaneously as engineering, religion, and self-government. What did J. Stephen Lansing's research reveal about the consequences of treating it as mere superstition?
10.6 The single surviving manuscript of the Nagarakretagama was found in a Balinese-ruled palace on Lombok. What does the survival of Classical Javanese literature on palm-leaf tell us about the relationship between Bali and the Javanese past?
10.7 The puputans of 1906 and 1908 were acts of mass ritual death. How does understanding Balinese concepts of power, honour, and the sacred help explain a choice that Dutch observers found baffling?
10.8 Margaret Wiener argued that Klungkung's power lay in "magical weapons acquired from invisible agents." Why might the Dutch have systematically misjudged the nature of Balinese kingship — and with what consequences?
