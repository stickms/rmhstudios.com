Learning Objectives

  • Explain why the Kutai yupa inscriptions of East Kalimantan (c. 400 CE) mark a decisive threshold from prehistory to history for the Indonesian archipelago.
  • Describe the three generations of the Kutai royal house — Kudungga, Asvavarman, and Mulavarman — and what their names reveal about the pace of Indianization.
  • Analyze the content of the principal Tarumanagara inscriptions (especially Ciaruteun and Tugu) and explain what they disclose about Hindu kingship ideology, brahmanical ritual, and royal statecraft.
  • Assess the strengths and structural limits of epigraphy and Chinese textual sources as evidence for this period.
  • Evaluate the competing scholarly theories about how Indian cultural influence reached the Indonesian archipelago and explain why modern scholarship stresses Southeast Asian agency rather than passive reception.

In the second half of the fourth century of the Common Era — while Rome was fragmented and the Gupta Empire was beginning its classical age in India — a Sanskrit-literate priest in what is now East Kalimantan took a chisel to a stone post and carved the first known written words in the history of the Indonesian archipelago. The inscription was not long: a few lines in elegant Sanskrit commemorating a king's gifts of cattle to Brahmin priests. But those lines crossed a threshold that archaeologists and historians cannot cross from the other side: the boundary from prehistory into history, from an era knowable only through bones, pots, and stone tools into one where human beings have left us, in their own language, an account of themselves. This chapter examines those first words — the yupa pillars of Kutai in East Kalimantan and the stone inscriptions of Tarumanagara in West Java — and asks what they tell us about the earliest recognizable kingdoms of the Indonesian archipelago, what questions they cannot answer, and how Indian ideas of kingship, ritual, and script came to be carved into the stone of islands ten thousand kilometres from their South Asian source.

Crossing the Threshold: From Prehistory to History

The distinction between prehistory and history is, at root, a distinction between two kinds of evidence. Prehistory is reconstructed from material remains — stone tools, skeletal fragments, charcoal residue, pollen cores. History, in the older conventional sense, begins where human beings have left written records about themselves. That boundary is artificial and increasingly blurred by disciplines such as oral history and historical archaeology, but it retains analytical force: written sources give access to names, intentions, genealogies, and religious ideas that material culture can only gesture toward.

For the Indonesian archipelago, that boundary falls in the final decades of the fourth century CE. The Austronesian-speaking farming and fishing communities who had inhabited the islands for more than two millennia before that moment are known entirely through archaeology — through their pottery, their burial practices, their bronze drums, and the genetic legacy that links them to the present-day population of the archipelago. They built no cities that have been excavated, left no texts, and cannot speak to us in their own words.1

Then, abruptly, there are words. Seven stone posts, each cut and polished in the manner of the Vedic sacrificial pillars called yupa, were erected at a sanctuary near the Mahakam River in eastern Borneo. Their Sanskrit inscriptions, carved in the early form of the Pallava script, introduce a king called Mulavarman and his lineage, record his munificent gifts to Brahmin priests, and invoke the gods and heroes of the Sanskrit literary tradition. They are the oldest known inscriptions in the Indonesian archipelago — and with their appearance, the pre-literate past gives way, however partially and incompletely, to something approaching documented history.2

The qualifier "however partially and incompletely" is essential. Written records do not simply illuminate the past; they illuminate a particular slice of it, and that slice in early Indonesia is extremely thin. Inscriptions from this period record what kings wished to memorialize, in the language of a learned priestly class, about ceremonial occasions. They say almost nothing about trade routes, tax systems, population movements, agricultural techniques, or the lives of the vast majority of people who left no stone monuments at all. Keeping this structural limitation in mind is the first task of historical reading.

prasasti — the Indonesian/Sanskrit term for a royal inscription carved on stone or metal, functioning as a public memorial of dynastic legitimacy, religious patronage, land grants, or public works. The prasasti is the primary documentary source for the Hindu-Buddhist era of Indonesian history.

Reading Stone: Epigraphy, the Pallava Script, and the Nature of the Evidence

The study of inscriptions — epigraphy — has supplied most of what historians know about the first millennium CE in Southeast Asia.3 In the absence of administrative archives, literary manuscripts from this period, or reliable coin sequences, the stone and copper-plate prasasti constitute the bedrock of the field. Exactly what can they tell us, and what do they inevitably conceal?

Inscriptions excel at certain kinds of testimony. Because they were carved in durable material for public display, they preserve a contemporaneous, fixed record of what their authors wished to assert — dynasties, rituals, land transactions, public works. They are not copies subject to scribal error or interpolation in the way manuscripts are. When a stone pillar in East Kalimantan announces that King Mulavarman gave twenty thousand cows to Brahmin priests, we know that somebody, at approximately that time, wished that assertion to be recorded permanently and publicly — and that is itself historically significant regardless of whether the number was literally accurate.4

But inscriptions share a structural bias that historians must account for. They are elite documents, commissioned by and about the powerful, recording what rulers and their priestly collaborators judged worthy of permanent commemoration. Ordinary people — the farmers who cleared the land, the carpenters who built the palaces, the sailors who carried trade goods — are invisible in the epigraphic record. Inscriptions present official self-image, not social reality. The Ciaruteun stone tells us that King Purnavarman of Tarumanagara wished to be remembered as an avatar of the god Vishnu; it tells us nothing about his subjects' assessment of that claim.5

The script in which both the Kutai and Tarumanagara inscriptions were carved is an early form of the Pallava script, so named for the Pallava dynasty of Kanchipuram in southern India, where it was developed in the fourth century CE from an antecedent called Tamil-Brahmi. The Pallava script is an abugida — a consonant-based writing system in which vowels are indicated by diacritical marks — and it served primarily as a vehicle for Sanskrit, the prestige language of South Asian learning and religion. Some scholars, notably the epigrapher Arlo Griffiths, prefer the label "Late Southern Brahmi scripts" on the grounds that the relevant varieties predate or are independent of Pallava court patronage, but "Pallava script" remains the dominant usage in Indonesian scholarship.6

The Pallava script traveled east along the maritime trade routes that connected South Asia to the archipelago, carried by priests, monks, merchants, and scholars. Crucially, it brought Sanskrit but not the South Indian vernacular languages in which most Indians of the period conversed: the Southeast Asian adopters learned to write in Sanskrit — the prestige register of Brahmanical religion and royal ideology — without any corresponding adoption of Tamil, Telugu, or Kannada. This pattern is itself a clue about the mechanism of Indianization: it points toward targeted, elite-level adoption of Indian prestige culture rather than mass immigration. Epigraphers place the Kutai pillars in the latest decades of the fourth century CE and the Tarumanagara inscriptions in the mid-fifth century, though both datings rest on paleographic analysis of letterforms rather than on any calendar date stated in the texts themselves — a point worth underlining, since many secondary sources write of "c. 400 CE" as though it were a calendrical fact.7

Pallava script — a South Indian Brahmic abugida developed at Kanchipuram in the fourth century CE; the parent script of Javanese, Balinese, Khmer, Thai, Lao, and dozens of other Southeast and South Asian writing systems. In Indonesia, it is the medium of the earliest known inscriptions at Kutai and Tarumanagara.

Kutai: Seven Pillars on the Mahakam

The earliest known inscriptions in the Indonesian archipelago were found not in Java — the later center of everything — but in the interior of Borneo, on the banks of a great river. The Mahakam River drains a vast highland plateau in what is now the Indonesian province of East Kalimantan, running for nearly nine hundred kilometres before emptying into the Makassar Strait. Near its middle course, at a site called Muara Kaman ("the meeting of the estuaries"), Dutch and Indonesian archaeologists recovered seven stone pillars between 1879 and 1940.8 These are the yupa inscriptions of the kingdom scholars call Kutai Martadipura — the oldest written records ever found in the archipelago.

The pillars themselves are shaped like traditional Vedic yupa posts: roughly cylindrical, tapering slightly at the top, cut from locally quarried stone. In India, yupa were ordinarily of wood and served as the post to which the animal victim was tethered in Vedic fire sacrifices; their function as stone monuments in Kutai reflects both the permanence intended by the royal patrons and the brahmanical ritual universe their court had adopted.9 The Kutai pillars are inscribed with Sanskrit verses in the Anustubh meter — the standard epic-and-inscription meter of Sanskrit literature, in which the Mahabharata and Ramayana were composed — carved in the early Pallava script. The foundational study of these inscriptions was made by the Dutch scholar J. Ph. Vogel, who published his analysis and translations in the Bijdragen tot de Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde in 1918, and all subsequent scholarship on Kutai rests on his work.10 The combined inscriptions total no more than approximately fifty lines of Sanskrit verse, making this body of evidence at once revelatory and frustratingly brief.

The inscriptions introduce three successive rulers. The earliest and most senior is Kudungga, described simply as narendra — "lord of men." Then comes his son Asvavarman, titled vaṃśa-kartṛ, "founder of the [royal] lineage." Finally there is Asvavarman's son Mulavarman, styled rājendra, "lord of kings" — the reigning king in whose honor the pillars were erected.11 The pillars were set up not by Mulavarman himself but by the "chiefs of the twice-born" — dvijamukhyaih, i.e., the Brahmin priests — as a record of their having received the king's extraordinary generosity.

The sanctuary where the pillars stood bore a name of Sanskrit derivation, Vaprakesvara — a compound that scholars have interpreted as connecting fire (vāpra, an earthen altar) and Shiva (Iśvara, "the lord"), suggesting a Shaivite cultic focus. The sanctuary thus gives us a glimpse of how brahmanical religion was institutionalized in this remote Bornean polity: a dedicated sacred precinct, named in Sanskrit, staffed by Brahmin priests with the knowledge and authority to compose and carve Sanskrit verses, and patronized by the royal house as the setting for ceremonial gifts and sacrifices.12

Three Generations: Kudungga, Asvavarman, and Mulavarman

The three-generation genealogy preserved in the Kutai yupa is more than a simple king-list; it encodes a narrative about the adoption of Hinduism by a local dynasty, and the details reward careful reading.

The founder, Kudungga, bears a name that is conspicuously not Sanskrit. His descendants are named with the Sanskrit suffix -varman ("shield" or "protector"), a kshatriya warrior-caste marker widely used across South and Southeast Asia; Kudungga's name fits no recognizable Sanskrit etymology. Mainstream scholarship interprets this as strong evidence that Kudungga was a local Bornean chief — possibly of Dayak ancestry, possibly connected to the broader Austronesian world of the east coast of Borneo — who had adopted Hinduism or had brahmanical contacts, but whose personal name still reflected his pre-Sanskritic cultural identity.13 Some scholars have noted a resemblance between "Kudungga" and Bugis names from the Sulawesi region (for instance, Kadungga), suggesting possible southern Sulawesi connections, though this remains speculative. A claim that he descended from India's Shunga dynasty has been assessed by historians as "unsubstantiated, with no historical records proving the claimed ancestors ever existed."14

His son Asvavarman is described as the vaṃśa-kartṛ — "he who established the lineage" — which in Indian usage means the person who formally inaugurated a dynasty according to brahmanical rites. The juxtaposition is illuminating: Kudungga was the political founder and the grandfather of a great king, but it was Asvavarman who performed the ceremonies (possibly a royal consecration, rājasūya) that gave the dynasty its Sanskrit legitimacy. This pattern — a local chieftain whose son or successor underwent formal brahmanical rites of kingship — has been observed across early Southeast Asia and suggests a model in which Hindu ceremonial adoption was a deliberate, generational process rather than a sudden conversion.15

Mulavarman, the king whose reign the pillars celebrate, emerges from the inscriptions as a figure of spectacular generosity and martial success. The texts compare him explicitly to Yudhishthira, the virtuous eldest Pandava of the Mahabharata, who after conquering all kings of the earth performed the grand royal consecration (rājasūya) and offered vast gifts to priests. Mulavarman's stated gift — twenty thousand cows to the Brahmin community, along with gold and land — situates him within the tradition of bahusuvarnaka (the "much-gold sacrifice") that Vedic literature associates with the highest grades of royal munificence.16 Whether twenty thousand cows were literally transferred, or whether the number represents a conventional literary expression of overwhelming generosity, is impossible to determine; but the ideological claim is clear: this was a king who played the role of the ideal Hindu monarch, sustaining the Brahmin priestly class whose ritual expertise legitimated his power.

yupa — in Vedic tradition, the sacrificial post to which an animal victim was tethered. In early South and Southeast Asian epigraphy, stone yupa pillars were erected to commemorate major royal sacrifices and the gifts made to Brahmin priests on those occasions. The Kutai yupa pillars are the oldest surviving examples in the Indonesian archipelago.

Tarumanagara: Java's First Kingdom in Stone

Roughly half a century after the Kutai yupa were erected in eastern Borneo, a different tradition of royal inscription appeared in western Java. The kingdom known as Tarumanagara — the name appears to combine taruma (possibly the name of a local plant used for dyeing, the indigofera) with nagara (Sanskrit for "city" or "realm") — left behind seven stone inscriptions scattered across the modern provinces of West Java, Jakarta, and Banten. All are in early Pallava script and Sanskrit; all celebrate a king named Purnavarman; and collectively they constitute the earliest known written record of a kingdom in Java.17

The geographic setting of Tarumanagara was the alluvial lowlands of the Citarum, Cisadane, and Ciliwung river systems — a well-watered landscape that would later develop into the densely populated hinterland of present-day Jakarta and Bogor. The kingdom's capital, known in Chinese sources as To-lo-mo and in later inscriptions as Sundapura, lay somewhere in the vicinity of modern North Jakarta or the Bekasi coastal plain.18 Unlike the interior sanctuary of Kutai, Tarumanagara's inscriptions are spread across an extensive coastal and riverine landscape, consistent with a kingdom whose power rested on control of productive agricultural land and access to seaborne trade.

Before examining the inscriptions, it is necessary to clear away a significant historiographical complication. A set of manuscripts known as the Wangsakerta codices, supposedly compiled under the authority of a Cirebon prince in the late seventeenth century, supply a detailed king-list for Tarumanagara, complete with founding dates, succession lengths, and the figure of twelve successive kings. Most historians treat these manuscripts with great caution: their late date of compilation, internal inconsistencies, and — according to a number of scholars — evidence of twentieth-century interpolation render the specific dates they supply essentially unverifiable.19 The "founding date" of Tarumanagara conventionally given as c. 358 CE, and Purnavarman's supposed reign dates of 395 to 434 CE, derive from these documents. The stone inscriptions themselves carry no calendar dates; Purnavarman's reign is placed in the mid-fifth century on paleographic grounds alone. This means that the entire chronological framework of Tarumanagara history rests on sources whose reliability is contested, and should be presented accordingly.

External corroboration comes, with its own complications, from Chinese sources. The Book of Liang (Liangshu), a history of the Liang dynasty compiled in the seventh century on the basis of earlier records, notes embassies from a kingdom transcribed as To-lo-mo reaching the Chinese court in 528 and 535 CE. Tang dynasty annals record further embassies in 666 and 669 CE.20 These references, brief as they are, confirm that a polity in western Java was in diplomatic contact with the Chinese empire during a period overlapping with Tarumanagara's presumed floruit. The matches between the Chinese phonetic transcription To-lo-mo and the Sanskrit Tārūmanagara are linguistically plausible, though the identification is not considered beyond all doubt.

Another Chinese account comes from the Buddhist pilgrim Faxian (c. 337–422 CE), who was blown by storms onto what he called Yeh-po-ti (Yavadvipa, likely Java) around 411–413 CE while returning from his extended pilgrimage to India, and remained there for approximately five months. Faxian reported that at Yeh-po-ti "the heretical Brahmans (i.e., Hindus) are very numerous, and the faith of Buddha is little known." This brief account, transmitted in his travel record the Foguoji, is one of the earliest external references to religious conditions in Java and is consistent with the brahmanical orientation visible in the Tarumanagara inscriptions.21 However, scholars debate whether Yeh-po-ti refers specifically to Java or more broadly to a Sumatran coastal point; the dominant view identifies it with Java.

The Seven Stones of Purnavarman: Divine Kingship in Sanskrit

The seven inscriptions left by or about Purnavarman constitute a coherent corpus of royal self-presentation, deploying a sophisticated set of Hindu divine identifications to legitimate his authority.22 They range across the landscape of his kingdom from the Banten coast in the south to the flatlands of North Jakarta, and their geographical scatter itself suggests a ruler marking his territory with the most permanent medium available.

The most celebrated of the seven is the Ciaruteun inscription, discovered in 1863 in the bed of the Ciaruteun River, a tributary of the Cisadane approximately nineteen kilometres northwest of modern Bogor. The inscription is carved on a basalt boulder weighing approximately eight tonnes, beside two large footprint carvings in relief. Its four-line Sanskrit verse, composed in Anustubh meter, reads:

vikkrāntasyāvanipateḥ / śrīmataḥ pūrṇṇavarmmaṇaḥ / tārūmanagarendrasya / viṣṇoriva padadvayam

"The powerful illustrious and brave king, the famous Purnavarman of Tarumanagara, whose pair of foot soles are like those of the god Vishnu." 23 The footprint image resonates deeply in Hindu devotional practice: the feet of Vishnu are among the most sacred and widely venerated objects in South Asian religion, and to carve one's footprints as analogous to the divine prints — viṣṇupāda — was to claim a profound sacral status. The inscription simultaneously names the kingdom (Tārūmanagara), identifies the king (Purnavarman), and asserts his divine equivalence: it is at once a territorial marker, a genealogical statement, and a theological declaration.

The Kebon Kopi I inscription (also called the "Tapak Gajah," or elephant-footprint, stone) employs a different divine identification. Instead of human footprints, it displays two large carved images of elephant feet, with a Sanskrit verse in Anustubh meter: "This pair of elephant-foot impressions resembles those of Airavata, [and belongs to] the successful king of Tarumanagara who holds full command."24 Airavata is the celestial elephant who serves as the vahana (divine mount) of the god Indra, king of the gods and lord of storms and battle. By equating his elephant with Airavata, Purnavarman positioned himself in the role of Indra — the divine warrior-king who maintained cosmic and political order through force. This Indra identification reinforces a motif of military dominance, distinct from the Vishnu theme of cosmic sovereignty in the Ciaruteun text.

The Jambu inscription, found in the hills of Bogor, combines the two divine identifications, praising Purnavarman as possessing "the bravery of Indra, the glory of the sun (Surya)" while his enemies cannot penetrate his armor. The language of invincibility and divine martial protection is consistent across the Kutai and Tarumanagara inscriptions: both corpora present rulers who have absorbed — and publicly proclaimed — the Sanskrit literary vocabulary of cosmic kingship.

The Cidanghiang inscription in Banten, the southernmost of the corpus, refers to the king by the title vikrānta — a word associated with the Trivikrama myth of Vishnu, in which the god reclaims the universe from the demon king Bali in three world-spanning strides. This Trivikrama reference reinforces the Vishnu identification of the Ciaruteun text and suggests a coherent theological programme: Purnavarman was not merely invoking random Hindu images, but building a consistent royal persona around specific Vaishnavite myths of cosmic sovereignty.25

The Pasir Awi and Muara Cianten inscriptions, both in the Bogor hills and first recorded by the Dutch official N. W. Hoepermans in 1864, present additional footprint imagery; the Muara Cianten text has not been fully deciphered and may represent an undeciphered local script element alongside the Sanskrit. Their very existence, however scattered and incomplete, confirms the spatial reach of Purnavarman's commemorative programme across a substantial geographic area.

devaraja — Sanskrit for "god-king"; the ideological concept in which the ruler is understood as an earthly manifestation or avatar of a major Hindu deity (most commonly Vishnu or Shiva). Expressed in the Tarumanagara inscriptions through the equation of Purnavarman's footprints with those of Vishnu, the devaraja concept was central to early Southeast Asian political theology and would recur throughout the Hindu-Buddhist era.

Hydraulic Kingship: The Tugu Inscription and Royal Public Works

Among the seven inscriptions of Purnavarman, the Tugu inscription stands apart for its content: rather than celebrating divine equivalences and royal invincibility, it records an act of state hydraulic engineering. The stone, discovered at Tugu in North Jakarta and now housed in the National Museum of Indonesia, is the longest of the Tarumanagara corpus and the most detailed as a historical source. Its Sanskrit text, in five lines encircling a round stone, records major works on two watercourses — the Candrabhaga and the Gomati — undertaken in the twenty-second year of Purnavarman's reign.26

The inscription records that the Gomati canal — identified by the text with the sacred Indian river of the same name — was dug by "the excellent king whose armour cannot be pierced" in the month of Phalguna (corresponding to roughly February-March). The canal measured 6,122 bows in length — an Indian unit of measurement that scholars convert to approximately eleven to twelve kilometres — and was completed in twenty-one days. Upon its completion, Purnavarman presented one thousand cows to the assembled Brahmin priests as a consecration gift, securing divine blessing for the hydraulic works.27

This inscription is remarkable for several reasons. First, it is among the earliest surviving records anywhere in Southeast Asia of a ruler undertaking large-scale hydraulic works for agricultural purposes. The canalization of what appear to be the Cakung River lowlands (the identification of the Sanskrit river names with specific modern watercourses remains debated) was evidently intended to address seasonal flooding and extend irrigation to rice paddies — precisely the kind of "hydraulic statecraft" that historians of Southeast Asia have identified as a key legitimating function of early agrarian kingdoms.28 Second, the combination of the engineering feat with the brahmanical consecration ceremony is characteristic of the "dharmaraja" ideal — the righteous king who provides material welfare for his subjects through works sanctioned by priestly ritual. Third, the specific mention of the twenty-second regnal year is the most precise chronological marker in the entire Tarumanagara corpus, even though it tells us nothing about absolute chronology without an anchor date for the start of Purnavarman's reign.

The Tugu inscription's record of cattle gifts — one thousand cows for the canal works; twenty thousand for the Kutai sacrifices — points to a pattern common across early Indianized polities: the brahmanical priesthood was sustained by royal land and cattle grants, and this mutual dependency between king and priest structured the political economy of the court. Brahmins provided ritual expertise, cosmological legitimation, and written Sanskrit — without which the entire ideology of divine kingship could not be articulated or recorded. Kings provided the material support without which brahmanical learning could not be maintained. The inscriptions are themselves artifacts of this exchange: composed and carved by priests, commemorating the kings who paid for them.

Other Early Polities and the Limits of Knowledge

Kutai and Tarumanagara are not the only polities that ancient sources associate with the Indonesian archipelago in the first several centuries CE, but they are the only ones with contemporary inscriptional evidence. A brief survey of other alleged or documented early kingdoms illustrates, in instructive contrast, both the possibilities and the stark limits of this period's evidence base.

Ho-ling / Kalingga

Chinese sources from the Tang period (618–907) describe a kingdom they call Ho-ling (訶陵) or, alternatively, Ka-ling — clearly a transcription of the Indian name Kalingga, connecting this polity to the Kalinga region of eastern India. The Xin Tangshu (New Tang History) locates Ka-ling "in the southern sea" to the east of Sumatra and places its capital somewhere on the northern coast of Java, in the vicinity of modern Pekalongan or Jepara (some scholars note that the modern Keling district in Jepara Regency may preserve the name).29 The kingdom appears in Chinese records from the mid-sixth to the early ninth century, and the Buddhist pilgrim Yijing, whose highly reliable accounts of Srivijaya and surrounding regions date to the 670s and 680s, located Ho-ling to the east of Srivijaya, "four or five days' sail."30

The most vivid account of Ho-ling centers on a queen known in Chinese transliteration as Shima (c. 674 CE), celebrated for the severity of her justice. According to the Tang records, she placed bags of gold in the road to test her subjects' honesty; when her own son accidentally touched one bag with his foot, she had his foot severed as punishment — and when courtiers pleaded that the punishment be reduced, she had only the foot cut off rather than the entire person.31 The story, which circulated across multiple Tang-era texts, clearly embellishes a reputation for exemplary rulership, but its very currency in Chinese diplomatic sources suggests a polity of significant regional standing. Ho-ling sent multiple embassies to the Tang court and received Chinese Buddhist monks who came to study Sanskrit and Buddhist doctrine with local scholars. The monk Huining, who visited Ho-ling in 664 CE and collaborated with local scholars on translating Buddhist texts, provides one of the most concrete early references to sophisticated learning in Central Java.

Crucially, Ho-ling has left no contemporary inscriptions of its own. All historical knowledge of it derives from Chinese annals — themselves composed decades or centuries after the events they describe, subject to phonetic transcription errors, and shaped by the conventions of Chinese diplomatic record-keeping. This makes Ho-ling's historical reconstruction necessarily tentative and its exact location permanently debated.

Salakanagara and the Problem of Legendary Origins

The case of Salakanagara illustrates in the sharpest possible terms the danger of treating all claimed early kingdoms as equally real. Salakanagara is alleged, in the Wangsakerta manuscripts and in some derivative Indonesian-language sources, to have been a kingdom in the Banten region of West Java that flourished from the second to the fourth century CE — potentially predating Tarumanagara. Some scholars have suggested a connection to Ptolemy's second-century Alexandrian geography, which mentions a place called "Argyre" on an island called Iabadiou (= Yavadvipa = Java).32

The problem is fundamental: Salakanagara has left not a single inscription, not a single archaeological site, not a single artifact that scholars agree is associated with it. Its primary documentary source is the Wangsakerta manuscript collection, which is at best of uncertain provenance and at worst a modern forgery. Historians of early Indonesia have largely concluded that Salakanagara should be treated as a legendary tradition — perhaps reflecting genuine oral memory of pre-literate polities that preceded Tarumanagara, perhaps a retrograde projection constructed by later Cirebon court scholars to provide West Java with an ancient pedigree — rather than as documented history.33 Its position in the present chapter is entirely cautionary: it is cited to explain why historians cannot simply accept every claimed early kingdom as real, and why the presence or absence of contemporary inscriptions or archaeological evidence is decisive.

A similar verdict applies to the kingdom of Kendan (claimed c. 526–612 CE, near modern Garut in West Java), which appears in the same Wangsakerta sources as the immediate precursor to the Galuh Kingdom. No inscriptions from Kendan are known, and the earliest verified epigraphic evidence for the Galuh-Sunda region dates to the fourteenth century, more than seven hundred years later.

A note on Chinese transcription: Several terms often cited as early Indonesian kingdom names — including Dvipantara — are not, in fact, drawn from Chinese sources. Dvipantara is a Sanskrit compound meaning "islands in between" (equivalent in meaning to Nusantara); it appears in the Ramayana and in Old Javanese inscriptions, but not in Chinese dynastic records. Students and researchers occasionally encounter claims that Chinese sources "mention Dvipantara" — this is a confusion between Sanskrit geographic vocabulary and Chinese textual evidence, and should be treated with caution.

How India Came to the Islands: The Indianization Debate

The emergence of Sanskrit-writing, brahmanical-ritual, Vishnu-invoking kingdoms in Borneo and Java raises an obvious historical question: how did Indian culture — the Sanskrit language, the Pallava script, the Vedic sacrifice of yupa and cattle, the Hindu theology of divine kingship — reach islands some ten thousand kilometres from India itself? Answering this question has occupied Southeast Asian historians for more than a century, and the scholarly debate it has generated is itself illuminating about the problems of explaining cultural transmission in the ancient world.

The foundational framework was supplied by the French scholar George Coedès (1886–1969) in his monumental synthesis Les États hindouisés d'Indochine et d'Indonésie (1944), translated as The Indianized States of Southeast Asia (1968). Coedès coined the term "Indianized states" to describe the polities of mainland and insular Southeast Asia that had adopted "an organized culture founded upon the Indian conception of royalty, Hinduist or Buddhist cults, the mythology of the Puranas and the observance of the Dharmasastras, expressed in the Sanskrit language."34 His synthesis proposed that Indianization had been a peaceful process of cultural diffusion, driven primarily by the movement of people and texts along the maritime trade routes of the Bay of Bengal, without military conquest.

Within the broad framework of peaceful diffusion, historians proposed several specific mechanisms:

The merchant hypothesis, favored by the early Dutch scholar N. J. Krom and in early formulations by Coedès himself, held that Vaishya (merchant-caste) traders carried Hindu-Buddhist practices to Southeast Asia as a byproduct of commercial contact. The objection is that Indian merchants, outside their own ritual contexts, were unlikely vectors for the transmission of complex Sanskrit learning, Vedic sacrificial rites, and palace architecture — all of which reached Southeast Asia in sophisticated form.35

The kshatriya (warrior) hypothesis, associated with R. C. Majumdar and R. Mookerji, proposed that Indian warriors had colonized Southeast Asian polities through conquest and direct transplantation. This theory has been effectively abandoned: there is no epigraphic or archaeological evidence of Indian military conquest in Southeast Asia, the caste system was never adopted, and the relationship between Indian and Southeast Asian cultures was selective rather than wholesale.36

The Brahmin hypothesis, developed by F. D. K. Bosch and Jan Gonda, proposed that Brahmin priests were invited by Southeast Asian rulers — perhaps in search of ritual legitimation for their authority, or attracted by the wealth of courts that could support learned specialists — and that it was these specialists who transmitted Sanskrit learning, Vedic ceremonial practice, and brahmanical political theology. The evidence in support is strong: it was indeed priests who composed and carved the Kutai yupa and who, by implication, maintained the Sanskrit learning that produced the Tarumanagara inscriptions.37 The hypothesis has the additional virtue of explaining why only the religious and legitimating functions of Indian culture were adopted, while caste hierarchy, the Indian family law, and the vernacular Indian languages were not: Southeast Asian rulers adopted what was useful for statecraft and ceremony, not a wholesale social system.

The deeper revision of the Indianization framework came from the Dutch historian J. C. van Leur (1908–1942), who objected to the implicit assumption that Southeast Asia was a passive recipient of Indian cultural exports. Van Leur argued that Southeast Asian societies were sophisticated civilizations with their own developmental trajectories, and that the adoption of Indian elements represented a deliberate, selective act by local elites — not a cultural vacuum being filled from outside. He proposed the famous image of Indianization as a "thin and flaking glaze" over the enduring structures of local society: real but superficial, covering indigenous forms without transforming them.38 Van Leur died in the Battle of the Java Sea in 1942, leaving his synthesis incomplete, but his insistence on Southeast Asian agency permanently altered the field.

The American historian O. W. Wolters (1915–2000) built on van Leur to develop the model of localization: the process by which Southeast Asian rulers and communities did not simply receive Indian ideas but remade them in local terms, extracting elements of Indian cosmological and political thought that "filled a gap in a ruler's search for authority and legitimacy" and recontextualizing them within pre-existing local frameworks of power and meaning. For Wolters, the Sanskrit inscriptions of Kutai and Tarumanagara were not evidence of Indian cultural dominance but of Southeast Asian rulers creatively deploying Indian cultural resources to make claims intelligible to their subjects and to visiting Indian traders and priests alike.39

The German historian Hermann Kulke proposed the related concept of cultural convergence: "India's culture did not reach Southeast Asia by an act of transplantation but through a complicated convergence of processes of state formation from both sides of the Bay of Bengal." Kulke argued that the subcontinent itself was undergoing processes of state-formation and brahmanical political theology at exactly the period when Southeast Asian polities were adopting Sanskrit culture, suggesting a synchronous regional process driven by similar social needs rather than a one-directional cultural export.40

The current scholarly consensus — if so fluid a field can be said to have one — rejects the kshatriya conquest model entirely, treats the merchant hypothesis as insufficient on its own, accepts the Brahmin vector as the most plausible specific mechanism, and firmly insists on Southeast Asian agency and selectivity as the primary interpretive frame. The term "Indianization" itself is increasingly questioned: scholars such as Michael Vickery have called Coedès's framework "obsolete" as a general theory, even while acknowledging the value of his documentary work.41 The alternative terms "localization" and "convergence" better capture the active, selective process through which the rulers of Kutai and Tarumanagara made Sanskrit and brahmanical ceremony their own — tools of statecraft in a specific Bornean or Javanese context, not transplanted replicas of Indian originals.

Looking at the Kutai inscriptions with this framework in mind, we can see the localization process at work in the very structure of the genealogy. Kudungga's non-Sanskrit name suggests a local chief receiving brahmanical contacts; Asvavarman's formal inauguration of the Sanskrit-named dynasty suggests a deliberate, generational decision to adopt brahmanical statecraft; and Mulavarman's spectacular cattle-gift inscriptions — composed by Indian-trained priests using Mahabharata literary allusions, but commemorating a king whose name and lineage are locally rooted — represent the mature product of that selective adoption. Similarly in Tarumanagara, the canal engineering of the Tugu inscription is a local hydraulic achievement celebrated in the idiom of Sanskrit royal dharma. The content is Javanese; the ceremonial language is Indian; the political claim — that this king provides for his people through divinely sanctioned works — is legible to anyone fluent in the traditions of either civilization.

localization — the scholarly concept, associated especially with O. W. Wolters, describing the process by which Southeast Asian societies did not passively receive Indian cultural exports but actively selected, remade, and recontextualized Indian ideas within their own political and religious frameworks. The alternative to "Indianization" as a description of early Hindu-Buddhist cultural transmission.

Summary

The Indonesian archipelago crosses the threshold from prehistory into history in the final decades of the fourth century CE, when brahmanical priests in East Kalimantan carved the Sanskrit yupa inscriptions of King Mulavarman of Kutai — the oldest known written records in the archipelago. These seven pillars introduce a three-generation royal house (Kudungga, Asvavarman, Mulavarman), record spectacular cattle-gift ceremonies at the Vaprakesvara sanctuary, and demonstrate that a fully formed brahmanical-Hindu court culture had established itself in the interior of Borneo by c. 400 CE. Roughly half a century later, the West Javanese kingdom of Tarumanagara produced seven stone inscriptions — all in Sanskrit and Pallava script — celebrating King Purnavarman as the local avatar of Vishnu and Indra, recording a major hydraulic canal project, and marking the kingdom's territory across a wide arc of the Bogor-Jakarta-Banten landscape. Both corpora reveal the central mechanisms of early Hindu kingship: brahmanical ritual legitimation, divine identification of the ruler, cattle sacrifice as royal munificence, and Sanskrit as the prestige medium of political self-assertion. Knowledge of this period rests on thin and structurally biased evidence — the inscriptions record only what kings chose to commemorate in stone — supplemented by limited and phonetically problematic Chinese textual sources. The alleged kingdoms of Salakanagara and Kendan are not corroborated by contemporary evidence and should be treated as legendary. The scholarly debate over how Indian culture reached these islands — spanning more than a century from Coedès's "Indianization" through van Leur's "local agency" to Wolters's "localization" and Kulke's "convergence" — has converged on a picture of selective, agentive Southeast Asian adoption rather than passive cultural reception: the rulers of Kutai and Tarumanagara made Sanskrit kingship their own on their own terms.

Review & Discussion Questions

5.1 What does it mean to say that the Kutai yupa inscriptions are "paleographically dated" rather than "calendrically dated," and why does this distinction matter for historical reliability?
5.2 The name "Kudungga" is not Sanskrit, while his son Asvavarman's is. What does this generational pattern suggest about the process of Hinduization in the Kutai kingdom, and how does it complicate a simple narrative of "Indian influence"?
5.3 Compare the divine identifications of Purnavarman in the different Tarumanagara inscriptions (Vishnu in Ciaruteun, Indra in Kebon Kopi I, Vishnu again in Cidanghiang). Does the choice of deity appear random, or do you see a coherent ideological programme?
5.4 The Tugu inscription records both a hydraulic engineering project and a cattle-gift ceremony to Brahmins. What is the relationship between these two elements, and what does their combination reveal about the political function of brahmanical ritual in early Indonesian statecraft?
5.5 Salakanagara is described in the Wangsakerta manuscripts but has left no inscriptions, archaeology, or contemporary foreign references. On what grounds do historians conclude that it is legendary rather than historical? What would it take to change that assessment?
5.6 Compare the type of historical evidence available for Kutai and Tarumanagara (inscriptions) with that for Ho-ling/Kalingga (Chinese annals). What different kinds of information does each type of source provide, and what are the respective limitations?
5.7 George Coedès coined the term "Indianization." Why has this term come under criticism from later historians, and what alternative frameworks (van Leur, Wolters, Kulke) have been proposed? Which do you find most convincing, and why?
5.8 The inscriptions of both Kutai and Tarumanagara record what kings gave to Brahmin priests, not what they did for ordinary subjects. What does this structural bias in the epigraphic record prevent us from knowing about early Indonesian society, and how might future archaeological work fill some of those gaps?
