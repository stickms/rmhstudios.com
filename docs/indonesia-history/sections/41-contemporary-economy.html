  • Assess Indonesia's transition to upper-middle-income status and its significance as Southeast Asia's largest economy and a G20 member.
  • Explain the economic logic and international controversies of hilirisasi (downstreaming), from the nickel ore export ban to EV-battery aspirations.
  • Trace the environmental and economic dimensions of Indonesia's palm oil dominance, including the costs of deforestation and peatland fires.
  • Evaluate the infrastructure transformation under President Joko Widodo — toll roads, the Jakarta MRT, and the Whoosh high-speed railway.
  • Analyse the rationale, contested progress, financing struggles, and environmental criticism of the Nusantara (IKN) capital-relocation project.
  • Describe the emergence of Indonesia's digital economy and the rise of super-app platforms through Gojek and GoTo.
  • Assess persistent structural challenges: inequality, informality, corruption, and the "middle-income trap" debate framed by the 2045 development vision.

In the quarter-century since the fall of Suharto in 1998, Indonesia has engineered one of the most remarkable economic turnarounds of the modern era. From a country whose currency had collapsed by 80 percent and whose GDP had shrunk by thirteen percent in a single crisis year, Indonesia rebuilt into Southeast Asia's largest economy, formally crossing into upper-middle-income status in 2023. By 2024 its nominal gross domestic product stood at approximately $1.4 trillion; on a purchasing-power-parity basis it ranked among the world's eight largest economies. The country produces the world's largest shares of nickel and palm oil, has constructed more than 2,400 kilometres of toll roads in a decade, opened its first rapid-transit rail network, and launched Southeast Asia's first high-speed railway. Yet the same economy grapples with a Gini coefficient that belies its aggregate wealth, an informal sector employing three in five workers, a corruption watchdog weakened by political interference, and an audacious — some would say reckless — plan to replace Jakarta with an entirely new capital carved from Borneo rainforest. These tensions, between impressive headline growth and stubborn structural weakness, between commodity bonanza and ecological cost, between prestige infrastructure and the needs of ordinary Indonesians, define the contemporary economy.

Southeast Asia's Largest Economy

Indonesia's twenty-first-century economic story begins with a number that might surprise those who still associate the archipelago with colonial-era spice routes and twentieth-century underdevelopment. In 2024 Indonesia's nominal GDP stood at approximately $1.4 trillion, making it the sixteenth-largest economy in the world and by far the most substantial in Southeast Asia — surpassing Thailand, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Malaysia combined.1 Its GDP measured at purchasing-power parity was estimated at $4.66 trillion by the International Monetary Fund, ranking Indonesia seventh or eighth globally, a figure that better captures the depth of an economy with more than 283 million consumers.2 As a founding member of the G20 in 1999 and a regular participant in the forums of global economic governance, Indonesia occupies a regional leadership role that was unimaginable during the chaos of 1998.

This scale reflects a sustained recovery from the 1997–98 Asian financial crisis and two decades of political consolidation during the Reformasi era (discussed in Chapters 36–39). Average GDP growth between 2000 and 2019 ran at slightly above five percent per year — a rate that, while consistently below the target of seven or eight percent set by successive presidents, was sufficient to halve the poverty rate from roughly twenty-four percent in 1999 to below ten percent on the eve of the COVID-19 pandemic, a reduction that the World Bank estimates lifted approximately 64 million people out of poverty over two decades.3 Growth resumed after a brief pandemic contraction, reaching 5.31 percent in 2022 and a still-solid 5.03 percent in 2024.4

In July 2023 the World Bank formally reclassified Indonesia as an upper-middle-income country, with a gross national income per capita of $4,870 by the Atlas method — just above the threshold of $4,496 that marks the lower floor of that band.1 The proximity to the boundary is not merely a statistical curiosity: it underscores how recently Indonesia's income gains have crossed this symbolic line and how much ground remains before the next, far more difficult ascent — to high-income status — can be contemplated. High-income status requires a per-capita GNI above approximately $13,935, nearly three times Indonesia's current level. That gap, and the question of whether Indonesia possesses the structural conditions to close it, shapes every major economic debate of the contemporary era.

Despite the headline achievements, the national poverty figures conceal persistent pockets of hardship. As of September 2024 approximately 24 million Indonesians — 8.57 percent of the population — remained below the national poverty line, defined by BPS (Badan Pusat Statistik, the central statistics agency) at approximately 550,000 rupiah ($35) per person per month in that period.3 By the international upper-middle-income poverty standard of $6.85 per person per day, used by the World Bank for comparisons within Indonesia's peer group, the share of Indonesians classified as poor rises substantially. Economic growth has disproportionately benefited urban areas and Java, leaving eastern and remote-island provinces far behind — a geographic inequality that the government's infrastructure push and capital-relocation plan are explicitly designed, though debatably positioned, to address.

Palm Oil: Prosperity and Peril

No single commodity captures the contradictions of the Indonesian economy as starkly as palm oil. Indonesia is the world's largest producer and exporter of crude palm oil, generating an estimated 47 million metric tonnes in the 2023/24 season — roughly 54 percent of global supply — with an export value of $22.67 billion in 2023.5 The sector contributes approximately 4.5 percent of GDP and directly or indirectly employs more than 16.2 million people, from multinational concession holders to the smallholders who cultivate more than 40 percent of the planted area across Sumatra and Kalimantan.5 Processing, refining, and oleochemical manufacturing built around the crop add a further employment and value-added dimension that makes palm oil arguably the single most economically significant agricultural commodity in the country's post-New Order history.

The economic logic behind palm oil's expansion is easy to follow: oil palms produce eight to ten times more oil per hectare than any competing crop, and crude palm oil remains the cheapest vegetable oil on the global market. For a large developing country seeking to raise rural incomes and generate foreign-exchange earnings without heavy industrial investment, the palm oil model was deeply attractive to successive post-Suharto governments. Its social footprint — providing livelihoods for millions of households in provinces such as Riau, South Kalimantan, and East Kalimantan that had few comparable alternatives — gave it strong political protection against reform.

The ecological cost, however, has been severe. Research collated by the Stockholm Environment Institute through the Trase supply-chain transparency platform estimates that oil-palm expansion has driven approximately one-third of Indonesia's old-growth forest loss — around three million hectares — over the past two decades.6 Four provinces in Kalimantan alone accounted for 72 percent of all palm-related deforestation between 2018 and 2022. The fires of 2015 provided the most alarming single image of this cost: more than 2.6 million hectares of land burned across Sumatra, Kalimantan, and Papua that season, producing a regional haze that blanketed Singapore, Malaysia, and much of island Southeast Asia for months.7 Researchers at Harvard and Columbia universities estimated that the smoke from the 2015 fires may have caused approximately 100,000 premature deaths. The fires were partly accidental, partly deliberate land-clearing, but were disproportionately concentrated on drained and degraded peatlands within palm-oil and timber concessions — precisely the ecosystems that store the densest accumulations of carbon in the tropical world.

Indonesia's government has taken corrective measures of varying ambition. A peatland regulation (Government Regulation PP 57/2016) rezoned degraded peatlands and established a Peatland Restoration Agency. An official moratorium on new primary-forest and peatland concessions, announced in 2011 and extended periodically, slowed — but did not halt — new clearance. The state-run Indonesian Sustainable Palm Oil (ISPO) certification scheme requires environmental and social audits, though independent observers note that self-reporting and weak enforcement limit its effect. The rate of industrial palm-oil-driven deforestation in 2018–22 (roughly 32,400 hectares per year) was only about 18 percent of its peak in 2008–12, a genuine improvement attributable to a combination of regulation, market pressure from European buyers, and the fact that the most accessible lowland forest had already been cleared.

External pressure intensified when the European Union enacted its EU Deforestation-Free Regulation (EUDR) in June 2023, requiring that palm oil, soy, beef, wood, coffee, cacao, and rubber entering the EU market be verified deforestation-free after December 2020.8 Indonesia and Malaysia — together producing roughly 85 percent of the world's palm oil — denounced the measure. Indonesia's coordinating minister for economic affairs described the EUDR as "regulatory imperialism" and protectionism dressed as environmentalism, a charge repeated in diplomatic communications to Brussels and at multilateral forums.8 European officials countered that the EU is a sovereign market entitled to set conditions for market access and that the burden was not on consumers but on producers who had benefited from cheap land acquired through deforestation. The impasse reflects a structural tension in the global political economy: the costs of Indonesia's palm-oil model have been diffused into the global atmosphere and into the habitats of endangered species, while the benefits remain concentrated in a small number of corporate balance sheets and a large number of smallholder households with few alternative income sources.

hilirisasi — the Indonesian government's "downstreaming" policy of prohibiting or taxing exports of raw mineral commodities to force value-added processing to occur domestically, capturing industrial rents rather than exporting them as unprocessed ore or concentrate. Strongly associated with President Joko Widodo's second term (2019–24) and most prominently applied to nickel and bauxite.

Nickel, Hilirisasi, and the Battery Dream

Indonesia holds the world's largest known nickel reserves — approximately 55 million metric tonnes of contained metal, amounting to roughly 42 percent of the global total according to the United States Geological Survey — and has in recent years become the world's dominant producer, accounting for an estimated 51 percent of global mine output in 2023, or some 1.8 million metric tonnes.9 For most of the country's history this extraordinary endowment translated into modest export earnings, because Indonesia shipped the bulk of its nickel as low-value laterite ore to smelters in China and Japan. Changing that logic became the signature industrial policy of the Joko Widodo administration and the most internationally controversial economic measure of the Reformasi era: the blanket export ban on unprocessed nickel ore.

Indonesia had periodically attempted to restrict raw mineral exports under the 2009 Mining Law, but consistent enforcement proved elusive in the face of industry lobbying. The decisive move came on 1 January 2020, when the government reinstated a comprehensive ban prohibiting exports of all nickel ore regardless of grade.11 The policy rested on the logic of hilirisasi: by denying foreign buyers access to Indonesian ore, the government intended to force investment in smelting and refining facilities on Indonesian soil. President Jokowi spoke repeatedly of Indonesia's aspiration to become "the Saudi Arabia of electric vehicles," capitalising on nickel's centrality to lithium-nickel-manganese-cobalt (NMC) battery chemistry as electric vehicles displaced internal combustion engines.

The results in export-value terms were transformative. Nickel-sector export earnings rose from approximately $1.1 billion in 2014 to more than $30 billion in 2022 — a roughly twenty-seven-fold increase in eight years — as a wave of Chinese-invested nickel pig iron and ferronickel smelters, concentrated in the Indonesia Morowali Industrial Park (IMIP) in Central Sulawesi and the Weda Bay complex in North Maluku, came on stream.12 IMIP alone became one of the world's largest industrial parks, housing rotary kiln electric furnace (RKEF) smelters alongside battery precursor plants attracting investment from CATL, LG Energy Solution, and Hyundai. The sheer speed of this industrial build-out — accomplished in under a decade largely through Chinese state-linked capital — was without modern parallel in the Indonesian economy.

The European Union challenged the export ban at the World Trade Organization in June 2019, arguing that it violated Article XI:1 of the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), which prohibits quantitative restrictions on exports except in narrowly defined circumstances. A WTO dispute-settlement panel (case DS592) ruled against Indonesia in November 2022, finding that the measures had been in place for years and that Indonesia had failed to demonstrate the "critical shortage" that could have justified them under GATT exceptions, or that less trade-restrictive alternatives were unavailable.11 The panel recommended that Indonesia bring its measures into conformity with GATT obligations. Indonesia appealed on 8 December 2022 — a procedural move that exploits the long-standing paralysis of the WTO Appellate Body, which has been unable to constitute quorums since the United States began blocking new appointments in 2017 — effectively suspending any binding outcome indefinitely and allowing the export ban to continue unchallenged in practice.11

Emboldened by the nickel model's revenue success, Jokowi extended hilirisasi to other minerals. On 10 June 2023 a ban on exports of raw bauxite ore came into force, with the stated aim of building domestic alumina-refining capacity.10 The government simultaneously announced plans for similar restrictions on copper concentrate and tin. Critics of the strategy point to significant risks that the aggregate export figures obscure. Nickel prices on international markets fell sharply through 2023 as the Indonesia-and-Philippines supply surge outpaced demand growth from the EV sector, squeezing the margins of smaller smelters and exposing the overcapacity risk inherent in centrally-directed industrial expansion. The heavy reliance on Chinese investment and technology in the smelter sector raises questions about how much of the "downstreaming" benefit is genuinely captured by Indonesian workers and firms, versus accruing to Chinese investors, and whether the coal-powered energy systems that underpin the RKEF smelters are compatible with Indonesia's climate commitments. These debates are unresolved, and scholars disagree about whether hilirisasi represents a sophisticated industrial policy analogous to East Asian developmental statism or an opaque resource-nationalism that generates environmental and fiscal liabilities while funnelling rents abroad.

The Infrastructure Decade

When Joko Widodo was first elected president in July 2014, Indonesia's infrastructure was widely acknowledged as a structural drag on growth. The country ranked poorly on international logistics benchmarks; inter-island shipping costs were high; and Java's highway network, built piecemeal over decades, did not yet form a continuous corridor. Jokowi's signature domestic programme — frequently summarised in the slogan "connect Indonesia" — was infrastructure investment on a scale that no predecessor had attempted, and it became the material legacy on which his administration's reputation most clearly rests.

The results in quantitative terms were striking. Between 2014 and 2024 the Ministry of Public Works and Public Housing (PUPR) oversaw the completion of approximately 2,433 kilometres of new toll roads — a figure representing some 72.7 percent of the total toll-road network that Indonesia had built in its entire history up to that point.13 The centrepiece was the Trans-Java Toll Road, a 1,065.5-kilometre corridor connecting the port of Merak in the west to Probolinggo in East Java, slashing the Jakarta–Surabaya driving time from more than fourteen hours to under eight and integrating Java's economic corridor into a single logistics chain for the first time.13 A parallel Trans-Sumatra project, aimed at linking Aceh to Lampung across 2,994 kilometres of the island's spine, had approximately 850 kilometres operational by the end of 2024, with further sections under construction toward a 2029 completion target. Port investment complemented the road push: the Patimban Deep Sea Port in West Java, inaugurated in December 2020 with $3.29 billion in JICA financing, was designed to relieve the perpetual congestion at Tanjung Priok and to give automotive exporters a direct Pacific-facing outlet.16

The most symbolically freighted of Jokowi's urban transport projects was the Jakarta Mass Rapid Transit. Phase 1 of the MRT — running 15.7 kilometres underground and elevated between Lebak Bulus in the south and Bundaran HI at the heart of the central business district, with thirteen stations — opened on 24 March 2019 after decades of planning and delays dating to a 1985 government study that had never been executed.14 Japan financed the construction through a JICA concessional loan of approximately $1.2 billion at an extraordinary interest rate of 0.1 percent — a soft-loan arrangement that reflected Japan's long-standing strategic interest in Indonesian urban development. In a city that had earned an unenviable reputation as one of the world's most congested metropolises, the MRT was greeted with genuine popular enthusiasm: average daily ridership exceeded 89,000 passengers in its first year, above the initial target of 65,000, and the system carried more than 120 million total passengers in its first five years of operation.

The more politically conspicuous infrastructure project of the Jokowi era was the Jakarta–Bandung High-Speed Railway, which opened under the brand name "Whoosh" on 17 October 2023, becoming Southeast Asia's first genuine high-speed rail service.15 Its 142.8 kilometres of track connect Jakarta's Halim station to Tegalluar on the outskirts of Bandung at a maximum operating speed of 350 kilometres per hour, reducing the inter-city travel time from roughly three hours to approximately thirty-five minutes. The project was built by Kereta Cepat Indonesia China (KCIC), a joint venture in which a consortium of Indonesian state-owned enterprises holds 60 percent and a Chinese railway consortium the remaining 40 percent, with 75 percent of the $7.3 billion total cost financed by the China Development Bank on a 40-year loan with a 10-year grace period.15

The Whoosh line encapsulates both the ambition and the risks of Jokowi's infrastructure agenda. The project was originally contracted in 2015 after Indonesia chose a Chinese-funded proposal over a Japanese bid that had been the frontrunner, beginning the commercial partnership that would also shape the IKN capital project and the nickel smelter build-out. Construction was supposed to conclude in 2019 at a cost of $5.5 billion. Land-acquisition disputes, engineering challenges in Java's volcanic terrain, and the COVID-19 pandemic pushed the timeline back four years and added roughly $1.2 billion in costs that the Indonesian state ultimately absorbed through a sovereign guarantee injection, a commitment that some Indonesian economists describe as a "financial time bomb."15 By October 2025 the railway had carried more than 12 million passengers, suggesting genuine demand, but debate persisted about whether the prestige of Southeast Asia's fastest train was worth the fiscal exposure, and whether a line primarily serving middle-class commuters between two wealthy Javanese cities addressed the deeper connectivity deficit of eastern Indonesia.

Nusantara — in its contemporary usage, the name given to Indonesia's new capital city under construction in East Kalimantan, designated by UU No. 3/2022. The word historically denotes the classical Malay concept of the wider Malay-speaking archipelago (used in this sense as early as the Majapahit-era Nagarakretagama) and remains the conventional Indonesian word for the archipelago as a geographic and cultural whole. Its adoption as the capital's name deliberately invokes classical archipelagic unity.

Nusantara: Reimagining the Capital

The most audacious policy initiative of the Reformasi era — and the one that has drawn the most intense scholarly and popular debate — is the plan to relocate Indonesia's national capital from Jakarta to a purpose-built city in East Kalimantan on the island of Borneo, officially named Nusantara. The plan brings together longstanding anxieties about Jakarta's physical unsustainability, a desire to redress Java's economic primacy, and a presidential vision of Indonesia as a modern, green, technologically sophisticated state. Whether it will fulfil any of those aspirations remains deeply uncertain.

Jakarta's problems are real and extensively documented. The city of approximately eleven million — and the greater metropolitan area (Jabodetabek) of more than thirty-four million — sits on a delta plain composed of soft alluvial soils that are compacted under the weight of construction and depleted by massive groundwater extraction. Jakarta's piped-water network serves fewer than a quarter of its households, leaving the rest dependent on groundwater pumps.18 As a result, large swaths of the city are sinking at between ten and 150 millimetres per year, with the worst-affected districts in North Jakarta subsiding at rates approaching 270 millimetres annually.18 Approximately 40 percent of Jakarta already lies below mean sea level; scientists project that up to 95 percent of North Jakarta could be submerged by 2050 if present subsidence and sea-level-rise trends continue unchecked.18 Compounding the physical threat, Java holds approximately 56 percent of Indonesia's population and accounts for roughly 60 percent of national GDP, a concentration that every Indonesian president since Sukarno has intermittently proposed to alleviate by moving the capital east.

Joko Widodo transformed this perennial proposal into enacted law. The IKN Law (UU No. 3/2022) was passed by the House of Representatives on 18 January 2022 and signed into force on 15 February 2022 — just 111 days from the bill's introduction, making it, as observers noted, one of the fastest major pieces of legislation in Indonesian parliamentary history.17 The law designated roughly 256,000 hectares in East Kalimantan's Penajam Paser Utara and Kutai Kartanegara regencies as the Capital City Authority Zone (Kawasan Ibu Kota Nusantara, or IKN). Total estimated construction cost was placed at approximately Rp 466 trillion — around $32.7 billion — with completion targeted in phases through 2045.19

The financing model was revealing of the project's structural tension. The state budget was committed to covering only approximately 19 percent of total costs; state-owned enterprises would contribute a further 26 percent; and the remaining 54 percent — the majority — was designated as private-sector investment to be attracted from domestic and foreign investors.19 This structure reflected both Indonesia's real fiscal constraint (its debt-to-GDP ratio is legally capped at 60 percent) and a somewhat optimistic assumption that Nusantara would prove sufficiently attractive as a "smart" and "green" planned city to draw commercial capital on its own merits in what is currently sparsely populated equatorial forest.

Construction of the core government precinct began in mid-2022. By mid-2024 the Garuda Palace (Istana Garuda) — a building whose soaring roofline is sculpted to evoke the outspread wings of the national bird — had been completed alongside a State Palace, ministry buildings, roads, and housing blocks for a planned initial cadre of civil servants. On 17 August 2024 — Indonesia's 79th Independence Day — President Jokowi held a ceremony at the Garuda Palace in Nusantara, attended by President-elect Prabowo Subianto and some 5,000 guests, visibly demonstrating that the central government precinct was at least ceremonially functional.20 For Jokowi, the ceremony was a personal milestone: it placed his presidency in a tradition of Indonesian leaders who have sought to reshape the physical landscape of the nation as a monument to their tenure.

Yet by the time of the August 2024 ceremony the project's vulnerabilities were already apparent. Of the 369 letters of investment intent that the IKN Authority had received — approximately 40 percent from foreign parties — not a single binding foreign memorandum of understanding had been signed as of April 2024.21 In June 2024 the IKN Authority's chairman, Bambang Susantono, and his deputy, Dhony Rahajoe, both resigned without public explanation, signalling management dysfunction at the project's highest level. Jokowi himself delayed his own planned residential relocation, citing unfinished water-supply and electricity infrastructure — facilities that had failed to meet their July 2024 completion targets. Prabowo Subianto, who assumed the presidency on 20 October 2024, publicly reaffirmed commitment to the project and announced plans to move his own office to Nusantara by the centennial Independence Day of 17 August 2028, but his administration simultaneously redirected significant budgetary resources to other priorities, including a flagship free-school-meals programme estimated at $25.5 billion over five years.

Academic criticism of the IKN project has been pointed. Anders Kirstein Moeller, writing in New Mandala in October 2025, argued that Nusantara is fundamentally "a high-modernist and speculative development project" rather than a city, constructed around 64 distinct and often mutually contradictory discursive frames — "green city," "smart city," "forest city," "equity instrument," "investment magnet" — that function as a "political black box" allowing any policy ideal to be attached to it without subjecting it to rigorous scrutiny.22 Moeller observed that East Kalimantan is already Indonesia's second-wealthiest province by GDP per capita, which undermines the premise that relocating the capital there will meaningfully address Java-centric inequality. Perhaps most critically, the planning process involved no meaningful public consultation with the indigenous Dayak, Balik, and Paser Maridan communities whose ancestral and sacred lands overlap with the IKN development zone.

The environmental record has given additional cause for concern. More than 20,000 hectares of primary forest were cleared for the IKN site by 2024, with a further 31,000 hectares at risk from planned expansion; against this, only about 1,441 hectares had been reforested by 2023 against a stated target of 120,000 hectares by 2045.23 An investigation by Mongabay in September 2024 found that construction had begun clearing a 3,000-hectare mangrove belt in Balikpapan Bay — critical habitat for an estimated 3,900 proboscis monkeys (an endangered species), whose territory overlaps some 41 percent with the development footprint, as well as for Irrawaddy dolphins, dugongs, and saltwater crocodiles.23 Biodiversity management guidelines were not issued by the IKN Authority until March 2024, nearly two years after construction had commenced. Whether Nusantara will ultimately be remembered as a visionary act of national reinvention or as one of the most expensive political gestures in Indonesian history is a question that will not be answered within the present decade.

The Digital Revolution: Super Apps and E-Commerce

While governments debated capital relocation and commodity export bans, Indonesia's digital economy was growing with a velocity that surprised even its most optimistic forecasters. According to the annual e-Conomy SEA report produced by Google, Temasek, and Bain & Company, Indonesia's digital economy generated a gross merchandise value of $82 billion in 2023, rising to $90 billion in 2024, and was projected to reach $180 billion by 2030 — the largest digital economy in Southeast Asia by a considerable margin, representing roughly 40 percent of the regional total.26 E-commerce alone generated $65 billion in GMV in 2024, digital-payments gross transaction value reached $404 billion, and food delivery and online transport together added several billion more.

The company most closely identified with Indonesia's digital transformation is Gojek, whose founding story has become an emblem of the twenty-first-century archipelago. On 13 October 2010 Nadiem Makarim — a Harvard Business School graduate who had worked at McKinsey & Company — launched a twenty-driver call centre offering a smartphone-mediated version of Jakarta's traditional motorcycle-taxi (ojek) service, combining GPS-based dispatch with transparent pre-agreed fares.25 The innovation addressed a real inefficiency: ojek drivers spent hours each day waiting for customers while commuters wasted time searching for available transport. When Gojek launched a full mobile application in 2015, orders jumped almost overnight from 3,000 to 10,000 per day. By 2017 the platform was handling 300,000 orders daily and had added GoFood (food delivery), GoPay (digital wallet), GoSend (courier), and dozens of ancillary services, evolving into Indonesia's first fully realised "super app" and its first startup valued above $10 billion.25

super app — a mobile application platform that consolidates multiple services — ride-hailing, food delivery, digital payments, e-commerce, financial products, and others — within a single interface and unified user identity. Gojek's GoApp was the pioneering Indonesian example; the model had antecedents in China's WeChat. Super apps are particularly suited to markets where smartphone adoption outpaced legacy banking and retail infrastructure.

Gojek's trajectory intersected with national politics in October 2019, when President Jokowi appointed Nadiem Makarim as Minister of Education and Culture — a move that simultaneously acknowledged Gojek's national significance and removed its most public face from the company. The consolidation of Indonesia's digital landscape accelerated when, on 17 May 2021, Gojek and Tokopedia — the dominant domestic e-commerce marketplace, founded in 2009 by William Tanuwijaya and Leontinus Alpha Edison — announced a merger that formed the GoTo Group, with a combined valuation of approximately $18 billion and operations spanning ride-hailing, food delivery, logistics, e-commerce, and financial technology.25 GoTo completed an initial public offering on the Indonesia Stock Exchange on 11 April 2022, raising $1.1 billion in what was one of the country's largest-ever technology listings.

The competitive landscape shifted again in 2023. In September, responding to complaints from domestic merchants about social-media platforms integrating instant commerce, the Indonesian government banned the practice of social commerce on social-media platforms — effectively suspending TikTok Shop, which had rapidly gained market share by combining short-video entertainment with embedded purchasing. ByteDance, TikTok's parent company, chose re-entry through GoTo rather than market exit: in December 2023 TikTok agreed to acquire a 75.01 percent controlling stake in Tokopedia, with the transaction completed on 31 January 2024 at an acquisition price of $840 million, accompanied by a commitment to invest more than $1.5 billion in the enlarged entity.24 GoTo retained a 24.99 percent stake while continuing its operational partnership. The arrangement was unusual in global platform history: a government regulatory intervention had redirected a major foreign-technology investment into a domestic partnership structure rather than producing simple market exclusion.

The digital economy's dynamism raises distributional questions that aggregate GMV figures conceal. E-commerce growth has benefited urban consumers and the logistics sector, and GoTo Financial's digital-payment and micro-lending products have extended some services to previously unbanked Indonesians. Yet the platform-gig model — of which Gojek's ojek drivers were the pioneer — occupies a labour-law grey zone, with drivers classified as independent contractors without access to formal social security, health insurance, or minimum-wage protections. The explosive growth of e-commerce has also threatened traditional street markets (pasar) and small warung shops that provide informal-sector livelihoods for millions of households across the archipelago. How the digital economy's benefits are shared remains a defining question for the Prabowo administration and beyond.

Inequality, Informality, and Corruption

Indonesia's headline achievements in poverty reduction and growth coexist with patterns of inequality that aggregate figures obscure. The Gini ratio reported by BPS stood at 0.381 in September 2024 — down from 0.388 in March 2023, but still substantially above the 0.30–0.35 range that would be considered moderate by comparative East Asian standards.27 Urban Indonesia, with a Gini of 0.402, is measurably more unequal than rural areas (0.308), partly reflecting the concentration of high-income professional and financial-sector employment in Jakarta and a handful of secondary cities.27 The top decile of the Indonesian income distribution receives a share of national income that greatly exceeds the combined income of the bottom four deciles — a gap that has proven stubbornly resistant to redistribution through the social-protection programmes (Kartu Indonesia Pintar, Program Keluarga Harapan, and others) that have expanded considerably since 1998.

Regional disparity is even more stark than the national Gini suggests. In September 2024 the poverty rate in Highland Papua stood at 32.97 percent; in Central Papua, 29.76 percent; in West Papua, 21.66 percent — rates three to four times the national average of approximately 9 percent, and reflecting decades of armed conflict, geographic isolation, and chronic underinvestment in public services.27 Papua and Maluku together contain some of the country's richest mineral deposits and most extensive fisheries, yet their populations rank among the poorest in Southeast Asia. Meanwhile, more than half of the absolute poor by headcount live on Java — a reminder that aggregate Javanese prosperity conceals a large residual of deep poverty.

The structural root of much of Indonesia's inequality lies in the size of the informal economy. According to BPS labour-force surveys, approximately 60 percent of Indonesia's employed workforce — some 87 million workers — were in informal employment as of early 2024, a share that actually increased by four percentage points compared with 2019, partly because the pandemic destroyed formal-sector jobs.27 In the agricultural sector, informality reaches 87.3 percent; even in non-agricultural sectors, 46.4 percent of workers are informal. Informal workers lack access to the formal social security system (BPJS Ketenagakerjaan and BPJS Kesehatan) in practice, are not effectively covered by minimum-wage enforcement, and have little recourse in the event of injury, illness, or dismissal. The expansion of platform-gig work through Gojek, Grab, and their competitors has added millions of workers to this informal category while obscuring the fact behind branded uniforms and smartphone interfaces.

Corruption remains one of the most consistently cited structural drags on Indonesian development. Transparency International's Corruption Perceptions Index placed Indonesia at a score of 34 out of 100 in 2023 — ranking it 115th of 180 countries — a figure that had barely changed in over a decade despite considerable investment in anti-corruption institutions.28 The score for 2024 was reported as 37, an apparent improvement, but Transparency International Indonesia noted that the methodology had been adjusted by the reintroduction of a World Economic Forum indicator that had not been used in 2022–23; on a comparable methodology, the score would remain around 34 to 35.28

The Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (KPK) — the Corruption Eradication Commission founded in December 2003 and for nearly a decade regarded as one of the most effective anti-corruption bodies in Southeast Asia — experienced a marked institutional decline after 2019. The KPK Law revision (Law 19/2019), passed by the DPR in September 2019 over the protests of civil-society groups, reclassified the KPK from an independent state institution to an executive-branch government agency, converted its investigators and prosecutors to civil servants subject to bureaucratic procedures, and required that all wiretapping operations receive prior approval from a new Supervisory Board.29 A "national insight test" administered to all KPK staff in 2021 was widely condemned by legal scholars as unconstitutional and politically motivated; it resulted in the dismissal of 75 employees including the prominent investigator Novel Baswedan, who had previously survived an acid attack in 2017 that was widely attributed to his anti-corruption work and had never been fully prosecuted.29 The nadir came in November 2023, when KPK Chairman Firli Bahuri was named a suspect by the Jakarta Metropolitan Police for allegedly extorting former Agriculture Minister Syahrul Yasin Limpo — an official under KPK investigation — of approximately $479,000.30 Firli resigned in December 2023, and the KPK's public trust rating fell to its lowest since the institution's founding.

The Middle-Income Trap and the 2045 Vision

The central anxiety of contemporary Indonesian economic governance is whether the country can escape what development economists call the "middle-income trap" — the tendency of countries that have successfully industrialised at low-wage levels to stall before reaching high-income status, unable to compete with the wage costs of newer industrialisers below and equally unable to match the knowledge intensity of established high-income economies above. Indonesia's per-capita GDP in 2024 stood at approximately $4,925 in nominal terms — just above the World Bank's lower boundary for upper-middle income — but the high-income threshold stands at approximately $13,935 per capita, nearly three times Indonesia's present level.1 Closing that gap within a generation is the ambition encoded in the government's long-range planning framework.

The government has anchored its response in a programme called "Golden Indonesia 2045" (Indonesia Emas 2045), timed to coincide with the centennial of independence.31 The framework targets a per-capita income of $23,050 by 2045, positioning Indonesia among the five largest economies in the world. Achieving it would require sustained annual growth of six to seven percent for approximately two decades — a rate that Indonesia has not consistently achieved outside the commodity-boom years of the mid-2000s. President Prabowo announced an even more ambitious target of 8 percent growth by 2029; the IMF projected a more modest 4.8 percent annually through 2027.31

Structural analysis identifies several barriers that make the high growth targets difficult. Manufacturing, which drove East Asia's successful development transitions in South Korea, Taiwan, and earlier Japan, has been in relative decline in Indonesia: the manufacturing sector's share of GDP fell from approximately 27 percent in 2002 to roughly 18 percent by 2022, a phenomenon that economists term "premature deindustrialisation" — industry contracting before the workforce has been fully absorbed into formal manufacturing employment, a pattern also observed in several Latin American middle-income countries that stalled.31 The Asian Development Bank, in a 2024 study examining whether Indonesia faces a "development miracle or middle-income trap," identified three structural barriers: persistently low manufacturing productivity growth, a severe human-capital deficit, and governance weaknesses that raise the cost and risk of productive investment.32 On human capital, only about 10 percent of Indonesia's workforce holds university degrees, and the formal education system continues to perform poorly on international benchmarks of reading, mathematics, and science (PISA).

Indonesia's demographic position adds urgency to the structural reform agenda. The country's demographic dividend — the growth-enabling period when the working-age population is large relative to dependants — is expected to peak in the 2030s, when approximately 68 percent of Indonesians will be at productive working age.31 But Indonesia has already crossed the threshold demographers use to define an "ageing society," with 11.97 percent of the population aged 60 or above as of 2024, having officially entered that phase. The window for capturing the productivity gains of a youthful workforce is narrowing. Once the demographic transition tilts, as it is projected to do in the 2040s, Indonesia will face rising pension and healthcare costs at a per-capita income far below the level at which today's high-income countries underwent their own ageing transitions.

The debate about the middle-income trap is not merely academic — it shapes investment priorities, trade policy, educational spending, and the logic of hilirisasi itself. Proponents of commodity downstreaming argue that it represents precisely the kind of deliberate industrial policy — analogous to South Korea's chaebol-backed heavy industrialisation in the 1970s — needed to force Indonesia up the value chain in sectors where it holds a genuine resource advantage. Critics counter that Indonesia lacks the state capacity, educational infrastructure, and institutional quality to execute state-directed industrial policy successfully, that the heavy reliance on Chinese capital in the smelter sector risks importing production without technology transfer, and that the ecological costs of nickel processing (coal power, tailings, industrial waste) will compound rather than resolve Indonesia's long-term development constraints.

These debates are ultimately empirical rather than ideological: they turn on whether hilirisasi generates meaningful backward and forward linkages into the Indonesian economy, whether infrastructure investment raises total-factor productivity or primarily produces ribbon-cutting ceremonies, and whether the young Indonesians who drove Gojek's growth and shop on Tokopedia will find, within Indonesia, an economy sophisticated enough to employ their expanding education and expectations. The answers will not be known for decades. What is already clear is that Indonesia in the early twenty-first century has placed a series of very large bets — on nickel processing, on a new capital, on digital platforms, on infrastructure — and that its ability to manage those bets, and to distribute their proceeds equitably, will determine whether the "Golden Indonesia 2045" vision becomes a development milestone or a cautionary tale of misallocated ambition.

demographic dividend — the period in a country's development when the working-age population (roughly 15–64 years) is large relative to the dependent population (children and elderly), producing a favourable ratio that — if supported by productive employment, education, and health investment — generates accelerated economic growth. Indonesia's dividend window is projected to peak in the 2030s.

Summary

Indonesia in the early twenty-first century is Southeast Asia's largest economy, a G20 member, and — since 2023 — an upper-middle-income country with a nominal GDP of approximately $1.4 trillion. This standing rests on commodity wealth (palm oil and nickel are its two largest export earners), a decade of aggressive infrastructure investment under President Joko Widodo, and a digital economy of $90 billion GMV in 2024. The government's "hilirisasi" policy of banning raw mineral exports, most notably nickel ore since 2020, generated an extraordinary expansion of processing capacity financed largely by Chinese capital, and provoked a WTO dispute with the European Union. The Nusantara capital-relocation project, legislated in 2022 at an estimated cost of $32.7 billion, aims to move the government from sinking, congested Jakarta to a new city in East Kalimantan, but faces financing shortfalls, management upheaval, environmental criticism, and contested investor confidence. Persistent structural weaknesses — a Gini ratio of 0.381, 60 percent informal employment, a weakened anti-corruption commission, and manufacturing's declining share of GDP — raise genuine questions about whether Indonesia can escape the "middle-income trap" and achieve the "Golden Indonesia 2045" target of high-income status.

Review & Discussion Questions

40.1 Indonesia joined the ranks of "upper-middle-income" countries in 2023, yet roughly 24 million Indonesians remain below the national poverty line. How do you reconcile these two facts, and what does the gap between aggregate and household measures tell us about the distribution of Indonesia's growth?
40.2 Evaluate the hilirisasi policy. Does forbidding raw nickel ore exports constitute sound industrial policy analogous to East Asian developmentalism, or does it impose costs on Indonesian consumers, workers, and the environment while primarily benefiting Chinese investors? What evidence would you need to adjudicate between these positions?
40.3 Compare the Jakarta MRT and the Whoosh high-speed railway as infrastructure investments. Which better addresses Indonesia's development priorities, and why? What criteria should governments use when choosing between urban mass transit and inter-city prestige infrastructure?
40.4 Assess the case for relocating Indonesia's capital to Nusantara. How compelling are the arguments from Jakarta's physical unsustainability and Java-centric inequality? What alternative policies might address the same problems at lower cost and lower environmental impact?
40.5 Gojek was founded in 2010 as a twenty-driver call centre and became one of Southeast Asia's largest technology companies within a decade. What structural features of the Indonesian economy — in transport, payment infrastructure, income levels, and urban geography — help explain the particular form that digital-platform growth took in Indonesia?
40.6 The 2019 revision to the KPK Law and the subsequent "civics test" that dismissed 75 staff are widely seen as politically motivated attacks on Indonesia's anti-corruption commission. What does the trajectory of the KPK reveal about the limits of institutional reform within a democratic system where elected politicians are also potential targets of anti-corruption prosecution?
40.7 Indonesia's demographic dividend is expected to peak in the 2030s. What policies — in education, labour law, manufacturing, and social protection — would most improve Indonesia's chances of converting this temporary demographic advantage into sustained productivity growth before the window closes?
40.8 Palm oil is simultaneously one of Indonesia's most economically important sectors (employing 16 million people, earning $22.67 billion in exports in 2023) and one of its most environmentally damaging (driving a third of old-growth forest loss). Can this tension be resolved through certification and regulation, or does it require a more fundamental restructuring of Indonesia's export economy?
