Learning Objectives

  • Identify the main categories of primary source — inscriptions, court chronicles, foreign accounts, colonial archives, and oral traditions — and assess the methodological strengths and limits of each.
  • Trace the historiographical shift from colonial "Eurocentric" scholarship to the "autonomous" Indonesia-centric programme pioneered by J.C. van Leur and developed at institutions including Cornell University and the KITLV in Leiden.
  • Explain how the great historiographical debates — over Indianization, the reach of Majapahit, the causes of Islamization, the demographic impact of the Cultivation System, and the responsibility for 1965 — reflect deeper arguments about evidence, agency, and interpretive authority.
  • Analyse the role of the Indonesian state, particularly the New Order, in producing, policing, and suppressing historical knowledge, and assess what a genuinely decolonized Indonesian historiography might require.

Every history book rests, invisibly, on choices: what counts as evidence, whose testimony is credible, which silences can be spoken across. For Indonesian history those choices are peculiarly fraught. The written record begins only in the fourth century CE, is fractured across languages — Sanskrit, Old Javanese, Malay, Dutch, Arabic, Chinese, and Bahasa Indonesia each carrying different fragments of the story — and has been systematically shaped by colonial authority and postcolonial nationalism alike. Deliberate suppression has compounded the problem: the mass killings of 1965–66, among the largest in twentieth-century history, became an official non-event for three decades, their memory policed out of textbooks, archives, and public discourse. This capstone chapter examines the historiography of Indonesia: the sources historians use, the schools they have formed, the great debates they have fought, and the still-contested question of who has the authority — and the tools — to write the history of the world's largest archipelago.

The Primacy of Stone and Palm: Inscriptions and Indigenous Texts

The earliest datable traces of Indonesian history are not narrative texts but ceremonial inscriptions carved on stone pillars. Seven yupa — Vedic sacrificial posts — discovered at Muara Kaman in East Kalimantan bear Sanskrit verses in Pallava script attributing grand sacrificial gifts to King Mulavarman of Kutai, dated to approximately the fourth century CE.1 They are, in the strict sense, the oldest evidence of writing in the archipelago. Their importance for the historian is not what they say — gift-giving and lineage-listing, in the panegyric register of royal self-glorification — but what their existence implies: that by the fourth century CE, rulers on the eastern edge of Borneo were employing Brahmin priests trained in Sanskrit, participating in Indian religio-political culture, and commissioning durable inscriptions for posterity. The yupa are evidence not of events but of a world.

prasasti — a Sanskrit-derived term for an inscription, typically on stone, copper plate, or bronze, recording royal edicts, land grants, genealogies, or religious dedications. Prasasti are the primary source base for the political history of the Hindu-Buddhist period.

Hundreds of subsequent prasasti — in Old Malay, Old Javanese, and Sanskrit — illuminate the politics of Srivijaya, the Sailendra dynasty, Kediri, Singhasari, and Majapahit. Epigraphy (the study of inscriptions) has been the cornerstone of the field since Dutch and British scholars began systematically collecting and editing inscriptions in the nineteenth century, with the work of the KITLV (Koninklijk Instituut voor Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde, Leiden, founded 1851) and its journal Bijdragen tot de Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde (BKI, in continuous publication since 1853) providing the institutional infrastructure for this enterprise. The limitation of epigraphy is structural: only rulers, temples, and their patrons generated inscriptions. The vast majority of the archipelago's population — farmers, traders, sailors, women — left no epigraphic trace.

A second, richer but more treacherous source category is the court literature preserved on lontar (palm-leaf manuscripts). The greatest single monument in this tradition is the Nagarakretagama, or Desawarnana, an Old Javanese kakawin (metric poem) composed in 1365 CE by the court poet Mpu Prapanca during the reign of Hayam Wuruk, the high king of Majapahit. The five-volume critical edition prepared by Theodore Pigeaud and published by KITLV between 1960 and 1963 remains the indispensable scholarly apparatus for this text.2 Stuart Robson's condensed English translation (1995) made it accessible to a wider scholarly audience and addressed its historiographical status directly in the introduction: the poem is a royal panegyric, not a chronicle in the modern sense, and its famous list of polities acknowledging Majapahit's "overlordship" — from Bali to Maluku, Borneo, and the Malay Peninsula — must be read as ritual assertions of cosmological centrality rather than as evidence of administrative or military control.3 The gap between the Nagarakretagama's ceremonial claims and Majapahit's demonstrable territorial reach is one of the most consequential interpretive chasms in Indonesian historiography, and it returns under the heading of the great debates below.

babad — a Javanese court chronicle, typically in verse (tembang), recounting dynastic history in a form that blends genealogy, mythology, and cosmological legitimation with occasional historical detail. The most influential is the Babad Tanah Jawi ("Chronicle of the Land of Java").
hikayat — a Malay prose narrative or chronicle, often recording the history and genealogy of a royal court. The Hikayat Raja-Raja Pasai is the oldest surviving Malay chronicle, recording the Islamization of Samudera-Pasai.

The Dutch philologist C.C. Berg was the most sceptical of the Javanese chronicle tradition. Working on the Babad Tanah Jawi and related babads, Berg argued that these texts served primarily religio-political functions — encoding mythological charters for dynastic legitimation — rather than recording verifiable events, and that the historical content of Majapahit-era court literature was almost irrecoverable behind layers of mythological elaboration.4 Berg's position was influential but was widely criticized as too extreme: subsequent scholars demonstrated that babads do preserve recoverable historical information, albeit requiring careful triangulation against other evidence. The balance between literary criticism and historical recovery remains a live methodological problem. The Hikayat Raja-Raja Pasai, the oldest Malay chronicle, poses analogous challenges: Russell Jones's critical edition based on Raffles MS 67 showed how the text mingles genealogical legend with what appear to be historical memories of the Islamization of northern Sumatra, without clear internal markers to distinguish them.5

lontar — a palm-leaf manuscript, made from dried strips of the Borassus palm. Lontar manuscripts are the principal vehicle for Old Javanese and Balinese literary and religious texts; the tropical climate has destroyed many, giving surviving examples exceptional value.
kakawin — a genre of Old Javanese court poetry composed in Sanskrit quantitative metres, drawing on Indian literary models. Kakawin include the Nagarakretagama and the Kakawin Sutasoma, source of the republic's motto Bhinneka Tunggal Ika.

Outside Eyes: Chinese, Arabic, and Other Foreign Accounts

The internal record left by Indonesian polities is supplemented — and for the earliest periods substantially replaced — by accounts from outside. Chinese, Arabic, and later European sources offer perspectives unavailable in the indigenous literary tradition, but each comes freighted with its own conventions, categories, and silences.

The Chinese monk Yijing (I-Tsing) stopped at "Fo-shih" — almost certainly Srivijaya, near modern Palembang — for six months in 671 CE on his way to India, and again for seven years between 685 and 695 CE. His account is the earliest substantial textual attestation of Srivijaya as a flourishing Buddhist polity, recording a community of over a thousand monks and recommending it as a place for Chinese pilgrims to study Sanskrit before attempting the voyage to India.6 Yijing wrote in the Chinese Buddhist pilgrim-biography genre, and historians must interpret his descriptions against both hagiographic convention and Chinese political categories, which had no direct equivalent for the fluid, port-centred polity he observed. O.W. Wolters's monograph Early Indonesian Commerce (1967) synthesised Yijing and other Chinese accounts with Arabic and Indian sources to produce the foundational scholarly treatment of Srivijaya's origins, establishing the methodological model — triangulating foreign textual accounts with epigraphy and material culture — for all subsequent work on pre-Islamic Indonesia.7

The Chinese account of Ma Huan, who accompanied Zheng He's treasure-fleet voyages to Southeast Asia in 1413, 1421, and 1431, provides the single most detailed surviving description of Majapahit Java on the eve of Islamization, recording court ceremonies, markets, coinage, religious practices, and social customs in remarkable ethnographic detail. J.V.G. Mills's complete English translation (1970) is the standard scholarly edition.8 Zhao Rugua's Zhu Fan Zhi (c. 1225) provided an earlier Chinese commercial geography of the region, and Kenneth Hall drew on it alongside Arabic accounts to argue that Srivijayan political authority rested fundamentally on controlling the terms of international exchange rather than territorial domination.9 Among Arabic sources, the ninth-century accounts of Sulayman the Merchant and the geographer al-Masudi, and the fourteenth-century travel narrative of Ibn Battuta (who visited the northern Sumatran port of Samudera-Pasai in 1345–46 and recorded a Muslim sultanate already fully formed), offer an Indian Ocean perspective on the archipelago largely absent from Chinese sources. The interpretive challenge posed by all these foreign texts is analogous to that of indigenous court literature: they were written within intellectual traditions that imposed their own categories on what they observed, and the gap between description and underlying social reality requires constant critical negotiation.

The Colonial Archive and Its Shadows

The most voluminous documentary record of Indonesian history is a paradox: it was produced by the colonisers. The Dutch East India Company (VOC) created an enormous administrative apparatus whose records — orders, correspondence, contracts, registers of trade, judicial proceedings, population counts — began accumulating from 1602 and continued through the colonial period. After the VOC's dissolution in 1799 the colonial state's record-keeping expanded further. These archives are now distributed between the Nationaal Archief in The Hague and the Arsip Nasional Republik Indonesia (ANRI) in Jakarta, which holds the largest collection in Southeast Asia. For the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries in particular, the colonial archive is an indispensable tool for social and economic history.

The methodological problem with the colonial archive is not simply that it is incomplete but that its silences and categories are themselves historical artefacts. Ann Laura Stoler's Along the Archival Grain (2009), based on nineteenth-century Netherlands Indies administrative documents, made this argument with programmatic force: rather than mining colonial records as sources of data about Indonesian society, Stoler reads them "along the grain" — attending to the social epistemologies, racial anxieties, and emotional registers that governed what colonial officials noticed, recorded, suppressed, and filed.10 In an earlier article (2002) she described colonial archives as "monuments of states" and "sites of state ethnography," in which the very forms of record-keeping encoded relationships of power — racial classifications, legal designations, and property registers serving as instruments for producing and policing the colonial social order.11 Stoler's challenge to the field was radical: the archive must be treated not as a neutral repository of evidence but as a historical subject in its own right, whose production was "a consequential act of governance" with violent effects on the lives it documented.

The practical consequences of Stoler's critique are wide-ranging. Local resistance, everyday negotiations over land and labour, women's lives, the perspectives of enslaved and indentured workers, the interior social worlds of the hundreds of ethnic groups across the outer islands — all these either left no archival trace at all or appear only refracted through the alien optic of colonial administration. The oral traditions, adat (customary law) texts, and local memory practices that carried these histories have been systematically undervalued by a discipline long dominated by literate, European-derived methodologies. Bridging the archive's gaps requires not merely supplementing colonial records with indigenous sources but rethinking the epistemological ground on which historical knowledge is claimed.

Toward Autonomous Indonesian Historiography: Van Leur, Smail, and the Indonesia-Centric Turn

The critique of colonial-era Indonesian historiography as "Eurocentric" — writing the archipelago's past from the perspective of the European intruder rather than from within Indonesian society — has its landmark in the posthumously published essays of Jacob Cornelis van Leur (1908–1942). Van Leur, a Dutch economic historian who died in the Battle of the Java Sea at the age of thirty-three, left behind a series of essays that were collected and published in 1955 as Indonesian Trade and Society.12 His most famous formulation objected to writing Indonesian history "from the deck of the [European] ship, the ramparts of the fortress, the high gallery of the trading house" — positioning colonisers as the only agents and Southeast Asian societies as passive backgrounds to European adventure. Against this, van Leur insisted that Indonesian commercial history had to be analysed from within the region's own social and economic logic, applying Weberian categories he derived from the comparative sociology of world religions. His Weberian-influenced characterisation of Indonesian trade as "peddler commerce" has been criticised as itself dependent on European social-science frameworks — a point Eric Jones made in a 2022 reassessment arguing that van Leur's reputation as the founder of autonomous historiography had been overstated, because his deductive method imposed theses on evidence rather than building up from it.13 The criticism is fair as a description of method; van Leur's importance was less methodological than rhetorical — he posed the right question, even if his own answers remained caught in European categories.

The theoretical programme van Leur sketched was more rigorously formulated by John Smail in his 1961 article "On the Possibility of an Autonomous History of Modern Southeast Asia," which called for "a Burma-centered history of Burma, a Vietnam-centered history of Vietnam" — histories that treated Asian societies as the primary subjects of their own pasts rather than as the passive setting for Western expansion.14 Smail coined the term "autonomous history" to denote this methodological orientation, and the article became one of the most-cited in the entire field. The programme it described was given institutional form in the 1965 volume An Introduction to Indonesian Historiography, edited by the Indonesian intellectual and diplomat Soedjatmoko together with Mohammad Ali, G.J. Resink, and George McT. Kahin — the founding director of Cornell's Southeast Asia Program.15 Bringing together twenty-two scholars to survey source materials and methodological approaches, the volume established the vocabulary for debating colonial-period distortions and the possibilities of recovery. Its publication in Ithaca rather than Jakarta or Leiden was itself symptomatic of where the institutional centre of gravity then lay.

Two institutions above all shaped the Anglophone and Dutch scholarship on Indonesia through the second half of the twentieth century. Cornell University's Southeast Asia Program, founded in 1950 under Kahin, whose Nationalism and Revolution in Indonesia (1952) was immediately recognized as the foundational sympathetic academic treatment of the Indonesian revolution, trained successive generations of scholars in Indonesian history, politics, and language.16 The Cornell Modern Indonesia Project produced monographs, translations, and — most controversially — the "Cornell Paper" of 1966/1971. Across the Atlantic, KITLV in Leiden provided the institutional home for Dutch scholarship: its series of translations, linguistic dictionaries, and critical text editions, published through Martinus Nijhoff and later Brill, underpins the philological foundation of the field. BKI, now in its 180th year of publication and available open-access through Brill, remains the leading peer-reviewed venue for the humanities and social sciences of Southeast Asia. The relationship between these Western institutions and Indonesian scholarship was never entirely equal — and the "Indonesianisasi" of the field, the gradual empowerment of Indonesian scholars and institutions to control their own national history, was an ongoing political as well as intellectual project, reaching a decisive stage with the expansion of Indonesian universities after independence and the establishment of the Lembaga Ilmu Pengetahuan Indonesia (LIPI, Indonesian Institute of Sciences) in 1956. Anthony Reid's two-volume Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce, 1450–1680 (1988, 1993) demonstrated what autonomous history could achieve at scale — a Braudelian reconstruction of material life, gender relations, and political culture across the archipelago drawing on indigenous, Chinese, Arabic, and European sources in concert, vindicating the programme van Leur had only imagined.17

The Great Debates: Indianization, Majapahit, Islamization, and the Cultivation System

Beneath the procedural disputes about sources and methods lie the substantive historical controversies that have defined the field. Four stand out as case studies in how evidence, ideology, and interpretive framework interact.

The nature of Indianization. The broadest question in early Indonesian historiography is how to account for the pervasive Indian cultural, religious, and linguistic influence visible in the archaeological record from the fourth century onwards — the Sanskrit inscriptions, Hindu and Buddhist temples, Indic concepts of divine kingship, and Indian-derived literary forms. The French scholar George Coedès, in his comparative synthesis The Indianized States of Southeast Asia (originally 1948, English translation 1968), argued for a model of Indian "colonisation" in which Brahmin priests and merchants transmitted a package of Indian civilisational elements to receptive Southeast Asian rulers seeking political legitimation.18 F.D.K. Bosch, the foremost Dutch scholar of Hindu-Buddhist temple art in Indonesia, refined this to a "Brahmin mission" thesis, distinguishing between the demonstrable arrival of Indian cultural forms and the much less evidenced arrival of Indian populations.19 Van Leur countered that the analysis gave too little credit to the Southeast Asian societies that actively selected and appropriated Indian elements for their own purposes — a critique developed most fully by O.W. Wolters in History, Culture, and Region in Southeast Asian Perspectives (1982, revised 1999).20 Wolters's concept of "localisation" — the process by which Southeast Asian rulers and priests domesticated Indian elements, reshaping them to serve indigenous political and spiritual needs — and his companion concepts of the "mandala" (an overlapping field of personal loyalty rather than a bounded territorial state) and "men of prowess" (leaders whose authority derived from charismatic spiritual power) have become the standard analytical vocabulary for pre-modern Indonesian political history. The Indianization debate is not merely academic: it touches the question of whether Indonesian civilisation was fundamentally derivative or generative, a question with obvious political resonances in both the colonial and postcolonial eras.

The reach of Majapahit. The nationalist assertion that Majapahit constituted a proto-Indonesian empire — a pre-colonial unity encompassing the entire archipelago — rests principally on the Nagarakretagama's list of tributaries and on later celebratory references to Gajah Mada's Palapa oath. Pigeaud's commentary in his five-volume edition carefully disentangles which cantos describe demonstrable Majapahit political reach (largely restricted to East Java and Bali) from which represent ceremonial tribute relationships and which are simple panegyric inflation.21 Robson's translation confirmed that the "overlordship" asserted for distant polities was more cosmological claim than administrative fact.22 The scholarly consensus has long been that Majapahit was a powerful east Javanese kingdom with significant influence in the immediate region, not a centralised empire controlling the entire archipelago. The nationalist inflection — which the New Order in particular cultivated, using Majapahit as an ideological precedent for the unitary Indonesian state — was a political construction laid over a more modest historical reality, a point Michael Wood documented in detail in his study of New Order official history.23

The causes of Islamization. How Islam spread through the archipelago between the thirteenth and seventeenth centuries — and why it succeeded where Buddhism and Hinduism had preceded it — has been one of the most contested questions in the field. Multiple mechanisms have been proposed: the conversion of port-city rulers attracted by the commercial networks of Muslim merchants from India and Arabia; the role of Sufi missionaries and teachers who adapted Islamic spirituality to local cosmological sensibilities; the prestige attached to membership in a global religious community whose political power was visible in the Ottoman and Mughal empires; and the gradual Islamization of coastal populations through intermarriage and trade. Soedjatmoko's 1965 collection devoted significant attention to the evidential basis for each of these claims, noting that the surviving sources — largely the chronicles of Muslim sultanates and the accounts of foreign observers — were produced by the converted elite and tell very little about how Islamization was experienced by agricultural communities or what it meant for women.24 The debate remains unresolved, and its resolution is complicated by the fact that "Islamization" was not a single event but a centuries-long, regionally varied process.

The Cultivation System and its demographic costs. The Cultivation System (Cultuurstelsel), introduced in 1830, required Javanese peasants to devote a share of their cultivated land or labour to producing export crops for the Dutch state. The debate over its human costs — whether it caused famine, depopulation, and structural impoverishment, or whether it underwrote demographic growth and agricultural development — is one of the sharpest in colonial economic history. J.C. van Leur's methodological programme was less relevant here than the post-World War II generation of Dutch and Indonesian economic historians who worked on the colonial archive's production statistics and population registers. The morality play aspect of the debate has never been far from the surface: Dutch apologists sought to demonstrate net economic benefits; Indonesian nationalists emphasised its exploitative character as the foundation of the national awakening narrative. Reid's broad reconstruction of pre-colonial social conditions provided a baseline against which colonial-era changes could be assessed, demonstrating how much can be recovered about Indonesian society from non-colonial sources.25 The demographic debate remains technically contentious because of the quality of the colonial census data, but the consensus among recent scholars is that the Cultivation System caused severe regional famines in the 1840s and contributed to chronic structural poverty in parts of Central Java.

Constructing National History: The New Order's Official Narrative

The relationship between historical scholarship and political power is nowhere more visible in Indonesia than in the New Order period (1966–1998). Suharto's regime constructed an official national history that was simultaneously a charter of political legitimation and a system of social control, encoding that history in school textbooks, monuments, museums, and the curriculum of Indonesian universities under close state supervision.

Michael Wood's Official History in Modern Indonesia (2005) provides the most systematic analysis of this construction.26 The New Order master narrative proceeded from ancient proof of Indonesian unity (Majapahit as proto-Indonesia) through the nationalist awakening, the Japanese occupation, and the revolution to the New Order's self-presentation as the saviour of the nation from the communist betrayal of 1965. Pancasila — the five principles formulated by Sukarno in 1945 — was retooled as an obligatory ideological framework to which all political parties, social organisations, and educational institutions were required to subscribe, making its history a site of perpetual state intervention. Benedict Anderson's theory of "imagined communities" — arguing that nations are constructed through print-capitalism and shared cultural practices — offered a powerful analytical framework for understanding how the New Order used the education system and mass media to produce Indonesian national identity in a particular, politically managed form.27

Agus Suwignyo's detailed study of Indonesian secondary-school history textbooks in the post-1998 reformasi period found, alarmingly, that the fundamental master narrative inherited from the New Order remained largely intact despite formal curriculum reform: the treatment of 1965, communist-era politics, and ethnic and regional diversity continued to reflect a Jakarta-centric, Javanese-inflected national history that marginalised alternative voices.28 Institutional inertia, the continuing influence of New Order-era textbook authors, and the political sensitivity of the contested periods all contributed to this persistence.

The monumental landscape of New Order Indonesia was itself a history lesson in built form. The National History Museum (Museum Sejarah Nasional) in Jakarta presented Indonesian history as a teleological progression culminating in the New Order state. Taman Mini Indonesia Indah ("Beautiful Indonesia in Miniature"), opened in 1975 under the patronage of Suharto's wife, staged the nation's ethnic diversity as a spectacle of harmonious unity presided over by the central government. Regional monuments across Java and beyond commemorated battles of the revolution in ways that consistently elevated the army's role and minimised civilian, left-wing, and regional contributions. LIPI's historical research programme operated under conditions that required political sensitivity to topics the regime found dangerous — above all, the events of 1965.

The Silenced Past: 1965 and the Battle over Memory

No episode in Indonesian history has been more consequentially distorted than the events of September–October 1965 and the mass killings that followed. The New Order's founding legitimacy rested on a specific account: the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI) had organised a coup attempt against the army leadership on the night of 30 September–1 October 1965; Suharto had heroically suppressed it; and the subsequent destruction of the PKI — along with the killing of between 500,000 and one million suspected communists and their families — had been a spontaneous, justified popular response to communist atrocity. This narrative was embedded in the name given to the movement: "G30S/PKI" (Gerakan 30 September/Partai Komunis Indonesia), eliding "PKI" so that the coup and the party became a single criminal entity in popular usage, and inscribed in a feature film (Pengkhianatan G30S/PKI, "Treachery of the September 30 Movement/PKI") that was compulsorily screened in schools and on national television every year until 1998.

The first systematic scholarly challenge to this narrative was the "Cornell Paper" — formally, A Preliminary Analysis of the October 1, 1965, Coup in Indonesia — completed on 10 January 1966 by Benedict Anderson and Ruth McVey at Cornell University, with contributions from Frederick Bunnell.29 Working from Indonesian press reports and internal logic, Anderson and McVey argued that the 30 September Movement was an internal army affair — a conspiracy of disaffected middle-ranking officers in the Diponegoro Division — rather than a PKI-organised operation. The paper directly challenged the regime's legitimating narrative and was treated accordingly: the Suharto government banned both Anderson and George Kahin from Indonesia, where they remained persona non grata for decades. The Cornell Paper circulated clandestinely in Indonesia and was not officially published until 1971 by the Cornell Modern Indonesia Project.

Four decades of subsequent scholarship have substantially advanced beyond the binary of the Cornell Paper versus the official narrative. John Roosa's Pretext for Mass Murder (2006) drew on the previously classified personal confession of the PKI operative Sjam to argue that there was indeed a real conspiracy behind the 30 September Movement, but that it was small, compartmentalised, and involved only a fraction of the PKI leadership — providing Suharto with a genuine pretext that he then deliberately, systematically enlarged into a justification for annihilating the entire PKI and hundreds of thousands of people connected to the Indonesian left.30 Geoffrey Robinson's The Killing Season (2018) placed the killings in a systematic comparative framework alongside other twentieth-century mass atrocities, demonstrating through declassified US, British, and Australian diplomatic records that the massacres were planned, directed, and facilitated by the army's senior leadership, and that Western governments provided logistical support, intelligence assistance, and political cover.31 Jess Melvin's The Army and the Indonesian Genocide (2018) brought the most direct documentary evidence yet to bear: over three thousand pages of classified military operational orders and chain-of-command communications she discovered in the Aceh Government Library showed the army coordinating mass killings in Aceh before the full scale of the 30 September Movement's collapse was even known, suggesting a pre-existing plan rather than a reactive purge.32

The recovery of 1965 memory inside Indonesia is a story of the post-1998 democratic opening. Hilmar Farid, the Indonesian intellectual and co-founder of the Institute of Social History (ISSI), deployed Marx's concept of "primitive accumulation" to argue that the killings served not merely political but fundamental economic functions — enabling massive transfers of agricultural land and the restructuring of the Indonesian economy to serve foreign capital and domestic oligarchs.33 His framework opened a decolonial line of analysis that moved the question of 1965 beyond Cold War politics into the longue durée of Indonesian political economy. The International People's Tribunal 1965, convened in The Hague in November 2016, found the Indonesian state guilty of crimes against humanity for its role in the killings — a verdict without legal force but with significant symbolic weight in the ongoing Indonesian public debate. Despite the democratic opening, Rommel Curaming's survey of Indonesian nationalist historiography noted that structural obstacles remained: the military's archival secrecy, the political costs of confronting the New Order's legacy in a society where many perpetrators remained alive, and the absence of a formal truth and reconciliation process.34

Toward a Decolonized History: Limits and Future Directions

To speak of "decolonizing" Indonesian historiography is to raise a question that is at once methodological, institutional, and political. Three decades after Suharto's fall, the scholarly landscape is genuinely more plural than at any previous moment — Indonesian scholars work in a wider range of institutional settings, on a wider range of topics, with less direct state censorship of their conclusions — but the structural conditions for fully autonomous scholarship remain incomplete.

The most concrete obstacle is archival access. The military archives for the New Order period — including records bearing directly on the 1965 killings, the East Timor occupation, operations in Aceh and Papua, and the events of May 1998 — remain substantially closed. The partial opening of ANRI in Jakarta has made pre-independence colonial records more accessible, but the most politically sensitive post-1945 records are controlled by the Indonesian National Armed Forces (TNI) and the State Intelligence Agency (BIN), neither of which has undertaken systematic declassification. This creates a structural asymmetry: the better-documented Dutch colonial archive is more open to scholars than the Indonesian state archive for the period of national independence.

Institutional geography remains a second structural issue. The pre-eminent journals and presses in the field — BKI, the Journal of Southeast Asian Studies, Cornell SEAP publications, Brill — are located in the Netherlands, Singapore, and the United States. The expense of access to these publications, and the linguistic barriers created by a field that requires facility in Dutch, Old Javanese, Arabic, and English as well as Indonesian, disadvantages scholars at Indonesian provincial universities where library resources and international connectivity are limited. Curaming identified this as one of the key structural reinforcers of the asymmetry between Indonesian and Western scholarship, noting that the Soedjatmoko volume itself was published in Ithaca and that much of the most influential work on Indonesian history continued to be produced outside Indonesia.35

Methodological frontiers offer reasons for optimism. Digital humanities projects — the digitisation of ANRI holdings, the online availability of BKI's full archive, collaborative mapping projects for VOC trade routes, and the development of searchable corpuses of Old Javanese and Malay texts — are transforming access to source material. Environmental history, represented by the preceding chapter of this book, has opened new questions about the relationship between ecological change and political history largely invisible in the textual record. Gender history and the history of sexuality, long marginalised by an almost exclusively male documentary tradition, have begun to reconstruct the lives of women, nyai (indigenous concubines of colonial men), female Islamic scholars, and women's organisations from the nationalist period forward, using a combination of Dutch administrative records, indigenous literary sources, and oral history. The history of Indonesia's Chinese, Arab, and South Asian diasporas — examined in the preceding thematic chapter — integrates the archipelago into global circulation histories that transcend the national frame. Oral history projects in the outer islands, Papua, and Aceh are recovering local perspectives on events that colonial and national archives record, if at all, only from the outside.

The deepest methodological challenge, as this book's long journey from the Yupa inscriptions of Kutai to the 2024 election has repeatedly demonstrated, is not the shortage of evidence but the abundance of contested interpretation. When the same Nagarakretagama is cited simultaneously to claim Majapahit as the historical foundation of the unitary Indonesian state and as evidence of nothing more than a regional East Javanese kingdom's ritual self-aggrandisement; when the VOC archive is used both to reconstruct the social world it administered and to interrogate the power relations that produced the categories of that record; when the events of 1965–66 remain a live political dispute in which perpetrators, victims' families, and the Indonesian state are still present actors — historiography cannot pretend to be neutral. Writing Indonesian history is itself a political act, not because historians choose to make it so, but because the history of Indonesia has not finished happening. The suppression of the PKI's history, the unresolved status of East Timor's incorporation, the ongoing conflict in Papua, the fate of Chinese Indonesians' collective memory of 1998 — these are not merely historiographical problems to be solved by better archives or more sophisticated methods. They are questions about justice, recognition, and the distribution of power in a democratic society still in the process of coming to terms with its own past.

The generations of historians surveyed in this chapter — from van Leur writing on a burning ship in the Java Sea to Jess Melvin unboxing three thousand classified documents in an Aceh government library — have together produced something that was not possible a century ago: a multi-perspectival, seriously critical, richly sourced account of Indonesian history that no single government or institution controls. That account is, and must remain, unfinished. History, as the great debates of this book have shown, is not a completed inventory of the past but an ongoing argument about what the past means and who has the right to claim it. To study Indonesian history is to participate in that argument — and that, in the end, is the most important thing a book like this one can teach.

Summary

Indonesian historiography rests on four principal source categories — inscriptions and epigraphy (from the Kutai yupa onwards), indigenous court literature (the Nagarakretagama, babads, hikayats), foreign accounts (Chinese, Arabic, European), and the colonial archive — each carrying characteristic strengths and structural biases. Ann Laura Stoler's work on the colonial archive as an instrument of power rather than a neutral repository has been transformative for methodology. The shift from colonial "Eurocentric" scholarship to the "autonomous history" programme, pioneered by J.C. van Leur and codified in the 1965 Soedjatmoko volume, was institutionalised at Cornell University and KITLV Leiden. The great historiographical debates — over Indianization, Majapahit's true extent, the causes of Islamization, the human costs of the Cultivation System, and above all the responsibility for 1965 — are as much arguments about evidence and interpretive authority as about facts. The New Order's official history, encoded in textbooks and monuments, has proven resilient even in the post-1998 democratic era. The silencing and partial recovery of 1965 memory — through the Cornell Paper, Roosa, Robinson, Melvin, and Indonesian scholars including Hilmar Farid — represents the field's central ongoing struggle. Future directions include digital access, gender and environmental history, oral history in the outer islands, and the decolonisation of institutional geography — but the deepest challenge remains the recognition that writing Indonesian history is itself a political act in a society still negotiating its past.

50.3 J.C. van Leur is credited with inaugurating "autonomous" Indonesian historiography, yet critics argue that his analysis was itself dependent on European social-science frameworks. Can a European scholar write genuinely "Indonesia-centric" history? What are the conditions under which this might be possible?
50.4 The Nagarakretagama has been used to support both a maximalist claim (Majapahit as proto-Indonesian empire) and a minimalist one (a regional East Javanese kingdom with limited tributary reach). What does this divergence tell us about the relationship between primary sources and historiographical interpretation?
50.5 How did the New Order regime use history — in textbooks, monuments, and official narratives — as an instrument of political legitimation? What evidence from this chapter suggests that this legacy has outlasted the regime itself?
50.6 Compare the accounts of the 1965 killings offered by Anderson and McVey (1966/1971), Roosa (2006), Robinson (2018), and Melvin (2018). In what ways do they agree, and where do they diverge? What does the trajectory of this historiographical debate suggest about how historical truth is established?
50.7 The chapter argues that "writing Indonesian history is itself a political act." Do you agree? Is this true of all history, or is Indonesia's case distinctive in important ways?
50.8 What would a fully "decolonized" Indonesian historiography look like? What structural changes — in archival access, institutional geography, language, and methodology — would be required to achieve it?
