Learning Objectives

  • Trace the rise of Sarekat Islam as Indonesia's first mass movement and explain why it became the arena where Islamic nationalism and communism first collided.
  • Describe how the Indische Sociaal-Democratische Vereeniging (ISDV) transformed into the PKI in 1920 and explain the significance of its designation as "the first communist party in Asia."
  • Analyse Tan Malaka's argument that pan-Islam and communism were compatible anti-colonial forces, and assess the consequences of the Comintern's rejection of that argument.
  • Distinguish between the reformist Islam of Muhammadiyah and the traditionalist Islam of Nahdlatul Ulama, and explain the social and political stakes of that distinction.
  • Describe Sukarno's early ideological synthesis of nationalism, Islam, and Marxism and the founding of the PNI in 1927.
  • Assess the causes and consequences of the 1926–27 uprisings and the founding of the Boven Digul internment camp as instruments of colonial repression.

The decade and a half between the founding of Sarekat Islam in 1912 and the crushing of the communist uprisings of 1926–27 produced the ideological architecture of independent Indonesia. In those years, three great currents — Islam, communism, and secular nationalism — coalesced, competed, and clashed inside a single anti-colonial movement, bequeathing fault lines that would resurface at every major political crisis: the Madiun Affair of 1948, the catastrophe of 1965, and the identity politics of the twenty-first century. To understand modern Indonesia is, in large measure, to understand how these streams formed, how they briefly ran together, and why they finally — and violently — parted.

Three Streams, One Movement

The pergerakan — the Indonesian nationalist awakening — was never monolithic. From the moment an Islamic traders' cooperative was transformed into a mass political organization in 1912, the movement contained within itself radically different answers to the question: against what, precisely, are we struggling, and for what kind of society? Historians including Takashi Shiraishi and Ruth McVey have shown that the pergerakan cannot be understood as a simple linear progress from colonial subjecthood to national independence; it was a tense, creative, and ultimately violent struggle over the very meaning of liberation.1

For the santri — the devout Muslim who oriented his daily life around prayer, Quranic learning, and the authority of the kiai — the answer was clear: liberation meant an Islamic social order freed from Dutch colonial interference in religious life, land, and trade. For the radical who had absorbed European socialist thought in the Indies' Dutch-language schools or in the Netherlands itself, the enemy was capitalism as much as colonialism, and the goal was class liberation alongside national liberation. For the secular intellectual formed by Western liberal and nationalist theory, the mosque and the Marxist cadre were potential partners in a coalition that must ultimately be commanded by a nationalist, not a cleric or a party secretary. These three currents did not occupy sealed compartments; they flowed into each other, borrowed from each other's vocabularies, and argued within shared organizations. The history of the period 1912–1930 is in large part the history of how they attempted, and failed, to sustain a common front.

Pergerakan — literally "the movement"; the broad Indonesian nationalist awakening of the early twentieth century, encompassing Islamic, communist, and secular-nationalist tendencies within a shared anti-colonial project.

Sarekat Islam: The First Mass Movement

The organization that brought the nationalist movement to the masses was neither a communist party nor a secular political club but an Islamic trading association. Sarekat Islam (the Islamic Union) grew out of a batik traders' cooperative founded in Solo (Surakarta) in 1911–12, initially as a self-help body for Javanese Muslim merchants squeezed between Dutch plantation interests and Chinese commercial intermediaries. It was formally constituted under the leadership of Raden Mas Hadji Oemar Said Tjokroaminoto — a charismatic Javanese aristocrat who combined Islamic piety with a gift for mass oratory that his followers compared, not always metaphorically, to prophecy.

Growth was extraordinary. By the Central Sarekat Islam congress of 1916 the organization had attracted approximately 360,000 members across Java; by 1919 the figure had risen to something approaching two million, making SI the first genuinely mass Indonesian political organization.2 Its appeal was broad because it was theologically populist: it offered an Islamic framework capacious enough to accommodate grievances as varied as Chinese economic competition, Dutch land appropriation, and the brutalities of plantation labor. George McTurnan Kahin, whose foundational study of Indonesian nationalism drew on interviews with the participants themselves, describes SI's rapid expansion as a social earthquake, an almost spontaneous aggregation of grievances that Tjokroaminoto channeled but did not wholly control.3

Yet the very breadth of SI's tent was its structural weakness. Inside the organization, literate young workers and urban craftsmen were imbibing Dutch socialist and Marxist literature alongside Quranic commentaries. The intellectual atmosphere of colonial Java in the 1910s was charged with multiple imports from Europe: theosophical spiritualism, Social Democracy, and — after November 1917 — the galvanizing example of the Russian Revolution. Tjokroaminoto's own writings attempted a synthesis: his 1924 book Islam and Socialism argued that Islam already contained a complete social philosophy superior to both capitalism and European Marxism, a claim that Lin Hongxuan's study of the "sickle as crescent" identifies as the foundational text of the Islamic-socialist current in Indonesian politics.4 But Tjokroaminoto's synthesis could not contain the tensions for long.

Santri — a pious Muslim, often specifically one who has studied in a pesantren (Islamic boarding school); in the sociological literature on Indonesian Islam, used to describe the devout Muslim segment of Javanese society, in contrast to the more nominally Muslim abangan.

The First Communist Party in Asia: From ISDV to PKI

The catalyst for the radicalization of SI from within was a Dutch socialist. Hendricus Josephus François Marie Sneevliet arrived in the Netherlands East Indies in 1913 and in May 1914 founded the Indische Sociaal-Democratische Vereeniging (ISDV — Indies Social-Democratic Association) in Surabaya. Oliver Crawford's recent study confirms that the ISDV started with approximately sixty members, almost entirely Dutch nationals.5 Sneevliet immediately recognized the paradox of his position: a tiny European socialist circle would remain politically irrelevant unless it could connect to the mass movement already forming among Indonesians. His solution was a tactic later codified in Comintern doctrine as the "bloc within" — ISDV members joined Sarekat Islam individually, worked their way into positions of local influence, and turned SI branches in railway towns and plantation centres into arenas of Marxist agitation and trade union organizing.

Dov Bing's comparative study of Sneevliet's career traces how the "Sneevlietian strategy" — entry into a nationalist mass movement rather than construction of a separate communist vanguard — was the organizational innovation carried from the Indies to China when Sneevliet (under the alias "Maring") served as the Comintern representative at the founding congress of the Chinese Communist Party in 1921.6 The Indonesian model became, briefly, the template for communist strategy in Asia.

Sneevliet was expelled by the Dutch colonial authorities in 1918, but the Javanese figures he had mentored continued his project. Semaun, born in Surabaya in 1899 and employed as a railway worker, had joined the ISDV while still a teenager, learned to read Dutch, and by 1917 was editing socialist publications in Malay. Darsono, another young Javanese intellectual, became the PKI's principal theorist of class struggle. Together they led the "red SI" (SI Merah) faction, pushing the organization toward labor militancy, plantation strikes, and confrontation with both the colonial state and SI's conservative Islamic leadership.

In May 1920, at a congress in Semarang, the ISDV reconstituted itself as the Perserikatan Kommunist di India — the Communist Association of the Indies, later rendered as Partai Komunis Indonesia (PKI). Crawford's research confirms that contemporaries recognized the organization as "the first of its kind in Asia," predating the Chinese Communist Party (founded July 1921) and all other Asian parties by at least a year.7 Semaun, then twenty-one years old, became the PKI's first chairman.8 The PKI was immediately affiliated with the Communist International (Comintern), whose Third Congress Semaun attended in Moscow in 1921, and it operated simultaneously as an independent party and as a faction within the sprawling body of Sarekat Islam — a dual existence that guaranteed conflict.

PKI (Partai Komunis Indonesia) — the Communist Party of Indonesia, constituted at Semarang in May 1920 from the Dutch colonial socialist group ISDV. Widely considered the first communist party established in Asia, it played a central role in Indonesian politics from its founding until its destruction in 1965–66.

Red and White: The Discipline Clause and the Great Split

The conflict between the PKI faction and the Islamic majority inside Sarekat Islam reached its decisive moment at the Fifth National Congress of the Central Sarekat Islam (CSI) in October 1921. Under sustained pressure from Tjokroaminoto and conservative Islamic leaders who were alarmed by what they saw as communist infiltration of a religious organization, the congress adopted a "discipline clause" (disiplin partai): SI members were henceforth required to hold no other party membership. Since the PKI was by then a formally constituted party with its own discipline and Comintern affiliation, the clause effectively expelled the communist faction from the CSI's central structure. Ruth McVey, in her definitive monograph on the rise of Indonesian communism, states the outcome plainly: "The acceptance of party discipline by the October 1921 congress ended the bloc within the Central Sarekat Islam but not in the SI as a whole."9

Local SI branches in heavily industrialized areas of Java — particularly in the railway towns of Central Java and among Surabaya dock workers — remained under communist influence for several more years, producing a de facto two-SI system: a "white" network loyal to Tjokroaminoto's Islamic nationalism and a network of "red" local branches that functioned as PKI auxiliaries. The split was not merely organizational; it reflected a genuine sociological division between the devout merchant and landowning class who backed white SI and the wage-laboring and artisan strata who formed the PKI's constituency. McVey's analysis shows how this class-inflected religious division would harden over the following decade until the two tendencies became irreconcilable enemies.10

The discipline clause of 1921 was a turning point in Indonesian political history whose echoes would be heard for generations. The institutional severance between communist and Islamic politics inaugurated a pattern of mutual suspicion and episodic violence that culminated in the Madiun Affair of 1948, when Republican troops loyal to Sukarno crushed a PKI-affiliated uprising, and ultimately in the catastrophic anti-communist mass killings of 1965–66.

Tan Malaka and the Question of Pan-Islam

The most searching and restless intellect produced by the early PKI was Ibrahim Sutan Malaka — known to history as Tan Malaka. Born in 1897 in the Minangkabau highlands of West Sumatra, educated partly in the Netherlands at a teacher-training college, and radicalized by both the Russian Revolution and the anti-colonial nationalist movement, Tan Malaka rose to the chairmanship of the PKI in 1921 before being expelled from the Indies by the Dutch colonial authorities in 1922. He would spend the remaining quarter-century of his life in permanent exile across Asia and Europe, hunted by colonial police forces, intermittently imprisoned, and finally executed by Indonesian Republican troops in 1949 — a victim not of colonialism but of the revolution he had devoted his life to advancing. Rudolf Mrázek's psychological-historical study of Tan Malaka traces how this trajectory of perpetual displacement shaped a political personality of extraordinary originality but fatal impracticality: a thinker always ahead of his organizational moment.11

At the Fourth World Congress of the Communist International in Moscow in November 1922, Tan Malaka delivered a speech that challenged one of the Comintern's foundational assumptions. The Congress, following Lenin's framework, tended to treat Islam as a form of bourgeois religious ideology that communists must ultimately oppose, despite Lenin's tactical recognition of its anti-imperialist potential. Tan Malaka insisted this was wrong for Asia. He argued that for the 250 million Muslims living under colonial rule, pan-Islamism was not a reactionary ideology but the living form of national liberation: "Pan-Islamism now means the brotherhood of all Muslim peoples, and the liberation struggle not only of the Arab but also of the Indian, the Javanese, and all the oppressed Muslim peoples."12 He cited the recent cooperation between SI and PKI branches — joint demands for factory workers' councils, land reform, and wage improvements — as evidence that the alliance between Islamic piety and communist class struggle was genuinely productive in the Indies. His proposal was that the Comintern should endorse, rather than attack, pan-Islamic sentiment as a progressive anti-imperialist force.13

The Comintern rejected his argument. The official position insisted that Islam, as a religious ideology, inevitably served ruling-class interests and must be combated as colonized peoples developed a secular class consciousness. Mrázek argues that this rejection crystallized Tan Malaka's gradual disenchantment with Moscow's authority over Asian revolutionary movements: Comintern strategy, developed by Europeans in European conditions, could not be mechanically transferred to a society where the mosque was often the only available space of mass political assembly, where the hajj pilgrimage network connected activists across the colonial world, and where Islam was not the opium of the masses but the idiom in which the masses imagined liberation itself.

The figure of Haji Mohammad Misbach of Surakarta — known in the Indies press as "the Red Pilgrim" — embodied this synthesis at a popular level. A genuinely devout Muslim who had performed the hajj, Misbach edited both an Islamic newspaper (Medan Moeslimin) and a communist paper (Islam Bergerak), insisting that authentic Islam demanded the abolition of capitalist exploitation. Lin Hongxuan's research into what he calls "the sickle as crescent" documents how Misbach and associated figures developed a vernacular political theology in which the oppression of the Javanese peasant by Dutch plantation capitalism was simultaneously a sin against God and an injustice against the working class — a fusion that made sense precisely because colonial Java's material conditions did not map neatly onto European class categories.14

After the 1926–27 uprisings destroyed the PKI's legal existence, Tan Malaka founded PARI (Partai Republik Indonesia, 1927) outside Comintern discipline — a clandestine independence organization whose nationalism was primary and whose Marxism was instrumental. Helen Jarvis, assessing his legacy, argues that he became the prototypical revolutionary orphan: too nationalist for the Comintern, too Marxist for the Islamic movement, and too uncontrollably independent for the PNI's coalition nationalism.15 His trajectory anticipated the failures of the postcolonial left across Asia.

Pan-Islamism — the political idea that the world's Muslim communities share a common identity and should act in solidarity, especially against imperial powers. In the early twentieth century, Ottoman-centered pan-Islamic sentiment intersected with Asian anti-colonial nationalism; Tan Malaka's 1922 Comintern speech was the pivotal attempt to reconcile pan-Islamism with Marxist communism.

Reform and Tradition: Muhammadiyah and Nahdlatul Ulama

While communists and Islamic nationalists fought over the body of Sarekat Islam, Indonesian Islam itself was undergoing its own internal restructuring — a division between reformism and traditionalism whose consequences for Indonesian politics have been as profound as the communist-Islamic split, and more durable.

The reformist current had its origins in the late nineteenth century, when Javanese Muslims who had studied in Mecca and Cairo returned to the Indies carrying the ideas of Muhammad Abduh, the Egyptian scholar who argued that Islam must be purified of later accretions and reinterpreted through rational engagement with modernity. In the Indies, this program meant two things: first, the systematic critique of popular Javanese Islamic practice, which reformers regarded as corrupted by animist (kejawen) and Hindu-Buddhist elements; and second, the establishment of modern schools — combining Islamic religious instruction with Western-style secular subjects — to equip Muslims to compete on equal terms with Dutch-educated Christians and with the Chinese commercial community.

The chief institutional vehicle for Islamic reformism in Java was Muhammadiyah, founded in the Kauman district of Yogyakarta on 18 November 1912 by Kyai Haji Ahmad Dahlan, a court religious official (penghulu) at the Yogyakarta sultanate. Deliar Noer's authoritative study of the modernist Muslim movement describes how Dahlan, drawing directly on Abduh's Egyptian reform program, built Muhammadiyah as an educational and social welfare organization rather than a political party: it established schools, clinics, and orphanages in the Dutch institutional style, competing directly with Christian missionary organizations for social influence over the Javanese Muslim population.16 By 1938 the organization had established 852 branches across the archipelago with more than 250,000 members, making it one of the largest Islamic organizations in the world.

The traditionalist response came a decade and a half later. The kiai of the Javanese pesantren world — the rural Islamic scholars who led the pesantren (traditional boarding schools) and maintained the classical Shafi'i legal tradition and Sufi devotional practices that reformists attacked as innovations — organized their own countervailing force. The immediate political context was urgent: the Wahhabi movement in Arabia, backed by the Saudi conquest of the Hijaz in 1924, threatened to abolish the four Sunni legal schools and the practices of grave visitation and saint veneration that Indonesian rural Muslims held dear. The reformist challenge within the Indies compounded the threat.

On 31 January 1926, twenty-four traditionalist kiai meeting in Surabaya founded Nahdlatul Ulama (NU — the Rising of the Scholars). The organization's founding chairman, Rais Akbar, was Kyai Haji Hasyim Asy'ari — the most respected Islamic scholar in East Java, head of the great Tebuireng pesantren in Jombang, and a figure of such moral authority that his endorsement could move entire communities. Cornelis van Dijk's study of Indonesian Islamic politics traces how NU positioned itself explicitly against both Wahhabism and Muhammadiyah modernism, insisting on the validity of the classical legal schools and the authenticity of local Islamic devotional practices rooted in centuries of Javanese pesantren tradition.17

Harry Benda's account of Indonesian Islam before the Japanese occupation emphasizes that the reformist-traditionalist split was not merely theological: it mapped onto sociological and economic differences. Muhammadiyah's modernist program appealed disproportionately to urban traders, administrative officials, and the educated middle class; NU's traditional networks were deeply rooted in the rural pesantren world of East Java, Madura, and the Outer Islands, where the kiai commanded networks of personal loyalty extending across entire districts. Both organizations were officially non-political in their early decades — both explicitly avoided electoral participation and direct anti-colonial agitation, focusing instead on education, social welfare, and religious organization — but both would be powerfully drawn into political life as independence approached.18

Nahdlatul Ulama (NU) — "the Rising of the Scholars," a traditionalist Islamic mass organization founded in Surabaya on 31 January 1926 under Kyai Haji Hasyim Asy'ari. NU defended classical Sunni legal traditions and pesantren-based Islamic practice against both reformist modernism and Wahhabi influence; it became one of the largest Islamic organizations in the world.
Muhammadiyah — a reformist Islamic mass organization founded in Yogyakarta on 18 November 1912 by Kyai Haji Ahmad Dahlan. It promoted the purification of popular Islamic practice from animist accretions and built a nationwide network of modern schools, hospitals, and social welfare institutions. Along with NU, it remains one of the two largest Islamic organizations in Indonesia.

The Muhammadiyah-NU cleavage created a permanent structural tension within Indonesian Islam between a modernist, scripturally oriented, organizationally modern Islam associated with the urban trading class and professional intelligentsia, and a traditionalist, jurisprudentially grounded, charismatically led Islam rooted in the rural peasantry and the pesantren network. This tension would shape every major political contest in independent Indonesia — from the constitutional debates of 1945 over whether Indonesia would be an Islamic state, to the politics of the 1955 election, to the culture wars of the twenty-first century.

Secular Nationalism: Study Clubs and the PNI

The third stream of the pergerakan was secular and non-confessional. Its leaders were educated Indonesians who had passed through the Dutch colonial school system — sometimes including the STOVIA medical school in Batavia, or the Technische Hoogeschool (Technical College) in Bandung — and who had absorbed Western liberal, nationalist, and Marxist thought alongside their technical education. Unlike the Islamic nationalists who grounded their politics in the umma, or the PKI cadres whose loyalty was to the international proletariat, the secular nationalists oriented themselves toward the idea of Indonesia itself — a modern, territorially defined nation whose citizens would be equal regardless of religion, ethnicity, or class.

The institutional form of this tendency in the early 1920s was the studieclub (study club) — informal discussion circles in which educated young Indonesians read newspapers, debated politics, and formulated their vision of a future Indonesia. Leo Suryadinata's analysis of the pre-war youth movement documents how Jong Java, Jong Sumatranen Bond, and other ethnically defined student organizations were by the mid-1920s beginning the slow transition toward a pan-Indonesian identity, partly under the gravitational pull of the Perhimpunan Indonesia (PI, the Indonesian Association) that Hatta and other students were radicalizing in the Netherlands.19 The most significant studieclub on the archipelago itself was the Bandung Study Club, established in 1926 by a twenty-five-year-old civil engineering student named Sukarno.

Sukarno — born Kusno Sosrodihardjo in Surabaya in 1901, the son of a Javanese teacher and a Balinese Hindu mother, and educated partly in the household of Tjokroaminoto — was already by his mid-twenties a political figure of unusual synthetic intelligence. His intimate exposure to SI's Islamic nationalism coexisted in his mind with a voracious absorption of European political theory: Marxism (particularly in the Leninist anti-imperialist variant), the revolutionary nationalism of Mustafa Kemal and the Young Turks, Indian nationalism as articulated by Tilak and Gandhi, and — most decisively — a reading of world history that placed the colonial condition of Indonesians within a global structure of imperial exploitation. In 1926, while still a student, Sukarno published a series of essays in the nationalist journal Suluh Indonesia Muda that became the canonical statement of the Indonesian nationalist synthesis.

In Nasionalisme, Islamisme dan Marxisme (Nationalism, Islam, and Marxism), Sukarno argued that the three ideological currents were not enemies but complementary forces: nationalism supplied the goal (a free and united Indonesia), Islam supplied the moral energy and mass mobilization capacity of the country's Muslim majority, and Marxism supplied the structural analysis of how colonial capitalism exploited the Indonesian worker and peasant. Rather than forcing a choice among the three, Sukarno proposed a grand coalition in which all three streams directed their energy against the common colonial enemy before arguing over the future.20 Lin Hongxuan identifies this essay as the pivotal moment when the three-current synthesis became an explicit political program, anticipating by three decades the NASAKOM formula (Nasionalisme-Agama-Komunisme) that Sukarno would make official doctrine under Guided Democracy in the late 1950s.21

On 4 July 1927, in Bandung, Sukarno and a circle of colleagues — including several who had been educated at Dutch and Indonesian universities, all with strong ties to the study-club network — founded the Partai Nasional Indonesia (PNI — Indonesian National Party). Unlike all earlier nationalist organizations, the PNI declared its explicit and non-negotiable goal to be full Indonesian independence from Dutch colonial rule — not autonomy, not gradual devolution, but independence.22 Its theoretical framework was what Sukarno termed Marhaenisme, derived from Marx's concept of the exploited proletariat but adapted to Indonesian conditions. Most Indonesians were not factory workers but smallholding peasants — the marhaen, a word Sukarno coined from the name of a Sundanese farmer he reportedly encountered near Bandung, who owned a tiny plot of land and a simple tool but was still crushed by colonial taxation and market structures. Marhaenisme declared that this class of smallholders, sharecroppers, petty traders, and artisans — who possessed the means of production in a technical sense but were structurally oppressed by colonialism — was the revolutionary subject of Indonesian independence.23

Marhaenisme — Sukarno's adaptation of Marxist class analysis to Indonesian conditions, centered on the marhaen: the small peasant or artisan who technically owns the means of production but is structurally impoverished by colonialism. It allowed Sukarno to deploy a Marxist analytical register while constructing a specifically Indonesian revolutionary subject distinct from the European industrial proletariat.
PNI (Partai Nasional Indonesia) — the Indonesian National Party, founded in Bandung on 4 July 1927 by Sukarno and colleagues. The first Indonesian political organization to declare full independence as its explicit and immediate goal, and the first to formally articulate a secular, non-confessional nationalist ideology.

The PNI grew rapidly, recruiting educated professionals, students, and urban workers across Java. Its significance was as much symbolic as organizational: by making the demand for independence explicit and non-negotiable, it transformed the politics of the pergerakan. Where earlier organizations had debated autonomy and improved conditions within the colonial system, the PNI placed independence itself on the agenda, forcing all other political tendencies — Islamic, communist, and traditionalist — to position themselves in relation to that demand. John Ingleson's study of the nationalist movement under colonial repression emphasizes that Marhaenisme's deliberate avoidance of the word "communist" — while still deploying a structural analysis of colonial exploitation — was a calculated political strategy in the aftermath of the 1926–27 uprisings, which had made communist association a guaranteed route to prison.24

The 1926–27 Uprisings and the World of Boven Digul

The decade of organizational growth described in the preceding sections was interrupted, and in one case destroyed, by the PKI's decision to launch an armed insurrection against colonial rule. The story of those uprisings is at once a study in ideological overconfidence, internal party failure, and the full weight of colonial repression.

After the discipline clause of 1921 expelled the communist faction from the Central Sarekat Islam, the PKI had rebuilt its organizational base through trade unions, particularly among railway, sugar plantation, and dock workers, and through a network of local branches across Java and parts of Sumatra. By the mid-1920s the party was simultaneously at its strongest point organizationally and deeply divided internally. Tan Malaka, living in exile, sent messages arguing that a premature armed uprising would be suicidal — the party lacked sufficient mass support, a revolutionary situation did not yet exist, and the colonial military's capacity for repression was overwhelming. The internal faction led by Alimin and Musso, backed by the Comintern's Southeast Asian bureau in Singapore, overrode his objections. An uprising was planned for late 1926.

The revolt began in West Java in November 1926 — in the plantation hinterland of Batavia (Jakarta) and especially in Banten, a region with a deep tradition of Islamic millenarianism, anti-Dutch resistance, and rural unrest stretching back to the nineteenth century. A second wave broke out in West Sumatra in January 1927, particularly in the Minangkabau highlands where the PKI had organized among railway workers. Both uprisings were poorly coordinated, chronologically mismatched — separate regional uprisings that were supposed to be simultaneous — and quickly suppressed by the colonial army. In Shelton Stromquist's 1967 historiographical reassessment, a deliberately revisionist reading challenges the standard dismissal of the uprisings as mere revolutionary adventurism: he argues that the revolts had "decidedly nationalist characteristics," that their leaders drew on a range of non-communist motivations including Islamic millenarianism and genuine material grievance, and that the deterioration of peasant conditions under the colonial plantation economy provided a structural foundation for mobilization that went beyond pure PKI ideology.25 The historiographical debate over the relative weight of communist organization, Islamic millenarian tradition, and structural peasant grievance in explaining the uprisings has not been conclusively resolved — and probably cannot be, given the destruction of PKI organizational records in the subsequent repression.

The colonial response was swift, extensive, and deliberately exemplary. The PKI was outlawed, its leaders arrested and in some cases executed, and the party was effectively destroyed as a functioning organization within the Indies for the better part of two decades. The most significant and far-reaching institutional consequence was the creation of a dedicated political prison in the malarial jungle of southwestern Dutch New Guinea.

Boven Digul — formally the internment camp at Tanah Merah on a tributary of the Digul River in what is now Papua, Indonesia — was established in 1927. The site was chosen with deliberate calculation: it was weeks by boat from the nearest port, surrounded by impenetrable jungle and swamp, afflicted by malaria and other tropical diseases, and inhabited by Papuan communities with whom the internees had no cultural or linguistic connection. Elizabeth Chandra's study of Boven Digul in literary representation documents that the camp held approximately 1,300 prisoners in the late 1920s — a figure that included PKI organizers and activists, Islamic radicals associated with the Banten uprising, and, as the 1930s progressed, secular nationalist figures whose political activities the colonial state deemed threatening.26 Among those eventually exiled to Boven Digul were the journalist and novelist Marco Kartodikromo, one of the pioneers of the Indonesian-language press, and several figures who would later be prominent in the independence movement and the early Republic.

Takashi Shiraishi's study of what he calls "the phantom world of Digul" argues that Boven Digul was not simply a prison but a technology of colonial political governance — a calculated attempt to remove the most organizationally capable Indonesians from the archipelago while demonstrating to those who remained that the costs of radicalism were terrifying and permanent.27 The camp was administered with the involvement of the Politieke Inlichtingen Dienst (PID — the colonial Political Intelligence Service), which maintained extensive surveillance dossiers on every internee and used the camp population as a living index of the political landscape of the pergerakan. Marieke Bloembergen's research into PID methods in West Sumatra in the immediate aftermath of the 1927 uprising traces how the colonial state developed increasingly sophisticated techniques of "political policing" — constructing detailed maps of organizational networks, tracking the movements of exiles, and compiling intelligence portraits of potential future leaders — in the years following the uprisings.28

Tan Malaka, who had opposed the uprising from exile, responded to the PKI's destruction by founding PARI in 1927 as a clandestine network that operated outside Comintern discipline, moving between Singapore, Bangkok, and other Asian cities. Helen Jarvis describes him as the figure of the "revolutionary in permanent exile" — outside the organizational structures of every movement that might have used him, incapable of subordinating his judgment to party discipline, and therefore incapable of translating his intellectual vision into sustained political organization.29 The contrast with Sukarno — who was building the PNI's legal mass organization in precisely the same years — is instructive: two of the most brilliant intellects produced by the pergerakan, pursuing liberation through irreconcilable organizational logics.

Fault Lines Laid Down: Islam, Class, and Nation

By 1930, the three ideological streams had been separated from each other and each had been weakened. The PKI had been destroyed as a functioning party. Sarekat Islam, deprived of its communist wing and unable to compete with the better-organized Muhammadiyah on its reformist flank or with NU in the traditionalist heartland, was entering a period of slow organizational decline. The PNI was about to be decapitated: Sukarno and other leaders were arrested by the Dutch colonial authorities in December 1929 and tried in 1930 — a show trial at which Sukarno delivered his celebrated defence speech, Indonesia Menggugat (Indonesia Accuses), which was distributed clandestinely across the archipelago and enormously enlarged his reputation.

Yet the defeats of the late 1920s settled nothing. They drove the three streams underground or into new organizational forms — Muhammadiyah and NU expanded their social-welfare networks, the secular nationalists regrouped under different party names after the Dutch dissolved the PNI in 1931, and the surviving PKI cadres continued to organize in exile and through underground cells — but they did not resolve the fundamental question of what kind of country an independent Indonesia would be.

Three structural tensions bequeathed by the period 1912–1930 would run through the entire history of independent Indonesia. The first was the tension between Islamic politics and secular nationalism: could a Muslim-majority society be governed by a secular state, and if so, under what constitutional arrangements? The second was the tension between communist class politics and broad-front nationalist coalition: could the PKI be a genuine partner in an independence movement that included large Islamic organizations and a secular nationalist bourgeoisie, or was class struggle ultimately incompatible with the ideological pluralism required for national coalition? The third was the tension between revolutionary vanguardism and mass democracy: was the independence movement to be led by a disciplined revolutionary party, by Islamic mass organizations with their networks of popular loyalty, or by a charismatic individual who claimed to synthesize all streams at once?

Sukarno's 1926 synthesis had proposed that these tensions could be held in creative suspension under the banner of anti-colonial nationalism. In the short run, the discipline clause of 1921 and the uprisings of 1926–27 had already shown how fragile that suspension was. In the longer run, the synthesis would be attempted again under Guided Democracy and would collapse in 1965 in the bloodiest of all its failures. The fault lines laid down in the founding decade of the pergerakan were not contingent accidents but structural features of a movement divided about its own deepest purposes — and about whose liberation, exactly, liberation was for.

Summary

The period 1912–1930 saw three great ideological currents struggle for the soul of the Indonesian nationalist movement. Sarekat Islam, Indonesia's first mass organization, became the arena where Islamic nationalism and communism collided: the communist faction, organized through the PKI (founded 1920 — the first communist party in Asia), worked within SI through the "bloc within" tactic until the discipline clause of October 1921 expelled it from the central body. Tan Malaka, the PKI's most original thinker, proposed at the 1922 Comintern Congress that pan-Islamism was an anti-imperialist force communists should embrace; Moscow rejected him. Indonesian Islam itself split between Muhammadiyah's reformist modernism (founded 1912) and NU's traditionalism (founded 1926). Secular nationalism found its institutional voice in the study clubs and in Sukarno's PNI (founded 4 July 1927), whose Marhaenisme adapted Marxist class analysis to the conditions of Indonesia's peasant majority. The PKI's failed uprisings of 1926–27 were crushed by the colonial state, which exiled approximately 1,300 activists to the remote Boven Digul internment camp in New Guinea. By 1930 the three streams had been separated from each other, but the fault lines between them — Islam versus secularism, class struggle versus national coalition, religious mass organization versus secular vanguard — were laid down permanently, to recur at every major turning point of the republic's history.

Review & Discussion Questions

25.1 Explain the "bloc within" strategy as practised by Sneevliet and the ISDV inside Sarekat Islam. What made it effective in the short run, and what structural contradictions made it unsustainable?
25.2 Tan Malaka argued at the 1922 Comintern Congress that pan-Islamism was a progressive anti-imperialist force. The Comintern rejected this argument. Whose position do you find more historically persuasive, and why?
25.3 Compare the reformist and traditionalist models of Indonesian Islam represented by Muhammadiyah and Nahdlatul Ulama. What social and class differences underlay this theological split?
25.4 Sukarno's 1926 essay proposed that nationalism, Islam, and Marxism could be synthesized as a unified anti-colonial force. What were the intellectual strengths of this argument, and what were its political weaknesses as shown by subsequent events?
25.5 Historians disagree about whether the 1926–27 uprisings were primarily ideologically driven (by Comintern strategy), religiously inflected (by Islamic millenarianism), or structurally produced (by deteriorating peasant conditions). Assess the evidence for each interpretation.
25.6 What was the function of Boven Digul within the colonial political system? In what sense was it, as Shiraishi argues, not merely a prison but a technology of colonial governance?
25.7 The "discipline clause" of October 1921 is sometimes described as one of the pivotal moments in modern Indonesian history. Do you agree? What might have happened had the PKI remained inside Sarekat Islam?
25.8 The fault lines of 1912–1930 — Islam versus secularism, class versus nation — recurred at Madiun in 1948 and at the 1965 coup and killings. What does their persistence tell us about the deeper structure of Indonesian political conflict?
