Learning Objectives

  • Identify the Sailendra and the Sanjaya line as the two main dynastic forces of 8th–9th-century Central Java, and explain the scholarly debate about whether they were rival houses or branches of a single ruling family.
  • Describe the construction, structure, and spiritual programme of Borobudur, and explain why scholarly interpretation of the monument's cosmological meaning remains contested.
  • Explain the significance of the Prambanan temple complex and the Shivagrha inscription for understanding Hindu-Buddhist dynamics on the Kedu Plain.
  • Assess how the agrarian economy of volcanic Central Java — organised through sima land grants and royal patronage — funded the great monument-building programmes.
  • Evaluate the five competing scholarly theories for the mysterious shift of political power from Central to East Java around 929 CE.

In the eighth and ninth centuries CE, the fertile volcanic plain of Central Java was the site of the most ambitious stone-building programme in the history of Southeast Asia. Two dynasties — the Buddhist Sailendra and the Shaivite Hindu Sanjaya line — raised monuments that remain among the supreme achievements of world civilisation: Borobudur, the largest Buddhist temple on earth, and Prambanan, the grandest Hindu complex in the archipelago. Together they testify to the capacity of a densely settled agrarian society to mobilise resources, talent, and devotion on a scale that still astonishes. Yet the golden age ended abruptly. Around 929 CE the court moved east, the monuments fell silent, and within a century the jungle and the volcanos had begun to reclaim them. The reasons remain genuinely contested, and the abandonment of Central Java stands as one of the great unsolved riddles of Indonesian history.

The Kedu Plain: A World-Historical Stage

The Central Javanese monuments did not arise by accident of genius. They were the product of a specific landscape — the Kedu Plain, a broad, low-lying basin in what is today the province of Central Java, framed to the north-east by the smoking summits of Mount Merbabu and Mount Merapi and to the north-west by the paired cones of Mount Sundoro-Sumbing.1 The Progo and Elo rivers cross the plain in a natural system of irrigation, and the soil — continuously renewed by volcanic eruption — ranks among the most fertile in the tropics. The earliest inscriptional source from the period already registers this abundance: the Canggal inscription of 732 CE describes Java as "a land rich in grain and gold mines."2

This productivity was decisive for what came next. Monument-building at the scale of Borobudur or Prambanan required not only stone, tools, and engineering knowledge but an agricultural surplus large enough to feed and sustain thousands of workers across generations. The Kedu Plain, watered by mountain streams and farmed by Javanese wet-rice cultivators across centuries, provided that surplus. Central Java in the eighth and ninth centuries was, in material terms, one of the most productive patches of ground in the early medieval world, and it is no coincidence that it was here — rather than in the archipelago's drier or more maritime islands — that the great classical monuments arose.

The landscape also carried cosmic significance. In the Indic religious traditions that had taken root in Java over several centuries, mountains were the abode of gods, the earthly reflection of Mount Meru — the axis of the Hindu-Buddhist cosmos. Building a temple complex on a plain between active volcanos was not merely practical; it located the structure symbolically at the centre of the world.3 The builders of Borobudur and Prambanan inhabited a landscape already charged with divine meaning, and their monuments were designed to deepen and crystallise that charge.

Two Dynasties, One Plain: The Historiographical Problem

The golden age of Central Java is conventionally attributed to two dynasties: the Sailendra (sometimes written Shailendra), ardent Mahayana Buddhists who raised Borobudur and a cluster of surrounding Buddhist temples, and the Sanjaya line, the ruling house of the Shaivite Hindu kingdom that historians call Ancient Mataram or Medang. Yet the relationship between these two dynasties is one of the thorniest problems in the historiography of early Indonesia.

The older interpretation, developed by European scholars including the French epigraphist George Coedès and the Dutch art historian N.J. Krom in the early twentieth century, held that the Sailendra and the Sanjaya line were distinct, competing dynasties who alternated in power on the Kedu Plain.4 On this reading, the Sailendra were Buddhists from outside (perhaps Sumatran or even Indian) who temporarily supplanted a native Javanese Hindu ruling house; the great Shaivite monuments at Prambanan in the mid-ninth century represented the Hindu Sanjaya line's reassertion of power after a period of Buddhist dominance. The model had the appeal of dramatic clarity: two great monument complexes, two rival dynasties.

More recent scholarship has complicated this picture considerably. A growing body of epigraphic and archaeological analysis suggests that the Sailendra and Sanjaya may have been related branches of a single extended royal family rather than opposed dynasties — both claiming descent from ancestral mountain kings, both capable of patronising Buddhist and Hindu structures at different moments, and both operating from the same royal capitals on the Kedu Plain.5 The intermarriage of Rakai Pikatan (a Sanjaya-line king) with Pramodhawardhani (a Sailendra princess) in the mid-ninth century is, on this reading, not a diplomatic resolution of dynastic conflict but a natural intra-family succession. This chapter presents the evidence for both interpretations wherever the sources are genuinely ambiguous, and resists the temptation to resolve a debate that remains open.

candi — the Old Javanese (and modern Indonesian) word for a temple or mortuary shrine, derived from the name of the Hindu goddess Durga in her form as death deity (Candika). A candi typically houses a deity image or commemorates a royal ancestor. The term applies to both Hindu and Buddhist structures in the Central and East Javanese architectural tradition; Borobudur and Prambanan are both loosely called candi, though each is architecturally unique.

The Sailendra: Lords of the Mountain

The name Sailendra is Sanskrit: śaila (mountain) + indra (lord), translating as "Lord of the Mountain." The name may allude to Mount Meru, making the dynasty the lords of the cosmic mountain — a claim to universal sovereignty with both Hindu and Buddhist resonances that would have been immediately legible to the court culture of eighth-century Java.6

The geographic and ethnic origins of the dynasty are vigorously contested. Four main hypotheses have circulated in modern scholarship. The oldest, proposed by the Indian historian R.C. Majumdar in 1933, derived the Sailendra from the Indian region of Kalinga (modern Odisha), citing similarities in the Sanskrit formulae of their inscriptions. In 1934 the French scholar George Coedès proposed a Funan origin — arguing that the Sailendra were the displaced ruling house of the earlier Indianised kingdom of Funan in mainland Southeast Asia — but this view has been largely rejected, particularly by Cambodia specialists who find no corroborating evidence. A third hypothesis, built on the evidence of shared Mahayana Buddhist patronage and on early inscriptions in Old Malay associated with the Srivijayan sphere, held that the Sailendra were originally Sumatran. The hypothesis currently favoured by many specialists is a fourth: that the Sailendra were native Javanese, descended from Central Java's own mountain-worshipping polities, with their earliest attested ancestor a figure called Dapunta Selendra named in the Sojomerto inscription (of uncertain but probably seventh-century date).7

Whatever their origins, the Sailendra emerge into clear documentary history with the Kalasan inscription of 778 CE. This Sanskrit stele, written in the distinctive Pranagari script of northern India rather than the Pallava script common to earlier Javanese inscriptions, describes how a Sailendra spiritual preceptor — addressed as the "jewel of the Sailendra family" (Śailēndravamśatilaka) — persuaded the reigning Maharaja Panangkaran to build a temple dedicated to the Buddhist goddess Tara and to donate the village of Kalasa to a monastic community (Sangha) for its maintenance.8 The Kalasan inscription is thus the oldest surviving document to mention the Sailendra dynasty by name, and it places the dynasty squarely in the business of Buddhist religious patronage from its first appearance.

Four years later, the Kelurak inscription of 782 CE records King Indra of the Sailendra — formally titled "Sri Sanggramadhananjaya," destroyer of arrogant enemies — installing an image of the bodhisattva Manjushri at the temple complex later known as Candi Sewu (the "Thousand Temples"). What is most striking about the Kelurak inscription is its syncretic religious vocabulary: it invokes Brahma, Vishnu, and Maheshvara (Shiva) alongside Buddhist deities, suggesting that the Sailendra practised a form of Tantrayana in which Indic religious categories overlapped rather than competing sharply.9

The Sailendra cultivated close connections with the Srivijayan maritime empire of Sumatra. The ruler Samaragrawira married Dewi Tara, daughter of the Srivijayan Maharaja Dharmasetu, binding the Buddhist power of inland Java to the Buddhist maritime power of the Strait of Malacca.10 For a period the two domains appear to have been jointly administered — or at least to have shared ruling personnel — though the precise constitutional relationship remains debated. After Balaputradewa, son of Samaratungga, was defeated in a Javanese power struggle (alluded to in the 856 CE Shivagrha inscription), he retreated to Sumatra and became the paramount ruler of Srivijaya. He is confirmed in that role by the Nalanda copper-plate inscription of 860 CE — found at the great Buddhist monastery of Nalanda in Bihar, India — which identifies him as "king of Suvarna-dvipa" (the Island of Gold, i.e., Sumatra) and as a Sailendra descendant, and records his construction of a monastery at Nalanda funded from Sumatran revenues.11 The Sailendra lineage in Sumatra survived until the Chola naval raid of 1025 CE, when the South Indian emperor Rajendra I captured the last identifiable Sailendra ruler, Sangrama Vijayatunggavarman, ending Srivijayan power as a political force in the western archipelago.

Sanjaya's Line: The Hindu Mataram Kingdom

Alongside the Buddhist Sailendra, and at times interwoven with them in ways that frustrate clean categorisation, stood the Shaivite Hindu dynasty founded by King Sanjaya in the early eighth century. The document that opens this dynasty's historical record is the Canggal inscription, a Sanskrit stele in Pallava script found at the Gunung Wukir temple complex near Magelang, precisely dated to 732 CE. It is also the oldest dated Sanskrit inscription from Java — the first written record of a Javanese king known by name. The inscription records Sanjaya's erection of a lingga (the phallic symbol of Shiva) at a place called Kunjarakunja, describes how he overthrew an earlier king named Sanna, and praises the island in effusive terms: Java is "rich in grain and gold mines," its people prosperous under righteous royal rule.2

Sanjaya's kingdom — which historians call Ancient Mataram or Medang to distinguish it sharply from the much later Islamic Mataram Sultanate of the sixteenth to eighteenth centuries — was centred on Shaivite Hindu ritual, with the king understood as a devotee or earthly manifestation of Shiva. Yet the line's relationship with Buddhism was porous. Panangkaran, listed as a Sanjaya successor in later king-lists, appears in the Kalasan inscription as the Maharaja persuaded to build a Buddhist temple. Whether this means Panangkaran was himself Buddhist, or was pragmatically sponsoring a Buddhist monastic community within a broader Shaivite polity, is debated. It is part of what has led modern scholars to question whether "Sailendra" and "Sanjaya" always name truly separate dynasties rather than two religious tendencies within a single cosmopolitan ruling class.

The most important retrospective source for the Mataram king-list is the Mantyasih inscription of 11 April 907 CE, issued by King Balitung. This charter traces nine generations of royal succession back to Sanjaya, naming each king in sequence. The Wanua Tengah III inscription of 908 CE, also issued under Balitung, supplements it with specific regnal dates.12 Together they provide the backbone of the Mataram chronology — though scholars have noted that Balitung, whose claim to the throne may have been irregular, had political incentives to construct an authoritative genealogy, and the lists should be read with that in mind.

The political and cultural apogee of the Sanjaya line fell under Balitung (c. 898–910 CE), who left more than forty surviving inscriptions — an extraordinary documentary density for the early medieval archipelago. Balitung continued the expansion of the Prambanan complex, confirmed sima status for multiple religious foundations, and in a striking act of pragmatic religious pluralism decreed in 904 CE that Buddhist monasteries should be tax-free and administratively autonomous. A committed Shaivite king protecting Buddhist institutions: this was not contradiction but statecraft, the enhancement of royal authority through the patronage of multiple sacred traditions simultaneously.13 The Central Javanese golden age was not, in the end, a story of Buddhist-Hindu conflict; it was a story of competitive building and mutual accommodation under a shared Indic cosmological language.

Borobudur: Building the Mountain of Merit

Borobudur — the Javanese name may derive from an Old Javanese compound rendered in Sanskrit as Bhūmi Sambhāra Bhudhāra, "the mountain of accumulated merit" — is the largest Buddhist monument on earth. UNESCO inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 1991 under criteria (i), (ii), and (vi), describing it as "a masterpiece of human creative genius" and "an exceptional testimony to a significant human tradition."14 It was built under the Sailendra dynasty, construction beginning around 780 CE and completing in the early ninth century.

The French archaeologist and architect Jacques Dumarçay, working from detailed structural analysis in the 1970s, proposed a five-stage construction model. The Sailendra began the lower terraced structure around 780 CE; subsequent phases between approximately 792 and 824 CE elaborated and refined it; and the final stage, perhaps around 833 CE, added the three upper circular terraces and the crowning central stupa.15 No dedicatory inscription has ever been found for Borobudur — a remarkable absence that means there is no contemporary document naming who commissioned it, what it was called, or what ritual purpose it was meant to serve. The Javanese architectural tradition credits a semi-legendary figure named Gunadharma as its master builder, but this name appears only in later tradition, not in any contemporary inscription. The closest epigraphic evidence linking Borobudur to royal patronage is the Karangtengah inscription of 824 CE, issued in the names of King Samaratungga and his daughter Pramodhawardhani, which records the inauguration of a sanctuary called Bhumisambhara and the donation of sima lands to support it — a sanctuary many scholars identify as Borobudur, though the identification is not definitive.16

The physical scale of Borobudur is almost incomprehensible. The base measures approximately 118 metres on each side; the monument rises 35 metres above the surrounding plain. Some 1.6 million blocks of grey andesite — volcanic stone quarried from nearby river beds — were cut, dressed, and fitted without mortar using a system of interlocking joints, adapting the ancient Austronesian tradition of megalithic terraced architecture to a new Buddhist cosmological programme.14 No crane or iron tool was available: the builders worked with wooden levers, earth ramps, and braided ropes, coordinating thousands of workers across decades under a logistical capacity that remains astonishing. The Tri Tepusan inscription of 842 CE, issued in Pramodhawardhani's name, donates the revenue of three surrounding villages as sima lands specifically for the maintenance of the Bhumisambhara sanctuary — confirming that the monument was actively managed as a revenue-supported institution within living memory of its completion.16

stupa — a hemispherical Buddhist reliquary mound, originally housing physical relics of the Buddha or a distinguished monk. By the medieval period in South and Southeast Asia, stupas had expanded into large-scale monumental forms combining reliquary, cosmological, and devotional functions. Borobudur's upper structure is a large sealed stupa; seventy-two smaller perforated stupas ring its circular terraces, each containing a seated Buddha statue.

The Monument in Three Dimensions: Cosmos, Relief, and Debate

Borobudur is architecturally structured to be traversed as a pilgrimage circuit while being simultaneously legible from above as a three-dimensional mandala — a sacred diagram of the Buddhist universe. Its nine platforms are conventionally divided by scholars into three cosmological zones. The lowest level, an encased square base, corresponds to Kamadhatu — the realm of desire and worldly attachment. Above it, five square terraces constitute Rupadhatu — the realm of form, where the senses are refined but the world is not yet transcended. Above those, three circular platforms and the sealed central stupa represent Arupadhatu — the formless realm, pure consciousness, and ultimately the undifferentiated emptiness of Nirvana. A pilgrim ascending from the base to the summit, circumambulating each level clockwise in the traditional Buddhist pradaksina, enacts the path from worldly attachment to enlightenment in stone and space.17

mandala — in Hindu and Buddhist cosmology and ritual, a geometrically arranged sacred diagram representing the structure of the universe, the divine pantheon, or the path of spiritual attainment. Architectural mandalas — temple complexes whose spatial organisation replicates the mandala — were common in both Hindu and Buddhist Southeast Asia. Borobudur's concentric square-and-circle plan, read as a three-dimensional diagram of the cosmos, is the most celebrated example in the region.

This elegant tripartite reading must, however, be qualified. The kamadhatu/rupadhatu/arupadhatu framework was proposed by the Dutch art historian W.F. Stutterheim in the early twentieth century on the basis of what a recent peer-reviewed article in the SPAFA Journal characterises as "very fragmentary data," and its grounding in Buddhist scriptural categories has been disputed by subsequent scholars.17 Other researchers have questioned whether Borobudur was conceived as a Mahayana mandala at all, or whether it is better understood as a Theravada commemorative stupa, a Tantric initiatory structure, or a royal funerary monument for a deceased Sailendra king. The absence of any dedicatory text means these debates cannot be settled by documentary evidence. What is beyond question is that the monument was designed to be read in a specific sequence, and that its visual programme was extraordinarily ambitious in both scope and intellectual content.

The monument contains approximately 2,672 individual relief panels covering around 2,520 square metres of carved andesite surface — a quantity of narrative stone carving without parallel anywhere in the world.14 The panels are arranged in five thematic series. The lowest and most philosophically demanding — the 160 panels of the Karmavibhanga (Actions and Their Results) — illustrate Buddhist teaching on karma: the causal chain linking virtuous or sinful conduct to its inevitable consequences across rebirths. These panels are carved on the monument's lowest terrace and are now almost entirely hidden. The entire base was encased in a stone "foot" either during original construction or shortly after, for reasons still debated: structural reinforcement to prevent the base from splaying outward under the monument's enormous weight (the conventional view) or a deliberate ritual concealment, preventing pilgrims from dwelling on scenes of worldly action before they had begun the upward journey (an alternative reading).18 The encasement was discovered in 1885 by the Dutch engineer Jan Willem IJzerman; a section was photographed and then re-enclosed; only the southeast corner remains visible today.

Above the hidden base, the five terraces of the Rupadhatu carry the monument's great narrative sequences. The first gallery's inner wall depicts the Lalitavistara — the life of the historical Buddha Shakyamuni from his miraculous birth to his first sermon — in 120 elegantly composed panels. The outer walls of the same gallery and the succeeding higher galleries carry the Jataka tales (stories of the Buddha's previous lives as bodhisattva) and the Avadana (exemplary tales of bodhisattvas and saints), totalling some 620 panels. The uppermost gallery levels, spanning the top of Rupadhatu, depict the Gandavyuha — the final chapter of the great Flower Garland Sutra (Avatamsaka Sutra), in which the young pilgrim Sudhana travels through fifty-three spiritual teachers on his quest for perfect enlightenment. At 460 panels, the Borobudur Gandavyuha is by far the most extensive visual narration of this text in existence; the scene in which Sudhana encounters Maitreya, the Buddha-to-come, and enters his miraculous tower-palace is rendered with particular sculptural magnificence.14

On the three circular terraces of the Arupadhatu zone, the enclosing gallery walls disappear. The pilgrim emerges from the confined, relief-lined passageways into open sky, surrounded by seventy-two latticed stone stupas arranged in three concentric rings. Each contains a seated Buddha in the dharmachakra mudra (the gesture of teaching), visible through the diamond-shaped perforations of the casing — present but not fully revealed, a materialisation of the idea that the formless realm is accessible but not directly seen. The sealed central stupa at the summit is empty or, rather, it contains emptiness: it is the architectural embodiment of Nirvana, the state that transcends all concepts including presence and absence.17 Whether Borobudur was used for regular public pilgrimage or was a restricted site for royal ritual remains unknown; no ancient Javanese text describing a pilgrimage to the monument has survived.

Prambanan: A Shaiva Counterpoint in Stone

Roughly nineteen kilometres east of Borobudur, across the same fertile plain, stands the Prambanan temple compound — Candi Prambanan in the formal register, Loro Jonggrang ("the Slender Maiden") in local Javanese tradition. Where Borobudur expresses the Buddhist aspiration to transcend the world by ascending through its layers, Prambanan celebrates the Hindu gods within it — their creative and destructive energy, their epic narratives, their demands for royal devotion. The two complexes, built within a generation of each other on the same plain, form the twin poles of Central Java's sacred geography and together constitute one of the densest concentrations of sacred architecture in world history.

Construction of the Prambanan core is attributed to Rakai Pikatan, the Sanjaya-line king who was also the husband of the Sailendra princess Pramodhawardhani — the same royal marriage that, as discussed above, sits at the heart of the debate about dynastic continuity. The key documentary evidence is the Shivagrha inscription of 856 CE, which records the inauguration of the Shiva temple under King Lokapala (Pikatan's successor) on the specific calendar date of 12 November 856 CE — a precision rare in the epigraphic record of this period.19 The inscription also contains an allusion to a military victory, understood by most scholars as a reference to Pikatan's earlier defeat of Balaputradewa, the Sailendra claimant to the Javanese throne. On this reading, Prambanan was conceived in part as a monument to Shaivite triumph — the victorious dynasty raising a temple to Shiva as a declaration that Hindu sovereignty had been restored.

The scale of Prambanan is extraordinary, though its layout is entirely different from Borobudur's concentric rings. The original complex comprised 240 individual structures within a walled enclosure of roughly 390 metres per side.20 At the centre rose — and still largely stand — three tall tower-shrines dedicated to the Hindu Trimurti: Brahma the creator, Vishnu the preserver, and Shiva the destroyer. The central Shiva shrine reaches approximately 47 metres, making it the tallest pre-modern temple structure in Indonesia. Facing each Trimurti shrine is a smaller wahana temple housing the divine vehicle of that deity: Nandi the bull for Shiva, Garuda the eagle for Vishnu, Hamsa the swan-goose for Brahma. The six principal shrines are surrounded by concentric rows of smaller pervara (attendant) temples — originally 224 of them, in four rows — of which most have collapsed under the combined assault of centuries of earthquakes and abandonment. As of the 2020s, fewer than ten pervara temples have been fully reconstructed, and the restoration at current pace is projected to take centuries.

The sculptural programme of Prambanan is dominated by the Hindu epics. The inner ambulatory balustrade of the Shiva and Brahma temples carries a continuous relief narrative of the Ramayana — Prince Rama's quest to rescue his wife Sita from the demon king Ravana of Lanka, aided by the monkey general Hanuman and his armies. The Prambanan Ramayana reliefs rank among the finest narrative stone sculpture in Southeast Asia, rendered in an elegant Javanese idiom that transforms the Indian epic into something distinctly local in its figures, gestures, and emotional texture.21 The Vishnu temple's balustrade carries scenes from the Krishnayana, drawn from the Bhagavata Purana. Together, the sculptural cycles of Prambanan offered a comprehensive visual theology of the Trimurti for the elite devotional community of the ninth-century Mataram court.

Trimurti — the Hindu theological concept grouping three principal deities as complementary aspects of a single divine reality: Brahma (the creator of the universe), Vishnu (the preserver and sustainer), and Shiva (the destroyer and transformer, who clears the way for new creation). In Prambanan's architecture each member of the Trimurti receives a dedicated central shrine, with Shiva's being the largest and most elaborately decorated — reflecting the Shaivite orientation of the Sanjaya-line patrons.

UNESCO inscribed Prambanan as a World Heritage Site in 1991 under criteria (i) and (iv), calling it "a masterpiece of human creative genius" and an "outstanding example of a type of architectural ensemble representative of a significant stage of human history," specifically the consolidation of Hindu artistic traditions in Southeast Asia. The agency describes it as "the largest temple compound dedicated to Shiva in Indonesia" and the second-largest Hindu temple complex in Southeast Asia after Angkor Wat.22 Like Borobudur, Prambanan was abandoned within roughly a century of its completion, sustained major earthquake damage (probably in the sixteenth century), and was buried under rubble and vegetation until European documentation began in the eighteenth century. Systematic restoration using the anastylosis method (reconstructing original material without addition of new stone) began in 1930; the central Shiva temple was completed in 1953 and inaugurated by President Sukarno, who saw its restoration as an emblem of national civilisational pride in the postcolonial moment.

Ricefields, Sima Lands, and Stone: The Economy of the Golden Age

How did a pre-industrial agrarian kingdom sustain monument-building programmes of this magnitude? The short answer is: rice, volcanic soil, and institutional innovation. Java's repeatedly replenished volcanic earth produced wet-rice surpluses large enough to support not only farmers, warriors, and traders but an extensive priestly and monastic class, skilled craftsmen and sculptors, and the mobilised labour — whether tributary, corvée, or freely rendered as religious meritmaking — that quarried and carved the great temples.

The key institutional mechanism linking this agricultural surplus to sacred architecture was the sima grant. A sima was a land grant — a village, a tax district, or a paddy-field area — whose revenues were formally "freed" from the general royal treasury and redirected permanently to the maintenance of a specified temple or monastic community. The Karangtengah inscription (824 CE), the Tri Tepusan inscription (842 CE), and the Mantyasih inscription (907 CE) all record sima donations to specific sanctuaries, creating enduring revenue flows that gave the temples economic independence from the fluctuations of royal favour.16 The repeated appearance of sima grants across both Buddhist (Sailendra) and Hindu (Sanjaya) inscriptions is one of the clearest signs that the two dynasties operated within a shared institutional framework, whatever their theological differences.

sima — in the inscriptional record of ancient Java, a tax-exempt land grant whose revenues are assigned permanently to a specified religious foundation (temple, monastery, or shrine) rather than to the royal treasury. Sima grants are among the most frequently attested documents of the Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms, providing evidence for both the religious economy of the period and the formal relations between crown and sacred institution.

Some historians have applied Clifford Geertz's concept of the "theatre state" — developed in his 1980 study of nineteenth-century Bali, Negara — to the ninth-century Javanese polities. Geertz argued that in the Balinese polity he studied, mass ritual display was the primary activity of the state rather than an instrument for other ends; that temples, processions, and royal ceremonies were the state's essential text, with material power being a by-product of ceremonial precedence.23 Applied to Mataram, the argument would cast Borobudur and Prambanan not as curiosities on the margins of a "real" political history but as the central text of Sailendra and Sanjaya governance. This is an attractive and influential interpretive frame, but it should be treated with caution: Geertz's nineteenth-century Bali was a very different polity from ninth-century Java, and the direct application of his model involves an analogical leap that not all historians accept.

What the inscriptions reveal more concretely is an intensive agricultural heartland tied to a dense network of temples, courts, and foundations by flows of rice, tribute, and skilled labour. The Canggal inscription's description of Java as "rich in grain and gold mines" was a political assertion, but it reflected a real material base. Gold ornaments, fine cloth, wax, and forest products supplemented the rice surplus and funded the import of Indian religious texts, skilled craftsmen, and cosmopolitan luxury goods that characterise the material culture of the Sailendra and Sanjaya courts as we know it from archaeological discovery.

The epigraphy of the golden age deserves emphasis as a body of evidence in its own right. Written primarily in Sanskrit or in Old Javanese (Kawi), incised on stone, copper-plate, and andesite, these inscriptions constitute virtually the entire direct written record of the period. The Canggal inscription (732 CE) provides the foundational narrative of Sanjaya's kingdom. The Kalasan inscription (778 CE) gives the first Sailendra dynastic reference. The Kelurak inscription (782 CE) introduces Tantric syncretic imagery. The Karangtengah inscription (824 CE) links Borobudur to Samaratungga and Pramodhawardhani. The Tri Tepusan inscription (842 CE) confirms the monument's endowed status. The Shivagrha inscription (856 CE) precisely dates Prambanan's inauguration. The Mantyasih inscription (907 CE) provides the nine-king genealogy of Mataram, and the Wanua Tengah III inscription (908 CE) supplements it with regnal dates. One inscription stands geographically apart: the Ligor inscription, a two-sided stele found at Nakhon Si Thammarat in southern Thailand, whose Side B (c. 775 CE) names a Sailendra ruler and references religious construction — evidence that Sailendra cultural and political reach extended into the Malay Peninsula and that the "Central Javanese" golden age was connected to a wider Buddhist network of the western archipelago.24

The Great Riddle: Why Central Java Fell Silent

Around 929 CE, King Mpu Sindok moved the Mataram court from the Kedu Plain to the Brantas valley in East Java, establishing his capital at Watugaluh near present-day Jombang and founding what historians call the Isyana dynasty.25 The great monuments were left behind. Temple construction on the scale of the golden age never resumed. Within two or three generations, the Kedu Plain — the heartland of the most productive civilisation in archipelagic Southeast Asia — had been largely depopulated and the monuments were beginning their long sleep under the encroachment of volcanic debris and jungle. By the time the Majapahit court poet Mpu Prapanca mentioned Borobudur in his 1365 CE poem Nagarakretagama, the monument was evidently a ruin visited by curiosity rather than an active centre of Buddhist practice. The reasons for this transformation — genuinely one of the great unresolved questions of Indonesian history — have generated five competing scholarly explanations.

Volcanic catastrophe. The Dutch geologist R.W. van Bemmelen and the Indonesian epigraphist Boechari argued that a catastrophic eruption of Mount Merapi during the reign of Wawa (c. 924–929 CE) devastated the agricultural heartland of the kingdom, burying settlements and ricefields under volcanic deposits and making sustained habitation untenable. The Javanese tradition calls this event Pralaya Mataram — the annihilation of Mataram. The physical evidence is striking: the Sambisari temple, near Yogyakarta, was discovered in 1966 buried under approximately five metres of volcanic debris, and the compound had to be excavated from that depth before it could be seen.27 A catastrophic eruption could simultaneously destroy the agrarian base, traumatise the population, desacralise the existing ceremonial landscape, and impel the court to seek new sacred geography — the volcanic mountain Penanggungan in East Java was already associated with royal sanctity — making the shift both practical and cosmologically motivated.26

Military pressure from Srivijaya. The Wikipedia article on Mpu Sindok identifies both volcanic eruption and "invasion from Srivijaya" as probable contributing causes of the move.25 After Balaputradewa's defeat in Java, the Sailendra rulers of Srivijaya had every reason to contest Sanjaya supremacy. Srivijaya's naval power extended across the Strait of Malacca and into the Java Sea, and a sustained series of raids or a naval blockade of Java's north-coast trade ports could have destabilised the kingdom economically and militarily. The fact that Srivijaya later (in 1016 CE) incited its Javanese vassal Wurawari to destroy the Mataram capital in East Java demonstrates that this rivalry had enduring and deadly weight.26

Epidemic or demographic crisis. The Dutch historian N.J. Krom proposed that epidemic disease may have contributed to the abandonment of the densely settled Kedu Plain, particularly if a volcanic event had already weakened population resilience. A dense agricultural population, reliant on shared irrigation infrastructure, would have been acutely vulnerable to a post-eruption disease environment. This theory is difficult to confirm archaeologically, but demographic collapses linked to epidemic disease and environmental stress are well documented in other pre-modern societies facing volcanic disruption.26

Economic exhaustion from over-building. The economic historian B. Schrieke argued that the sustained demands of monument construction — the mobilisation of labour, the diversion of agricultural surplus to building projects, the accumulation of tax-exempt sima land by religious foundations, which removed revenue from the royal treasury — gradually undermined the agrarian base of the kingdom, leaving it fragile and unable to absorb external shocks. On this reading, the extraordinary productivity of the golden age contained the seeds of its own dissolution: the effort of building Borobudur and Prambanan was itself part of what made the civilisation that built them unsustainable.26

Strategic advantages of East Java. The Dutch epigrapher J.G. de Casparis offered a more positive framing: the move to East Java should be understood not primarily as flight from disaster but as a strategic choice. The Brantas River valley provided excellent access to Java's north-coast ports, through which much of the archipelago's long-distance trade in spices, textiles, and forest products flowed. As commercial networks intensified in the ninth and tenth centuries — particularly as Maluku spice trades passed through Java en route to China and India — East Java offered connectivity that the inland Kedu Plain could not match. The move east was, on this reading, an adaptation to a changing economic geography as much as a retreat from catastrophe.26

No scholarly consensus assigns primacy to a single cause. The prevailing tendency in recent scholarship is toward a multi-causal model: a volcanic event (or series of events) in the 920s destabilised the Kedu heartland; concurrent Srivijayan military pressure threatened the kingdom's western sea-lanes; the economic and demographic cumulative costs of a century of monument-building had already weakened the plain's resilience; and Mpu Sindok — who appears to have been governing territories closer to East Java before his formal succession — consolidated a gradual eastward migration of population and royal authority into a definitive relocation of the capital.28 The Sangguran (Minto) Stone, dated 928 CE and found near Malang in East Java, shows that King Wawa was already issuing land charters in East Java before Sindok's accession — suggesting the shift was well underway before it became official.28

What is certain is the long-term cultural continuity carried eastward. Sindok brought the Shaivite Hindu literary and ritual tradition, the Sanskrit-language inscriptional culture, and the artistic idiom of the Kedu Plain into his new kingdom on the Brantas. His successors maintained the intellectual inheritance of the golden age: the Kakawin Ramayana — a magisterial Old Javanese poetic rendering of the Indian epic — was composed in this period, and the Sanghyang Kamahayanikan, a Buddhist philosophical text, was committed to writing at the same court. Through Sindok's daughter and her successors, this legacy passed to Airlangga (r. c. 1019–1049), the great reconciling king of East Java, and through Airlangga's lineage to the Kediri and ultimately to the Majapahit empire — the civilisation that would create the Nagarakretagama, the Pararaton, and the wayang repertoire. The abandonment of Borobudur and Prambanan was not the death of Central Javanese civilisation. It was its transformation.

Colonial rediscovery and modern restoration. Borobudur was documented by British Governor Stamford Raffles in 1814 and systematically excavated by 1835; the first photographs were taken in 1872. A Dutch restoration (1907–11) rebuilt the upper terraces, and a major UNESCO-coordinated campaign (1973–1982) disassembled and relaid more than one million stones at a cost exceeding US$6.9 million. Prambanan was first formally documented by a VOC employee in 1733, surveyed under the British in 1811, and restored from 1930 onwards using anastylosis; the central Shiva shrine was completed in 1953. Both monuments were inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1991. Borobudur was confirmed by Guinness World Records in 2012 as the world's largest Buddhist temple.

Summary

Between roughly 780 and 930 CE, the volcanic Kedu Plain of Central Java supported one of the most prodigious monument-building civilisations in world history. Two dynastic forces — the Mahayana Buddhist Sailendra and the Shaivite Sanjaya line — raised Borobudur (the world's largest Buddhist monument: a nine-tiered cosmological mandala carved with over 2,670 relief panels) and Prambanan (a 240-temple compound dedicated to the Hindu Trimurti, inaugurated precisely on 12 November 856 CE). Whether these dynasties were rivals or branches of a single ruling family remains an open scholarly question. The agrarian economy of the plain, organised through sima land grants and royal patronage, funded the building programmes; a dense body of Sanskrit and Old Javanese inscriptions supplies the documentary framework. Around 929 CE, Mpu Sindok moved the court to East Java under pressures — volcanic, military, economic, and strategic — that scholars continue to debate. The cultural inheritance of the golden age travelled east with the court, laying foundations for the literary and artistic achievements of the Kediri, Singhasari, and Majapahit eras.

Review & Discussion Questions

7.1 What does the absence of a dedicatory inscription for Borobudur mean for our ability to interpret the monument's purpose? How should historians work with a great monument that has no contemporary written explanation?
7.2 Compare the two rival interpretations of the Sailendra–Sanjaya relationship (distinct competing dynasties versus branches of a single family). What evidence supports each reading, and what is at stake in choosing between them?
7.3 Stutterheim's kamadhatu/rupadhatu/arupadhatu framework for reading Borobudur has been questioned by recent scholarship. Does the contested nature of scholarly interpretations diminish the monument's significance, or is interpretive openness a feature of great art?
7.4 Balitung, a Shaivite Hindu king, issued a decree protecting Buddhist monasteries from taxation. What does this suggest about the nature of religious statecraft in ninth-century Java? How does it complicate the idea of Hindu-Buddhist rivalry?
7.5 Evaluate the five scholarly theories for the 10th-century shift from Central to East Java. Is a multi-causal model necessarily more convincing than a single-cause explanation, or does it risk avoiding the hard question of which factor mattered most?
7.6 The Canggal inscription calls Java "rich in grain and gold mines." How does this economic claim relate to the religious architecture that was built in the same period? What is the relationship between agrarian surplus and sacred monument?
7.7 Prambanan was restored and inaugurated by President Sukarno in 1953. What political and symbolic work was the Indonesian state performing by restoring a ninth-century Hindu monument in the years immediately after independence?
7.8 The Ligor inscription links the Sailendra to the Malay Peninsula; the Nalanda inscription links them to Bihar in India. What does this tell us about the geographic horizons of power and piety in 8th–9th century Central Java?
