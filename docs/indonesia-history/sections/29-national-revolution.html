Learning Objectives

  • Describe the founding institutions of the Republic — the 1945 Constitution, Pancasila, and the initial government — and explain the Jakarta Charter controversy.
  • Analyse the social revolution of 1945–46 and the role of pemuda (youth) militancy in shaping the revolution's character, drawing on regional case studies.
  • Assess the military and diplomatic dimensions of the revolution: the Battle of Surabaya, the Linggadjati and Renville agreements, and the two Dutch "Police Actions."
  • Evaluate the Madiun Affair of 1948 as both a military turning point and a historiographical problem, engaging with scholarly debates about its causes and long-term consequences.
  • Trace the path to sovereignty through guerrilla war, international pressure, the Round Table Conference, and the transfer of sovereignty on 27 December 1949.
  • Engage with the principal scholarly debates about the revolution's character, social aims, and unresolved legacies.

At ten o'clock on the morning of 17 August 1945, in the courtyard of a private residence at 56 Jalan Pegangsaan Timur in Jakarta, a slender man in a white dress uniform stepped forward and read forty words that had been drafted through the previous night. "We the people of Indonesia do hereby declare the independence of Indonesia," Sukarno — whose name Dutch colonial orthography rendered Soekarno, though the post-independence form is used throughout this chapter — read aloud, flanked by his vice-presidential partner Mohammad Hatta. "Matters concerning the transfer of power and other matters will be executed in an orderly manner and in the shortest possible time. In the name of the Indonesian people. Djakarta, 17–8–'05."1 No Allied official was present, no Dutch administrator had been informed, and Japan — whose atomic bombing two days before Emperor Hirohito's surrender broadcast had unmade the imperial order across Southeast Asia — still patrolled the city. The moment was quiet, almost anti-climactic. Yet it ignited a revolution, simultaneously national, social, and diplomatic, that would last four years, draw the United Nations into its first major decolonisation crisis, and compel the Netherlands to surrender sovereignty over what had been, for three centuries, the richest colonial possession in Asia.

The Rengasdengklok Affair and the Proclamation

The proclamation did not arise from calm deliberation. It was wrested from contingency, generational conflict, and the implosion of Japanese imperial authority across Southeast Asia. Japan's capitulation on 15 August 1945 created an immediate power vacuum across the Netherlands East Indies. The Allied Supreme Commander for Southeast Asia, Lord Mountbatten, was weeks away from landing forces; the Netherlands, still rebuilding after German occupation, had begun deploying the Netherlands Indies Civil Administration (NICA) but it remained at sea. For the Indonesian nationalist leadership, the moment was both opportunity and trap: act now, and claim independence before Dutch authority could be restored; hesitate, and risk being absorbed into whatever post-surrender arrangements the Allies imposed.

It was the pemuda — the youth generation formed under Japanese wartime mobilisation — who forced the decision. On the evening of 15–16 August, a group of young activists including Wikana, Chaerul Saleh, and Sukarni gathered at Sukarno's house and pressed for an immediate, unilateral proclamation of independence, emphatically without the blessing of the PPKI (Panitia Persiapan Kemerdekaan Indonesia, the Preparatory Committee for Indonesian Independence) through which the nationalists had hitherto worked under Japanese auspices. Sukarno and Hatta refused. They argued that the PPKI possessed legitimate authority, that a solo declaration might provoke Japanese suppression, and that the proper path was collective, considered action.2 The pemuda, as Benedict Anderson's foundational study of the period documents, frequently combined genuine revolutionary courage with a strategic impatience that the older nationalists found reckless.3

Before dawn on 16 August, a group of pemuda activists acted unilaterally. Driving to Sukarno's house, they effectively abducted both Sukarno and Hatta and transported them some seventy kilometres southeast of Jakarta to the small town of Rengasdengklok, where a unit of the Peta (Pembela Tanah Air, Defenders of the Homeland — the Japanese-sponsored Indonesian volunteer militia) was under sympathetic command.4 The intent was to isolate the two leaders from Japanese influence and to extract a commitment to an independent proclamation. Sukarno found the episode exasperating. Negotiations through the day proved inconclusive until Ahmad Subardjo, a senior nationalist and former Japanese naval liaison officer, drove to Rengasdengklok and pledged on his personal honour that the proclamation would be made the following morning. The two leaders were returned to Jakarta that evening.

In the small hours of 17 August, Sukarno, Hatta, and a circle of nationalist activists gathered at the Jakarta home of Rear Admiral Maeda Tadashi, a Japanese naval attaché known to be quietly sympathetic to Indonesian independence. There, Sukarno drafted the proclamation text. He consciously set aside the more confrontational language some pemuda had proposed — references to Japanese arms transfers and the seizure of power — in favour of spare, ambiguous prose that would not provoke the Japanese military command, which retained armed force in the city, into suppression.5 The text was typed on a Latin-alphabet typewriter — Japanese models used syllabic script — apparently borrowed through a German liaison officer. Sukarno and Hatta signed it "in the name of the Indonesian people," a formula that asserted popular authority rather than colonial transfer.6

The reading at 10 AM on 17 August was deliberately modest in scale. The original plan to hold the ceremony at the Ikada field (now Merdeka Square) was abandoned after Japanese protests about mass assembly. Perhaps three hundred people gathered outside Sukarno's house — nationalist committee members, pemuda activists, journalists, a handful of Japanese officers watching from a respectful distance. A student named Latief Hendraningrat raised a red-and-white flag, Sang Saka Merah Putih, sewn by Sukarno's wife Fatmawati the previous night. The ceremony lasted roughly twenty minutes. News spread by telephone, by runner, and by shortwave radio; within hours, the proclamation had reached cities across Java and, more slowly, Sumatra and the outer islands.7 Whether the independence proclaimed that morning could be defended was a question that would not be answered for more than four years.

Pemuda — Indonesian youth activists who came of age under the Japanese occupation (1942–45) and played a central, often forceful role in the independence proclamation, the social revolution, and the early fighting of the National Revolution. The term (literally "youth") encompasses a heterogeneous generation shaped by Japanese military training, nationalist education, and wartime mobilisation.

The 1945 Constitution and the Founding of the Republic

The institutional scaffolding of the new Republic was erected with remarkable speed and equally remarkable improvisation. On 18 August 1945, the day after the proclamation, the PPKI convened an emergency session in Jakarta. Established under Japanese auspices to oversee a planned independence ceremony, it was now repurposed as the founding body of the Indonesian state — a transformation that transferred Japanese colonial authority, however vestigially, into Republican legitimacy.8

The session adopted the Constitution (Undang-Undang Dasar) that had been drafted during June and July 1945 by a preparatory investigation committee. The 1945 Constitution — universally abbreviated UUD 1945 — was brief by any comparison: thirty-seven articles, a preamble, and transitional provisions. It vested executive authority in a strong presidency subject to the broad oversight of a People's Consultative Assembly (Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat, MPR). Its framers, aware they were writing in urgency, designed it as a transitional instrument. In practice it would prove remarkably durable: with interruptions during the federal period (1949–50), UUD 1945 remained the basic law of Indonesia until its fundamental amendment between 1999 and 2002.9

The preamble's most lasting contribution was the Pancasila — five principles Sukarno had first articulated in a speech on 1 June 1945 and that the drafting committee refined over subsequent weeks: belief in one God (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa); just and civilised humanity (Kemanusiaan yang adil dan beradab); the unity of Indonesia (Persatuan Indonesia); democracy guided by the wisdom of deliberation and representation (Kerakyatan yang dipimpin oleh hikmat kebijaksanaan dalam permusyawaratan/perwakilan); and social justice for all the Indonesian people (Keadilan sosial bagi seluruh rakyat Indonesia).10 The Pancasila was a masterwork of nationalist ambiguity — broad enough to encompass Muslim, Christian, secular, and socialist Indonesians, it deferred rather than resolved the fundamental question of whether the Republic would be a secular or Islamic state.

That question had very nearly split the nationalist movement in the final days before the proclamation. An earlier draft of the preamble — known to historians as the "Jakarta Charter" (Piagam Jakarta) — included the clause "with the obligation for adherents of Islam to observe Islamic law" (dengan kewajiban menjalankan syariat Islam bagi pemeluk-pemeluknya). On the morning of 18 August, Mohammad Hatta received representations from Christian leaders from eastern Indonesia who threatened that their regions would not join a Republic that imposed Islamic obligations. The clause was removed before the constitution was adopted. The resulting text made Indonesia formally secular in its political arrangements while affirming the centrality of religion.11 Sutan Sjahrir, who would soon become the Republic's first Prime Minister, had argued in Our Struggle (Perdjoeangan Kita, his 1945 political manifesto) that Indonesia's diversity made a secular, democratic orientation the only viable foundation for a lasting national unity.12 The debate the Jakarta Charter foreclosed would resurface in every subsequent constitutional moment of Indonesian history.

The PPKI confirmed Sukarno as President and Hatta as Vice-President by acclamation, and established the Komite Nasional Indonesia Pusat (KNIP, Central Indonesian National Committee) as a provisional legislature pending general elections. It was a thin institutional structure for a state that claimed the full territory of the former Netherlands East Indies — seventy million people spread across three thousand islands, many of which the Republican government had not yet reached and several of which would not acknowledge its authority for months. The Republic existed, in law. Whether it existed in fact was a question being answered, simultaneously and violently, across Java and Sumatra.

Pancasila — the five founding principles of the Indonesian state: belief in one God; just and civilised humanity; the unity of Indonesia; democracy guided by deliberation; and social justice for all. Formulated by Sukarno in June 1945 and enshrined in the preamble to the 1945 Constitution, the Pancasila functions as the state's philosophical foundation and a deliberately inclusive alternative to either secular liberalism or Islamic governance.

The Social Revolution and Pemuda Militancy

The Indonesian National Revolution was not a single event but a layered process in which the political struggle for independence intersected with — and was sometimes overtaken by — a social upheaval that challenged existing hierarchies of class, ethnicity, and hereditary privilege. Historians have debated the extent and character of this social revolution for decades. Anthony Reid, in his 1974 survey, argued that a genuine social revolution did occur, most visibly in mass uprisings against the traditional elites who had collaborated with Dutch and then Japanese rule.13 Others have emphasised regional variation and the contingent, disorganised quality of the upheaval. What is not in dispute is that the months between August 1945 and mid-1946 witnessed social violence — and social transformation — that the proclamation alone cannot explain.

The pemuda were the most visible force in this upheaval, but they were far from uniform. In the towns and cities of Java, they ranged from politically sophisticated students and former Peta soldiers to loosely organised neighbourhood militia, criminal networks, and former Japanese-trained auxiliary units. What they shared was a generational impatience with the older nationalist leadership and a conviction that independence had to be seized, not negotiated. By September and October 1945, pemuda groups across Java had begun seizing weapons from Japanese depots — rifles, machine guns, mortars — and organising into irregular military formations that operated largely outside the control of the nascent Republican army (Tentara Nasional Indonesia, TNI).14

In East Sumatra the social revolution took its most dramatic form. The region's plantation economy had been administered through a system of hereditary Malay and Batak sultans and rajas who had served as intermediaries for Dutch colonial rule. On 3 March 1946, armed pemuda militias and local committees launched coordinated attacks across approximately twenty-five native states, killing or driving out the hereditary rulers, burning palace records, and seizing aristocratic lands. The violence was brief but intense; eyewitness accounts collected by Mohammad Said and translated by Benedict Anderson document both the scope of the killings and the depth of accumulated resentment that made them possible.15 Anthony Reid's detailed study of northern Sumatra, drawing on Dutch and Indonesian archives and oral testimony, confirms that the upheaval effectively ended traditional feudal authority in the region; the sultanates never recovered.16

In Aceh, the social revolution took a different form, pitting the ulama (Islamic scholars) — who had resisted Dutch colonialism and whose prestige had been enhanced by the Japanese period — against the uleebalang (territorial nobles whose authority had rested on Dutch support). Between December 1945 and March 1946, fighting between the two groups left hundreds dead and definitively shifted power toward the religious leadership that would shape Acehnese politics for generations.55 In Central Java, Surakarta (Solo) experienced sustained social tensions between workers' committees, nationalist militias, and remnants of the Javanese aristocratic order associated with the Sunanate — tensions documented in detail by Soejatno's study of the period.17 In Bandung (West Java), John Smail's careful local history shows the development of both regular and irregular military organisations as "manifestations of a militant youth movement" that could not be easily subordinated to either civilian Republican authority or formal military command.18

In Jakarta itself, Robert Cribb's work on the city's people's militias reveals a dimension of the social revolution that went largely unacknowledged in nationalist historiography: the transformation of criminal and dockworker networks into revolutionary auxiliaries. These Jakarta laskar (irregular fighters) drew on existing kinship and patronage ties among urban poor communities, Batavian-Malay labour networks, and criminal gangs, giving the revolution a base in the urban slums that the educated nationalist leadership had never cultivated and would later — as Cribb documents — suppress.19

The edited volume Revolutionary Worlds (2023), bringing together Indonesian and Dutch historians, has expanded the geographic and social scope of this picture further, demonstrating that the revolution looked dramatically different depending on one's vantage — as an ethnic Chinese merchant in Java, an Ambonese soldier in the Dutch colonial army, a Eurasian family caught between administrations, or a Javanese woman in a village that changed hands multiple times.20 The "social revolution" was, in short, many revolutions simultaneously — some aimed at Dutch rule, some at traditional elites, some at non-Indonesian minorities, and some at each other. The Republican leadership found itself trying to channel, contain, and at times disavow this energy while simultaneously fighting for international recognition.

The Return of the Allies and the Battle of Surabaya

The first major military test of the revolution was not against the Dutch but against the British. Under the arrangements of the Allied Supreme Command for Southeast Asia (SEAC), British and British-Indian forces under Lord Mountbatten were responsible for taking the Japanese surrender in the Netherlands East Indies and for maintaining order pending the Dutch return. The first British-Indian units landed at Surabaya (East Java's largest city and main port) in late October 1945, with orders to disarm Japanese troops, liberate Allied prisoners and internees, and restore public order. What they found was a city already deeply immersed in revolution.

Surabaya had been one of the most thoroughly transformed cities of the revolution. Pemuda fighters had seized large quantities of Japanese arms — including artillery and armoured vehicles — and the city was defended by an estimated 120,000 to 140,000 armed Indonesians of varying organisation and discipline. William Frederick's meticulous study of the Surabaya revolution, drawing on new Indonesian and Dutch sources and interviews with survivors, establishes that the city's working-class and petty-bourgeois population had developed an intense, grass-roots nationalist commitment during the revolution's first weeks — a commitment that made Surabaya unusually resistant and the battle particularly ferocious.21

The initial British landings on 25–28 October 1945 triggered immediate clashes. British Brigadier A.W.S. Mallaby was killed on the evening of 30 October near the Jembatan Merah (Red Bridge), in circumstances that remain debated. J.G.A. Parrott's forensic investigation, published in the journal Indonesia in 1975, concluded on the basis of previously unexamined British military reports that the immediate trigger was a miscommunication between British Indian troops (6 Mahratta Regiment) and an Indonesian crowd, but that the fundamental responsibility for the breakdown lay in the British failure to understand the depth of revolutionary mobilisation in the city.22 General E.C. Mansergh, who replaced Mallaby, issued an ultimatum: all Indonesians were to surrender their arms by 10 AM on 10 November 1945.

When the deadline passed without compliance, the British assault began with naval bombardment, aircraft, and tank columns. The battle that followed lasted approximately three weeks and cost at minimum 6,300 Indonesian lives by conservative estimates — and perhaps as many as 16,000 — while British-Indian forces suffered approximately 295 killed and missing; some 200,000 civilians fled the city.23 The resistance was sustained above all by the radio broadcasts of Sutomo (known as Bung Tomo), who combined calls for Islamic duty with intense nationalist passion and who gave the battle's defenders a rallying point that resonated far beyond Surabaya. The date of the British assault, 10 November, is commemorated annually in Indonesia as Hari Pahlawan — Heroes' Day — a recognition that what happened in Surabaya was not merely a military defeat but the revolution's defining moment of self-sacrifice.24

The Battle of Surabaya had consequences that extended far beyond its immediate military outcome — which was, militarily, a Dutch and British success. It convinced the British that the Indonesian revolution was a serious mass movement that could not simply be administered away. It galvanised Indonesian opinion, creating a mythology of resistance that the Republican leadership would invoke throughout the subsequent diplomatic struggle. And, paradoxically, it alarmed the Dutch: if British forces could not pacify one city without weeks of fighting, what would a full-scale reconquest cost? The question would be answered — not satisfactorily — over the next four years.

NICA (Netherlands Indies Civil Administration) — the Dutch colonial administrative body, established in exile in Australia during the Japanese occupation, that followed Allied forces into Indonesia intending to restore Netherlands sovereignty. NICA's arrival alongside British troops sharpened the confrontation between Dutch reassertion of colonial authority and Republican claims to independence.

NICA, Diplomacy, and the Linggadjati Agreement

From the moment the first NICA officials landed in Java and Sumatra alongside British forces, the Indonesian revolution had two fronts simultaneously: military confrontation with the occupying and returning powers, and diplomatic struggle for international recognition. Sutan Sjahrir, who became the Republic's first Prime Minister in November 1945, understood that Indonesia could not win a purely military contest against a re-equipped Dutch army backed by a European power. His strategy was to internationalise the conflict: to make the Republic's cause one that the emerging postwar international order — and specifically the new United Nations — would be obliged to support.25

Sjahrir, as he had argued in his 1945 manifesto Perdjoeangan Kita (Our Struggle), believed that the Republic's best path lay in constitutional legality, international engagement, and the demonstration that Indonesian nationalists were responsible democrats, not chaotic revolutionaries.56 This strategy required managing the pemuda and the army's hardliners — who favoured total war — while negotiating with Dutch Lieutenant Governor-General Hubertus van Mook, who for his part was willing to recognise a limited Indonesian autonomy within a Dutch-led federal structure but not an independent sovereign Republic.

Negotiations through 1946 produced the Linggadjati Agreement, initialled at Linggadjati (near Cirebon, West Java) on 15 November 1946. In its initialled form, the agreement was genuinely significant: the Netherlands recognised the Republic's authority de facto over Java, Madura, and Sumatra; both parties agreed to cooperate in the formation of a federal United States of Indonesia (Republik Indonesia Serikat, RIS) by 1 January 1949; and the RIS and the Netherlands would together form a Netherlands-Indonesian Union under the Dutch Crown.26 David Wehl, who covered the negotiations as a journalist and published a near-contemporary account in 1948, observed that the agreement represented a genuine Dutch concession — recognition of the Republic's existence — but that the federal framework was designed to dilute Republican power by surrounding it with a ring of Dutch-sponsored states in Borneo, Sulawesi, and eastern Indonesia.27

The Dutch parliament, however, stripped the agreement of its most important concessions before formal ratification in March 1947. The version signed on 25 March 1947 reasserted Dutch sovereignty, made the planned RIS a Dutch-led construct, and contained provisions the Republican side regarded as a fundamental betrayal of what had been agreed. Within the Republic, the Linggadjati process had already been bitterly contentious: Sjahrir had been accused by his left-wing critics of negotiating away the revolution, and the ratified text confirmed their warnings.28 Ide Anak Agung Gde Agung, who participated in the negotiations as a Balinese aristocrat-politician and who would later serve as Prime Minister of the Dutch-sponsored State of East Indonesia, provides a perspective from within the federalist camp: the Dutch federal scheme, he documents, was undermined from the outset by the gap between its formal commitments and Dutch military preparations for reconquest.29

The Police Actions and the Renville Agreement

The first Dutch "Police Action" — called Operatie Product by the Dutch military, "Dutch Military Aggression I" (Agresi Militer Belanda I) by Indonesian historiography — began on 21 July 1947 and lasted until 4 August. It was, by any measure, a military success for the Netherlands. Dutch forces seized most of the Republic's export-producing territories: the sugar-growing areas of East and Central Java, the plantation and oil-producing regions of Sumatra. The Republic was pushed back onto the Javanese heartland. Strategically, the Dutch aimed to strangle the Republic economically while creating the conditions for a Dutch-led federal state through the regions they now controlled.30

The operation also triggered the most significant international response the Republic had yet received. India and Australia, both sitting on the UN Security Council and both newly independent or assertive postcolonial powers, demanded Security Council intervention. On 1 August 1947, the Security Council passed Resolution 27, calling for a ceasefire and peaceful settlement. A Committee of Good Offices (CGO) was established, composed of Australia (chosen by Indonesia, represented by Justice R.C. Kirby), the United States (chosen as neutral arbiter, represented by former University of North Carolina president Frank Porter Graham), and Belgium (chosen by the Netherlands, represented by former Prime Minister Paul van Zeeland).31 Alastair Taylor's authoritative study of the UN's involvement, conducted with access to the Security Council records and described by reviewers as the "fullest and least biased treatment" of the subject, documents how the CGO's composition reflected the alignment of interests rather than genuine neutrality: Belgium was sympathetic to Dutch federal aims, Australia to the Republic, and the United States suspended between its commitment to Dutch NATO membership and its anti-colonial principles.32

The CGO's first major product was the Renville Agreement, signed on the US Navy transport ship Renville on 17 January 1948. The Republic, exhausted and territorially diminished, was forced to accept far less than Linggadjati had promised: Republican armed forces were to withdraw behind the so-called "Van Mook line," leaving the Dutch in control of most of Sumatra and the most productive parts of Java. The Republic retained a rump of west and central Java, parts of Sumatra, and its capital at Yogyakarta — but had lost the economic base that might sustain prolonged resistance.33 The signing was politically catastrophic inside the Republic: Amir Sjarifuddin, the left-wing Prime Minister who had signed on behalf of the Republic, resigned within weeks, and in resigning revealed that he had been a secret communist — a disclosure that transformed Indonesian politics overnight.34

American diplomacy in this period was shaped by a deep structural tension that Robert McMahon's archival study of US policy reveals in full. The United States needed the Netherlands as a NATO ally in Europe and as a bulwark of Western stability; it had already committed to the Marshall Plan, through which the Netherlands was receiving substantial postwar reconstruction aid. Yet the US was simultaneously committed to an anti-colonial principle, anxious that Dutch military adventure was creating nationalist sentiment that Communists could exploit, and sensitive to the optics of backing a white colonial power against a nascent Asian democracy. US State Department documents from mid-1948 show American officials — including the Hague ambassador — receiving Dutch arguments that the Republic was a tool of Moscow, while privately doubting those arguments.35

The Madiun Affair and Its Long Shadow

In the autumn of 1948, the Indonesian National Revolution confronted its most dangerous internal crisis. The Renville Agreement had strengthened the Republican military (which had used the ceasefire to consolidate and rearm) but devastated the prestige of the civilian left. Amir Sjarifuddin, who had signed the agreement and then confessed to secret PKI (Partai Komunis Indonesia) membership, had joined a left-wing coalition — the Front Demokrasi Rakyat (People's Democratic Front, FDR) — that now positioned itself as an opposition force outside the ruling coalition. The PKI itself was undergoing a rapid transformation. In August 1948, Musso (Mohammad Mustofa), a veteran communist who had spent years in Moscow and had been schooled in the Cominform's post-1947 hard line, returned to Indonesia from Europe and assumed effective leadership of the PKI.36

The events of 18 September 1948 remain among the most contested in modern Indonesian history. On that day, PKI-affiliated forces in Madiun, a railway junction city in Central Java, clashed with Republican (Siliwangi Division) forces and proclaimed a "National Front" government — a challenge, at minimum, to Republican authority in the region. Whether this constituted a planned PKI coup attempt or an armed confrontation that escalated from a local incident — perhaps partly provoked by Republican forces — is disputed. Ann Swift's 1989 study of the uprising, based on archival and interview research, concluded that the PKI leadership had intended to seize power; the accumulated evidence of Musso's return and the PKI's hardened line made a deliberate insurrection plausible.37 Harry Poeze, in a 2009 article drawing on the Cominform context, argues that the Cold War dimension was more significant than earlier scholarship acknowledged — that Musso's mandate from Europe included directives to align the PKI with the Zhdanov doctrine's "two camps" world-view and to press aggressively against perceived accommodation with Western imperialism.38 Akiko Sugiyama's later study of the event's historiography shows how accounts of Madiun shifted dramatically across different political regimes: treated as a PKI coup under Sukarno, as the Communist betrayal of the Republic under Suharto's New Order, and then reopened cautiously after 1998.39

What is clear is what happened next. Sukarno delivered a radio address that he described as the most important of his life: "Choose between Sukarno-Hatta or Musso," he told the Indonesian people, framing the choice as between the legitimate Republic and a communist putsch.40 Republican military forces — principally the Siliwangi Division under Colonel A.H. Nasution, with Lieutenant Colonel Suharto commanding operations in part of the theatre — moved swiftly. The rebellion was suppressed within weeks. Musso was killed on 31 October 1948. Amir Sjarifuddin was arrested, tried in secret proceedings, and executed in December 1948 without formal sentence having been passed. Tens of thousands of PKI members and sympathisers were imprisoned.

The Madiun Affair cast a shadow over Indonesian politics that lasted decades. In the short term, it removed the left from any significant role in Republican governance and — crucially — demonstrated to the United States and to Western opinion that the Indonesian Republic was actively anti-communist, not a Soviet proxy. This shift in perception would prove decisive in the diplomatic endgame of 1949. In the longer term, the Madiun narrative — communist as internal traitor, enemy of the nation — was enshrined as official history under Suharto's New Order (1966–98) and was deployed again, with devastating consequences, in the 1965–66 anti-communist massacres. The "lesson of Madiun" was one of the most potent political inheritances of the National Revolution.

Guerrilla War and the 1 March General Offensive

The second Dutch Police Action, Operatie Kraai (Operation Crow), launched on 19 December 1948, shattered the diplomatic framework of the previous eighteen months. Dutch paratroopers captured Yogyakarta, the Republican capital, in a matter of hours. Sukarno and Hatta were arrested on 21 December; Sjahrir, who was not in the government at the time, was also taken. The Republican government was destroyed in a single blow — or so the Dutch believed.41

In fact, the Republic had prepared for exactly this contingency. An emergency Republican government (Pemerintah Darurat Republik Indonesia, PDRI) had been pre-arranged under Sjafruddin Prawiranegara and activated in Bukittinggi, Sumatra. The Republican armed forces dispersed into the countryside rather than defending urban centres, adopting the guerrilla strategy that General Sudirman had long advocated. And Sudirman himself, though so severely ill with tuberculosis that he had to be carried on a litter, refused to surrender. For seven months he led a guerrilla campaign through the mountains of Central and East Java, commanding Republican units from the jungle, coordinating through runners and shortwave radio.42

Sudirman's guerrilla resistance was simultaneously a military strategy and a symbolic act. George Kahin, whose fieldwork in Indonesia had begun in mid-1948, later testified to the extraordinary effect Sudirman's example had on Indonesian morale: the image of a dying general refusing to capitulate, refusing to be captured, embodied the revolution's refusal to accept defeat regardless of the military situation.43 The strategy — which Republican military doctrine called perang gerilya total (total guerrilla war) — made Dutch control of the cities militarily meaningless if the surrounding countryside remained contested.

The military climax of the guerrilla campaign was the General Offensive (Serangan Umum) of 1 March 1949. The operation was planned by Sultan Hamengkubuwono IX, the ruler of Yogyakarta who had sheltered the Republic in his court since 1946, and approved by Sudirman. In the pre-dawn hours of 1 March, Republican units converged on Yogyakarta from multiple directions. The field commander was Lieutenant Colonel Suharto — then a regional commander, later Indonesia's longest-serving president — who coordinated the multi-column assault on the Dutch garrison. By 6 AM, Republican forces had taken the city centre; they held it for approximately six hours before Dutch counter-attacks and the difficulty of holding urban positions with guerrilla forces forced a withdrawal.44

Six hours in a city the Dutch believed they controlled was, strategically, a spectacular success. It demolished the Dutch claim — which they had made in international forums — that the Republic no longer existed as a military force. It was broadcast over Republican radio and reached the UN Security Council within days. Harry Poeze's research on the parallel fate of Tan Malaka — the radical nationalist who had spent the revolution in opposition to both Sukarno's diplomacy and the PKI — underlines how narrow the margin of survival was in this period: after the fall of Yogyakarta, Tan Malaka fled into East Java, was captured by Republican forces (who viewed him as a destabilising rival), and was shot on 21 February 1949, eliminating the third major force of the Indonesian left.45

International Pressure, the Round Table Conference, and the Transfer of Sovereignty

The Dutch seizure of Yogyakarta and the arrest of the Republican leadership triggered the international response that the Dutch military planners had fatally miscalculated. On 28 January 1949, the UN Security Council passed Resolution 67, calling on the Netherlands to immediately discontinue all military operations, release the imprisoned Republican leaders, restore the Republican government in Yogyakarta, and begin negotiations for the transfer of sovereignty to the United States of Indonesia no later than 1 July 1950.46 The resolution was unprecedented in its specificity — a direct instruction to a colonial power — and reflected a convergence of American, Asian, and Australian diplomatic pressure that the Netherlands had not anticipated.

The decisive factor was American leverage. US Secretary of State Dean Rusk had telegraphed Philip Jessup at the UN on 23 December 1948, the day Dutch operations began, identifying the conflict as one that "brings into sharp conflict a number of important US national interests."47 The tension that Robert McMahon's research identifies — between protecting the Dutch as a NATO ally and preventing communist gains in Southeast Asia — had now resolved decisively in favour of the second: the Madiun Affair had demonstrated that the Republic was anti-communist, meaning Dutch military action was not protecting Western interests but creating the instability that Communism required.48 The US threatened to suspend Marshall Plan aid to the Netherlands — a threat, Taylor's archival study confirms, that carried more weight in The Hague than any UN resolution.49

Under this combination of international pressure and domestic financial constraint (the Netherlands could not afford the military campaign without Marshall Plan funds), the Dutch agreed to the Roem–van Roijen Agreement of 7 May 1949: Republican prisoners would be released, Republican forces would cease guerrilla operations, and negotiations for the transfer of sovereignty would proceed. Sukarno and Hatta returned to Yogyakarta in June 1949. Sudirman also returned; he was too ill to attend the formal ceremonies that followed and died of tuberculosis on 29 January 1950, six weeks after the transfer of sovereignty.

The Round Table Conference opened in The Hague on 23 August 1949 and concluded on 2 November. The delegations negotiated four major agreements: the transfer of sovereignty to a United States of Indonesia (a federal state comprising the Republic and the Dutch-created federal states); a Netherlands-Indonesian Union under the Dutch Crown (dissolved by Indonesia in 1954); the assumption by the RIS of the colonial debt of approximately 4.3 billion guilders; and — most controversially — the deferral of the status of West Irian (western New Guinea) for one year, during which time the parties were to negotiate its disposition.50 Ide Anak Agung, who led the delegation of the federal states, documents the enormous complexity of the debt and status negotiations and the ultimately unsatisfying character of the federal compromise: the Dutch-sponsored states were too weak to resist absorption by the Republic, and within eight months of the sovereignty transfer most had dissolved into a unitary Indonesian state.51

The transfer of sovereignty was enacted simultaneously in Amsterdam (where Queen Juliana signed the document transferring sovereignty on behalf of the Netherlands) and Jakarta (where a parallel ceremony transferred authority to the new state) on 27 December 1949. The United States of Indonesia was short-lived: by 17 August 1950 — the fifth anniversary of the independence proclamation — the federal structure had been dissolved and the unitary Republic of Indonesia proclaimed. The Netherlands-Indonesia Union, a relic of the Round Table compromise, was abolished unilaterally by Indonesia in 1954. The West Irian question, deferred rather than resolved at The Hague, remained a festering diplomatic crisis until 1962, when UN mediation and American pressure produced a Dutch transfer to UN administration and, ultimately, Indonesian control.52

What had the revolution achieved, and what had it left unresolved? The Republic of Indonesia existed as a sovereign state, internationally recognised, with its territorial integrity more or less intact (minus West Irian). The social revolution had dismantled certain forms of traditional authority — the East Sumatran sultanates, the Acehnese uleebalang — while leaving others intact or repositioned. The PKI, crushed at Madiun, survived as a political organisation and would by the late 1950s become the largest non-ruling communist party in the world, until a second crushing in 1965–66 destroyed it permanently. The military, which had managed the revolution's armed phase, had acquired a political role and an institutional self-consciousness that would shape Indonesian governance for the rest of the century. The colonial debt burden assumed at the Round Table constrained economic policy for years. And the figure who had read forty words in a Jakarta courtyard in August 1945 was now president of an independent nation — but one whose independence had been purchased at enormous cost and on terms that would require another generation of political struggle to consolidate.

Herbert Feith's authoritative study of Indonesian constitutional democracy between 1950 and 1957 opens with the observation that the revolution had produced a state capable of surviving, but not yet one capable of governing effectively the territory it had inherited — a gap between achievement and capacity that would shadow Indonesian politics through the Sukarno and Suharto eras alike.53 David van Reybrouck, whose oral-history account of the revolution synthesises hundreds of survivor testimonies, concludes with a similar ambivalence: the achievement of sovereignty was real and hard-won, but the violence, the social disruption, and the exclusions of the revolutionary period — the Eurasians who lost their homes, the Chinese who faced periodic pogrom, the Papuans whose future was bartered away — were not resolved by the flag-raising ceremonies of December 1949.54

Police Actions (politionele acties) — the Dutch euphemism for two major military offensives against the Indonesian Republic: Operatie Product (July–August 1947) and Operatie Kraai (December 1948–January 1949). Indonesian historiography terms them "Dutch Military Aggressions" (Agresi Militer Belanda). Both were eventually reversed under UN and US pressure, but both achieved significant Dutch military objectives in the short term.
Perang gerilya (guerrilla war) — the strategy adopted by General Sudirman and the Indonesian National Army after the Dutch seizure of Yogyakarta in December 1948. Rather than defending urban centres, Republican forces dispersed into the countryside and mountains, conducting harassment operations that denied the Dutch effective control of rural Java and demonstrated the Republic's continued military existence to the international community.

Summary

The Indonesian National Revolution of 1945–49 was one of the twentieth century's most consequential anti-colonial struggles. Triggered by Japan's sudden surrender, it began with Sukarno and Hatta's spare proclamation of 17 August 1945 and the rapid founding of a Republic backed by a new constitution enshrining the Pancasila principles. What followed was simultaneously a national independence struggle, a social revolution — dramatically visible in the dismantling of traditional elite power in East Sumatra and Aceh — and a prolonged military-diplomatic contest against Dutch attempts to restore colonial rule. The Battle of Surabaya (November 1945) established the revolution's capacity for mass armed resistance and became its enduring symbol. Two diplomatic agreements — Linggadjati (1947) and Renville (1948) — were overtaken by Dutch military force in the two Police Actions of 1947 and 1948. The Madiun Affair (1948), in which the PKI-affiliated Madiun uprising was crushed by Republican forces, proved pivotal in demonstrating the Republic's anti-communist credentials to the United States, whose Marshall Plan leverage over the Netherlands ultimately tipped the international balance toward sovereignty. Guerrilla resistance led by the gravely ill General Sudirman, and the symbolically crucial 1 March 1949 General Offensive on Yogyakarta, maintained the Republic's claim to exist as a fighting force. Under UN and US pressure, the Netherlands agreed to the Round Table Conference and transferred sovereignty on 27 December 1949. The revolution left unresolved the status of West Irian, the question of the colonial debt, and the social claims of groups whose aspirations the Republican leadership had mobilised but not fulfilled.

Review & Discussion Questions

28.1 Why did the pemuda activists kidnap Sukarno and Hatta to Rengasdengklok? What does the episode reveal about generational tensions within the nationalist movement and about the relationship between radical and moderate strands of Indonesian nationalism?
28.2 The Jakarta Charter's removal from the 1945 Constitution before ratification meant that Islamic law would not be obligatory for Indonesian Muslims. How significant was this decision for Indonesian political identity? What does it reveal about the framers' priorities?
28.3 Historians debate whether the upheavals of 1945–46 in East Sumatra and Aceh constituted a genuine "social revolution." What criteria would you use to evaluate that claim, and how does regional variation complicate any general assessment?
28.4 The Battle of Surabaya was a military defeat for the Indonesian side yet is celebrated as a national triumph. In what sense, and by what logic, can a military defeat constitute a political and symbolic victory?
28.5 The Madiun Affair of 1948 has been interpreted variously as a PKI coup attempt, as a conflict provoked by the Republican military, and as an expression of Cold War dynamics directed partly from Moscow. Which interpretation do you find most persuasive, and why does the answer matter for how we understand the revolution's internal politics?
28.6 How did the United States' structural position — as both a Dutch NATO ally and an anti-colonial power — shape its policy toward the Indonesian revolution? At what moment, and for what reasons, did US policy shift decisively toward supporting Indonesian sovereignty?
28.7 General Sudirman's refusal to surrender after the Dutch seizure of Yogyakarta, though he was gravely ill, is often treated as a heroic act. From a strategic standpoint, what did the guerrilla campaign achieve that conventional diplomacy could not?
28.8 The Round Table Conference produced Indonesian sovereignty but also an obligation to assume colonial debt, a federal structure quickly dissolved, and the deferral of West Irian. Was the settlement a genuine triumph or a compromise that locked in Dutch advantages? How do you weigh the gains against the costs?
28.9 The social revolution of 1945–46 raised demands — for land redistribution, for an end to hereditary privilege, for workers' rights — that the Republic of Indonesia never systematically addressed. What does this tell us about the relationship between a nationalist revolution and social transformation?
