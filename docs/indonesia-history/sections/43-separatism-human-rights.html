Learning Objectives

  • Explain the origins and current status of the West Papua separatist conflict, distinguishing the armed OPM/TPNPB from the political ULMWP, and assess the roles of resource extraction, transmigration, and media access restrictions in sustaining it.
  • Evaluate the legacy of impunity for mass atrocities committed under the New Order — including the 1965–66 killings, the Petrus extrajudicial executions, and the May 1998 violence — and explain why scholarly death-toll estimates vary widely.
  • Analyze the 1997–98 activist disappearances, identify the unit (Tim Mawar) and command chain involved, and assess the significance of Prabowo Subianto's election as president in 2024 for accountability prospects.
  • Reconstruct the murder of human rights defender Munir Said Thalib (2004) and explain why his case has become emblematic of state impunity in post-Suharto Indonesia.
  • Assess Indonesia's transitional justice record: trace the failure of the KKR law, the pattern of Human Rights Court acquittals, and the significance and limits of Jokowi's 2023 non-judicial acknowledgment.
  • Identify the specific legal instruments — the ITE Law, the 2019 KPK revision, and the 2022 Criminal Code (KUHP) — that scholars and human rights organizations link to democratic regression in the Reformasi era.

Indonesia's democratic transition after 1998 was genuine and consequential: four competitive presidential elections were held, civil society flourished, a free press emerged, and the military's formal political role was curtailed. Yet Reformasi left three deep problems largely unresolved. The first was territorial: in the western half of New Guinea, an armed insurgency that predated independence continued to simmer, suppressed by a security apparatus that operated with minimal outside scrutiny. The second was historical: the New Order's worst atrocities — a death toll in the hundreds of thousands across the 1965–66 killings, the 1980s death squads, and the violence of 1998 — were acknowledged only in fragments and never prosecuted. The third was structural: the legal and institutional architecture of democratic governance was repeatedly weakened by executive and legislative action, producing what scholars increasingly characterize not as a consolidated democracy but as a fragile hybrid regime. This chapter examines all three problems in turn, attending throughout to the scholarly debates they have generated and the voices — of survivors, human rights defenders, and researchers — that have refused to let them close.

West Papua: The Last Active Separatist Conflict

When the Aceh insurgency ended with the Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding in August 2005, West Papua remained the only territory in the Indonesian archipelago where armed conflict over sovereignty was actively ongoing. The roots of that conflict stretch back to the circumstances of incorporation: in 1962, under the New York Agreement brokered by the United States, the Netherlands transferred its half of New Guinea to the UN and then to Indonesian administration, over the strong objections of Papuan political leaders who had begun preparations for independence. The subsequent "Act of Free Choice" of July–August 1969 — which gave the region the name Western scholars would dispute as Orwellian — consisted of a small assembly of 1,025 hand-picked Papuans, rather than a universal plebiscite, voting unanimously for integration. The vote was never put to a genuine popular test and has been widely condemned, including by the United Nations General Assembly's Resolution 2504, which merely "took note" of the result without endorsing it.1

The Organisasi Papua Merdeka (OPM, Free Papua Movement) was formed in 1965, before the Act of Free Choice. Its armed wing, which by the 2010s operated under the name Tentara Pembebasan Nasional Papua Barat (TPNPB, West Papua National Liberation Army), has conducted sporadic guerrilla operations against Indonesian security forces for six decades. The violence is low-intensity but chronic: skirmishes in remote highland regencies — Nduga, Intan Jaya, Puncak Jaya — with civilian populations caught between the TPNPB and Indonesian military (TNI) and police operations. Alongside the armed movement, the United Liberation Movement for West Papua (ULMWP), founded in Vanuatu on 7 December 2014 as an umbrella for three political independence organizations, has pursued a diplomatic strategy, gaining observer status at the Melanesian Spearhead Group in 2015 and attracting support from Pacific Island governments. The ULMWP is led by Benny Wenda, who has lived in exile in the United Kingdom since 2003; it explicitly disavows violence and seeks a new, internationally supervised act of self-determination.2

The Indonesian government has consistently characterized the conflict as a matter of internal security, separatist criminality, and Communist-linked subversion — a framing that has remained remarkably stable from the New Order through the Reformasi era. Military operations designated successively under various code-names have resulted in documented atrocities that are difficult to quantify precisely because access to the region is so tightly controlled. Human Rights Watch documented hundreds of politically motivated arrests in the immediate post-2019 period: between October 2020 and September 2021 alone, an estimated 418 individuals were arrested on political grounds in Papua, of whom 245 were ultimately convicted; at any given time roughly 90 to 100 political activists remained imprisoned.3 A 2025 military campaign designated "Operation Habema," launched in the highlands following a TPNPB attack on miners in April 2025, prompted HRW to document the death of at least one confirmed child in subsequent military operations and to warn of broader civilian harm in the pattern of TNI airstrikes against suspected rebel positions.4

TPNPB (Tentara Pembebasan Nasional Papua Barat) — the West Papua National Liberation Army, the armed wing of the Free Papua Movement (OPM). Operating primarily in the central highlands, it is legally designated as a terrorist organization by Indonesia since 2021. It is legally and organizationally separate from the ULMWP, which is a purely political body.

Access Restrictions, Resources, and Demographic Transformation

A structural feature of the Papua conflict that distinguishes it sharply from past Indonesian insurgencies is the near-total exclusion of independent observers. For decades, international journalists wishing to report from Papua have been required to apply through an interagency "clearing house" supervised by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and involving eighteen working units drawn from twelve ministries, plus the National Police and the State Intelligence Agency. In practice, applications are frequently denied outright or left unprocessed in bureaucratic limbo. A May 2015 announcement by President Jokowi that accredited foreign media would have "unimpeded access" was never backed by any change to the clearing house system. All six Papuan provinces established under the 2022 administrative reorganization remain on Indonesia's restricted-travel list for foreign nationals as of the time of writing.5 The practical consequence is that the human rights record in Papua rests primarily on the work of Indonesian civil society organizations — Kontras, Yayasan HAM Papua, the Catholic Church — and of Papuan activists who relay information at significant personal risk, not on independent on-site verification.

Two structural features of the conflict — resource extraction and demographic displacement — recur in every serious analysis. The Grasberg mine in Mimika Regency, operated by Freeport-McMoRan under successive contracts of work with the Indonesian state, is the world's largest gold mine and one of its largest copper mines. The wealth generated is staggering relative to local living conditions: in Mimika Regency, an estimated 91 percent of gross domestic product derives from Freeport's operations, yet Papua and West Papua provinces consistently rank at the bottom of every Indonesian human development measure. Papua's maternal mortality rate is 565 deaths per 100,000 live births against Jakarta's 48; youth illiteracy in the central highlands stands at 15 percent against a national figure below 4 percent. Abigail Abrash documented for Cultural Survival Quarterly that Freeport's 1967 Contract of Work was signed before indigenous Amungme and Kamoro peoples had been consulted, that Freeport paid the Indonesian security forces more than nine million dollars in the mid-1990s in exchange for protection, and that Komnas HAM's own 1995 investigation found "clear and identifiable human rights violations" linked to the mine's security arrangements.6

Equally consequential is the Suharto-era transmigration programme, which relocated more than 78,000 families from Java, Bali, and other outer islands to Papua between the 1970s and the early 2000s, permanently altering the demographic composition of the coastal lowlands and the provincial capital Jayapura. Indigenous Papuans, who under Dutch administration had constituted the entire population, found themselves minorities in parts of their own territory. Land claims by Papuan communities have rarely been legally recognized against transmigrant settlers or corporate concessions. The 2019 unrest brought this demographic anxiety to the surface in painful ways: the riots in Wamena in September of that year, which left 33 people dead (eight Papuans, including two children, and 25 non-Papuan settlers, including three children), were triggered by a false rumour that a teacher had used a racial epithet against a Papuan student — but the underlying tension between indigenous and settler communities was structural and long-standing.7

The 2019 Uprising and the Internet Blackout

The most significant moment of Papua-related unrest in the Reformasi era began not in Papua itself but on the island of Java. On the evening of 17 August 2019, Indonesia's independence day, military officers and members of an ultranationalist militia surrounded a dormitory housing Papuan university students in Surabaya, East Java. The cause was an accusation that students had failed to raise the Indonesian flag; what followed was a scene that quickly spread across social media and ignited protests across the archipelago. Officers deployed tear gas, broke into the dormitory, and arrested forty-three Papuan students — all of whom were released that same night — while militia members and security forces called the students "monkeys," "pigs," and "OPM members." Similar incidents of racial harassment occurred at Papuan student dormitories in Malang and Semarang. Human Rights Watch described the attacks as reflecting "a longstanding pattern of racism and discrimination by Indonesian security forces against Papuans."8

Within days, protests spread to thirty Indonesian cities; demonstrators in Manokwari burned a provincial parliament building; rallies in Sorong and Jayapura drew thousands. The Indonesian government's response was swift and, to many observers, disproportionate: on 21 August 2019, the Ministry of Communication and Information Technology implemented a sweeping internet throttling across Papua, escalating to a near-total blackout that cut off the entire region from social media and messaging applications. A partial restoration came in early September; a second blackout followed the Wamena riots in late September. A Jakarta administrative court subsequently ruled in 2020 that the internet shutdowns had been unlawful, but no official was held accountable.9

The Anatomy of Impunity: 1965–66 and the Petrus Killings

Contemporary Papua cannot be understood in isolation from a broader architecture of impunity that Indonesia has never fully dismantled. The worst episode of mass killing in Indonesian history — the anti-communist violence of 1965–66 — remains, by any measure, one of the greatest unacknowledged atrocities of the twentieth century. Estimates of the death toll range from approximately 500,000 (the figure used by Geoffrey B. Robinson in his landmark 2018 study The Killing Season, which argues that the killings were "the product of a deliberate campaign, led by the Indonesian Army") to upward of one million, including political prisoners who died in detention over subsequent years.10 John Roosa's earlier study, Pretext for Mass Murder (2006), demonstrated through analysis of documents from the Sjam network that the G30S movement was used as a pretext for a campaign the army had been preparing: the label "communist" was applied far beyond the PKI membership to schoolteachers, union members, village officials, and anyone who had participated in civil society organizations affiliated with the left.11 Declassified United States government documents released in 2017 by the National Security Archive make plain that senior US officials had detailed knowledge of the killings as they occurred: a 21 December 1965 cable estimated that approximately 100,000 PKI members had been killed in the preceding weeks, while earlier cables from November noted that provincial officials were "executing their PKI prisoners."12

In July 2016, the International People's Tribunal on 1965 (IPT 1965), a non-binding but carefully convened civil society tribunal held in The Hague and presided over by former South African Constitutional Court Justice Zak Yacoob, issued a verdict finding that the Indonesian state had committed crimes against humanity on ten counts — including murder, enslavement, torture, and sexual violence — and that the evidence supported a finding of genocide. The tribunal estimated 500,000 to one million deaths and 1.7 million or more imprisonments. It also found that the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia bore a degree of international complicity through their facilitation and encouragement of the violence.13 The Indonesian government dismissed the verdict as having no legal force, and no Indonesian official has ever faced prosecution for the 1965–66 killings.

The 1980s added a second layer to this structure of impunity. Between 1982 and 1985, a government campaign known colloquially as Petrus — from penembakan misterius, "mysterious shootings" — resulted in the extrajudicial killing of an estimated 2,000 people (Komnas HAM's figure from its 2012 investigation), though estimates in other sources range from around 300 confirmed deaths to as many as 10,000. The targets were predominantly tattooed young men identified as recidivists and gang members; the method was execution-style killing, with bodies often left in public view or dumped in rivers to maximize the deterrent effect. Suharto himself acknowledged in his 1989 autobiography, co-authored with Dwipayana and Ramadhan KH, that the killings were deliberate state policy — what he called "shock therapy" — stating that those who resisted "had to be shot" because "they fought back." Scholarly work by Sachiko Imamura, published in the journal Asian Studies in 2019, has analyzed Petrus not only as a crime-suppression campaign but as the elimination of former intelligence operatives — Opsus covert agents from the Suharto era who had become liabilities — giving the killings a political dimension that the "public safety" framing obscured.14 In 2012, Komnas HAM formally classified Petrus as a gross human rights violation, making it eligible in principle for ad hoc human rights court proceedings; as with all such classifications before and after, no prosecution followed.15

Petrus (penembakan misterius) — "mysterious shootings," the popular term for the Indonesian state's campaign of extrajudicial killings of alleged criminals between 1982 and 1985. The "mystery" was deliberate: bodies were displayed in public without any acknowledgment of official responsibility, creating an atmosphere of terror. President Suharto retroactively claimed the campaign as his own in 1989, describing it as shock therapy to restore public order.

1997–98: Activist Disappearances, the May Violence, and Prabowo's Command

The final years of the New Order produced a third category of unacknowledged crime: the forced disappearances of political activists on the eve of Suharto's fall, and the mass violence that attended that fall in May 1998. Between 1997 and 1998, at least twenty-three activists — members of student organizations, the underground PRD (People's Democratic Party), and civil society networks — were abducted by a special operations unit of the Army Special Forces Command (Kopassus). Nine were eventually released; one, Leonardus Gilang, was found dead; thirteen have never been accounted for and are presumed dead. Among the missing is the poet and activist Wiji Thukul, whose spare, angry verse about poverty and repression had made him one of the most recognizable dissident voices of the late New Order period.16

The unit responsible was Tim Mawar (Rose Team), a covert sub-unit of Kopassus Group IV formed in July 1997 under Major Bambang Kristiono. A military tribunal convened in April 1999 convicted eleven Tim Mawar members for the abductions of the nine activists who survived; several of those convicted were subsequently promoted rather than imprisoned. The command responsibility question was addressed, obliquely, by the Dewan Kehormatan Perwira (Officers' Honour Council, DKP) convened in August 1998, which found that the abductions had been carried out "on the orders of and with the knowledge of the Kopassus leadership." The Kopassus commander from December 1995 to March 1998 was Major General Prabowo Subianto, Suharto's son-in-law; from March 1998 he commanded the Army Strategic Reserve (Kostrad). The DKP found Prabowo's actions reflected conduct unbecoming an officer and recommended his discharge; President Habibie granted him an honorary discharge with full pension in November 1998 — a framing that human rights groups contested as whitewashing. No criminal charges were ever filed. In a 2014 Al Jazeera interview, Prabowo stated that he had ordered the abduction of activists who were later released, claiming he had been "following orders" from superiors.17

The same weeks that produced the last disappearances also produced the worst urban violence since the Revolution. On 12 May 1998, security forces at Trisakti University in Jakarta shot and killed four unarmed students — Elang Mulia Lesmana, Heri Hertanto, Hafidin Royan, and Hendriawan Sie — who were returning from a peaceful demonstration. The killings unleashed four days of rioting across Jakarta, Medan, and Surakarta. The Joint Fact-Finding Team (TGPF) appointed by President Habibie estimated approximately 1,188 deaths, the majority from fires including the Yogya Plaza shopping centre fire; other estimates run as high as 2,244. Sexual violence, particularly against ethnic Chinese Indonesian women, was documented on an unprecedented scale: the Volunteer Team for Humanity recorded 168 cases in Jakarta alone; the TGPF documented 85 victims of sexual violence for the period 13–15 May 1998 — 52 rapes, 14 cases of rape combined with other violence, 10 instances of sexual torture, and 9 cases of sexual harassment. No individual was ever convicted for the killings or for any act of sexual violence during the riots. The Trisakti shootings were included in Jokowi's 2023 list of acknowledged violations (see below); the May 1998 riots were listed separately. Neither listing has been accompanied by any criminal proceeding.18

The elevation of Prabowo Subianto to the presidency in October 2024 — after two previous unsuccessful presidential bids — was greeted by human rights organizations with deep alarm. A 2005 US Congressional investigation had cited his command responsibility for the disappearances as the basis for a visa ban, which was only lifted in October 2020. As Donny Syofyan of the University of Melbourne argued in 2024, Prabowo's election and the fragmentation of Indonesia's human rights movement into those willing to work with his government and those refusing to do so reflects a deliberate strategy of dividing advocates who might otherwise mount unified pressure for accountability. Prabowo has signalled no intention of revisiting the 1997–98 disappearances and has framed the issue as one that should be "resolved" through dialogue with victims' families — a process which Kontras and other organizations have characterized as an attempt to manufacture the appearance of closure without genuine justice.19

The Murder of Munir Said Thalib

No single event captures the anatomy of post-Suharto impunity more precisely than the killing of Munir Said Thalib. Munir was Indonesia's most prominent human rights lawyer of the Reformasi generation: a co-founder of Kontras (the Commission for the Disappeared and Victims of Violence), a tireless advocate for the 1997–98 disappeared, and the recipient of the Right Livelihood Award in 2000. On 7 September 2004, he died on a Garuda Indonesia flight from Jakarta to Amsterdam, en route to pursue a master's degree in international law at Utrecht University. He was thirty-eight years old. The Dutch Netherlands Forensic Institute determined that he had been killed by arsenic, administered in a dose approximately three times the lethal level, and ingested during the Jakarta–Singapore leg of the journey.20

A Jakarta court convicted Garuda pilot Pollycarpus Budihari Priyanto in December 2005 of placing poison in Munir's meal after switching him from economy to business class during the Singapore stopover. The case's broader architecture, however, involved the State Intelligence Agency (BIN). Forty-one telephone calls between Pollycarpus and BIN deputy director Major General (ret.) Muchdi Purwopranjono were documented in the record. In 2004, Pollycarpus had delivered to Garuda's chief executive a letter from BIN deputy chairman M. As'ad placing him in the airline's corporate security division — a letter later found to be fabricated. Muchdi was tried for murder, but a South Jakarta court acquitted him on 31 December 2008, citing insufficient evidence; human rights organizations and the UN Special Rapporteur on extrajudicial killings noted credible reports of witness intimidation during the proceedings. A presidential fact-finding team appointed in 2004 produced a report implicating BIN; that report was never made public.21

Pollycarpus's legal history became an emblem of Indonesia's inconsistent justice. He was sentenced to fourteen years in December 2005; acquitted on appeal in October 2006 (prompting HRW's Brad Adams to call it "a test case for the Indonesian justice system" that the system had "failed"); reinstated with a twenty-year sentence on judicial review in January 2008; had his sentence reduced to fourteen years in 2013; and was paroled in November 2014. He died of COVID-19 in October 2020. Sixteen years after the murder, HRW concluded that "no senior figures in the security services have been held accountable" — a verdict that has not changed since. Amnesty International noted in 2014 that "the masterminds of Munir's killing remain free," a conclusion that remains accurate as of the time of writing.22

Transitional Justice: Repeated Failure and Partial Acknowledgment

Indonesia has attempted, and largely failed, to build institutional mechanisms for addressing its accumulation of unacknowledged atrocities. Ehito Kimura, writing in the Kyoto Review of Southeast Asia in 2018, described Indonesia's record on transitional justice as "one of abject failure, with no significant convictions" for past violations — a judgment supported by the systematic pattern of acquittals in the ad hoc human rights courts established under Law 26 of 2000. According to the International Center for Transitional Justice (ICTJ), of thirty-four individuals tried in those courts — for crimes related to East Timor (1999), the Tanjung Priok massacre (1984), and other incidents — all have either been acquitted at trial or had their convictions overturned on appeal. The courts lacked retroactive jurisdiction for crimes committed before 2000, further constraining their scope.23

The most serious attempt to create an extra-judicial reckoning mechanism was Law No. 27 of 2004, which established a Truth and Reconciliation Commission (Komisi Kebenaran dan Rekonsiliasi, KKR). The law, however, contained provisions that made it constitutionally and ethically untenable: amnesty would be available to perpetrators who acknowledged their crimes, and — critically — reparations to victims were conditioned on those victims' agreement to the perpetrator's amnesty request. KontraS and other civil society organizations filed a constitutional challenge. The Constitutional Court's Decision No. 006/PUU-IV/2006, issued on 7 December 2006, annulled the entire law; the court found the conditional reparations clause violated victims' rights under the constitution. The court ordered parliament to pass replacement legislation within two years. As of 2023, no replacement had been enacted.24

The closest Indonesia came to a formal executive reckoning was President Jokowi's statement on 11 January 2023. In a nationally televised address, Jokowi acknowledged twelve episodes of "gross human rights violations" committed between 1965 and 2003 and expressed that he "strongly regretted" that they had occurred. The twelve incidents named were: the 1965–66 mass killings; the Petrus shootings of 1982–85; the Talangsari massacre of 7 February 1989; operations at Rumoh Geudong and elsewhere in Aceh; the Kraras Incident in East Timor (KKA); the 1997–98 activist disappearances; the May 1998 riots; the Trisakti and Semanggi shootings; the Banyuwangi incidents of 1998; the Wasior massacre in Papua (2001); the Wamena violence of 2003; and the Jambo Keupok killings in Aceh (2003). The acknowledgment was the product of the Team for Non-Judicial Resolution of Past Gross Human Rights Violations (Tim PPHAM), established by Presidential Regulation No. 17 of 2022.25

Responses from survivors, civil society, and the international community ranged from cautious welcome to sharp criticism. The United Nations Human Rights Office called the statement "a step on the long road to justice" and urged the government to move toward "a meaningful, inclusive and participatory transitional justice process, guaranteeing truth, justice, reparations, and non-recurrence." Usman Hamid, executive director of Amnesty International Indonesia, argued that "The recognition is not enough. It should not have been only regret, but also apology" — and stressed that no one implicated in the twelve incidents faced any legal consequence. Lembaga Studi dan Advokasi Masyarakat (Elsam), which had spent years building the evidentiary record for transitional justice, warned that the PPHAM process risked legitimizing impunity by closing the files without accountability. The mechanism was explicitly "non-judicial" by design: no names of perpetrators were mentioned, no criminal referrals were made, and the document spoke of "restoration" of victims' rights through material assistance rather than prosecutorial action. The structural barrier identified by Kimura — a 1999 constitutional provision affirming that retroactive prosecution violates basic human rights, effectively foreclosing criminal proceedings for pre-2000 crimes — remains unreformed.26

Democratic Regression: The ITE Law, the KPK, and the New Criminal Code

Beyond the specific legacies of atrocity, Indonesian democracy has faced challenges internal to the post-1998 order itself: the progressive weakening of institutions designed to check executive power, and the deployment of broadly drawn legal instruments against critics. Three developments in particular have generated sustained concern among scholars, journalists, and human rights organizations.

The ITE Law and Online Expression

Law No. 11 of 2008 on Electronic Information and Transactions (Undang-Undang Informasi dan Transaksi Elektronik, UU ITE) was ostensibly designed to regulate online commerce and cybercrime. Its provisions criminalizing the online transmission of "defamatory" or "offensive" content proved almost immediately amenable to use against government critics, journalists, and ordinary citizens. The first high-profile case involved Prita Mulyasari, a patient who complained in a private email about treatment at Omni International Hospital in 2008; the email was forwarded without her knowledge, and she found herself facing both civil and criminal charges, sparking the "Coin for Prita" solidarity campaign. The Supreme Court overturned her conviction in 2012, but the chilling effect of the case was already deeply embedded.27

A 2016 amendment strengthened some procedural protections but left the substantive criminal provisions intact. Criminalization accelerated rather than receded: data compiled by SAFEnet and Amnesty International documented 74 ITE cases under President Yudhoyono (2009–2014) versus 233 under Jokowi (2014–2019) — figures representing over 241 individuals criminalized for online criticism of the administration. Between 2019 and 2024, Amnesty International documented more than 530 instances of individuals criminalized for online expression in Indonesia.28 Among the most significant cases of the Jokowi era were the charges brought under the ITE Law against human rights lawyer Haris Azhar and Kontras coordinator Fatia Maulidiyanti in 2021, following statements they made in a podcast interview about alleged connections between senior minister Luhut Binsar Pandjaitan and mining operations in Papua — the very region whose human rights situation they had been documenting. The charges against Haris Azhar were eventually dropped in 2023 after sustained domestic and international pressure, but the message to civil society was unmistakable.

The Weakening of the Corruption Eradication Commission

The Corruption Eradication Commission (Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi, KPK), established under Law No. 30 of 2002, was for over a decade one of the most effective and independent anti-corruption bodies in Southeast Asia, securing high-profile convictions of ministers, governors, regional legislators, and businesspeople. Its independence rested on its legal status as an institution not subordinate to any ministry or to the police and prosecutors' offices whose corruption it was often investigating. Law No. 19 of 2019, passed by the House of Representatives on 17 September 2019 after weeks of student protests against the bill, dismantled that independence through a series of structural changes: the KPK was reclassified from an independent body to a government institution under executive oversight; a Supervisory Board was created, appointed by the president, whose advance approval is now required for wiretaps and searches — tools central to KPK's investigative methodology; and all KPK staff were converted to State Civil Apparatus (ASN) status, meaning they could be rotated into or out of the commission by the government. A further blow came in May 2021, when a controversial civics test resulted in the dismissal of seventy-five KPK employees, including Novel Baswedan, one of the commission's most effective and celebrated investigators, who had himself been the victim of an acid attack in 2017 that gouged out his eye and was never fully prosecuted.29

The 2022 Criminal Code

On 6 December 2022, the House of Representatives unanimously passed a comprehensive revision of Indonesia's criminal code (KUHP). Signed into law on 2 January 2023 by President Jokowi, the code replaced the Dutch colonial-era penal code and is due to take effect on 2 January 2026. In many respects it represents a genuine modernization: it consolidates thousands of criminal provisions into a single code, adjusts penalties, and clarifies procedural rules. But a set of specific provisions attracted intense criticism from civil society, journalists, legal scholars, and human rights organizations.30

Among the most contested provisions are: Articles 218–220, which criminalize public "attacks on the honour or dignity" of the president or vice president, punishable by up to three years' imprisonment; Article 240, which prohibits "insults" against state institutions; Articles 300–305, which significantly expand the existing blasphemy provisions, criminalizing the "promotion" of atheism or the defamation of religion with penalties of up to five years; Article 302, which criminalizes inducing another person to become "irreligious" or to change religion (up to two years); Article 411, which criminalizes consensual extramarital sex including same-sex conduct (one year); and Article 412, which criminalizes cohabitation outside marriage (six months). Amnesty International Indonesia's executive director Usman Hamid called the code "a significant blow" to civil liberties and warned that it would "entrench obstacles to freedom of speech while criminalizing legitimate and peaceful dissent." The Alliance of Independent Journalists (AJI) identified seventeen provisions likely to be used against media organizations. Human Rights Watch noted that provisions on "insult" and "humiliation" were sufficiently elastic in their drafting that the government could define their meaning at will.31

KUHP (Kitab Undang-Undang Hukum Pidana) — Indonesia's Criminal Code. The colonial-era KUHP derived from Dutch law. The revised KUHP passed in December 2022 and entering force in January 2026 represents the first comprehensive revision since independence. Civil society criticism has focused in particular on the lèse-majesté (presidential insult), expanded blasphemy, and extramarital-sex provisions.

The cumulative picture drawn by Freedom House's annual assessments captures the trajectory: Indonesia scored 61 out of 100 on Freedom House's Freedom in the World index in 2020 and had declined to 57 by 2024, retaining its "Partly Free" designation but with a continuing downward trend across categories including judicial independence, freedom of expression, and government accountability. CIVICUS rated Indonesia's civic space as "Obstructed" — the second most severe category — as of 2024. These assessments should be read with appropriate caveats: aggregate scores compress complex realities, Indonesia retains genuine competitive elections and a pluralist media landscape, and the direction of travel under Prabowo (who took office in October 2024 and has signalled social conservatism combined with military-influenced governance) remains to be fully assessed. What the scholarly and human rights literature sustains beyond reasonable dispute, however, is that the legal infrastructure for restricting expression, undermining prosecutorial independence, and silencing critics has been substantially reinforced in the Reformasi era, not reduced.

Summary

Contemporary Indonesia carries the weight of multiple unresolved pasts. The West Papua conflict — the only active armed separatist insurgency remaining after the 2005 Aceh peace agreement — is shaped by the contested 1969 Act of Free Choice, decades of military operations conducted with minimal outside scrutiny, the resource wealth concentrated in indigenous lands, demographic displacement through transmigration, and systematic restrictions on media access. The 2019 protests, triggered by a racist attack on Papuan university students in Surabaya, exposed these tensions to a national audience; the government's internet shutdowns, subsequently ruled unlawful, suppressed rather than addressed them. The inherited impunity for New Order atrocities — the 1965–66 mass killings (500,000–1,000,000 dead), the Petrus extrajudicial executions, the 1997–98 activist disappearances, and the May 1998 violence — remains structurally intact. The ad hoc Human Rights Courts produced no sustained convictions; the Truth and Reconciliation Commission law was struck down in 2006; and Jokowi's 2023 acknowledgment of twelve past violations, welcomed as a symbolic step, was explicitly designed to avoid judicial accountability. The murder of Munir Said Thalib in 2004, in which a BIN deputy director was tried and acquitted under disputed circumstances, remains an open symbol of this impunity's persistence. Meanwhile, the ITE Law, the 2019 KPK revision, and the 2022 Criminal Code have collectively strengthened the legal tools available to restrict expression, weaken prosecutorial independence, and criminalize dissent — a trajectory that scholars and international monitors describe as democratic regression rather than democratic deepening.

Was it a meaningful step toward transitional justice, a form of closure that foreclosed accountability, or both? Draw on the specific positions of the UN, Amnesty International, and survivor organizations in your answer.
42.7 How does the ITE Law illustrate the gap between formal democratic institutions (free elections, independent media) and substantive democratic freedoms? What does the pattern of ITE cases suggest about which actors are most vulnerable to legal repression?
