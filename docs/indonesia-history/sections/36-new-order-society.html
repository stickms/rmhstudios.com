Learning Objectives

  • Describe the social consequences of New Order developmentalism: urbanization, expanding mass education, falling fertility, and the emergence of an urban middle class.
  • Analyze the official gender ideology of the New Order — Julia Suryakusuma's concept of "State Ibuism" — and explain how Dharma Wanita and the PKK institutionalized it.
  • Evaluate how the regime managed culture and collective memory through TVRI, the Pancasila Press doctrine, and the banning of dissident literature, including the works of Pramoedya Ananta Toer.
  • Explain the legal, cultural, and economic predicament of Chinese Indonesians under the New Order's assimilation policies, and the structural paradox of the cukong system.
  • Trace the Islamic revival of the 1980s–90s, Suharto's strategic accommodation of political Islam through ICMI (1990) and his 1991 hajj, and the unintended democratic consequences Robert Hefner identifies.
  • Situate dangdut music and the suppression of Pramoedya Ananta Toer within the New Order's contested cultural politics.

When Suharto's New Order consolidated power after 1966, it promised Indonesians pembangunan — development — in exchange for political acquiescence. By most macroeconomic measures the bargain was kept: within a generation, the archipelago was transformed from a country on the edge of famine and bankruptcy into one of Southeast Asia's showcase economies. But the social, cultural, and religious dimensions of that transformation were far more ambiguous. Development brought schools, falling birth rates, television, and a new urban middle class; it also brought a pervasive state ideology of family and womanhood, systematic suppression of Chinese cultural identity, and a managed press that policed the boundaries of permissible thought. And threading through the entire New Order period — a phenomenon the regime did not foresee and ultimately could not control — was a surging Islamic revivalism that would reshape both private piety and public politics in ways that outlasted Suharto himself.

The Development Dividend: Urbanization, Education, and Health

The material transformation wrought by the New Order between 1966 and 1998 was, by any comparative standard, remarkable. When Suharto's government took power, Indonesia was among the world's poorest countries: per capita income was barely above US$50, rice production was in deficit, and two in five adults could not read. By 1997, on the eve of the financial crisis, per capita income had reached approximately US$1,100, the country had achieved rice self-sufficiency and briefly become a net exporter, adult literacy had climbed above 85 percent, and infant mortality had fallen by more than half. These gains were real, widely distributed — in rural as well as urban areas — and form an indispensable part of any honest accounting of the New Order's record.

The single most consequential investment in that transformation was education. Beginning with the Second Five-Year Development Plan (Repelita II, 1974–79), Suharto's government launched the Instruksi Presiden Sekolah Dasar — the Presidential Instruction for Primary Schools, universally known as INPRES SD — and built 61,807 new school buildings between 1973 and 1978, roughly doubling per-capita school capacity at the primary level and deliberately targeting the districts with the lowest existing enrollment.1 Esther Duflo's seminal economic study of the INPRES program demonstrated that each additional school per 1,000 children raised adult educational attainment by 0.12 to 0.19 years and adult wages by 1.5 to 2.7 percent, implying returns to education of 7–10 percent — among the highest documented anywhere in the developing world at the time.1 The program was accompanied by compulsory attendance regulations, teacher training and deployment to remote areas, and a dramatic rise in secondary and university enrolment in the 1980s. Primary enrollment, roughly 57 percent of school-age children in 1973, exceeded 95 percent by the late 1980s.

Urbanization followed. In 1970 approximately 17 percent of Indonesians lived in cities; by the mid-1990s the proportion had nearly doubled, and Jakarta's population had grown from roughly 4 million to more than 9 million, surrounded by an ever-expanding *Jabotabek* conurbation of light industry and dormitory towns.2 The oil booms of 1973–74 and 1979–80 channeled petrodollars into infrastructure, state enterprises, and a rapidly expanding civil-service payroll that drew educated Javanese and Outer Islanders alike toward urban employment. Richard Robison's landmark political economy of the period traced how this process generated not merely growth but a new social structure: a "politico-bureaucratic bourgeoisie" fused with the military and the state, and, below it, a wage-earning urban middle class whose education, consumer aspirations, and eventually political ambitions would come to strain the authoritarian framework that had produced them.3

Health indicators improved in parallel. The deployment of village health posts (Puskesmas and Posyandu) brought basic immunization and maternal care to communities that had never before had access to biomedical services. Life expectancy at birth, approximately 46 years in 1965, had risen to above 63 years by 1995. These gains were not costless or free of coercion — as later sections will show, the family planning program embedded within this health infrastructure was a site of intense political management — but their scale is difficult to dispute. The development bargain, in its material dimensions, delivered for many millions of Indonesians, and this goes a long way toward explaining the genuine popular legitimacy the New Order enjoyed for much of its existence, even in retrospect.

Keluarga Berencana: Family Planning as State Project

No social programme of the New Order era was more ambitious, more intensively managed, or more consequential in its demographic outcomes than Keluarga Berencana (KB) — the national family planning programme. Indonesia's total fertility rate stood at roughly 5.6 live births per woman in the early 1970s; by the mid-1990s it had fallen to approximately 2.6, one of the most rapid fertility transitions recorded in the developing world.4 The demographic dividend this produced — a generation of unusually large cohorts of working-age adults in the 1990s and 2000s — was itself a significant contributor to Indonesia's sustained economic growth.

Keluarga Berencana (KB) — Indonesia's national family planning programme, launched under the Second Five-Year Plan and administered by BKKBN (Badan Koordinasi Keluarga Berencana Nasional, the National Family Planning Coordinating Board, est. 1970). The programme promoted the two-child ideal (Dua Anak Cukup, "Two is Enough") through a vertically integrated network of village health posts, women's organizations, and, controversially, military and government pressure on "acceptors."

Jeremy Shiffman's analysis of KB identifies the programme's success as primarily political rather than demographic: what distinguished Indonesia from comparable developing-country efforts was the New Order's capacity to translate central directives into village-level compliance through vertical command structures that ran from the presidential palace in Jakarta down to the village midwife and the local military garrison.4 Suharto personally championed contraception acceptance as a patriotic duty; governors and district heads faced accountability for "acceptor" targets, and military officers were deployed as motivators in reluctant communities. The programme's flagship delivery mechanism was the Posyandu — the integrated village health post — which combined maternal weighing, immunization, and contraceptive distribution into a single monthly community event, staffed primarily by volunteer women trained through the PKK network.

Paul Gertler and John Molyneaux's detailed econometric work on the 1982–87 period found that approximately 75 percent of Indonesia's fertility decline in those years could be attributed to increased contraceptive prevalence rather than to rising female education or income, underscoring the programme's independent contribution to demographic change.5 Anke Niehof and Firman Lubis's comprehensive edited volume on the thirty-year history of KB traces how the programme evolved through phases: early emphasis on voluntary "acceptance" gradually gave way to increasingly specific targets, quasi-quotas assigned to local officials, and social pressure on couples to adopt "modern" contraceptive methods — especially the IUD and the injectable Depo-Provera — rather than traditional spacing methods.6

Scholars have debated the extent to which KB constituted genuine coercion. In some regions, particularly in Java and Bali, evidence of outright compulsion — threats of denial of fertilizer subsidies, health services, or school places for families with more than two children — was documented, and the involvement of ABRI (the armed forces) in "motivating" rural women has been widely criticised.7 Others have argued that the programme also responded to genuine latent demand among women for contraception, particularly in densely populated Java, where land scarcity had long created cultural preferences for smaller families. What is not disputed is the outcome: Indonesia's fertility transition was extraordinarily rapid, with consequences for women's health, household economics, and the demographic structure of the population that reached well beyond the New Order period.8

State Ibuism: Gender, Dharma Wanita, and the Official Woman

The New Order's management of women extended far beyond reproductive policy. It was embedded in a systematic official ideology that the sociologist Julia Suryakusuma, in an influential 1996 essay, named Ibuisme Negara — "State Ibuism." Derived from ibu (mother, but also a deferential form of address), State Ibuism constructed Indonesian womanhood around five official duties: to be a loyal wife, a responsible mother, a competent household manager, a supplementary earner for the family, and a good Indonesian citizen. In this framework, women's civic existence was entirely mediated through their domestic and reproductive roles; their public identities were derivative of and subordinate to those of their husbands.9

State Ibuism (Ibuisme Negara) — term coined by Julia Suryakusuma (1996) for the New Order's official gender ideology, which constructed Indonesian womanhood as inherently domestic, reproductive, and subordinate — women as mothers and wives first, citizens second. State Ibuism was institutionalized through Dharma Wanita and PKK and functioned both as an ideological claim and as an organizational system for mobilizing women in support of developmental goals.

The organisational arm of State Ibuism in the civil service was Dharma Wanita (roughly, "Women's Duty"), established in 1974 as the obligatory association of wives of male government employees. Membership was not voluntary: every civil servant's wife was automatically enrolled, and the association's hierarchy precisely mirrored that of the civil service — the wife of a director general outranked the wife of a section head, regardless of the women's own education or professional standing. Suryakusuma identified this "appendage status" as the ideological core of the system: women's position in the most powerful state-linked organization in the country was entirely contingent on whom they had married, not on anything they had done or were.10

Complementing Dharma Wanita was PKK (Pembinaan Kesejahteraan Keluarga, Family Welfare Guidance), a village-level women's organization that reached into every hamlet in the archipelago and served as the primary community delivery vehicle for state programmes: KB contraception, childhood nutrition, hygiene education, and national ideology (P4, the guidelines on Pancasila practice). PKK was technically a non-governmental organization but in practice operated as an arm of the state, with mandatory participation for village women coordinated through the wives of village headmen.11 Susan Blackburn's careful study of women and the state documents how the New Order simultaneously expanded the space of women's organized public activity — PKK reached tens of millions of women — while funnelling that activity exclusively through state-sanctioned domesticity and suppressing independent women's organizations whose agendas were not aligned with regime goals.12

Scholars have noted important tensions within this framework. State Ibuism was powerful and pervasive, but it was not total. Blackburn shows that some women navigated Dharma Wanita and PKK to build genuine local networks and access resources that they then used for purposes the state had not intended. Suryakusuma herself acknowledges that the "good mother" ideology could be appropriated by women as a rhetorical shield for activities that pushed against its limits. The Islamic women's organizations Aisyiyah (linked to Muhammadiyah) and Muslimat NU (linked to Nahdlatul Ulama) maintained a degree of institutional autonomy within the New Order framework that gave their members some room for manoeuvre outside the Dharma Wanita hierarchy.13 These qualifications do not negate the force of the gender ideology — they index its pervasiveness by showing how much energy women had to expend to carve even small exceptions within it.

Managed Culture: TVRI, the Pancasila Press, and the Control of Memory

If developmentalism was the economic face of the New Order, pembangunan was its ideological face — and that ideology required the control of information, culture, and historical memory. The linguist and cultural critic Ariel Heryanto, in an early analysis of New Order discourse, showed how "development" had been transformed by the regime from a contested policy orientation into an unquestionable national telos: a moral obligation, a patriotic duty, a measuring stick against which all other claims — for political rights, for cultural autonomy, for historical truth — were found wanting.14 The media system was the primary vector for this ideological work.

TVRI (Televisi Republik Indonesia) — the state television network, established 1962, which held a complete monopoly on broadcast television in Indonesia until the licensing of the first private channel (RCTI) in 1989. TVRI's nightly news programme Berita was the authoritative voice on national affairs and was required to follow editorial guidelines set by the Ministry of Information.

Televisi Republik Indonesia (TVRI) was the New Order's single most powerful cultural instrument. From its expansion into a national network in the 1970s until RCTI's private licence in 1989, it held a complete monopoly on broadcast television, reaching an ever-larger proportion of the population as electrification extended to rural areas. TVRI's prime-time news was not merely state-inflected — it was state-controlled, with editors reporting directly to the Ministry of Information under guidelines that defined national news in terms of development successes, regime achievements, and social harmony. Coverage of labor unrest, regional separatism, corruption, or opposition activity was simply absent. The Pancasila values of unity and stability were, in this framework, prior constraints on reporting rather than political positions to be argued for.

The broader press operated under what was formally called Pers Pancasila — the Pancasila Press. Justito Adiprasetio's genealogy of this doctrine traces how "developmental journalism," a concept borrowed from Cold War modernization theory and refracted through Pancasila ideology, required all reporting to subordinate objectivity to the goals of national unity and development.15 Newspapers and magazines were licensed by the government and could have their licence revoked without judicial process — a power the Ministry of Information exercised decisively against Tempo, DeTik, and Editor in June 1994, silencing three of the country's most incisive publications in a single decree. Self-censorship was, as a result, pervasive in the surviving press.

Control of historical memory operated through a different but related apparatus. Katharine McGregor's study of the Armed Forces' role in constructing Indonesian historiography demonstrates how the military's history centre, under chief historian Nugroho Notosusanto, produced a canonical account of modern Indonesian history — textbooks, museums, films, and annual ceremonies — that placed the army at the centre of the national narrative, naturalised Dwifungsi as a historical necessity, and systematically erased the contributions of the Communist Party, labour movements, and socialist nationalists to the independence struggle.16 The 30 September Movement (G30S) commemoration was ritualised into an annual occasion for reinforcing the official narrative of communist treachery and military salvation; the state film Pengkhianatan G30S/PKI (The Treachery of the G30S/PKI, 1984) was mandatory viewing in schools every 30 September for over a decade.17

The Attorney General's office maintained an Index of banned books and publications, invoking powers under a 1963 law to prohibit works deemed subversive or threatening to public order. The works of leftist and nationalist writers who had not survived or made peace with the 1965 transition were systematically removed from libraries and bookshops. The most celebrated victim of this censorship was Pramoedya Ananta Toer, whose case is examined in the final section of this chapter; but he was not alone — the works of dozens of writers, historians, and social scientists were banned, and the effect on Indonesian intellectual life was cumulative and corrosive.

The Chinese Question: Assimilation, Exclusion, and the Cukong System

Of all the social formations managed and distorted by the New Order, none was more structurally paradoxical than the position of ethnic Chinese Indonesians. Numbering approximately 2.8–3 percent of the total population in the 1980s and 1990s, the Chinese community — known by the official term WNI keturunan Tionghoa (Indonesian citizens of Chinese descent) — occupied a position of simultaneous economic prominence and legal vulnerability that the regime did not resolve but deliberately sustained.

The legal architecture of exclusion was erected swiftly after 1966. Presidential Instruction No. 14 of 1967 (Inpres 14/1967) prohibited the public expression of Chinese cultural and religious practices, banned Chinese-language instruction and newspapers, and required Chinese religious observances to be conducted privately and without public display. Chinese-language schools, which had educated generations of the community, were closed; the Chinese-language press, which had been politically diverse and culturally vital, was eliminated. The instruction represented the most comprehensive legal suppression of a minority cultural identity in post-independence Indonesian history.18

Name-changing was actively encouraged and in many contexts effectively compelled. The government offered simplified procedures for ethnic Chinese to replace Chinese names with Indonesian ones; while nominally voluntary, the pressure was intense — a Chinese name marked its bearer for discrimination in civil service employment, military service (from which ethnic Chinese were effectively barred), and in many contexts commercial life. Charles Coppel's foundational study of Indonesian Chinese documents how the 1967 directive combined with broader informal exclusions — from the bureaucracy, the military officer corps, and formal political life — to create a legally precarious middle class with no institutional advocates and no permitted cultural infrastructure.19

cukong — Javanese/Indonesian term for a Chinese-Indonesian businessman acting as financial backer or commercial partner to a military officer or government official. The cukong system was the structural mechanism through which the New Order simultaneously excluded ethnic Chinese from formal political power and made itself dependent on Chinese capital for its developmental agenda.

The paradox at the heart of this system was the cukong arrangement. While ethnic Chinese were formally excluded from political institutions and cultural expression, the New Order's developmentalism required private capital that the indigenous (pribumi) business class, still nascent, could not supply at the required scale. Military commanders and senior officials routinely entered into symbiotic partnerships with Chinese businessmen, providing protection and contracts in exchange for equity stakes, financing, and informal access to commercial networks. Suharto's own inner circle of business associates — the Salim Group's Liem Sioe Liong being the most prominent — exemplified a system in which Chinese capital was indispensable to the regime's economic projects while Chinese businessmen remained politically invisible and legally exposed.20

Christian Chua's analysis of how the New Order "defined" Chineseness as a category argues that this double bind was not an accidental contradiction but a structural feature: keeping Chinese Indonesians culturally stigmatised and politically defenceless made them useful scapegoats whose periodic victimisation could be deployed by the regime to deflect popular anger or signal to nationalist constituencies.21 The anti-Chinese pogroms that accompanied economic downturns — most catastrophically in May 1998, when over a thousand people died in Jakarta and Chinese-owned businesses were systematically burned — were the terminal expression of a vulnerability the New Order had spent thirty years constructing. The regime that had nurtured Chinese capital for three decades could not, or would not, protect the community when the crisis came.

The Islamic Resurgence: Piety, Campus, and the New Middle Class

Among the most consequential and least anticipated developments of the late New Order was a broad and multidimensional Islamic revival that gathered force across the 1970s, accelerated through the 1980s, and reached a critical political inflection point around 1990. This revival was not primarily a political phenomenon in its origins — it was a cultural, spiritual, and sociological one, rooted in the specific experiences of the first generation to have been educated through the INPRES school system and to have migrated into urban environments in significant numbers.

The outward signs were unmistakable. The number of mosques in Indonesia roughly doubled between 1970 and 1990. University campuses — especially the science and engineering faculties of state universities, where students from secular, modernist, or syncretic Javanese backgrounds encountered the more intensely observant Islam of classmates from Sumatra, Sulawesi, and West Java — became hotbeds of Islamic study circles (usroh and liqo) inspired by Egyptian and Malaysian Islamist movements. The jilbab (headscarf) spread rapidly among young women in cities and on campuses, representing not merely religious observance but a deliberate statement of identity and modernity: these were educated, urban women choosing visible Islam, not village women inheriting it.22 The New Order government, alarmed at what it read as political Islamism, banned the jilbab from state schools and government offices through a 1982 directive — only to reverse the ban in 1991 in recognition of both its unenforceable character and the changed political context Suharto had himself helped create.

Suzanne Brenner's ethnographic work on a Javanese merchant community in Solo during the late-New Order period provides a richly textured account of how Islamisation and social mobility intersected at the household level. Women who had previously worked alongside their husbands in the batik trade were withdrawing from commercial activity, adopting Islamic dress, and reframing domesticity and female modesty as markers of middle-class status and spiritual achievement.23 This dynamic — in which the "Islamic turn" and the social aspirations of a newly prosperous urban class reinforced each other — was not specific to Solo. It was visible across the archipelago and produced a distinctive blend of consumerism and piety: Islamic bookshops, halal restaurants, Islamic banking, and a market for cassette tapes of Quranic recitation and Islamic pop music.

Julia Day Howell's study of Sufism and the Indonesian Islamic revival adds a further dimension often overlooked in political-science accounts: the inner or spiritual channel of Islamisation. Urban professionals, disillusioned with both the materialism of the development boom and the perceived aridity of modernist Islam, turned in significant numbers to Sufi brotherhoods and neo-Sufi urban retreat movements that offered contemplative practice and communal belonging outside the state's organizational reach.24 This interior dimension of the revival — oriented toward personal transformation rather than political action — helps explain why Islamisation penetrated so broadly across social classes and had effects far deeper than any organisational or political measure of "political Islam" could capture.

The regime's relationship with organised Islam was tense throughout the 1970s and early 1980s. The government's 1984 asas tunggal law, which required all mass organisations to adopt Pancasila as their sole ideological foundation and to drop all explicit ideological references to Islam, sparked fierce resistance from Islamic organisations — the large modernist Muhammadiyah and the traditionalist Nahdlatul Ulama (NU) eventually accepted the requirement after extended negotiation, but the confrontation left deep scars. In September 1984, a confrontation between security forces and Muslim protesters at Tanjung Priok, North Jakarta, ended in a shooting that killed at least dozens (by some accounts more than a hundred) of protesters who had gathered after soldiers desecrated religious property; the event became a symbol of the regime's brutal treatment of Islamic political assertion.25 For much of the New Order's middle period, Islam and the state faced each other with mutual suspicion.

Suharto's Green Turn: The Hajj, ICMI, and the Late New Order

By the late 1980s, the political context had shifted significantly. Suharto, increasingly anxious about the autonomy of the military establishment — particularly General Benny Moerdani, who had distanced himself from the presidential family — began to cultivate Muslim constituencies as a counterweight. The result was a set of moves that transformed the relationship between the regime and political Islam with extraordinary speed.

The most visible symbol came in 1991, when Suharto performed the hajj pilgrimage to Mecca and returned with the name "Haji Muhammad Suharto." The act was widely read as a political signal — a declaration that the head of state identified personally with Islam, not merely as one of the five recognised religions but as a source of personal legitimacy.26 Whether the decision also reflected genuine religious feeling is impossible to determine with certainty and has been debated by scholars; what is undeniable is its political timing and its political effects. Suharto's religious rebranding encouraged cabinet members, military officers, and senior officials to make similar public demonstrations of piety, and created social permission for a more visible Islamic presence in state and civic life.

The institutional expression of the turn came a year earlier: on 7 December 1990 in Malang, Suharto endorsed and then presided over the founding of ICMI — the Ikatan Cendekiawan Muslim se-Indonesia, the All-Indonesia Association of Muslim Intellectuals. B.J. Habibie, then Minister of Research and Technology and the president's closest technocratic ally, was appointed its first chairman. Robert Hefner's landmark study Civil Islam is the definitive account of ICMI's founding and trajectory. Hefner argues that Suharto's sponsorship of ICMI was a deliberate strategic calculation: by empowering and elevating Muslim professional and intellectual networks, the president sought both to shore up a new political constituency and to sideline the military officers aligned with Moerdani.27

ICMI (Ikatan Cendekiawan Muslim se-Indonesia) — the All-Indonesia Association of Muslim Intellectuals, founded 7 December 1990 with B.J. Habibie as chairman and Suharto's endorsement. ICMI brought together Muslim academics, professionals, and technocrats under state patronage and became the primary organisational vehicle of the late New Order's accommodation of political Islam.

The paradox Hefner identifies is striking: in trying to use Islamic civil society as a political tool, the New Order inadvertently strengthened the very pluralist, democratising tendencies within Indonesian Islam that would ultimately help bring it down. ICMI provided an organisational platform for Muslim intellectuals — including figures associated with NU's pluralist wing and with Muhammadiyah's reformist tradition — who used that platform to develop concepts of democratic accountability, civil rights, and rule of law that were incompatible with continued authoritarian rule.28 Abdurrahman Wahid (Gus Dur), chairman of NU from 1984, was a vocal critic of ICMI precisely because he saw Suharto's Islamic turn as both cynically political and dangerous — a legitimation of "communalist" religious politics that threatened Indonesia's religiously plural society. His alternative vision, of an Indonesian Islam that was culturally distinctive, democratically committed, and explicitly inclusive of non-Muslims, would prove influential after 1998.

The accommodation of political Islam also had implications for religious minorities. The five officially recognised religions — Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, and Buddhism — were inscribed in identity cards, and the Department of Religion managed their affairs through separate directorates. Indonesian citizens were required to list one of the five as their religion; the syncretic Javanese traditions of kebatinan and indigenous kepercayaan (spiritual beliefs) were legally recognized as cultural practices rather than religions, meaning their practitioners were expected to identify with one of the five official faiths. In practice this created sustained pressure on practitioners of indigenous traditions, particularly among animist communities in Kalimantan, Papua, and eastern Indonesia, to declare a formal religious affiliation.29 Christian missionary activity, long present in the Outer Islands, benefited from this pressure; so did Islamic dakwah (proselytization) organizations that grew significantly in the 1980s and 1990s. The late-New Order Islamisation did not simply intensify Muslim piety among the existing Muslim majority — it reshaped the religious landscape of the entire archipelago.

The People's Culture: Dangdut, Film, and Pramoedya's Banned Quartet

The New Order's cultural politics were not simply negative — not merely banning, suppressing, and censoring. The regime also actively promoted particular cultural forms that it believed served its developmental and integrative goals: state-sponsored batik exhibitions, regional dance performances on TVRI, and a calendar of national commemorations that wove the military narrative into civic identity. But the most consequential cultural developments of the New Order era often grew from below — from popular creativity, from the cassette economy, from underground literary networks — and the regime's relationship with them was one of ambivalence rather than control.

Nothing illustrates this better than dangdut. In the early 1970s, a new popular music genre crystallized from the encounter between Malay orkes melayu (orchestral popular music), North Indian film music, and electric guitar rock — a hybrid that was urban without being cosmopolitan, modern without being Western, and accessible to the new migrant working class that was filling Jakarta's kampungs and the industrial towns of Java's north coast. The genre's name came from the characteristic "dang-dut" percussive figure of its tabla-like hand drum, the kendang. Its dominant star was Rhoma Irama, a guitarist, singer, and actor who fused dangdut's sensuous rhythms with explicitly Islamic lyrics about faith, morality, and social justice, reframing what might have seemed like entertainment for the uneducated poor as a vehicle for Muslim values.30

William Frederick's 1982 study — the first serious English-language scholarly treatment of dangdut as a cultural form rather than a symptom — documents how Rhoma Irama's synthesis was both musically innovative and politically subversive in a specific sense: it gave a socially despised urban underclass a musical identity grounded in pride rather than shame, and placed Islamic morality in creative tension with the New Order's secular developmental ideology.30 TVRI's gatekeepers were ambivalent about dangdut — its associations with migrant workers, its sensuous dance culture, and its political undertones made it an uncomfortable fit with the station's mandate to project "national culture" — but its mass popularity meant that outright suppression was impossible. The result was a negotiated, unstable presence: dangdut appeared on TVRI but in formats that domesticated its edge, while the cassette market gave it a parallel circuit of distribution beyond state reach.31

Andrew Weintraub's cultural history of dangdut traces how the genre's symbolic meanings shifted across the New Order period: from a stigmatized "low-class" form in the 1970s and early 1980s, to an increasingly Islamicised medium in the late 1980s (as Rhoma Irama deepened his religious messaging and aligned his career with the broader Islamic revival), to a contested marker of national identity after 1998.32 The New Order's attempt to manage national culture through TVRI was only partially successful precisely because the cassette-based popular culture economy was decentralized, cheap to produce, and responsive to popular taste in ways the state broadcasting network was not.

If dangdut represented the vitality of popular culture working around the edges of state control, the case of Pramoedya Ananta Toer represented the state's most naked exercise of literary suppression. Pramoedya, born in Blora, Central Java, in 1925, had been one of Indonesia's most celebrated writers since the 1950s — a novelist, short-story writer, and essayist whose work engaged unflinchingly with the colonial experience, the revolution, and the social inequalities of the new republic. In the aftermath of October 1965 he was arrested without charge, held in Jakarta for several years, and in 1969 transported to the Buru Island prison camp in Maluku, where he remained for ten years without trial.33

On Buru, denied paper and writing materials for much of his detention, Pramoedya composed his most celebrated works orally — narrating them to fellow prisoners, who transcribed and hid them. The result was the four-volume Buru Quartet: Bumi Manusia ("This Earth of Mankind"), Anak Semua Bangsa ("Child of All Nations"), Jejak Langkah ("Footsteps"), and Rumah Kaca ("House of Glass"), a historical novel sequence set in the late-colonial Indies of the early twentieth century and centred on the story of Minke, a Javanese student who becomes one of the first Indonesian nationalist writers. The books were finally published after Pramoedya's release in 1979 by the small Jakarta press Hasta Mitra, beginning with Bumi Manusia in 1980.

The Attorney General's office banned the first two volumes by regulation in 1980 and moved against the remaining volumes as they appeared. Razif Bahari's scholarly reading of the quartet argues that the New Order's ban was not merely a political reflex but a recognition of what the books actually did: through the historical novel form itself, they gave Indonesian readers a counter-history in which the nationalist subject was not a military commander but a writer, a thinker, a product of cross-cultural encounter — a narrative that made the military-centred New Order version of nationalism look impoverished.34 Adrian Vickers, who uses Pramoedya's life as a structuring thread through his history of modern Indonesia, observes that the banning of a writer who had survived the Japanese occupation, the revolution, and years of Buru imprisonment, and who was by the 1980s attracting international literary recognition, said more about the New Order's insecurity than about any genuine threat Pramoedya's novels posed to public order.35

Pramoedya was released from formal detention in 1979 but kept under city arrest in Jakarta and forbidden to publish or speak publicly until 1992. In 1995, while still officially a banned author in his own country, he was awarded the Ramon Magsaysay Award for Journalism, Literature, and Creative Communication Arts — the most prestigious prize in Asia — and was repeatedly nominated for the Nobel Prize in Literature. The international recognition and domestic suppression operating simultaneously encapsulated the New Order's cultural contradictions with particular clarity: a government that claimed to be building a modern, educated nation was banning one of the most important literary voices that nation had produced.

The working class that consumed dangdut and that populated the urban kampungs in which both Pramoedya's fiction and Rhoma Irama's music found their largest audiences was itself a product of the New Order's industrialization — and itself a site of social management. Vedi Hadiz's political sociology of Indonesian labor documents how the New Order corporatized the labor movement through the state-controlled SPSI union federation, suppressed independent organizing, and kept wages structurally low as an explicit strategy for attracting foreign capital.36 The urban working class that the New Order had created through its industrial policy was thus kept simultaneously productive and politically demobilized — able to purchase cassette tapes and televisions, but not to strike, not to organize, not to vote for a party that represented their interests. The cultural vitality of the kampung and the political impotence of the factory floor were two faces of the same developmental bargain.

Pramoedya and the Politics of Memory
The suppression of Pramoedya Ananta Toer was part of a broader New Order campaign to control the past. The regime banned not only works by communist-affiliated writers but also scholarly histories that complicated its nationalist narrative, oral histories of 1965 that circulated in communities affected by the killings, and any cultural production that suggested the revolution had been a collective effort rather than a military achievement. When the New Order fell in 1998, one of the first symbolic acts of Reformasi was the lifting of the ban on Pramoedya's works. Bumi Manusia has since become one of the best-selling novels in Indonesian publishing history.

Summary

The social, cultural, and religious landscape of New Order Indonesia was shaped by four overlapping forces. First, the regime's developmentalism produced real and significant gains — in education, health, urbanization, and living standards — but embedded those gains in an authoritarian social architecture of gender ideology, cultural management, and labor control. The INPRES school-building programme and the Keluarga Berencana family-planning initiative were simultaneously genuine modernization projects and instruments of state penetration into households and bodies. Second, an official gender ideology — Julia Suryakusuma's "State Ibuism" — deployed Dharma Wanita and PKK to construct Indonesian womanhood around domesticity and male-derived status, while channeling women's organized energy into the service of developmental and reproductive goals. Third, a comprehensive system of cultural management — TVRI, the Pancasila Press, military historiography, and the Attorney General's banned book list — policed the boundaries of permissible thought, with the suppression of Pramoedya Ananta Toer as its most internationally visible expression. And fourth, an Islamic revival that neither the New Order nor its scholarly observers fully anticipated transformed the country's religious landscape from below, drove Suharto toward a strategic accommodation of political Islam through ICMI and the hajj, and set in motion institutional and intellectual developments — Robert Hefner's "civil Islam" — that would outlast the regime and help shape the democratic politics of the Reformasi era.

Review & Discussion Questions

35.1 Esther Duflo's study of the INPRES school-building programme found dramatic returns to education. Does a successful development programme justify the authoritarian system that delivered it? What are the limits of this argument?
35.2 Julia Suryakusuma argues that "State Ibuism" was a coherent ideological system, not merely a bundle of social policies. What evidence does she offer, and how convincing do you find the argument that Dharma Wanita and PKK were primarily instruments of ideological control rather than welfare delivery?
35.3 The New Order simultaneously excluded ethnic Chinese from political and civic life and became economically dependent on Chinese capital through the cukong system. How do scholars explain this paradox, and what does it suggest about the relationship between economic development and ethnic discrimination?
35.4 Robert Hefner argues that Suharto's sponsorship of ICMI inadvertently strengthened democratic and pluralist tendencies within Indonesian Islam. Is this a case of unintended consequences, or is there a deeper logic to how authoritarian regimes create the conditions for their own downfall?
35.5 Dangdut music was simultaneously popular, Islamically inflected, and politically ambiguous. What does its reception by both the state and the urban poor reveal about the limits of the New Order's cultural management project?
35.6 Pramoedya Ananta Toer composed the Buru Quartet while imprisoned without trial and had it banned after publication. What does the New Order's treatment of Pramoedya reveal about the relationship between literary culture and authoritarian politics? Why do authoritarian regimes so often perceive literature as a threat?
35.7 To what extent was the New Order's Islamic revival a product of the regime's own social transformations — urbanization, mass education, the new middle class — rather than an autonomous religious development? How would you distinguish these two explanations?
35.8 Women in Indonesia during the New Order period were simultaneously subjects of demographic engineering (KB), ideological domestication (State Ibuism), and a rapidly rising literacy rate. How do these three developments interact with each other? Which do you think had the most lasting effects?
