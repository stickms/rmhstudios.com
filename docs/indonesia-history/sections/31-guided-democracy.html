Learning Objectives

  • Explain why Indonesia's parliamentary system collapsed in the late 1950s and how Sukarno used regional rebellions as a pretext for centralizing power.
  • Describe the PRRI/Permesta rebellions of 1958, including covert US/CIA involvement and the consequences of their suppression.
  • Understand the proclamation of Guided Democracy in 1959, the reinstatement of the 1945 Constitution, and the ideological framework of NASAKOM and MANIPOL-USDEK.
  • Trace the growth of the PKI into one of the world's largest communist parties and analyze the deepening army–PKI rivalry under Guided Democracy.
  • Examine the West Irian campaign (Operation Trikora, 1961–62), the New York Agreement, and the contested 1969 Act of Free Choice.
  • Analyze the Konfrontasi campaign against Malaysia (1963–66) and its domestic as well as international consequences.
  • Assess the economic collapse and cult of personality that characterized Sukarno's final years, setting the stage for the crisis of 1965.

On 5 July 1959, President Sukarno walked onto the steps of the State Palace and read aloud a presidential decree dissolving the Constitutional Assembly and reinstating the 1945 Constitution. The act took less than ten minutes; its consequences would shape Indonesia for the next four decades. The parliamentary experiment that had consumed the republic since independence was swept away, replaced by what Sukarno called Demokrasi Terpimpin — Guided Democracy — a system in which the president himself would serve as the compass guiding the ship of state. What followed was one of the most dramatic and ultimately catastrophic periods in modern Indonesian history: a decade of ideological grandiloquence, Cold War adventurism, social mobilization, and economic deterioration, culminating in the mass violence of 1965–66 that killed hundreds of thousands and transformed the country beyond recognition.1

The Regional Rebellions: PRRI and Permesta, 1957–1961

The crisis that ended parliamentary rule did not begin in Jakarta but in the outer islands, where military commanders and civilian politicians had grown increasingly restive about what they saw as Javanese domination of the republic. The grievances were real and long-standing. Sumatra and Sulawesi together produced the lion's share of Indonesia's export earnings — rubber, copra, oil — yet they received in return what their leaders considered a meagre fraction of the national budget. The Dutch transfer of sovereignty in 1949 had concentrated administrative and military power in Java, and by the mid-1950s regional commanders had begun to act as quasi-independent governors, controlling local revenues and running their own economic arrangements with foreign companies.2

The deterioration of parliamentary politics in Jakarta accelerated the centrifugal forces. Herbert Feith's exhaustive study of the period documented how the cabinet system produced six governments between 1950 and 1957, each collapsing in factional manoeuvre before any consistent policy could take hold. The elections of 1955 — Indonesia's first and, until 1971, last free general elections — returned a legislature so fragmented among the PNI (Sukarno's Indonesian National Party), Masyumi (the modernist Muslim federation), Nahdlatul Ulama, and the PKI that effective coalition government became nearly impossible.3

By late 1956 several regional military commanders in Sumatra formed councils (dewan) that challenged central authority. The most significant was the Dewan Banteng (Buffalo Council) in West Sumatra under Lieutenant Colonel Ahmad Husein. When Jakarta failed to meet their demands — and Sukarno returned from a visit to the Soviet Union and China visibly energized by single-party discipline — the dissident colonels concluded that negotiation was futile. On 15 February 1958 Husein proclaimed a rival Pemerintah Revolusioner Republik Indonesia (PRRI — Revolutionary Government of the Republic of Indonesia) from Padang, demanding that the Djuanda Cabinet resign, that former Vice-President Mohammad Hatta and the Sultan of Yogyakarta form a new government, and that Sukarno return to a purely constitutional role. Sjafruddin Prawiranegara, the respected former finance minister and head of Bank Indonesia, accepted the position of PRRI prime minister. Two days later, on 17 February, the Permesta movement in North Sulawesi — launched the previous year under Colonel Ventje Sumual — formally pledged its alliance with the PRRI, transforming what might have been a Sumatran affair into a republic-wide rebellion.4

PRRI/Permesta — the twin regional rebellions of 1958. PRRI (Pemerintah Revolusioner Republik Indonesia, "Revolutionary Government of the Republic of Indonesia") was proclaimed in West Sumatra on 15 February 1958 by dissident officers and civilian politicians opposed to Jakarta's centralizing policies. Permesta (Piagam Perjuangan Semesta Alam, "Charter of Universal Struggle") was the parallel Sulawesi movement that joined the PRRI on 17 February. Both were militarily suppressed by late 1961.

What made the PRRI/Permesta crisis international was covert United States involvement. The Eisenhower administration, alarmed by Sukarno's non-alignment and his apparent toleration of the PKI, authorized the CIA to support the rebel forces. In the months before the PRRI proclamation the CIA was already preparing weapons packages for Sumatran dissidents; by spring 1958 it had supplied B-26 bombers and P-51 Mustang fighters — operated under the cover of the CIA proprietary Civil Air Transport (CAT) — which formed the insurgent air force (AUREV) based at Manado airfield. Weapons, substantial funds, and an international cast of CIA operatives from the Philippines, Taiwan, and elsewhere flowed into the rebel-controlled zones.5

The CIA operation unravelled spectacularly on 18 May 1958. An American pilot, Allen Pope, flying bombing missions for the rebels, was shot down over the Moluccas. He was captured carrying not only his American military identification but operational paperwork that left no room for deniability. The exposure of direct US involvement proved catastrophic for the rebels' cause. Washington, forced to acknowledge the debacle, abruptly curtailed its support; Sukarno, at once infuriated and emboldened, exploited the episode to rally nationalist sentiment and denounce imperialist interference.6 Audrey and George Kahin's study of the episode, drawing on declassified documents, concluded that the operation "backfired disastrously," strengthening the very forces the United States had sought to contain and accelerating Indonesia's turn toward Soviet arms.7

The central government's military response was swift and ultimately overwhelming. Under General Abdul Haris Nasution, army operations pushed the PRRI out of the major Sumatran cities by mid-1958. The last rebel bands were driven into the jungle. Operation Callback in late 1960 persuaded many officers to surrender; Ahmad Husein himself turned himself in on 29 May 1961, followed by former Masyumi leader Mohammad Natsir on 28 September 1961. The defeat of the regional rebellions had lasting structural consequences. Masyumi and the Indonesian Socialist Party (PSI), both tainted by association with the rebels, were subsequently banned by Sukarno in August 1960, removing two of the republic's most capable reformist political formations. The army's successful campaign, moreover, gave it both the military cohesion and the enhanced political legitimacy that would prove crucial in subsequent years. And the failure of American covert action pushed Indonesia into a deeper military relationship with the Soviet Union, which by 1961 had supplied Indonesia with submarines, jet aircraft, and warships worth billions of dollars.8

The Decree of 5 July 1959: Sukarno's Constitutional Revolution

The regional rebellions provided Sukarno with both a pretext and an opportunity. Under the parliamentary system he had chafed at a largely ceremonial role; the Provisional Constitution of 1950 reserved executive power to the cabinet, leaving the president as a figurehead. Sukarno had been publicly urging a "return to the spirit of 1945" — meaning the original constitution drafted in the final days of the Japanese occupation, which concentrated power in a strong executive — since at least 1956. The political deadlock of the Constitutional Assembly (Konstituante), elected in 1955 to draw up a permanent constitution, gave him his moment. The assembly had been paralyzed by an unresolvable dispute between those who wanted Pancasila as the ideological basis of the state and those — primarily Masyumi and allied Islamic parties — who insisted on Islamic law as the foundation. Neither side could muster the necessary two-thirds majority.9

Daniel Lev's authoritative account of the transition demonstrates that Sukarno carefully cultivated army support before acting. Army Chief of Staff General Nasution — himself distrustful of parliamentary parties and sympathetic to strong executive rule — signalled his acquiescence. On 5 July 1959, citing the constitutional deadlock and the national emergency, Sukarno issued Presidential Decree No. 150 dissolving the Konstituante and unilaterally reinstating the 1945 Constitution. Under that document, the president was both head of state and head of government, appointed the cabinet, and could rule by decree when the People's Consultative Assembly (MPR) was not in session. The cabinet was no longer responsible to parliament but solely to the president. Guided Democracy had begun.10

The international scholarship has debated whether the Sukarno of 1959 was a revolutionary democrat frustrated by factional paralysis or an aspiring autocrat who exploited a crisis to secure personal power. M.C. Ricklefs, in his standard history, inclines toward a nuanced middle position: Sukarno's frustrations with parliamentary government were genuine, and there were real structural problems in the system, but the decree nonetheless "swept away the last formal constraints on presidential power" and inaugurated a period in which dissent was progressively criminalised.11 J.D. Legge's political biography of Sukarno portrays a man who genuinely believed he had a unique capacity to articulate the national will — that parliamentary bargaining between parties was alien to Indonesian political culture and that the goal-oriented unity he projected from the podium was authentically more democratic in a deeper sense than Westminster procedure. Whatever the sincerity of that belief, its practical effect was one-man rule increasingly buttressed by coercion.12

Demokrasi Terpimpin (Guided Democracy) — the political system proclaimed by Sukarno after the decree of 5 July 1959, nominally based on the 1945 Constitution but in practice concentrating power in the president, with the army and the PKI as the two major extraparliamentary power bases. It lasted until Suharto's takeover in 1966–67.

NASAKOM, MANIPOL-USDEK, and the Ideology of Guided Democracy

Sukarno was above all an orator and an ideologue; he felt that every political system required a philosophical foundation, and he was prolific in supplying one. Guided Democracy rested on two interlocking ideological constructs that he elaborated between 1959 and 1963: NASAKOM and MANIPOL-USDEK.

NASAKOM was an acronym for the three main currents Sukarno believed must be united for Indonesia to function: Nasionalisme (nationalism), Agama (religion), and Komunisme (communism). The concept was not entirely new — Sukarno had been preaching the unity of these three streams since the 1920s — but under Guided Democracy it became official doctrine, a formula meant to reconcile the four major political forces: secular nationalists, Islamic organizations, the Indonesian Communist Party, and the armed forces.13 NASAKOM was institutionalized in government appointments, in the composition of advisory bodies, and in the ceremonial life of the state, with representatives of each stream routinely included in formal occasions.

NASAKOM — the Sukarno-era ideological concept combining Nasionalisme (nationalism), Agama (religion, primarily Islam), and Komunisme (communism). It served as the philosophical foundation of Guided Democracy, projecting national unity by formally incorporating communist as well as Islamist representation within the political order.

MANIPOL-USDEK was a layered acronym encoding the entire political programme. The outer term, MANIPOL, stood for Manifesto Politik — the Political Manifesto that Sukarno proclaimed in his Independence Day speech of 17 August 1959, entitled "Rediscovery of Our Revolution" (Penemuan Kembali Revolusi Kita). Within that manifesto the five core principles were compressed into USDEK:

  • U — Undang-Undang Dasar 1945 (the 1945 Constitution)
  • S — Sosialisme ala Indonesia (Indonesian-style socialism)
  • D — Demokrasi Terpimpin (Guided Democracy)
  • E — Ekonomi Terpimpin (Guided Economy)
  • K — Kepribadian Indonesia (Indonesian Personality/Identity)

The Provisional People's Consultative Assembly (MPRS) formally adopted MANIPOL-USDEK as the State Policy Guidelines in 1960 through TAP MPRS No. 1/1960, making it the binding ideological framework of the republic.14 Its practical effects ranged from the relatively benign — a renewed emphasis on Indonesian cultural identity in education — to the suppressive. Civil servants, teachers, and military officers were required to study and pass examinations on MANIPOL-USDEK. Independent newspapers that deviated from the political line were shut down. The political manifesto was to be "memorized and discussed by all students," and its recitation became a ritual of public life akin to a secular catechism.15

MANIPOL-USDEK — the official state ideology of Guided Democracy, deriving from Sukarno's 1959 Independence Day "Political Manifesto." The acronym USDEK encoded five principles: the 1945 Constitution, Indonesian-style socialism, Guided Democracy, Guided Economy, and Indonesian Identity. It was mandatory study for all civil servants and students.

The PKI's Ascent and the Army's Growing Unease

The central domestic drama of Guided Democracy was the extraordinary growth of the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI) and the deepening antagonism between it and the Indonesian Army — an antagonism that Sukarno could manage for a decade but not indefinitely.

Under the leadership of Dipa Nusantara Aidit, who became General Secretary in January 1951, the PKI underwent a transformation from a nearly destroyed organization — it had been crushed after its failed 1926–27 revolts and again decimated by internal purges — into a mass movement of extraordinary scope. Membership grew from approximately 3,000–5,000 in 1950 to 165,000 in 1954 and roughly 1.5 million by 1959. By 1965, on the eve of the catastrophe that would obliterate it, the PKI had approximately three million card-carrying members, making it the third-largest communist party in the world after those of the Soviet Union and China — and the largest communist party that had never held state power.16 Aidit's strategy was to build not just a party but a social ecosystem: the PKI maintained affiliated mass organizations that between them claimed to encompass a fifth of the Indonesian population, including the Central All-Indonesian Workers' Organization (SOBSI), the People's Youth (Pemuda Rakyat), the Women's Movement (Gerwani), and the Peasants' Front of Indonesia.17

Rex Mortimer's scholarly analysis of the PKI under Guided Democracy identifies the party's fundamental strategic choice as "national democratic" rather than revolutionary: Aidit concluded that seizing state power by force was premature and that the correct strategy was to embed the party deeply within Sukarno's coalition, supporting NASAKOM and building mass organizations that would shift the balance of forces gradually in the PKI's direction. The party welcomed Guided Democracy enthusiastically as a "multi-class united front" compatible with its immediate objectives, and it provided Sukarno with consistently loyal mass mobilization.18

The army viewed this strategy with deepening alarm. Harold Crouch's landmark study of civil-military relations identifies the period 1960–65 as one in which the army's institutional interests were directly threatened by PKI expansion. The communists' rural organizing — and particularly their campaign for implementation of the 1960 Basic Agrarian Law, which threatened the local landholding interests of village officials often linked to the army — was experienced as an assault on the social order the military saw itself as protecting. In 1964 the PKI adopted what it called "unilateral actions" (aksi sepihak), organizing peasants to occupy land in anticipation of land reform before legal processes had run their course. These actions brought violent clashes with landowners and their allies, and the army moved to suppress them, regarding the PKI's rural mobilization as a direct challenge to its own authority.19

Sukarno's role was to manage this triangle — army, PKI, and himself — by ensuring that neither of the two major forces felt secure enough to move against the other without his mediation. As long as he remained the indispensable balancing figure, his position was safe. But the system required ever more frantic juggling: each concession to the PKI alarmed the army; each sign of army displeasure had to be compensated with concessions to the communists. By 1964–65 the balance had become precarious, and both sides were preparing, in different ways, for a confrontation that could not indefinitely be deferred.20

West Irian Liberated: Operation Trikora and the New York Agreement

Running alongside the domestic political drama was a foreign policy campaign that Sukarno pursued with genuine nationalistic passion: the recovery of the western half of New Guinea, which the Dutch had retained at the time of the 1949 sovereignty transfer. The Dutch had justified their retention on ethnographic grounds — the Papuan people were Melanesian, culturally distinct from the Malay-Indonesians of the archipelago — and on the stated intention of eventually guiding the territory to self-determination. Indonesian nationalists rejected both arguments as colonial pretexts, insisting that the boundary of the Netherlands East Indies defined the boundary of the Indonesian nation, whatever its peoples' ethnic composition. West New Guinea — which Indonesians called Irian Barat (West Irian) — thus became the one piece of colonial territory that remained outside the republic, and its recovery became a consuming national obsession and a running wound in Indonesian-Dutch relations.21

Through the 1950s Indonesia pursued the claim through every available diplomatic channel: bilateral negotiation, the United Nations, the Non-Aligned Movement. All failed. In August 1960 Sukarno broke off diplomatic relations with the Netherlands. In December 1961, addressing a mass rally in Yogyakarta, he issued the Tri Komando Rakyat — the People's Triple Command, known by its acronym Trikora — demanding that the Netherlands be prevented from establishing a puppet state in West Irian, that the Indonesian flag be raised there, and that the people be mobilized for its liberation.22 Indonesia had by this point assembled, largely from Soviet suppliers, one of the most formidable military forces in Southeast Asia: submarines, Tu-16 jet bombers, MiG-21 fighters, and an array of warships representing a Soviet military investment estimated at some $2.5 billion — substantially larger than Soviet military aid to any other Third World country at the time.23

The military campaign that followed was modest by comparison to the rhetoric. Infiltration operations — paratroop drops and seaborne landings — began in early 1962, and a naval engagement in the Arafura Sea in January 1962 resulted in the sinking of an Indonesian torpedo boat. These operations were less militarily decisive than diplomatically useful: they signalled Indonesian seriousness and alarmed both the Dutch and the Americans, who had no desire to see the crisis push Indonesia further toward Moscow. US diplomat Ellsworth Bunker mediated urgently between the parties, and on 15 August 1962 the New York Agreement was signed at UN headquarters. The Netherlands agreed to transfer administration to a UN Temporary Executive Authority (UNTEA) on 1 October 1962, with transfer to Indonesia to follow on 1 May 1963. The agreement specified that Indonesia would be bound to give the Papuan population the right to choose their political future by 1969, "in accordance with international practice."24

The Act of Free Choice, 1969: Controversy and Scholarly Debate

The commitment to eventual self-determination became the Act of Free Choice (Penentuan Pendapat Rakyat, or Pepera), held between 14 July and 2 August 1969. What actually took place bore almost no resemblance to what the New York Agreement had envisioned, and the controversy it generated has not been resolved in the decades since.

The fundamental problem was procedure. Rather than a universal adult plebiscite, the Indonesian authorities — under the direction of General Sarwo Edhi Wibowo — assembled 1,025 representatives drawn from the territory's population of roughly 800,000 and arranged for them to vote on Indonesia's behalf. These representatives were not elected; they were personally selected by Indonesian officials. The vote itself, held in a series of regional meetings, took the form of public show of hands after participants had been coached on the expected statements. Reuters journalist Hugh Lunn, who was present, documented that selected voters faced threats of violence against themselves and their families, that protesters outside meeting places were "thrown into army trucks and driven away," and that Lunn himself was threatened at gunpoint for photographing proceedings. The result was unanimous: all 1,025 representatives voted to remain with Indonesia.25

The UN's role was equivocal at best. Its special representative, Fernando Ortiz-Sanz, was entirely dependent on Indonesian authorities for transport, housing, and access; his staff were followed everywhere by officials. His report to the Secretary-General acknowledged "an act carried out in accordance with Indonesian practice" — a notably careful phrasing that fell conspicuously short of certifying compliance with international standards. The UN General Assembly, in Resolution 2504 of November 1969, "took note" of the result without endorsing its legitimacy — a diplomatic formulation that reflects the ambiguity with which the international community regarded the process.26

Scholarly assessments have been almost uniformly critical. John Saltford's 2003 study, drawing on British, Dutch, American, and UN archival sources, concludes that the process amounted to a systematic betrayal of the Papuan population by all parties — Indonesia, the UN, the United States (which prioritized strategic interests over self-determination), and the Netherlands (which, exhausted by the affair, accepted any face-saving formula). The book's subtitle, "The Anatomy of a Betrayal," reflects that verdict.27 Pieter Drooglever's monumental 2009 Dutch government-commissioned study — based on three decades of research across multiple national archives — reached the same conclusion: the act as conducted was fundamentally incompatible with the right to self-determination guaranteed in the 1962 agreement, and a substantial majority of Papuans, if given a genuine choice, would have voted for independence.28 These scholarly verdicts do not carry legal weight within Indonesian domestic law, and Indonesia's official position remains that the Act of Free Choice settled the question definitively. The conflict between that position and the ongoing demands of the Free Papua Movement (OPM, Organisasi Papua Merdeka), which has maintained a low-level insurgency since the late 1960s, is addressed further in Chapter 34.

Historiographical note: The Act of Free Choice remains one of the most contested events in modern Indonesian history. Indonesian scholarship and official histories treat it as a legitimate exercise of self-determination that closed the chapter on colonialism. International scholars — particularly Saltford, Drooglever, and the political scientists working with the International Coalition for Papua — treat it as a fraudulent process that legitimised annexation. Readers should be aware that sources originating from Indonesian government and educational institutions will reflect the former view, while most Western academic scholarship reflects the latter. This chapter presents both positions but notes that the archival evidence gathered by Saltford and Drooglever has not, to date, been seriously contested on factual grounds.

Konfrontasi: The War Against Malaysia, 1963–1966

Even as the West Irian campaign was reaching its diplomatic resolution, Sukarno was pivoting to a new foreign policy adventure: opposition to the formation of Malaysia. The emergence of Malaysia was itself a product of British decolonization — the proposed merger of the Malay Federation with Singapore and the British Borneo territories of Sarawak and North Borneo (Sabah) into a single state, formally proclaimed on 16 September 1963. Indonesia's opposition was grounded in a mix of strategic calculation, nationalist ideology, and Sukarno's personal political needs.

The immediate trigger was the Brunei Revolt of December 1962, an armed uprising against the proposed federation led by the Partai Rakyat Brunei with at least partial Indonesian encouragement. Its suppression by British forces did not quell Indonesian hostility. On 20 January 1963 Foreign Minister Subandrio formally announced a policy of Konfrontasi — confrontation — against what Sukarno called "Malaysia," a neologism he used mockingly to suggest that the new state was a British-sponsored neocolonial project designed to extend the Commonwealth's strategic footprint in the region. "We will gobble Malaysia raw," Sukarno told a mass rally in what became one of his most quoted speeches.29

Military operations began with the infiltration of Sarawak. On 12 April 1963 an Indonesian-backed force attacked the police station at Tebedu in the First Division of Sarawak, marking the first recorded military incident of the confrontation. British, Australian, and New Zealand Commonwealth forces were rapidly reinforced in Borneo, and what followed was a sustained jungle war of company- and platoon-sized operations on both sides of the Sarawak-Kalimantan border. The Commonwealth forces — under the doctrine of proportional deterrence that their commanders called "Claret" — carried out secret cross-border raids into Kalimantan that were never publicly acknowledged, so as to avoid provoking open war while demonstrating that Indonesian infiltration was not cost-free.30

On 3 May 1964, following Malaysia's election to the UN Security Council (an event that provoked Sukarno to withdraw Indonesia from the United Nations in January 1965 — one of the very few times a member state has done so), Sukarno proclaimed the Dwikora (People's Dual Command), escalating the confrontation. By August 1964 Indonesian paratroopers and seaborne raiding parties were operating in Peninsular Malaya itself, attacking targets near Labis and Pontian. These operations achieved little militarily but confirmed in the minds of Malaysia, Britain, and their allies that Sukarno was prepared to continue indefinitely.31

At the institutional level, the Konfrontasi was generating a paradox that Harold Crouch identifies as crucial to understanding the 1965 crisis: the Indonesian Army high command, led by General Ahmad Yani, was privately obstructing the campaign. Convinced that a prolonged external military adventure would dissipate the army's resources and attention at precisely the moment when the domestic threat from the PKI demanded military vigilance, army leaders dragged their feet on escalation, maintained secret back-channels to Malaysian and British interlocutors, and resisted the creation of a "volunteer" force that would have placed civilian — including PKI-affiliated — fighters outside army command structures. The Konfrontasi thus deepened rather than papered over the army-PKI split, because both sides understood that the conflict over the "volunteers" was really a conflict over whether the PKI would gain independent armed capacity.32

The Konfrontasi ended with surprising speed once Sukarno's power was broken. Formal peace negotiations began in May 1966, and a peace agreement recognizing Malaysia was signed on 11 August 1966. The episode cost Indonesia its international standing, accelerated the departure of foreign capital, and contributed to the economic deterioration it had no resources to address — while achieving none of its stated objectives.

Economic Collapse, the Cult of Sukarno, and the Countdown to 1965

Throughout the years of revolutionary rhetoric and foreign policy adventurism, the Indonesian economy was quietly disintegrating. The causes were structural and policy-induced simultaneously. In 1957–58 the government nationalized the Dutch-owned enterprises that had formed the backbone of the colonial economy — banks, plantations, trading companies. The nationalizations were popular as an assertion of sovereignty but economically catastrophic: managers with knowledge were expelled, enterprises were placed under military officers with no commercial experience, and capital fled. Export revenues, already insufficient, began to shrink as nationalized plantations fell into disrepair and the infrastructure needed to get commodities to market deteriorated from lack of investment.33

The "Guided Economy" of MANIPOL-USDEK proved in practice to be neither guided nor economical. State enterprises ran at massive losses, financed by the central bank's printing press. The money supply expanded; prices followed. By the early 1960s Indonesia was experiencing significant inflation; by 1964–65 that inflation had become hyperinflation, with official estimates of annual price increases exceeding one thousand percent at the time of Sukarno's displacement from power. Export earnings from rubber had fallen to roughly twelve percent of pre-war levels, and oil — which would become Indonesia's economic lifeline under the New Order — was contributing only a fraction of what its reserves should have generated. Factories operated at minimal capacity for lack of spare parts and raw materials. The developmental promises of independence — schools, hospitals, roads, rising living standards — went largely unfulfilled for the majority of Indonesians, particularly outside Java.34

Against this backdrop of material deprivation, Sukarno invested ever more heavily in symbolic politics, in what his critics called an elaborate personality cult and his admirers saw as revolutionary mobilization. He was "Bung Karno" (Brother Sukarno), the "Father of Indonesian Nationalism," the "Mouthpiece of the Indonesian People" (Penyambung Lidah Rakyat Indonesia) — a title he himself coined. The Provisional People's Consultative Assembly, in a gesture of sycophancy that alarmed even some of Sukarno's supporters, named him "President for Life" in 1963. His collected speeches ran to dozens of volumes, mandatory in schools and universities. Massive portraits hung in every government office. Monuments, stadiums, and the Hotel Indonesia rose in Jakarta with money the country could not afford. The 1962 Asian Games, hosted in the capital — from which Indonesia excluded Israel and Taiwan in what the Olympic movement considered a political provocation — were, in Sukarno's framing, proof that the revolution was young, vigorous, and victorious.35

The foreign policy of Konfrontasi and the domestic cult of revolutionary energy served a specific political function: they kept the question of Indonesia's direction in a perpetual state of mobilization, thereby forestalling the moment of reckoning when the army and the PKI would have to resolve their incompatible visions of the republic's future. Sukarno himself was not oblivious to the contradiction he was managing. He coined the phrase NASAKOM but knew that its components were not genuinely reconciled. He championed the PKI against army pressures while depending on the army's guns for his own security. He orchestrated mass demonstrations but feared spontaneous violence he could not control. The system held as long as his charisma was sufficient to project the illusion of unity — and it was sufficient for longer than most outside observers predicted. But it could not hold indefinitely.36

The Triangle of Power and the Road to Crisis

By 1964–65 the three-cornered tension among Sukarno, the PKI, and the army had reached a point of structural instability that made some form of violent resolution difficult to avoid. Each of the three actors had reasons to escalate, and none had sufficient incentive to de-escalate.

The PKI's position looked strong in numerical terms — three million members, affiliated organizations encompassing tens of millions — but was constitutionally vulnerable, since it held no significant armed forces of its own. Aidit pressed for the formation of a "fifth force" that would arm workers and peasants alongside the four conventional armed services, ostensibly for the Konfrontasi but in practice as a militia that would give the PKI military capacity independent of the army. Sukarno showed interest; the army high command made clear that it regarded this proposal as an existential threat and would oppose it by whatever means necessary.37

Within the army, a group of officers — later designated as the "Council of Generals" in PKI and radical nationalist narrative — shared intelligence reports and concerns about PKI penetration of the armed forces, of the civil service, and of the presidential household. Factionalism within the army complicated the picture further: a group of "palace" officers loyal above all to Sukarno (and open to accommodation with the PKI) coexisted uneasily with the mainstream corps under Yani, who was anti-communist but also constitutionally loyal to the commander-in-chief. The two groups' rivalry introduced an additional dimension of uncertainty into an already volatile situation.38

Sukarno's own health added urgency. He suffered serious kidney failure in the course of 1965, and rumours of his imminent death circulated widely. For the PKI, Sukarno's survival was essential — without his protection, the party was exposed. For the army, his death offered the possibility of reconstruction on terms favourable to the military. For both sides, the consciousness that the patron holding the system together might soon be gone created powerful incentives to act before, rather than after, that event.

In early August 1965 Sukarno himself, in a speech to a PKI congress, publicly denounced the "Council of Generals" as a counter-revolutionary conspiracy and warned that traitors within the army were preparing a coup against the revolution. Whether this was based on real intelligence, fabricated intelligence supplied by the PKI, or Sukarno's own reading of the situation is a question that scholars continue to debate — and that directly intersects with the still-contested interpretations of the events of 30 September–1 October 1965, which are examined in Chapter 31. What is not in doubt is that by the final months of 1965 the Indonesian political system was operating under conditions of extreme stress, mutual suspicion, and barely suppressed violence — the product of six years of Guided Democracy's institutionalized tensions coming to term simultaneously.39

Summary

The period from 1957 to 1965 saw Indonesia undergo a fundamental political transformation. The regional rebellions of the PRRI and Permesta — aided covertly by the CIA but ultimately crushed by the central army — discredited parliamentary politics and handed Sukarno the pretext for his decree of 5 July 1959, which reinstated the 1945 Constitution and inaugurated Guided Democracy. Under this system, power was formally concentrated in the president, with the army and the PKI as the two major extraparliamentary forces contending for influence within a NASAKOM framework. The PKI grew to become the world's largest non-ruling communist party, while the army watched its expansion with growing alarm. Sukarno launched the West Irian campaign (Operation Trikora, 1961–62), recovering the territory through the New York Agreement; the subsequent "Act of Free Choice" of 1969 — conducted through 1,025 hand-picked representatives with no free vote — has been almost universally condemned by international scholarship as a fraudulent exercise that denied Papuans genuine self-determination. The Konfrontasi against Malaysia (1963–66) consumed resources, isolated Indonesia internationally, and deepened army-PKI tensions. Throughout, the economy deteriorated into hyperinflation while Sukarno invested in monuments, foreign adventures, and ideological spectacle. By 1965 the triangle of power — Sukarno, army, PKI — had become so unstable that a violent resolution was widely anticipated. Chapter 31 examines how that resolution came.
