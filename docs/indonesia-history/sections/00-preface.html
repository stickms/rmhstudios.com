This book tells the history of one of the world's largest, most populous, and least understood nations — and of the archipelago that long preceded it. It is written for the reader who wants more than a traveler's sketch: a connected account, from the first hominins of Java to the politics of the present, that takes the islands seriously as a center of world history rather than a periphery of it.

How to read this book

The book runs chronologically, in ten parts. Parts I–II carry the story from deep prehistory through the Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms; Parts III–V cover the arrival of Islam, the European trading companies, and the colonial state; Parts VI–IX trace the national awakening, the revolution, and the modern Republic down to the 2024 election and after. Part X then revisits the whole span through themes — culture, religion, economy, diaspora, gender, environment, and the writing of history itself. You can read straight through, or use the chronology, glossary, and index in the back matter to navigate. Each chapter opens with learning objectives and closes with a summary and review questions.

A note on names, dates, and spelling

Indonesian spelling has changed over time, and many names appear in more than one form. This book uses the modern standard — Sukarno and Suharto rather than the older Soekarno and Soeharto, Srivijaya rather than Sriwijaya — while noting older forms where a reader is likely to meet them in other works. Dates use CE/BCE, with c. ("circa") for approximations. Foreign terms are italicized on first use and defined in the glossary.

On sources and historiography

History is an argument about evidence, and the evidence for the Indonesian past is uneven: rich for the colonial and modern eras, thinner and more contested for the classical kingdoms, and genuinely fragmentary for prehistory. This book is a synthesis. It draws on the standard scholarly histories — among them the works of M. C. Ricklefs, Adrian Vickers, Anthony Reid, Jean Gelman Taylor, and R. E. Elson — and on primary inscriptions, chronicles, and archaeological reports, and it cites those sources in the notes to each chapter. Where historians disagree — over the reach of Majapahit, the scale and authorship of the 1965–66 killings, the events of 1998, or the status of Papua — the book presents the debate rather than asserting a single verdict. Readers are encouraged to follow the notes outward into that literature.

This is a survey written to be accurate and honest about its limits, not a work of original archival research. Its ambition is to give a reader a trustworthy, connected, and genuinely deep picture of Indonesian history — and a map of where to read further.
