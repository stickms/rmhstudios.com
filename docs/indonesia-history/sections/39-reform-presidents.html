Learning Objectives

  • Trace how B. J. Habibie transformed Indonesia's political landscape through press freedom, party liberalization, and the East Timor referendum, and assess why his accountability speech was rejected.
  • Evaluate Abdurrahman Wahid's achievements in pluralism — especially for Chinese Indonesians and Confucians — alongside the governance failures and constitutional crisis that ended his presidency.
  • Explain how Megawati Sukarnoputri stabilized Indonesia's economy, created the KPK anti-corruption agency, and responded to the 2002 Bali bombings.
  • Analyse Jemaah Islamiyah's origins, ideology, and major attacks, and assess the counterterrorism architecture — including Detachment 88 — built to dismantle it.
  • Assess Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono's two terms: the Aceh peace agreement, macroeconomic growth, the anti-corruption court, and the competing scholarly verdicts of "stability" versus "stagnation."

Between Suharto's resignation on 21 May 1998 and Joko Widodo's inauguration in October 2014, four presidents governed Indonesia across sixteen momentous years. None of them was inevitable. B. J. Habibie was Suharto's vice president, appointed barely ten weeks before becoming the country's third head of state. Abdurrahman Wahid was a half-blind Islamic cleric who could barely read a text, elected by parliamentary manoeuvre rather than popular vote. Megawati Sukarnoputri was the daughter of Indonesia's founding president, who reached the highest office only because her predecessor was impeached. Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono was a retired general who became the first Indonesian president chosen by direct popular ballot. Together, these four shaped what Indonesians came to call the Reformasi era: a decade and a half of democratic construction, economic recovery, ethnic and religious reconciliation — and of terrorist bombs, corruption scandals, and the stubborn legacies of authoritarian rule.

Engineering the Democratic Opening: Habibie, 1998–1999

Bacharuddin Jusuf Habibie was, in many ways, an unlikely democrat. An aerospace engineer who had spent decades building aircraft and bureaucratic empires for Suharto, he was widely regarded as the New Order's most loyal technocrat — a man who owed his career entirely to presidential patronage. When Suharto named him vice president in March 1998, at the height of the Asian financial crisis, the appointment was widely understood as a favour rather than a succession plan.1 Yet when student protesters forced Suharto from the palace on 21 May 1998, it was Habibie who took the oath of office — and within months he had dismantled more of Suharto's authoritarian architecture than anyone expected.

The speed of the transformation was striking. In June 1998, within weeks of taking power, Habibie lifted the Suharto-era restrictions on political parties, ended censorship, and took the extraordinary step of dissolving the Ministry of Information — the instrument through which the New Order had controlled the press for three decades.2 Political prisoners were released, including democracy activist Sri Bintang Pamungkas, labour leader Muchtar Pakpahan, and East Timorese resistance figure Xanana Gusmão. A Presidential Instruction issued in September 1998 formally prohibited the use of the terms pribumi and non-pribumi — the binary that had long coded discrimination against ethnic Chinese Indonesians into official language.

The legislative architecture of democratization followed swiftly. In February 1999 the Habibie administration enacted a Political Parties Law that swept away the New Order's three-party system: under the new law, political parties were free to form, were not required to adopt Pancasila as their sole ideology, and could compete nationally without government supervision.3 The result was an explosion of political activity: forty-eight parties registered to compete in the June 1999 elections. In May 1999 the government passed the Regional Autonomy Law (Law No. 22/1999), beginning the process of decentralizing authority from Jakarta to the provinces and districts that would reshape Indonesian governance in the following decade. R. William Liddle, one of the most careful scholars of Indonesian democracy, argued in an early assessment that Habibie had made at least three transformative decisions — party liberalization, holding genuine elections, and the East Timor referendum — any one of which would have been remarkable for an incumbent who benefited from the existing order.4

The press law was particularly consequential. Law No. 40/1999, signed by Habibie on 23 September 1999 and studied in detail by media scholar Janet Steele, established a right to publish without a government licence and explicitly prohibited censorship. It was, Steele argues, not merely a decree from above but the product of intense negotiation between journalists, civil-society groups, and officials who understood that a free press was inseparable from the democratic project they were attempting to build.5 Within months, hundreds of new newspapers, magazines, and radio stations had appeared; by 1999 Indonesia had one of the most open media environments in Southeast Asia.

Reformasi — the post-1998 era of democratic reform in Indonesia following Suharto's fall; broadly denotes the political, legal, and institutional transformation begun under Habibie and carried forward by his successors. See also the Glossary.

East Timor: The Vote That Changed Everything

Of all Habibie's decisions, the most consequential — and the most personally costly — was the one he made about East Timor. Indonesia had occupied the former Portuguese colony since 1975, and the occupation had been a running sore in relations with the West for more than two decades: roughly 100,000 Timorese had died under Indonesian rule, and the Santa Cruz massacre of 1991, in which Indonesian soldiers killed at least 250 peaceful demonstrators, had been filmed and broadcast worldwide. Habibie, unlike Suharto, was willing to regard East Timor as a problem to be solved rather than a status quo to be defended.

On 27 January 1999 Habibie announced, without consulting the military high command, that Indonesia would offer East Timor a choice between special autonomy within the republic and independence. The announcement startled virtually everyone: politicians, diplomats, and generals alike had assumed that the new government would at most tinker around the edges of the occupation policy. Scholars have debated why Habibie made the offer. Some read it as a genuine democratic impulse — a desire to resolve a costly international liability that was poisoning Indonesia's reform credentials. Others argue it was a calculated gamble, made on the assumption that most East Timorese would accept a generous autonomy package and that Habibie would gain credit for the offer without losing the territory. Whatever his motivation, the gamble failed: on 30 August 1999 the United Nations Mission in East Timor (UNAMET) organized the referendum, and 78.5 per cent of Timorese voters rejected autonomy and chose independence.6

The result triggered a wave of retaliatory violence by Indonesian-backed militias. In the weeks following the vote, some 60 to 80 per cent of East Timor's public and private infrastructure was destroyed; hundreds of people were killed; and an estimated 250,000 to 400,000 were displaced. Habibie, under intense international pressure, finally authorized the entry of a UN-mandated multinational force (INTERFET), led by Australia, on 15 September 1999. The crisis demonstrated both the limits of Habibie's authority over the military — generals who had opposed the referendum watched the violence before acting — and the depth of Indonesia's political culture of impunity. Kirsten Schulze, whose contemporaneous study remains the sharpest account of the crisis's domestic resonance, argues that the East Timor outcome was the single greatest factor in delegitimizing Habibie's presidency in the eyes of nationalist politicians and military officers.7

The political consequences came swiftly. At the MPR general session in October 1999, Habibie delivered his accountability speech — the constitutionally required report by a president to the assembly at the end of his mandate. It was rejected by 355 votes to 322, a decisive enough margin to signal that his political support had collapsed. Recognizing that he could not win re-election, Habibie withdrew his presidential candidacy.8 His presidency had lasted 517 days — the shortest in Indonesian history — but its consequences would shape Indonesian politics for a generation. The free elections of June 1999, which Habibie had convened, had returned forty-eight parties to the parliament, with Megawati Sukarnoputri's Indonesian Democratic Party of Struggle (PDI-P) winning the largest share of the popular vote — 33.7 per cent and 153 seats in the DPR (House of Representatives) — followed by Golkar with 120 seats and the National Awakening Party (PKB) with 51 seats.9

MPR (Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat) — the People's Consultative Assembly, Indonesia's supreme legislative body under the 1945 Constitution as it stood before the 1999–2002 amendments; it comprised the DPR (House of Representatives) plus appointed military and regional representatives. Until 2004, the MPR elected the president. Post-reform amendments converted it into a bicameral body and transferred presidential elections to direct popular vote.

Gus Dur: Islam, Pluralism, and Presidential Disorder, 1999–2001

The man who became Indonesia's fourth president on 20 October 1999 was one of the most unusual figures in modern Asian politics. Abdurrahman Wahid — universally known by his pesantren nickname "Gus Dur" — was the grandson of Hasyim Asy'ari, the founder of Nahdlatul Ulama (NU), the traditionalist Islamic organization that claimed forty million members and constituted the largest civil-society organization in the world.10 Wahid had himself served as NU's chairman from 1984 to 1999, in which capacity he had withdrawn NU from party politics, promoted pesantren reform, and positioned the organization as the principal champion of a tolerant, pluralist interpretation of Indonesian Islam. As the political scientist Robert Hefner argued in an influential study of this era, Wahid and NU embodied what Hefner termed "civil Islam" — the idea that Islamic organizations could and should sustain democratic culture rather than threaten it.11

Nahdlatul Ulama ("Revival of the Religious Scholars") — Indonesia's largest Islamic organization, founded in 1926 as a traditionalist counterweight to reformist/Wahhabi influences. Organized around the pesantren (Islamic boarding school) network of rural Java; claimed approximately forty million members during Wahid's era. Its political wing, PKB, emerged after 1998.

Wahid was elected president not by popular vote — that would not come until 2004 — but by the MPR, in a process that reflected the calculations of parliamentary factions rather than the electorate's preferences. The PDI-P, which had won the most votes in June, expected its leader Megawati to become president; instead, a coalition of Islamic parties calling itself the "Central Axis" (*Poros Tengah*), led by Amien Rais of the National Mandate Party (PAN), threw its support behind Wahid. On 20 October 1999 he was elected by 373 votes to Megawati's 313.12 Megawati, to forestall the risk of riots by disappointed PDI-P supporters, accepted the vice presidency the following day.

In office, Wahid pursued a pluralism agenda that broke genuinely new ground. On 17 January 2000 he issued Presidential Decree No. 6, annulling the 1967 Presidential Instruction (No. 14/1967) that had banned public celebration of Chinese New Year and the display of Chinese-language characters, effectively confining Chinese cultural expression to the private sphere for three decades. In 2001 he declared Chinese New Year an optional national holiday. Also in 2000, his government formally recognized Confucianism (Konghucu) as Indonesia's sixth official religion alongside Islam, Catholicism, Protestantism, Hinduism, and Buddhism, restoring a status it had held before Suharto removed it in 1979 in a climate of anti-Chinese discrimination.13 Taufiq Tanasaldy's careful study of anti-Chinese sentiment in Indonesia credits Wahid's decrees as a decisive legislative moment in the rehabilitation of Chinese-Indonesian cultural rights, even as it notes that social discrimination persisted in practice well beyond the legal changes.14

The achievements were real, but they were accompanied by a governing style that alarmed virtually all observers. Wahid was partially blind, prone to exhaustion, and given to impulsive decisions: he dismissed cabinet ministers without explanation, made unpredictable foreign-policy announcements (including controversial visits to Israel and suggestions of normalizing relations), and was frequently abroad — logging more foreign travel in his first year than any previous Indonesian president — when domestic crises demanded his presence. Greg Fealy, the leading foreign scholar of NU and the closest outside observer of Wahid's career, assessed his first hundred days in terms that would prove prophetic: the president's erratic style, Fealy argued, reflected not mere disorganization but a genuine belief that the president could govern by personality and network rather than through institutional processes — a style that had worked within NU but that was unsuited to commanding a sprawling state.15

Two financial scandals fatally damaged Wahid's credibility in 2000. In the first, known as *Buloggate*, some four million dollars was reported missing from BULOG (the national logistics agency), allegedly collected by Wahid's personal masseur; the funds were eventually returned, but the episode fed accusations of impropriety. In the second, *Bruneigate*, Wahid was accused of failing to account for a two-million-dollar donation from the Sultan of Brunei intended for Acehnese humanitarian relief. Neither case was conclusively proven in court, but together they provided the DPR with grounds to issue a formal memorandum of censure in February 2001, followed by a second in April. Wahid responded by threatening to declare a state of emergency and dissolve parliament — a threat that, if carried out, would have destroyed the constitutional order he was supposedly defending.16

The denouement was swift. On 23 July 2001 the MPR convened a special session and voted unanimously to dismiss Wahid from office; he briefly refused to leave the State Palace before departing for medical treatment in the United States, and Megawati was sworn in as Indonesia's fifth president. Years later, Indonesian institutions recognized the complexity of Wahid's legacy: in 2024, the MPR formally annulled the impeachment resolution, and in November 2025 he was posthumously declared a National Hero. Theodore Friend, an American historian of Indonesia, captured the paradox in an FPRI essay: Wahid's intellectual gifts and moral courage were genuine, but his "people were crying for vision" and received instead "scattered direction."17 He had given Indonesia a new pluralism but had failed to give it a functioning government.

Megawati: Sukarno's Daughter in the Presidential Palace, 2001–2004

When Megawati Sukarnoputri took the oath of office in July 2001, the symbolic freight of the moment was enormous. She was the daughter of Sukarno, the founding father who had declared independence, coined Pancasila, and been destroyed by Suharto — and she was, at last, where many Indonesians had expected her to be since 1999. Her rise had been marked by patience and suffering. As PDI-P leader in the 1990s, she had been repeatedly blocked by Suharto's machinery; in 1997 government-backed thugs had stormed her party headquarters in Jakarta. She had waited while Habibie ran the transition and while Wahid occupied the palace she had expected to inherit. Now she arrived not in triumph but by succession after an impeachment.18

The economy she inherited was precarious. Indonesia still carried more than US$105 billion in external debt accumulated during the New Order and the Suharto-era bailouts of the Asian financial crisis; institutional confidence was low and the rupiah remained volatile. Megawati pursued stabilization through a combination of macroeconomic orthodoxy and direct negotiation with creditors: her government attended meetings of the Paris Club and London Club, successfully delaying some $5.8 billion in debt payments, and achieved GDP growth of approximately 4.1 per cent — modest but far better than the negative growth of the crisis years — with inflation suppressed to around 5 per cent.19 She also moved to reduce the International Monetary Fund's direct influence over Indonesian economic policy, a process SBY would complete in 2006 by repaying IMF debt early.

The most durable institutional legacy of Megawati's presidency was the creation of the Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi — the Corruption Eradication Commission, universally known as the KPK. Law No. 30/2002 was passed in December 2002 with technical support from the Asian Development Bank and, reportedly, the encouragement of the IMF, which had made anti-corruption reforms a condition of post-crisis structural adjustment. The KPK became operational in late 2003, equipped with extraordinary powers: the authority to investigate, prosecute, and litigate — bypassing the Attorney General's office, which was itself widely regarded as corrupt — and staffed by investigators who could not be prosecuted while performing their official duties. Sofie Arjon Schütte's careful examination of the commission's founding argues that it represented an unusual case of effective institutional design against long odds, precisely because its drafters — drawing on lessons from comparable agencies in Hong Kong and Singapore — built in both political independence and prosecutorial capacity from the outset.20

KPK (Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi) — Indonesia's Corruption Eradication Commission, established by Law No. 30/2002 under the Megawati presidency and operational from 2003. Equipped with powers of investigation, prosecution, and adjudication independent of the existing judicial structure; achieved a near-100-per-cent conviction rate in its early years and became a symbol of the anti-corruption dimension of Reformasi.

Megawati's record on privatization was more contested. The most divisive single transaction of her presidency was the sale of a 41.94-per-cent stake in Indosat — the state telecommunications company that operated Indonesia's satellite network — to Singapore Technologies Telemedia (a subsidiary of Temasek, the Singapore state holding company) in December 2002 for US$630 million. The sale provoked a storm of nationalist criticism: Indosat was both profitable and strategically important, and selling it to a Singaporean government-linked corporation felt to many like selling the country's infrastructure to a foreign state. The Indosat Workers Union filed suit; parliamentary factions demanded inquiries; and the transaction became a persistent weapon used against Megawati — and against the broader neoliberal current in Indonesian economic policy — for years afterward.21 In retrospect, scholars have debated whether the privatizations accelerated economic reform or simply transferred rents to foreign investors while failing to address the structural weaknesses of state capitalism. The controversy underscored a tension that would recur across the Reformasi era: between the imperatives of macroeconomic stabilization and the popular nationalism that had always been central to Indonesian political identity.

Terror in the Archipelago: Jemaah Islamiyah and the Bali Bombings

At 11:05 p.m. on 12 October 2002, three coordinated bombs exploded in the Kuta district of Bali. The first, a suicide bomber's explosive vest, detonated inside Paddy's Pub. Seconds later, a van packed with more than a tonne of potassium-chlorate explosive obliterated the Sari Club across the street. A third, smaller device was detonated near the United States Consulate in Denpasar. Two hundred and two people died — 88 of them Australian tourists — and 209 were injured. It was the deadliest terrorist attack in Indonesian history and the deadliest al-Qaeda-affiliated attack since 11 September 2001.22

The perpetrators belonged to Jemaah Islamiyah — "Islamic community" — a clandestine network whose origins lay in the old Darul Islam movement of the 1940s and in the radicalization of Indonesian Islamic activists in Malaysia. The organization had been formally constituted on 1 January 1993 by Abu Bakar Ba'asyir and Abdullah Sungkar while the two men were living as political exiles in Malaysia, having fled Indonesia in the late 1970s to escape Suharto's repression of radical Islam. After Suharto's fall, Sungkar established contact with al-Qaeda's network through Riduan Isamuddin — known as Hambali — who became JI's military commander and directed its regional operations. The structure the International Crisis Group mapped in a landmark report published just weeks after the Bali bombings was hierarchical and cellular: JI was organized into regional divisions (*mantiqi*) that stretched from southern Thailand through Malaysia and Indonesia to Australia, each subdivided into local cells (*wakalah*) with operational autonomy.23

Jemaah Islamiyah ("Islamic community") — an Islamist militant network formally established in 1993 by Abu Bakar Ba'asyir and Abdullah Sungkar, with roots in the Darul Islam movement. Responsible for the 2002 Bali bombings and subsequent attacks; linked to al-Qaeda through Hambali. Subject to ongoing scholarly debate about the depth and nature of its al-Qaeda integration. Declared dissolution by senior members in 2024.

The nature of JI's relationship to al-Qaeda has been a matter of scholarly debate. Zachary Abuza, in the most comprehensive early study of Islamist militancy in Southeast Asia, argued for a close organizational and financial integration — JI members trained in Afghan camps, received funds from al-Qaeda, and coordinated operationally with bin Laden's network to the point where the Bali bombings could be read as part of a transnational jihadist campaign.24 Other analysts — including the ICG's Sidney Jones and later scholars such as Elena Pavlova, who made the most detailed study of JI's founding charter — emphasize the organization's indigenous roots and argue that its links to al-Qaeda were more personal and episodic than structural. In this reading, JI was primarily a product of Indonesian Islamist politics and the specific grievances of the Suharto era, rather than a franchise of global jihad.25 Most subsequent scholarship has converged on a middle position: al-Qaeda provided money, training, and ideological prestige to JI, but the organization had its own logic, its own leadership, and its own trajectory.

The Bali bombings shocked the Indonesian government into an unprecedented counterterrorism response. Three of the principal perpetrators — Amrozi bin Nurhasyim, Imam Samudra, and Ali Ghufron (also known as Mukhlas) — were rapidly arrested, tried, convicted, and eventually executed by firing squad on 9 November 2008. Hambali was captured in Thailand in August 2003 and transferred to CIA custody, eventually reaching Guantánamo Bay. In June 2003 the Indonesian National Police established Detachment 88 (*Densus 88*), a specially trained counter-terrorism unit funded partly by the United States and Australia, drawing on forensic, intelligence, and tactical expertise developed during the Bali investigation. Between its formation and 2009, Densus 88 arrested more than 466 individuals connected to JI.26

Densus 88 (*Detasemen Khusus 88* — Special Detachment 88) — Indonesia's elite counter-terrorism police unit, established in June 2003 in the wake of the 2002 Bali bombings. Named for the eighty-eight Australian victims of the attack; funded partly by Australia and the United States; credited with degrading Jemaah Islamiyah through hundreds of arrests over the following decade.

The counterterrorism effort continued under SBY: the 2003 JW Marriott Hotel bombing in Jakarta (twelve killed, primarily Indonesians), the 2004 Australian Embassy bombing (nine killed), and the 2005 second Bali bombings (twenty killed) all demonstrated that the JI network had not been broken. But the cumulative effect of Densus 88's operations — combined with JI's own internal fractures between hardliners (who favoured continued attacks) and pragmatists (who sought to rebuild the organization covertly) — progressively degraded the network's capacity. The death of Noordin Mohammad Top, JI's most active bomb-maker, in a police siege in September 2009, is generally regarded as the moment when the organization lost its ability to launch mass-casualty attacks. Kathrin Rucktäschel and Christoph Schuck, in the most thorough recent scholarly assessment of Indonesia's post-Bali counter-terrorism architecture, credit Densus 88 with transforming Indonesia from one of the world's most active jihadist theatres into a model of counter-terrorism effectiveness within a single decade — while noting ongoing human rights concerns about the unit's methods, including several extrajudicial killings of suspects.27

SBY: The First Directly Elected President, 2004

The 2004 election marked a constitutional watershed. Under a 2002 amendment to the 1945 Constitution — itself one of several significant revisions made by the MPR between 1999 and 2002 — both the president and vice president would henceforth be elected by direct popular vote rather than chosen by the assembly. The change transformed Indonesian politics: it shifted power from the horse-trading of parliamentary factions to the electorate itself, and it elevated the importance of public appeal, name recognition, and electoral organization in a way that parliamentary selection had never done.28

The candidate who best understood this new landscape was Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono — known universally by his initials, SBY. A retired army general who had served as Megawati's Coordinating Minister for Political and Security Affairs before resigning in 2004, SBY had founded the Democratic Party (*Partai Demokrat*) in 2001 precisely to provide himself a political vehicle for the new direct-election era. Marcus Mietzner, whose authoritative study of civil-military relations under Reformasi offers the deepest scholarly account of SBY's military background, emphasizes that he was unusual among Indonesian generals for his academic credentials (he held degrees from US military staff colleges and spoke fluent English), his reputation for restraint during the 1998 crisis, and his deliberate cultivation of a "thinking general" image that distanced him from the heavy-handed military culture of the New Order.29

In the first round of the presidential election on 5 July 2004, SBY won 33.5 per cent of the vote, ahead of incumbent president Megawati at 26.0 per cent and former armed forces commander Wiranto at 22.0 per cent; in the runoff on 20 September 2004, SBY and his running mate, Jusuf Kalla, won 60.62 per cent to Megawati's 39.38 per cent. R. William Liddle and Saiful Mujani, surveying the election in a contemporaneous scholarly analysis, attributed SBY's victory above all to his image of competence and incorruptibility at a moment when voters were exhausted by the instability of the Wahid and Megawati years and hungry for a president who seemed to know what he was doing.30 Megawati notably refused to attend his inauguration and reportedly never offered congratulations — a personal coolness that would complicate coalition politics for years.

Aceh: War's End and the Helsinki MOU, 2005

The most celebrated achievement of SBY's first term was the resolution of the Aceh conflict — a thirty-year insurgency that had cost tens of thousands of lives and absorbed enormous military and economic resources. The Free Aceh Movement (*Gerakan Aceh Merdeka*, GAM), founded in 1976 by Hasan di Tiro, had fought for independence from Indonesia across three decades of intermittent but brutal warfare. Previous attempts at negotiated settlement — including the 2002 Cessation of Hostilities Agreement — had collapsed within months under the pressure of military operations and mutual recriminations. By 2003, Jakarta had placed Aceh under martial law and launched a large-scale military offensive that inflicted heavy casualties on GAM but failed to extinguish it.31

The catalyst that reopened the door to negotiation was catastrophic: the Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami of 26 December 2004. The wave that struck the coast of Aceh was the most destructive of the disaster: an estimated 165,000 to 170,000 people died in the province, entire coastal communities were obliterated, and the scale of the destruction was such that both the Indonesian government and GAM temporarily suspended military operations. The sudden influx of thousands of international relief workers and journalists into a province that had been largely closed to outside observers for years made it practically impossible for either side to resume conventional military operations without international condemnation. SBY, who had been in office barely two months, moved to seize the moment: he ordered negotiations with GAM rather than a resumption of military pressure, and approached the former Finnish president Martti Ahtisaari to lead mediation under the auspices of the Crisis Management Initiative.32

The negotiations unfolded through five rounds of talks in Helsinki between January and July 2005. Ahtisaari's strategy was to bind the parties to a comprehensive framework rather than a sequential agreement — "nothing agreed until everything agreed" — which prevented either side from extracting early concessions and then walking away. GAM gave up its long-standing demand for independence and accepted a formula of "self-government within the framework of the unitary state of the Republic of Indonesia," in exchange for the right to form local political parties (a major departure from Indonesian constitutional law), control over a larger share of Aceh's natural-resource revenues, and a phased withdrawal of non-organic Indonesian military and police forces from the province. Edward Aspinall's policy study, the most careful contemporaneous scholarly analysis of the agreement, assessed it as "a more promising basis for peace" than its predecessors, while cautioning that its success would depend heavily on how the Indonesian military accepted a reduced role.33

The Memorandum of Understanding was signed in Helsinki on 15 August 2005. Michael Morfit, who had insider access to the negotiation process, has written the most detailed account of the diplomatic mechanics, stressing how SBY's personal commitment — his willingness to accept terms that involved genuine concessions to separatist demands — was indispensable: without a president prepared to override the military's preference for a military solution, no peace agreement was possible.34 The Aceh peace held. It was followed by the implementation of the Law on Governing Aceh (2006), democratic local elections won by former GAM leaders, and a remarkable demobilization of both GAM fighters and Indonesian troops. By any measure it stood as one of the most successful negotiated peace settlements in the history of Southeast Asian conflicts.

GAM (Gerakan Aceh Merdeka — Free Aceh Movement) — the Acehnese independence organization founded in 1976 by Hasan di Tiro; fought a thirty-year insurgency against Indonesian rule. Signed the Helsinki MOU in August 2005, accepting self-government within Indonesia in exchange for political rights and resource revenues; former GAM leaders won subsequent provincial elections as the Partai Aceh.

Stability and Stagnation: The Long SBY Decade, 2004–2014

Measured by macroeconomic indicators, the SBY decade was one of the most successful in Indonesian history. GDP per capita rose from approximately US$1,188 in 2004 to US$3,490 in 2014. The debt-to-GDP ratio fell from 56.6 per cent to 23 per cent. In 2006 Indonesia repaid its IMF debts ahead of schedule — SBY announced he wanted to prevent the country from being "dictated to by the IMF" — and the country was widely regarded as having emerged definitively from the shadow of the Asian financial crisis. A new Indonesian middle class was growing: estimates suggested that by 2012 roughly forty to fifty million Indonesians had crossed the consumption threshold that economists use to define the middle class. Indonesia joined the G20, hosted the ASEAN Summit, and attracted rising volumes of foreign direct investment. A country that in 1998 had seemed on the verge of disintegration now appeared to many observers as a model of democratic consolidation in the Muslim world.35

The anti-corruption project that Megawati had begun with the KPK bore significant fruit under SBY's first term. The KPK grew rapidly in capacity and ambition, achieving a conviction rate that approached one hundred per cent in its early years. SBY demonstrated his personal commitment to the cause in 2005 when the KPK arrested Aulia Pohan, deputy governor of Bank Indonesia and the father of SBY's own daughter-in-law — the first case in Indonesian history in which a close relative of a sitting president was prosecuted for corruption. An anti-corruption court (*Pengadilan Tindak Pidana Korupsi*, Tipikor) was established to try cases brought by the KPK, providing a dedicated judicial forum less susceptible to the systemic corruption of the ordinary courts. Simon Butt's authoritative study of anti-corruption reform in Indonesia commends the KPK-Tipikor architecture while noting the contradictions at its heart: the commission's very success made it a target for counter-attacks by the vested interests whose power it was dismantling.36

Those counter-attacks intensified in SBY's second term, which began with his re-election in July 2009 with approximately 60 per cent of the vote in the first round — the first time any Indonesian presidential candidate had cleared the 50-per-cent threshold outright. The "Cicak vs. Buaya" ("Gecko vs. Crocodile") affair of late 2009 provided a graphic illustration of the stakes: two KPK commissioners were arrested by the National Police on dubious charges of bribery, in an episode widely understood as a police institution retaliating against a commission that had been investigating it. Public outrage — expressed through massive social-media mobilization, still a relatively new phenomenon in Indonesia — forced the government to walk back the arrests, but the episode exposed the fragility of the KPK's political foundations.37

More damaging was the corruption that engulfed SBY's own Democratic Party in the second term. Bank Century, a small private bank that had received a government bailout of approximately Rp6.7 trillion during the 2008 financial crisis, became a political scandal when investigators alleged that some of the funds had been diverted to finance the Democratic Party's 2009 election campaign. The party chairman, Anas Urbaningrum, was eventually convicted of corruption in 2014 — sentenced to eight years in prison, later increased to fourteen on appeal — in a case involving the Hambalang sports complex project. The spectacle of the president's own party machine implicated in the very corruption that SBY had claimed to be eradicating was acutely damaging. Marcus Mietzner's study of Indonesian political financing argues that the Demokrat party's trajectory illustrated a broader structural problem: parties in Indonesia's money-politics environment required enormous campaign funds that the formal party-finance system could not provide, creating structural incentives for corruption regardless of individual politicians' personal ethics.38

The broader verdict on the SBY era has generated one of the more illuminating debates in contemporary Indonesian scholarship. The title of the flagship scholarly assessment of his presidency — The Yudhoyono Presidency: Indonesia's Decade of Stability and Stagnation, edited by Edward Aspinall, Marcus Mietzner, and Dirk Tomsa — captures the core tension. SBY's defenders, and partially SBY himself, argued that maintaining stability in a fractious, multi-party democracy that had only recently emerged from authoritarian rule was an achievement not to be dismissed; that consensus-building across oversize governing coalitions was the only viable governing strategy given Indonesia's institutional landscape; and that the macroeconomic record, the Aceh peace, and the anti-corruption gains demonstrated a government that was genuinely accomplishing things. His critics, including Mietzner and several contributors to the Aspinall volume, contended that SBY's excessive caution — his habit of forming oversized coalitions whose internal contradictions he preferred to blur rather than resolve, his reluctance to push reforms that might alienate coalition partners, his tendency to project an image of decisive leadership while in practice avoiding decisions — had left Indonesia stuck at a "low-quality democracy," stable enough to persist but not institutionally strong enough to be called consolidated.39 The nickname that circulated in Jakarta was pointed: critics said "SBY" stood for Saya Belum Yakin — "I'm not sure yet."

What SBY did not do in his second term was reverse Indonesia's democratic trajectory. He did not attempt to extend his tenure beyond the constitutionally mandated two terms; he accepted the independence of the Constitutional Court even when it ruled against his government; and the 2014 election — which delivered a remarkable upset, the first genuine outsider (Jokowi) defeating the established party-political elite — was free, fair, and peaceful. In that fundamental sense, the democratic framework that Habibie had engineered in 1998 and 1999, that Wahid and Megawati had each in their different ways sustained, and that SBY had consolidated without transforming, was passed on intact. Whether the next chapter of Indonesian democracy would build on that foundation or erode it remained, in 2014, an open question.

Contested history: the four presidents and their legacies. Each presidency in this chapter is the subject of active scholarly debate. Habibie's motivation in offering the East Timor referendum remains contested: was it democratic principle, strategic miscalculation, or both? Wahid's impeachment is still argued: were the financial scandals genuine grounds for dismissal or pretexts used by an MPR that had never accepted his election? Megawati's record is split between defenders who credit her with stabilization and institution-building and critics who focus on privatization policies and her silence on human-rights abuses in Papua and Aceh. SBY's legacy divides between the "stability" and "stagnation" camps outlined above. This chapter has tried to present these debates as debates; the primary sources and the scholarship cited in the notes should be consulted for the full complexity of each case.

Summary

The four presidents who governed Indonesia between 1998 and 2014 collectively constructed a democratic political order from the ruins of Suharto's New Order. Habibie, an unlikely reformer, enacted press freedom, party liberalization, and regional autonomy in a single year, before losing power over the East Timor referendum. Wahid brought pluralism and genuine moral courage to the presidency but governed so erratically that the DPR and MPR combined to remove him in 2001. Megawati stabilized the economy, created the KPK anti-corruption agency, and built the counterterrorism architecture that eventually degraded Jemaah Islamiyah — but her privatization policies alienated nationalists and she lost the landmark 2004 direct election. SBY served two full terms as Indonesia's first popularly elected president, ending the Aceh conflict with the 2005 Helsinki MOU, overseeing strong economic growth, and sustaining the democratic framework — while drawing increasing criticism for a consensual governing style that critics characterized as stagnation. Across the whole era, the 2002 Bali bombings and the subsequent rise and eventual dismemberment of Jemaah Islamiyah demonstrated that the democratic transition had not resolved Indonesia's deeper tensions over the role of political Islam.

Review & Discussion Questions

38.1 Habibie has been described both as Suharto's loyal technocrat and as the architect of Indonesia's democratic opening. What evidence supports each characterization? Which seems more historically accurate to you, and why?
38.2 Why did Abdurrahman Wahid — a cleric from a party that had won only 12 per cent of the legislative vote — become president in 1999, and what does the mechanism of his election reveal about how Indonesian democracy worked (and did not work) in this period?
38.3 Wahid's pluralism agenda benefited Chinese Indonesians and Confucians in concrete, measurable ways. To what extent did his broader governance failures undermine the lasting impact of those gains?
38.4 The KPK was created under Megawati but became most powerful under SBY — and also came under sustained attack during SBY's second term. What does this trajectory tell us about the relationship between institutional design and political will in fighting corruption?
38.5 Scholars debate whether Jemaah Islamiyah was primarily a product of Indonesian domestic politics or an affiliate of al-Qaeda's global jihadist network. What evidence bears on this debate, and why does the answer matter for understanding Indonesia's counterterrorism response?
38.6 The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami is often cited as the catalyst for the Aceh peace agreement. How much weight should we assign to the tsunami versus other factors — SBY's personal commitment, GAM's military position, Ahtisaari's mediation technique — in explaining why the Helsinki process succeeded where all previous attempts had failed?
38.7 The title of a major edited volume on SBY calls his presidency "Indonesia's Decade of Stability and Stagnation." Is it possible to have both simultaneously, and which side of this equation do you find more historically significant?
