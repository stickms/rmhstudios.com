Learning Objectives

  • Explain how structural vulnerabilities in Indonesia's financial system — corporate dollar debt, weak banking supervision, and crony capitalism — made it the hardest-hit victim of the 1997 Asian Financial Crisis.
  • Analyse the IMF intervention: the conditionality programs, the significance of the Camdessus photograph, and the scholarly debate over whether Fund demands worsened the crisis or managed it.
  • Trace the political trajectory from Suharto's seventh reelection in March 1998 to the occupation of parliament and his resignation on 21 May 1998, including the roles of the student movement, military factionalism, and elite defection.
  • Assess the May 1998 riots and their aftermath honestly — the scale of destruction, the anti-Chinese targeting, the documented sexual violence against ethnic-Chinese women, the competing accounts of orchestration, and the continuing failure of accountability.

On the morning of 21 May 1998, Suharto sat before the television cameras at Merdeka Palace in Jakarta and delivered a four-minute statement that ended thirty-two years of authoritarian rule. "I have decided to declare my resignation from my position as President of the Republic of Indonesia as of the time I read this statement on Thursday, 21 May 1998." It was the end of the Orde Baru — the New Order — the longest-surviving authoritarian system in post-colonial Southeast Asia. The question of how one of Asia's most celebrated development success stories became, in barely nine months, a country on the edge of disintegration — and how a regime that had survived every challenge since the 1966 coup now collapsed with such sudden finality — is one of the hinge stories of modern Asian history. The answer lies in the collision of a global financial contagion, a spectacularly ill-timed austerity program, a political system hollowed of legitimacy after three decades of managed stasis, and nine days of student protest, urban catastrophe, and elite desertion that the world watched in disbelief.

A Vulnerable Miracle: Indonesia Before the Storm

By the mid-1990s, three decades of New Order development had produced economic statistics of genuine impressiveness. Between 1967 and 1996, annual GDP growth averaged approximately 7.6 percent, lifting tens of millions from poverty and generating a new urban professional and consumer class.1 Poverty rates that had stood near 60 percent of the population when Suharto took power fell to roughly 11 percent; basic infrastructure, mass schooling, and the Green Revolution in rice transformed rural life on Java and beyond.2 The World Bank's influential 1993 report, The East Asian Miracle, ranked Indonesia among the high-performing Asian economies worth emulating, and Western development economists praised Suharto's technocrats — the so-called "Berkeley Mafia," graduates of American economics programmes — for their macro-economic discipline. It seemed, to many observers, like an unqualified success story.

Beneath the growth figures, however, structural vulnerabilities had accumulated that the boom years concealed. The most dangerous was the architecture of private-sector debt. Indonesian corporations — many of them controlled by members of Suharto's family or by business partners who owed their positions to presidential patronage — had borrowed heavily in foreign currency, primarily US dollars, at short maturities and without purchasing currency protection. This practice was rational only as long as the rupiah remained stable; any significant depreciation would instantly multiply the domestic-currency cost of those debts beyond servicing capacity.3 The banking sector that financed this borrowing was equally fragile: loan-to-deposit ratios exceeded 100 percent in 1997, and the ratio of non-performing loans to total loans had already reached approximately 27 percent by September of that year — a figure that would have triggered regulatory intervention in any robustly supervised system.4

These financial vulnerabilities were inseparable from the political economy of the New Order itself. The political scientist Jeffrey Winters characterised the Suharto system as a form of "sultanistic oligarchy" — a patronage structure in which the president had created Indonesia's wealthiest businesspeople and retained personal control over their access to contracts, monopolies, credit, and regulatory protection.5 By the mid-1990s, Suharto's six children had accumulated interests in toll roads, airlines, television networks, petrochemicals, a national car project, and a state-enforced clove-buying monopoly; his long-time associate Liem Sioe Liong controlled a banking-and-food empire built on protected access to flour imports.6 The historian R.E. Elson, in his authoritative political biography of Suharto, notes that the system functioned as long as growth continued and the distribution of rents satisfied enough of the military, bureaucratic, and business elites whose loyalty sustained it; the moment growth stopped, the rents, the debts, and the political arrangements would all become simultaneously illegitimate.7 Adam Schwarz's analysis added a further observation: the patronage structure had made serious economic reform politically impossible, because any structural adjustment would require dismantling the very network on which Suharto's power rested. That contradiction would prove fatal in 1997–98.

Orde Baru — Indonesian: "New Order." The authoritarian regime established by Suharto after the 1965–66 political crisis. Resting on a trinity of the military (ABRI), the state party (Golkar), and a managed bureaucracy, it lasted from 1966 to 1998 and oversaw both rapid economic development and systematic repression of political opposition.

Contagion: The Rupiah's Fall, July 1997–January 1998

The financial crisis that destroyed Indonesia's economy began not in Jakarta but in Bangkok. On 2 July 1997, Thailand's government, under acute pressure from currency speculators, abandoned the baht's peg to the US dollar and allowed it to float, losing roughly 20 percent of its value within days. Contagion spread rapidly to other "tiger" economies — Malaysia, the Philippines, South Korea — whose currencies bore similar structural vulnerabilities. Indonesia initially responded by widening the rupiah's trading band, then on 14 August 1997, with speculative pressure mounting, abandoned the peg entirely and allowed the currency to float. The rupiah stood at approximately Rp 2,436 to the dollar on 11 July; by the day of the float, it had already fallen to Rp 2,663. What followed was a collapse of extraordinary severity.8

Through the final months of 1997, the rupiah fell further: to Rp 4,000 in October, Rp 5,000 in November. The announcement in November that the government, under IMF guidance, would close 16 insolvent private banks — intended to signal fiscal discipline — produced the opposite of its intended effect. Indonesian depositors, without deposit insurance, rushed to withdraw funds from all private banks. Bank Indonesia responded with massive emergency liquidity injections, a programme that spawned the "Bank Indonesia Liquidity Support" (BLBI) scandal, in which billions of rupiah were diverted by bank owners before the institutions collapsed anyway — one of the largest financial frauds in Indonesian history.9 By January 1998, the rupiah had lost approximately 70 percent of its mid-1997 value, and in the first days of January it lost half its remaining value within a week, at one point trading as low as Rp 14,800–16,000 to the dollar.10 GDP, which had grown 7.8 percent in 1996, contracted by 13.1 percent in 1998; domestic inflation reached approximately 65 percent as the cost of imports and food staples surged.11 Unemployment multiplied; the urban middle class found its savings destroyed; the rural and urban poor could no longer afford basic goods.

The economists Steven Radelet and Jeffrey Sachs, writing in the Brookings Papers on Economic Activity at the height of the crisis, offered the most influential scholarly critique of what had gone wrong: they argued that Indonesia's crisis was fundamentally a liquidity panic — a self-fulfilling flight by creditors — rather than an insolvency crisis rooted in genuine fiscal or economic failure. On this reading, the structural conditions imposed as part of the IMF rescue programme had deepened investor anxiety rather than restoring confidence, because they signalled that the Fund did not trust Indonesia's government to implement basic measures without coercion, and because the required restructurings created new uncertainties rather than resolving existing ones.12 The debate between the "liquidity panic" view and the "structural insolvency" view remains one of the most important scholarly controversies about the Asian Financial Crisis.

The Doctor and the General: IMF Conditionality and the Camdessus Photograph

Indonesia turned to the International Monetary Fund in October 1997. The first Letter of Intent, signed on 31 October, committed Indonesia to a comprehensive programme of bank restructuring, trade liberalisation, and fiscal consolidation; a total international rescue package assembled by the IMF, World Bank, Asian Development Bank, and bilateral donors — principally Japan, Singapore, and the United States — was valued at approximately $43 billion, the largest financial rescue package assembled to that date.13 The programme sought not only to stabilise the currency but to impose structural reforms that would dismantle the crony-capitalist monopolies that, in the Fund's diagnosis, had distorted the Indonesian economy. These included Suharto's youngest son Tommy's national car project (the Timor), his clove-buying monopoly, and various Suharto-family utilities and import licences.

Suharto's reaction to these conditions was resistant from the outset. In January 1998, he publicly floated the idea of a currency board — a mechanism that would have fixed the rupiah to the dollar at a set rate, eliminating the Fund's leverage over monetary policy. The IMF, backed by the Clinton administration, strongly opposed the currency board idea, fearing it would destabilise Indonesia's foreign exchange reserves. President Clinton telephoned Suharto on three occasions — 8 January, 15 January, and 13 February 1998 — to press him to comply with Fund conditionality and abandon the currency board plan. A National Security Archive compilation of declassified US documents confirms the content and sequence of these calls.14

The second Letter of Intent, containing a 50-point structural reform programme, was signed on 15 January 1998 at a ceremony at the presidential palace in Jakarta, attended by IMF Managing Director Michel Camdessus. A photograph taken at the ceremony — Suharto seated, pen in hand, signing the document, with Camdessus standing slightly behind him with arms folded across his chest — became one of the most discussed images of the crisis. In Indonesia and throughout the developing world, it was read as a tableau of submission: the "colonial overlord" extracting concessions from a subjugated leader. The image was described, in one of its many glosses, as "laden with heavy symbolism: Western 'imperialism' made Indonesia capitulate."15 The photograph circulated in Indonesian newspapers as evidence that the government had surrendered sovereignty to foreign creditors.

The photograph's meaning was, however, genuinely contested. The World Bank's country director in Jakarta later described it as "profoundly unfair" to Camdessus, noting that he was standing where he had been directed to stand and that he habitually crossed his arms as a physical posture; the staging, not any intent to humiliate, had produced the image.16 Camdessus himself, at his retirement press conference several years later, offered a remark that gave the controversy a different register altogether: "We created the conditions that obliged Suharto to leave his job." Whether he meant this as a description of an outcome the Fund had not anticipated, or as a candid admission of policy intent, remains debated; the statement suggests that whatever the posture in the photograph, the Fund was not entirely displeased with the political consequences of its intervention.17

The conditionality programme itself continued to draw scholarly criticism. Radelet and Sachs argued that demanding deep structural reforms simultaneously with monetary stabilisation — in conditions of acute illiquidity rather than solvency failure — had been counterproductive: rather than reassuring investors, the 50-point list of required changes convinced markets that the crisis was far deeper than it appeared and that Indonesia's political system might not survive the reforms.18 A third Letter of Intent, signed in April 1998, notably eased conditionality to allow food-price subsidies following Suharto's fall — an implicit acknowledgement that the earlier programme had been excessively harsh.19

The political consequences of conditionality played out most explosively in May 1998. On 4 May, the Indonesian government implemented a set of IMF-mandated price increases: kerosene rose by 25 percent, diesel by 60 percent, and gasoline by 71 percent. Electricity rates also rose.20 The price increases fell hardest on the urban poor, for whom kerosene was an everyday cooking fuel and whose rickshaws, minibuses, and market transport depended on diesel. Within days, riots broke out in Medan, North Sumatra, and in Ujung Pandang; in Medan, there was an ominous targeting of ethnic Chinese-owned shops — a sign of what was to come on a far larger scale nine days later.

KKN — abbreviation of korupsi, kolusi, nepotisme ("corruption, collusion, nepotism"). The phrase became the central slogan of the student protest movement in 1997–98, encapsulating demands for clean government and the dismantling of Suharto-era patronage networks. It framed the political demand of the Reformasi movement.

A Seventh Term: Political Crisis Alongside Economic Catastrophe

As the economic crisis deepened through the first months of 1998, Indonesia's political system underwent a parallel collapse of legitimacy. The trigger was the session of the People's Consultative Assembly (Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat, MPR) on 10–11 March 1998, at which — as had happened in every election since 1973 — Suharto was returned to the presidency without contest. It was his seventh consecutive term, extending a personal rule that had already lasted thirty-two years.21 The MPR also confirmed as his Vice President Bacharuddin Jusuf Habibie — the technology minister, aerospace engineer, and long-time protégé whom Suharto had cultivated since the 1950s and who was, in the popular imagination, a kind of political son. Students across the country immediately derided the new cabinet, presented on 14 March, as a "duplicate Suharto": it included family members and business cronies at a moment of national economic catastrophe.22

The choice of Habibie as vice president was not politically neutral. It elevated a figure with no military or parliamentary power base, entirely dependent on Suharto, and widely regarded outside the Javanese nationalist tradition as an eccentric bet on prestige technology (Habibie had championed the IPTN aircraft programme as a development strategy, to persistent scepticism from technocrats). Many in the armed forces, the bureaucracy, and the business community regarded him as unfit for the succession. His appointment meant that, if Suharto were removed, his replacement would be a man with no independent power base of his own — which made him, paradoxically, both a safe and a dangerous choice.

The phrase that defined the political moment was korupsi, kolusi, nepotisme — KKN — corruption, collusion, nepotism. The formula, already in circulation among student activists and civil society critics, suddenly captured the public mood with precision. Theodore Friend, in his comprehensive narrative history of Indonesia, describes the atmosphere of early 1998: prices were rising, queues for basic goods were lengthening, and the gap between the official narrative of "development" and the experienced reality of economic devastation was impossible to bridge. Students who had begun demonstrating in January — first at the Bandung Institute of Technology, then at campuses across Java — returned from the March MPR session with newly sharpened demands. Anti-KKN banners gave way, in the weeks that followed, to the word that would name the entire era: reformasi.23

The Mahasiswa: Students, Campuses, and the Logic of Reformasi

Indonesian university students — mahasiswa — carried a tradition of political intervention stretching back to 1966, when student organisations had been mobilised by the military against Sukarno. In 1974, a student demonstration against Japanese investment and corruption was suppressed; in 1978, after a second round of campus protests, Suharto imposed the Normalisasi Kehidupan Kampus/Badan Koordinasi Kemahasiswaan (NKK/BKK) regulations, which banned autonomous student political organisations and confined campus councils to cultural and welfare activities. The effect was to drive student political organising underground, into informal "study groups" (kelompok studi) that kept intellectual and oppositional life alive through the 1980s and early 1990s.24 From these networks emerged, in 1994, the People's Democratic Party (Partai Rakyat Demokratik, PRD), an above-ground organisation linking student, worker, and peasant activists, which the government would attempt to criminalise as a "PKI reincarnation" after the 1996 Megawati riots in Jakarta.

The 1998 movement was distinct in its deliberate strategy and its moral clarity. Student leaders, meeting across campuses and in coalition bodies like Forum Kota, adopted a "campus strategy": demonstrations would remain within university grounds, protecting protesters from direct military confrontation and placing the legal burden on security forces who would have to enter campuses without invitation to suppress them. University rectors often endorsed this strategy, positioning themselves as the moral protectors of the students' civic conscience.25 The demands evolved rapidly: early protests called for an end to KKN and price reductions; after Suharto's March reelection, they shifted to systemic reformasi — democratisation, abolition of the military's dual socio-political function (Dwifungsi), and free elections. By late April, increasing numbers of protesters were demanding Suharto mundur: Suharto out.

The movement widened steadily. By March and April, Erin Russell's account documents, "worker activists, professionals, housewives, even nuns began joining" campus demonstrations; artistic performances integrated cultural protest with political demand; and the movement's peaceful discipline maintained a moral high ground that the government could not easily occupy.26 Coalition actors included the Islamic student organisations, the PRD, secular nationalist networks, and civil society figures linked to Abdurrahman Wahid (head of Nahdlatul Ulama, the country's largest Islamic organisation) and Amien Rais (head of Muhammadiyah, the second-largest). Both men had withdrawn their support for Suharto and become, in effect, leaders of an emerging political opposition.

Within the armed forces, the movement intersected with a factional conflict that would shape the final days of the regime. Marcus Mietzner's authoritative study of Indonesian military politics identifies two principal factions: a "red-and-white" (nationalist) faction led by Armed Forces Commander General Wiranto, which was committed to constitutional order and institutional control; and a "green" faction associated with Lieutenant General Prabowo Subianto, Suharto's son-in-law and commander of the Army Strategic Reserve (Kostrad), which was associated with Islamist networks and which reportedly favoured a harder line against protesters.27 Declassified US intelligence documents released by the National Security Archive confirm that by early 1998, US officials in Jakarta were aware that Prabowo's command had been involved in the kidnapping and enforced disappearances of student and labour activists — operations that were never publicly acknowledged and for which no prosecution has followed.28

In the weeks leading up to the May crisis, the student movement maintained remarkable discipline. Aspinall's study of the 1998 transition emphasises that the movement's nonviolent character — its insistence on staying within campuses, its explicit rejection of violence, and its moral framing as a demand for clean government rather than a revolutionary class uprising — was strategically deliberate and instrumentally essential: it denied the military a justification for crackdown and gradually eroded the will of military commanders to defend a regime they were losing faith in.29 The moment that dissolved the movement's discipline was the moment the military fired on it.

Trisakti: The Shootings of 12 May 1998

On the afternoon of 12 May 1998, Indonesian Army soldiers opened fire on students conducting a peaceful demonstration outside the gates of Trisakti University in West Jakarta. Four students were killed: Elang Mulia Lesmana, a twenty-year-old student of architecture; Heri Hertanto, a student of engineering; Hafidin Royan, a student of civil engineering; and Hendriawan Sie, a student of economics. Approximately fifteen additional students were wounded. Autopsies confirmed that all four had been killed by live rounds, contradicting the military's initial claim that only rubber bullets had been fired.30

The sequence of events on that day was documented in detail. Over 6,000 students had assembled on campus and lowered the university flag to half-mast. A planned march toward the People's Representative Council (Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat, DPR) building was blocked by security forces positioned at the West Jakarta Mayor's office; the university's Dean, Adi Andojo Soetjipto, persuaded the students to return to campus around 3:30 PM. Security forces, composed of units including the Police Mobile Brigade, the 9th Cavalry Battalion, and infantry battalions of the Jakarta Military Command, had positioned themselves around the campus. Beginning at approximately 5:00 PM, officers on rooftops and at ground level opened fire into the student crowd. Lesmana and Sie were killed near the rectorate entrance; Hertanto and Royan in the campus central plaza. A student holding a red-and-white Indonesian flag was photographed bleeding; the image became an immediate emblem of the movement.

The accountability that followed was minimal. Two police lieutenants received sentences of ten months and four months respectively for procedural violations — neither was prosecuted for the killings themselves. Announced trials of sixteen additional officers never proceeded. Komnas HAM (the National Commission on Human Rights) reopened the investigation in 2002, identified approximately 50 suspected perpetrators, and classified the incident as a pelanggaran HAM berat — a gross human rights violation. The Attorney General's office repeatedly returned the file as procedurally incomplete. As of the mid-2020s, no one has been convicted for the deaths of the four Trisakti students.31 Amnesty International has continued to call for justice, noting the case as emblematic of Indonesia's broader impunity for New Order–era violations.32 President Habibie, weeks after Suharto's fall, would call the four students pahlawan reformasi — heroes of reform — a designation that did not translate into prosecution.

The May Catastrophe: Riots, Anti-Chinese Violence, and Sexual Violence

News of the Trisakti shootings spread across Jakarta within hours. By the following morning, 13 May 1998, fires were burning in the capital. Over three days — 13–15 May in Jakarta, 14–15 May in Surakarta (Solo) — Indonesia experienced the worst urban violence since the 1965–66 killings, and one of the most destructive episodes of urban riot in twentieth-century Asian history. Suharto was in Cairo at the time, attending a G-15 summit, and did not return to Jakarta until the evening of 14 May; his absence during the first days of the violence became, in retrospect, the final signal that the centre could not hold.

The scale of destruction in Jakarta alone was staggering. By the city government's count, 5,723 buildings were destroyed and 1,948 vehicles damaged; 40 shopping malls were burned, 2,479 shop-houses damaged, 13 markets destroyed. In the most catastrophic single incident, approximately 400 people — almost all of them looters who had entered the Yogya Klender shopping centre in East Jakarta — were burned to death when the building caught fire and exits were blocked.33 Death toll estimates varied widely by source: Jakarta's city government reported 288 deaths; police reported 463; the Volunteer Team for Humanity (Tim Relawan untuk Kemanusiaan, TRuK) counted 1,109 fire deaths and 27 deaths from gunshots; Komnas HAM reported at least 1,188 deaths in Jakarta alone. Most scholarly accounts use the figure of approximately 1,200 total deaths, inclusive of other affected cities.34 Violence was not confined to Jakarta: in Surakarta, the city of Solo in Central Java, rioters burned the Chinese-owned commercial district in scenes of similar intensity, and smaller episodes occurred in Palembang, Medan, and Surabaya.

The Anti-Chinese Character of the Violence

The riots had a pronounced and deliberate anti-Chinese character that distinguished them from generalised urban disorder. Ethnic Chinese-Indonesian (Tionghoa) property — shops, homes, temples, and commercial offices — was specifically targeted. Rioters were reported to shout "Bunuh Cina!" (Kill the Chinese) and "Babi Cina!" (Chinese pigs) while marking non-Chinese-owned buildings to spare them from burning. Human Rights Watch documented systematic targeting: "it is clear that Chinese-owned shops and homes were targeted, and hundreds of Chinese were physically attacked." An estimated 10,000 to 100,000 ethnic Chinese Indonesians fled the country in the weeks following, primarily to Singapore, Hong Kong, and Australia.35

The sociologists Samsu Rizal Panggabean and Benjamin Smith, in a peer-reviewed comparative analysis of anti-Chinese riots in late twentieth-century Indonesia, offered the most rigorous scholarly explanation for why riots occurred in some cities and not others. Their central finding was that anti-Chinese riots occurred only in cities — primarily Medan and Solo — where security forces had previously failed to contain the spatial reach of anti-government student demonstrations. When military and police commanders allowed student marches to move beyond campuses and into commercial zones in the weeks before May 1998, they created opportunities for crowd composition to shift and for anti-Chinese targeting to develop. In cities such as Yogyakarta and Surabaya, where commanders maintained tighter spatial control over the student movement, anti-Chinese riots did not occur. Panggabean and Smith characterise this as a "frame-shifting" strategy: by allowing or enabling riots against a minority target, security forces could redirect mass anger away from anti-government protest, protecting their own institutional position in a moment of regime crisis.36 This interpretation is consistent with — though not identical to — the hypothesis of deliberate orchestration from above, and it remains one of the most cited scholarly frameworks for the May 1998 violence.

Jemma Purdey's monograph Anti-Chinese Violence in Indonesia, 1996–1999 places the May 1998 events in a longer trajectory, arguing that anti-Chinese sentiment had been politically instrumentalised throughout the late New Order period; the regime had maintained a deeply ambivalent relationship with the Chinese-Indonesian minority — relying on Tionghoa business figures as economic actors while allowing official and popular anti-Chinese discourse to serve as a political safety valve. The riots of May 1998 were, on this reading, not an eruption of spontaneous popular hatred but the catastrophic culmination of decades of managed resentment.37

Sexual Violence: Documentation and Debate

The aspect of the May 1998 violence that has generated the most sustained scholarly, political, and legal controversy is the documented mass sexual assault of ethnic Chinese-Indonesian women during the riots. The evidence comes from multiple independent investigations of varying methodologies, which reached convergent findings on the fact of widespread sexual violence while diverging in their counts and on the question of whether the attacks were deliberately organised.

The most comprehensive official investigation was conducted by the Joint Fact-Finding Team (Tim Gabungan Pencari Fakta, TGPF), established by President Habibie on 23 July 1998 and composed of seventeen members drawn from government ministries, Komnas HAM, and civil society organisations. After three months of investigation, the TGPF released its Final Report on 23 October 1998 and an executive summary published the following year by Komnas Perempuan (the National Commission on Violence Against Women). The TGPF found: 52 rapes, 14 cases of rape accompanied by other violence, 10 instances of sexual torture, and 9 cases of sexual harassment — covering Jakarta, Medan, and Surabaya. The TGPF concluded that the evidence showed "intentional elements" in the organisation of the violence, and that the Armed Forces had "failed to anticipate" and "responded tardily" to the riots. The team further alleged that Lieutenant General Prabowo Subianto had met with military and civilian figures on the night of 14 May to discuss organising the violence, though it stopped short of a definitive legal finding, and Prabowo denied the allegation.38

The Volunteer Team for Humanity (TRuK), using a broader self-report methodology, recorded 168 cases of sexual assault in the period: 152 in Jakarta during 13–15 May, and 16 in Surakarta, Medan, Palembang, and Surabaya. At least 20 victims had died by 14 July 1998. The discrepancy between TRuK's 168 cases and the TGPF's 85 cases (combining all categories) reflects a difference in evidentiary standards — TRuK recorded reports; the TGPF required investigative corroboration — and the gap does not represent a factual disagreement about the occurrence of violence but a methodological one about how to count it. Both figures, however, were contested at the time by government officials and military spokesmen who disputed the events' occurrence.

The UN Special Rapporteur on Violence Against Women, Radhika Coomaraswamy, conducted an official mission to Indonesia from 20 November to 4 December 1998 and subsequently reported to the UN Commission on Human Rights that "the pattern of violence described by victims, witnesses, and human rights defenders clearly indicated that such rape was widespread." Coomaraswamy documented systematic intimidation: Chinese-Indonesian community members and activists received death threats and menacing letters designed to silence witnesses; the murder of activist Ita Martadinata Haryono in October 1998 — days before she was scheduled to testify about the rapes before the US Congress — added to the atmosphere of suppression. The Special Rapporteur also noted that the stigma attached to rape in Indonesian society meant that many victims had not reported their assaults, suggesting the documented cases undercounted the true extent of the violence.39

Human Rights Watch documented both the violence and the official response to it. Its September 1998 report "Indonesia: The Damaging Debate on Rapes of Ethnic Chinese Women" characterised the sexual attacks as "almost certainly orchestrated, with anti-Chinese sentiment manipulated by mob leaders," and criticised Indonesian government officials for attempting to suppress evidence of the rapes. Indonesia's State Intelligence Coordinating Agency chief stated publicly that the rape allegations were "spread for political purposes to defame Indonesia"; the national police commander threatened to prosecute organisations that published rape accounts without complete documentation. HRW documented how this official hostility served to intimidate survivors and witnesses.40

The scholarly debate on the May 1998 sexual violence centres on three overlapping questions: (1) the numbers and their methodological basis; (2) whether the attacks were spontaneous or organised, and if organised, by whom; and (3) why accountability has been so systematically denied. On the question of orchestration, the TGPF's finding of "intentional elements," the NSA's declassified US intelligence suggesting military involvement, and the Panggabean-Smith frame-shifting analysis all point toward deliberate manipulation of crowds — but no criminal court has found any individual responsible. The failure of accountability has itself become a political issue: in June 2025, Indonesia's Minister of Culture Fadli Zon publicly described the documented rapes as mere "rumours" (rumor), prompting an immediate rebuttal from Asia Justice and Rights (AJAR), which cited the TGPF, Komnas Perempuan, and the UN Special Rapporteur's report as definitive counter-evidence against denial.41

The murder of Ita Martadinata Haryono has remained a symbol of the impunity surrounding the May 1998 violence. As Resty Woro Yuniar reported in 2025, no perpetrators of either the sexual violence or the activist killing have been brought to justice; Chinese-Indonesian community organisations continue to mark 13–15 May as a day of memorialisation and advocacy, using the slogan "resist forgetting."42 Arzia Tivany Wargadiredja's documentary journalism, and Monika Swasti Winarnita's scholarly reflection, have placed the failure of justice in the context of Indonesia's broader unresolved relationship with its authoritarian past.43 Komnas Perempuan, founded directly in response to the May 1998 violence by Presidential Decree No. 181/1998, has continued to document cases and to urge prosecution — without, to date, legal result.44

Evidentiary note. The documented sexual violence of May 1998 rests on findings by the TGPF (a government-appointed body), the UN Special Rapporteur (an international independent investigator), Human Rights Watch, and Komnas Perempuan (the National Commission on Violence Against Women). Scholarly consensus holds that widespread sexual violence occurred. The questions of exact numbers and deliberate orchestration remain contested; but denial of the violence's occurrence — as illustrated by the 2025 ministerial statement — is contradicted by the weight of documented evidence and has been formally rejected by international human rights investigators.

The Fall: Parliament Occupied, the Military Steps Aside, Suharto Resigns

By 18 May 1998, the political situation had reached a tipping point. In Jakarta alone, tens of thousands of university students from campuses across the city converged on the Gedung DPR/MPR — the parliamentary complex in the Senayan district — and occupied its grounds, its roof, and ultimately its interior, declaring they would not leave until Suharto resigned. At the peak of the occupation, an estimated 20,000 to 50,000 students were present; the images of young Indonesians draped in national flags across the roof of the parliament building were transmitted around the world.

On that same day, an extraordinary defection occurred. Harmoko — the chairman of Golkar, the regime party, and the Speaker of the DPR, a man who had served Suharto loyally for decades — announced at a press conference that Suharto should resign "for the sake of national unity." He gave a five-day deadline, threatening that the DPR would initiate proceedings if Suharto did not act. That a figure of Harmoko's closeness to the regime should deliver this statement signalled a collapse of elite consensus so complete that it amounted to the withdrawal of institutional support — the final strut being pulled from beneath the president.45 Aspinall's analysis emphasises that the parliamentary occupation, combined with elite defections, created a constitutional path: the threat of an MPR session to remove Suharto by formal procedure meant that continued resistance would require extra-constitutional military action — which Wiranto was unwilling to provide.46

On 19 May, the Muslim intellectual Nurcholish Madjid met with Suharto at the palace and proposed a formula — a cabinet reshuffle and a "reform committee" — that Suharto initially appeared to consider; by the following day, it was clear that such measures were too limited to satisfy either the streets or the elite. On 20 May, the national holiday of Hari Kebangkitan Nasional (National Awakening Day, marking the founding of Budi Utomo in 1908), an estimated 500,000 demonstrators took to the streets across Indonesia — in Jakarta, Yogyakarta, Bandung, Surabaya, and Medan.47 On the same day, 14 cabinet ministers announced they would not serve in any new cabinet that Suharto proposed, a coordinated withdrawal that left the president without a functioning government. Amien Rais, who had proposed a "million-person march" on the capital for that day, cancelled it when General Wiranto's forces made clear that Prabowo Subianto's units were still deployed in the capital and their behaviour could not be guaranteed.

The role of the military in these final days was decisive and divided. Mietzner's account argues that Wiranto's decision not to enforce a security decree that Suharto reportedly issued on 18 May — conferring authority on Wiranto to use any measures necessary to restore order — was the decisive act of the transition. By effectively declining to exercise that authority against the students and the parliament, Wiranto removed the final coercive prop from beneath Suharto's rule.48 Declassified US intelligence suggests that Prabowo Subianto came to Merdeka Palace on the night of 21 May demanding command of military forces, and that Habibie, already being prepared for the presidential succession, briefly left the palace; the episode, whose precise details remain contested, illustrates the degree to which the transition was occurring in a climate of acute factional military uncertainty.49

At 9:00 AM on Thursday, 21 May 1998, Suharto delivered his resignation statement before the cameras. He cited "the difficult circumstances of the national situation" and expressed his hope that "the unity and the integrity of the nation" would be maintained. He announced that authority would pass to the Vice President in accordance with Article 8 of the 1945 Constitution. The text was brief; the moment was historic. Theodore Friend describes the scene: Suharto read with apparent composure, but his hands, as he set down the paper, were trembling.50 Immediately following the statement, Habibie was sworn in as Indonesia's third president before a panel of Supreme Court justices. The thirty-two-year New Order was over.

On 22 May, Wiranto removed Prabowo from his command of Kostrad; Prabowo was subsequently dismissed from the military entirely, though he would eventually re-enter politics, running for president four times before winning the election of 2024. Elson notes that the constitutional character of the transfer — vice president succeeding president under the existing constitution, without military coup or foreign intervention — was itself an achievement of sorts: the New Order died within its own legal framework rather than through rupture.51

Habibie's Accession and the Opening of Reformasi

B.J. Habibie's presidency lasted seventeen months (May 1998–October 1999), and it inaugurated a process of democratisation that neither he nor his sponsors had planned. The student movement greeted his accession with scepticism — he was, in their phrase, "Suharto's duplicate," a symbol of the very nepotism the movement had opposed — but Habibie moved more rapidly and more fundamentally than most observers had anticipated. Harold Crouch's comprehensive study of Indonesian political reform judges the Habibie period as having "set in motion changes that were, in themselves, sufficient to constitute a democratic transition": the release of political prisoners, lifting of press restrictions, legalisation of new political parties, removal of the military's reserved parliamentary seats (fraksi ABRI), regional autonomy laws, and the unprecedented decision to allow a referendum on East Timorese independence.52

Several of Habibie's early acts were specifically responses to the May 1998 violence. Presidential Decree No. 181/1998 established Komnas Perempuan (the National Commission on Violence Against Women), directly in response to the documented sexual violence. The TGPF itself was established on 23 July 1998 as a presidential initiative, and Habibie issued a formal apology in July 1998 for the failure to protect citizens during the riots — though the apology was not followed by prosecution of perpetrators. The East Timor referendum of August 1999, which resulted in an overwhelming vote for independence and precipitated Indonesia's most consequential post-Suharto international crisis, ultimately cost Habibie his presidency: he lost the October 1999 parliamentary vote on his accountability report to Abdurrahman Wahid, who became Indonesia's fourth president and the first elected through a genuinely contested process.

The international dimension of Suharto's fall generated its own retrospective debates. Former US Secretary of State Lawrence Eagleburger, interviewed after the fall, stated: "We supported the IMF as it overthrew Suharto." Camdessus, at his retirement, used the formulation quoted above: "We created the conditions that obliged Suharto to leave his job." John McBeth, whose investigative journalism drew on the declassified Clinton phone records and diplomatic cables, argued that the Clinton administration's insistence on structural conditionality — removing monopolies and subsidies that hit the political and popular foundations of the New Order simultaneously — was a key accelerant of the political crisis, even if it was not intended as a tool of regime change.53 This argument, that the IMF programme served as an inadvertent political solvent, sits uneasily with the Fund's institutional self-presentation but has substantial evidential support in the sequence of events.

Reformasi — "Reform." The post-1998 era of Indonesian democratic transformation, beginning with Suharto's resignation on 21 May 1998. Reformasi encompassed constitutional amendments, free elections, press freedom, decentralisation, and the gradual dismantling of New Order political institutions. The word was the central slogan of the 1998 student movement before becoming the name of the era it produced.
TGPF — Tim Gabungan Pencari Fakta (Joint Fact-Finding Team). Established by President Habibie on 23 July 1998 to investigate the May 1998 riots. The team's Final Report, released 23 October 1998, documented 85 victims of sexual violence in total — 52 rapes, 14 cases of rape combined with other violence, 10 instances of sexual torture, and 9 cases of sexual harassment — concluded that "intentional elements" were present in the organisation of violence, and recommended criminal prosecution — recommendations that were never implemented.

Summary

The 1997–98 crisis was not a bolt from the blue but the delayed reckoning of structural vulnerabilities accumulated across three decades of New Order growth: unhedged corporate dollar debt, a fragile banking sector, and a patronage economy incapable of reform from within. When the Thai baht's collapse triggered financial contagion, Indonesia suffered more than any other regional economy. The rupiah lost more than 80 percent of its value; GDP contracted 13.1 percent; inflation hit 65 percent. The IMF programme that was supposed to stabilise the economy instead generated intense political resentment — the Camdessus photograph crystallised a global narrative of imposed humiliation — and the fuel price increases of 4 May ignited the political catastrophe that followed. Suharto's seven-week passage from his seventh reelection (10 March) to his resignation (21 May) traced the collapse of all four supports of his rule: the economic mandate (destroyed by the crisis), popular legitimacy (destroyed by KKN and the price rises), military loyalty (fractured between Wiranto and Prabowo, ultimately withdrawn by the former), and elite consensus (dissolved by Harmoko's defection and the cabinet refusals). The three days of May 1998 rioting left approximately 1,200 dead and produced mass sexual violence against ethnic-Chinese women — documented by a government fact-finding team, a UN Special Rapporteur, and Human Rights Watch — for which no perpetrator has been convicted. The hinge of modern Indonesian history was violent, contested, and unresolved.

Review & Discussion Questions

36.1 What structural features of Indonesia's economy made it more vulnerable to financial contagion in 1997 than, say, Malaysia or the Philippines? How do Radelet and Sachs's "liquidity panic" argument and the "structural insolvency" view differ, and what are the implications of each for evaluating the IMF's programme?
36.2 Analyse the Camdessus photograph as a political event. Why did the image of Camdessus standing over the seated Suharto become so charged, and what does the controversy it generated reveal about Indonesia's relationship to sovereignty and foreign economic intervention?
36.3 The student movement of 1997–98 adopted a deliberate "campus strategy" that proved effective. What were its advantages and its limitations? Under what circumstances might it have failed?
36.4 Panggabean and Smith argue that anti-Chinese riots in May 1998 occurred where security forces had previously allowed student protests to spread beyond campuses — a "frame-shifting" strategy. Assess the evidence for this interpretation. What are its implications for assigning responsibility for the violence?
36.5 The TGPF documented 52 rapes plus other sexual violence; TRuK documented 168 cases of sexual assault. The discrepancy has been used both to dismiss the claims of widespread violence and to argue the true figures are higher than the official count. How should historians use figures from investigations that used different methodologies and evidentiary standards?
36.6 Suharto's fall was caused, according to different accounts, primarily by: (a) the economic crisis; (b) IMF conditionality; (c) the student movement; (d) military factionalism; or (e) elite defection. Which factor do you find most causally significant, and how do the others relate to it?
36.7 Why did Habibie, a figure associated with the Suharto patronage network and widely distrusted by the student movement, preside over what Harold Crouch calls "sufficient" democratic transition? What does this tell us about the dynamics of elite-led democratisation?
36.8 The mass rapes of May 1998 remain judicially unresolved more than a quarter-century later. In June 2025, a government minister publicly described them as "rumours." What political and institutional factors explain the persistence of impunity, and what is at stake — for victims, for Indonesian democracy, and for historical truth — in the ongoing denial?
