Learning Objectives

  • Explain Indonesia's role in organizing the 1955 Bandung Conference and assess its lasting legacy for the Non-Aligned Movement and the Global South.
  • Describe Indonesia's founding role in ASEAN, its claim to de facto regional leadership, and the limits of that leadership under the "ASEAN Way."
  • Analyze the tensions in Indonesia's relationship with China — economic interdependence alongside strategic wariness over the North Natuna Sea.
  • Situate Indonesia's security partnerships with the United States, Australia, and Japan within the bebas-aktif framework.
  • Assess Indonesia's "middle power" aspirations as expressed through the G20 presidency, BRICS accession, OECD candidacy, and Palestinian solidarity.
  • Evaluate the West Papua question as a dimension of Pacific diplomacy and its implications for Indonesian sovereignty claims.

On 2 September 1948, with Indonesia still fighting for its independence against Dutch forces, Vice-President Mohammad Hatta rose before the Working Committee of the Central Indonesian National Committee to deliver what would become the foundational text of Indonesian foreign policy. The Cold War had barely begun, yet the pressure on the young republic was already intense: Moscow urged alignment with the communist bloc, Washington expected allegiance to the West. Hatta refused both. Indonesia, he declared, would steer a course that was bebas — free, beholden to no foreign bloc — and aktif — active, constructive, and engaged in shaping a more just world order. That formula, bebas-aktif, has governed Indonesian diplomacy for more than seventy-five years. It has survived the Cold War, the fall of Suharto, the rise of China, and the deepening rivalry between Beijing and Washington that now reorganizes the entire Indo-Pacific. Understanding how Indonesia has practised, adapted, and at times strained that doctrine is essential to understanding its place in the contemporary world.

Bebas-Aktif: Freedom as a Foreign Policy

The bebas-aktif principle was not a product of academic theorizing but of immediate political necessity. Indonesia in 1948 was a republic recognized by neither of the Cold War superpowers in any permanent sense, dependent on international support to resist Dutch re-colonization, yet deeply suspicious of the strings that would come with alignment. Hatta's formulation sought a third way: Indonesia would be "free" in the sense of not submitting to any great-power agenda, and "active" in the sense of playing a constructive role in world affairs rather than retreating into isolation. In a 1953 article in Foreign Affairs, Hatta elaborated the doctrine, framing it as a principled refusal to allow Indonesia to become a passive object of superpower competition.1

The distinction between bebas-aktif and neutrality is significant. Neutral states, in the classical European tradition, seek to abstain from involvement in others' conflicts. Bebas-aktif was something more assertive: an insistence that Indonesia had interests and values of its own to advance — decolonization, the peaceful resolution of disputes, the dignity of post-colonial nations — and would pursue them vigorously, on its own terms. The "active" element committed Indonesia to engagement with the United Nations, multilateral institutions, and regional organizations, even as it declined to join any military alliance.2

In practice, bebas-aktif has been a doctrine under constant pressure. Sukarno bent it toward an anti-Western radicalism in the 1960s; Suharto bent it toward a pro-Western developmentalism in the New Order years without formally abandoning its language. In the democratic Reformasi era, every president from Habibie to Prabowo has invoked bebas-aktif while pursuing policies that observers variously read as balanced non-alignment, strategic ambiguity, or calculated multi-alignment. The phrase functions simultaneously as strategic doctrine, domestic legitimating narrative, and diplomatic calling card — a flexibility that explains both its durability and the sometimes heated debates about what it actually requires in any given situation.

Bebas-aktif — Indonesia's foundational foreign policy doctrine, meaning "free and active": independence from great-power bloc alignment combined with proactive engagement in international affairs to promote peace, decolonization, and a just world order. First articulated by Vice-President Mohammad Hatta in 1948.

The Spirit of Bandung: April 1955

The most dramatic early expression of bebas-aktif was Indonesia's hosting of the Asian-African Conference in April 1955, in the hill city of Bandung. Five nations co-sponsored the gathering — Burma, India, Indonesia, Pakistan, and Sri Lanka — and twenty-nine governments from across Asia and Africa attended, representing more than half of the world's population, most of them recently independent or still striving for independence from colonial rule.3 No comparable assembly of post-colonial nations had ever taken place.

Sukarno opened the conference on April 18, 1955, with a speech of extraordinary rhetorical force, hailing the gathering as the expression of what he called "the undying, the indomitable, the invincible spirit" of peoples who had struggled against colonial domination, and framing it as the moment when the long-silenced majority of humanity was finally speaking with its own voice.4 The phrase captured the mood of a generation for whom independence was still new and its consolidation still uncertain.

The conference's Final Communiqué adopted ten foundational principles — known as the Dasa Sila Bandung (the Ten Bandung Principles) — which included respect for sovereignty and territorial integrity, non-interference in internal affairs, non-aggression, equality among nations large and small, and the peaceful resolution of disputes. These built upon the Panchsheel (Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence) that India and China had articulated in 1954 but expanded them into a broader post-colonial declaration of how the new nations of the Global South wished to conduct international relations.5 The principles anticipated in several respects the norms that would be codified in the UN Charter's decolonization resolutions and eventually in the NAM's own founding documents.

The Bandung Conference did not immediately produce formal institutions, but its intellectual and political legacy was enormous. It directly inspired the creation of the Non-Aligned Movement (NAM) in 1961, at the first NAM summit in Belgrade under Tito, Nasser, Nehru, Sukarno, and Nkrumah, and the Group of 77 (G77) developing nations in 1964 — the institutional vehicles through which the Global South sought to speak collectively on economic and political questions in the UN system.6 Indonesia remained a prominent voice in both forums throughout the Cold War, and Sukarno invested heavily in cultivating Third World solidarity as a counterweight to superpower pressure.

Yet the "spirit of Bandung" was, from the start, more fragile than its rhetoric suggested. The solidarity it generated had visibly weakened by the end of the 1960s, as most of the original participants had changed governments or shifted alignments under Cold War pressure — Indonesia itself most dramatically, with Suharto's decisively pro-Western New Order replacing Sukarno's radical non-alignment after 1966.7 What endured was not a coherent bloc but a powerful collective memory and a normative claim: that the post-colonial nations have a distinctive voice in world affairs and a right to organize it on terms they themselves define. That claim remains alive in BRICS, in the G77, and in Indonesia's own self-presentation as a bridge between North and South.

ASEAN: Building the Architecture of Southeast Asia

If the Bandung Conference expressed Indonesia's global ambitions, the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), founded in 1967, was where those ambitions acquired institutional form close to home. The timing is significant: ASEAN emerged just as Suharto's New Order was consolidating power and turning away from Sukarno's confrontational approach. Where Sukarno had waged Konfrontasi — armed confrontation — against Malaysia and Britain between 1963 and 1966, Suharto's government dispatched Foreign Minister Adam Malik to help design a new regional organization alongside Malaysia, the Philippines, Singapore, and Thailand.8

The Bangkok Declaration of August 8, 1967, signed by five foreign ministers at the Royal Thai Golf Club before a formal ceremony in Bangkok, officially established ASEAN as an organization for regional cooperation in economic, social, cultural, and security matters. Adam Malik signed for Indonesia alongside Malaysia's Tun Abdul Razak, the Philippines' Narciso R. Ramos, Singapore's Sinnathamby Rajaratnam, and Thailand's Thanat Khoman. The new organization was deliberately non-ideological in tone: it sought to promote regional stability, support economic development, and maintain enough distance from both superpower blocs to preserve members' freedom of action — an institutionalization, at the regional level, of Indonesia's bebas-aktif instinct.9

Within ASEAN, Indonesia has occupied a position of de facto leadership that its formal equality with other members does not fully express. It is ASEAN's largest member by population (more than 280 million people), land area, and economy; it sits astride the Strait of Malacca and Lombok Strait, the critical chokepoints of regional maritime trade; and since the early 1970s it has typically provided the diplomatic initiative that gave ASEAN its direction. Indonesia brokered the 1991 Paris Agreement on Cambodia after a decade of regional divisions over Vietnamese occupation, pioneered the ASEAN Regional Forum in 1994 as a platform for regional security dialogue, and under President Jokowi's administration formulated the ASEAN Outlook on the Indo-Pacific (AOIP), adopted in 2019, which positioned ASEAN collectively as a strategic bridge between the Indian and Pacific oceans rather than a passive arena of great-power competition.10

The concept of "ASEAN centrality" — the insistence that ASEAN and its associated frameworks (the East Asia Summit, the ASEAN Defence Ministers' Meeting Plus, and related forums) should be the primary platforms through which great powers engage Southeast Asia — is essentially Indonesia's foreign policy preference elevated into a regional norm. For Jakarta, ASEAN centrality is the institutional expression of bebas-aktif: it ensures that no single great power sets the agenda for the region's affairs, and that smaller Southeast Asian states can find collective cover for policies they could not sustain individually.

ASEAN's limitations, however, are also well understood. The organization operates by consensus and observes strict non-interference in members' domestic affairs — what is often called "the ASEAN Way." This has repeatedly prevented unified responses to crises: Cambodia and Laos, deeply economically dependent on China, have blocked ASEAN consensus statements on South China Sea incidents; Myanmar's 2021 military coup produced years of internal ASEAN deadlock as the junta systematically ignored the organization's own Five-Point Consensus.11 Indonesia is acutely aware of these structural limitations but has consistently defended the ASEAN framework as the least-bad alternative to competing arrangements that would force members into great-power alignments.

The Dragon's Embrace: Economic Interdependence with China

Relations between Indonesia and China have never been simple, and their contemporary complexity has deep roots. The New Order's virulent anti-communism led to the suspension of diplomatic ties in 1967 — following China's perceived support for the PKI (Indonesian Communist Party) in the turbulent period before 1965 — and their restoration only in 1990, after more than two decades of mutual suspicion. Throughout the New Order, ethnic Chinese Indonesians (Tionghoa) faced discriminatory legislation, identity restrictions, and periodic violence, with the 1998 riots that accompanied Suharto's fall including targeted attacks on Chinese-Indonesian communities in Jakarta and other cities. The emotional and political weight of this history continues to shadow an economic relationship that has, in raw terms, become Indonesia's most consequential bilateral connection.

China is today Indonesia's largest single trading partner by a wide margin, receiving roughly 22 percent of Indonesian exports and supplying a comparable share of imports. Chinese investment in Indonesia accelerated dramatically after 2013 under President Xi Jinping's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), transforming infrastructure, energy, and manufacturing sectors across the archipelago. The most visible and contentious BRI project was the Jakarta-Bandung High-Speed Railway — the first high-speed rail line in Southeast Asia — funded largely through loans from the China Development Bank and built by a Chinese-Indonesian consortium. Construction began in 2016, but the project was plagued by land-acquisition difficulties and feasibility shortfalls: by the time of its partial opening in 2023, the project had exceeded its original budget by approximately US$2 billion, requiring emergency allocations from the Indonesian state budget despite initial government pledges that no public funds would be needed.12 The episode became an object lesson in the risks embedded in large-scale BRI infrastructure transactions — not because the Chinese partner had acted in bad faith, but because optimistic feasibility studies and the complex political economy of land acquisition in Indonesia had been systematically underestimated.

The second, economically far more consequential strand of Chinese investment is in the nickel sector. Indonesia holds the world's largest nickel reserves, a mineral critical to the manufacture of electric vehicle batteries. The Jokowi government, from approximately 2020 onward, pursued a policy of "hilirisasi" (downstreaming) — banning the export of unprocessed nickel ore to force foreign investors to establish processing facilities within Indonesia rather than simply extracting raw material. Chinese firms, led by Tsingshan Holding Group, responded at scale, constructing the Morowali Industrial Park in Central Sulawesi — described as Asia's largest nickel processing complex — and a network of associated smelters and battery-component plants funded through Chinese state-owned bank loans.13 By 2023–24, Chinese-backed operations dominated Indonesia's nickel processing industry.

This industrial integration produced precisely the outcome Jokowi intended: Indonesian manufacturing employment expanded, value was captured domestically rather than exported as raw ore, and GDP growth in resource-processing regions accelerated. But it also embedded Chinese capital so deeply in Indonesia's most strategically important mineral sector that the economic relationship acquired a structural character that will be difficult to reverse. Several large facilities imported thousands of Chinese workers, generating social tensions and accusations that the "downstreaming" policy was benefiting Chinese firms at the expense of local employment. The debate over how to maximize Indonesian gains from Chinese investment while managing its political and social side-effects is ongoing in policy and academic circles.

The North Natuna Sea: Where the Nine-Dash Line Meets Indonesian Sovereignty

Indonesia has consistently maintained that it is not a claimant state in the South China Sea disputes — an accurate formal position, since Indonesia makes no claims to the Spratly or Paracel Islands. But this formulation masks a significant underlying tension. China's nine-dash line — the U-shaped boundary through which Beijing asserts "historic rights" over most of the South China Sea — overlaps at its southernmost extent with the northwestern corner of Indonesia's Exclusive Economic Zone around the Natuna Islands, specifically in the gas-rich Natuna D-Alpha block northeast of the islands themselves.14

Indonesia's response has been carefully calibrated. Rather than pursue the kind of legal challenge that the Philippines brought to the Permanent Court of Arbitration (winning a 2016 ruling that rejected China's nine-dash line under UNCLOS), Indonesia has insisted it has no territorial dispute with China — because sovereignty over the Natuna Islands is not in question — while simultaneously asserting that its EEZ rights under international law are non-negotiable. In a pointed symbolic act in 2017, Indonesia's Coordinating Maritime Affairs Ministry renamed the body of water north of the Natuna Islands "Laut Natuna Utara" (North Natuna Sea), using official cartography to establish Indonesian nomenclature over waters that China's maps still mark within its claimed maritime boundaries.15

The underlying tension has erupted periodically into direct confrontations. In October 2024, Indonesia's state energy company PT Pertamina was conducting seismic surveys in the Natuna D-Alpha block — routine exploration in Indonesia's own EEZ — when a Chinese Coast Guard vessel, CCG 5302, entered Indonesia's continental shelf on October 16. Indonesian Navy and maritime security forces from Bakamla deployed multiple vessels and repeatedly expelled the Chinese ship, which returned each time; the standoff continued for days. In a notable departure from Indonesia's traditionally quiet diplomatic approach, the Indonesian Navy and Bakamla publicly released photographs and video footage of the confrontation, a transparency strategy that observers compared to the Philippines' practice under President Ferdinand Marcos Jr.16

President Prabowo Subianto, barely two weeks into his presidency when the standoff unfolded, faced a structural dilemma that his predecessors had also confronted: the largest economy in ASEAN, whose investment the Indonesian government actively courts, is simultaneously the state whose maritime forces periodically violate Indonesia's EEZ. His government's decision to publicize the confrontation rather than manage it quietly signaled a recalibration toward greater firmness on sovereignty while preserving the economic relationship.17 Shortly afterward, in December 2024, the Prabowo government agreed to a bilateral "maritime resources development" pact with China covering the Natuna area — a pact criticized by Indonesian legal scholars as potentially lending indirect credibility to Chinese maritime claims, though the government insisted it contained no such concession. The episode illustrated the perpetual tension in Indonesia's China policy between economic calculation and sovereign assertion.

Nine-dash line — China's claimed maritime boundary in the South China Sea, depicted as a U-shaped line on Chinese maps encompassing approximately 90 percent of that sea. The 2016 Permanent Court of Arbitration ruling (in the Philippines v. China case) found it incompatible with UNCLOS. Indonesia does not accept that its EEZ rights are affected by this claim.

Washington, Canberra, Tokyo: The Democratic Partners

While China dominates the economic dimension of Indonesia's external relations, the United States, Australia, and Japan play indispensable roles in the security dimension — and together constitute what Indonesian strategists have described as a structural counterweight that prevents any single power from dominating the archipelago.

Relations with the United States have been complicated by the long shadow of the New Order. Washington largely muted its human rights concerns during the Suharto decades, when Indonesia was valued as an anti-communist anchor in Southeast Asia, but after the 1991 Santa Cruz massacre in East Timor and the military violence surrounding the 1999 East Timorese independence referendum, the United States imposed restrictions on military assistance to Indonesia's armed forces (TNI). Those restrictions were not fully lifted until 2005, creating a deep freeze of more than a decade in the US-Indonesian military relationship — a gap that was also an opening, since restoration of ties after 2005 could be negotiated on more equal democratic terms than the patron-client framework of the Cold War.18

Today US-Indonesia security cooperation is substantial: more than 170 military-to-military engagements occur annually, including the Garuda Shield exercise (an annual bilateral exercise that has grown into a major multilateral event, "Super Garuda Shield," involving multiple regional partners) and Cooperation Afloat Readiness and Training (CARAT). In 2025–26, the two governments unveiled a Major Defence Cooperation Partnership (MDCP) focused on autonomous military systems, reflecting both Indonesia's interest in advanced defense technology and Washington's strategic recalibration in the Indo-Pacific. Indonesia has been careful to frame this partnership within bebas-aktif: it does not accept a formal alliance, rejects the permanent stationing of US troops, and insists it is building indigenous military capability rather than joining any containment architecture directed at China.

The Australia-Indonesia relationship has a distinctive character shaped by geographic proximity, a shared maritime environment, and a history of deep strategic interest mingled with periodic friction — over East Timor, over Australian surveillance activities, over the treatment of asylum seekers. The Lombok Treaty, signed on November 13, 2006, by Australian Foreign Minister Alexander Downer and Indonesian Foreign Minister Hassan Wirajuda, established a bilateral framework for defense cooperation, law enforcement, counter-terrorism, intelligence, and maritime security — the first formal security architecture between the two neighbors.19 It was itself a recovery from the chill that followed Australia's role in facilitating East Timor's 1999 independence vote, which many Indonesians experienced as an external intervention in national territorial integrity.

In February 2026, the relationship took a qualitative step further when Prime Minister Anthony Albanese visited Jakarta to sign the Australia-Indonesia Treaty on Common Security — informally called the "Jakarta Treaty" — which elevated defense cooperation to a treaty-level commitment and permitted the armed forces of each nation to operate from the other's territory, a degree of formal military interoperability without precedent in the bilateral relationship.20 The Jakarta Treaty represents Australia's most explicit statement yet that its security depends, in a structural sense, on Indonesia's stability and goodwill — and vice versa.

Japan's relationship with Indonesia has a longer and more complex history, marked by the traumatic memory of the 1942–45 occupation, the subsequent war reparations, and a half-century of economic partnership that evolved into genuine strategic alignment. Diplomatic normalization came with the Japan-Indonesia Peace Treaty of 1958, which included Japanese reparations commitments that effectively became the first tranche of Japan's Official Development Assistance (ODA) to Indonesia. Between 1967 and 1999, Indonesia was the single largest recipient of Japanese ODA loans, receiving some 3,432 billion yen (approximately US$34 billion), or 18.6 percent of Japan's total ODA loan portfolio — an extraordinary transfer that underwrote highway networks, irrigation systems, ports, and early industrial infrastructure across the archipelago.21

The economic relationship was deepened by the Indonesia-Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (IJEPA), signed on August 20, 2007, by Prime Minister Shinzō Abe and President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono, entering into force on July 1, 2008. In the decade following implementation, Japanese direct investment in Indonesia rose from an average of 2.6 percent of Japan's total regional investment portfolio (2000–2008) to 28.9 percent (2009–2017); nearly 2,000 Japanese companies now operate in Indonesia, employing approximately 7.2 million Indonesians in automotive, electronics, and energy sectors.22 In September 2023, marking the 65th anniversary of diplomatic relations, the two nations elevated their bilateral relationship to a "Comprehensive and Strategic Partnership," adding new tracks covering Indonesia's new capital Nusantara, renewable energy transition, maritime security, disaster risk reduction, and advanced manufacturing.23

The common thread running through all three of these relationships is that Indonesia accepts deep practical cooperation — military exercises, intelligence sharing, treaty-level defense commitments — without accepting formal alliance membership. This is not cognitive dissonance but strategic design: the United States, Australia, and Japan each provide specific capabilities that Indonesia cannot yet supply from its own resources (advanced defense technology, maritime surveillance, infrastructure finance), while Indonesia's refusal to join any formal alliance preserves its bebas-aktif credentials and keeps it from becoming a pawn in anyone else's strategic calculations.

The G20, BRICS, and the Middle Power Project

Indonesia's most visible signal of its global ambitions in the contemporary era came when it held the G20 presidency in 2022. Indonesia is the only ASEAN member in the G20 — a structural singularity that gives Jakarta unusual leverage in the world's primary forum for economic governance and a platform that no other Southeast Asian state can access.

The 2022 G20 summit, held in Bali on November 15–16 under the theme "Recover Together, Recover Stronger," was Indonesia's most prominent moment on the global diplomatic stage since the Bandung Conference. President Jokowi inherited the presidency at an extraordinarily difficult moment: Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 had divided G20 members along political lines, with Western nations demanding condemnation of Russia while China and India refused, threatening to destroy any joint declaration. Indonesia's diplomatic task was to produce summit language that enough members could accept — no small achievement given that two G20 members were actively at war.24

The Bali declaration's language on Ukraine was carefully hedged: it stated that "most members strongly condemned" the war while acknowledging that "there were other views and different assessments of the situation." Critics argued this formula fell short of a genuine condemnation; defenders pointed out that obtaining any consensus language at all on an active war, given the G20's composition, was itself a significant diplomatic result. The deeper questions — whether G20 commitments on global health financing, food security, and debt restructuring for developing nations would be implemented — remained open after Bali, as they generally do after summit declarations.25 What was less contested was the symbolic significance: a Southeast Asian Muslim-majority democracy had successfully chaired the G20 at a moment of profound geopolitical crisis, while maintaining workable relationships with the United States, China, Russia, and the European Union simultaneously.

Indonesia's accession to BRICS, formalized on January 6, 2025, was in many ways the logical extension of this middle-power logic. Indonesia had been invited to join at the 2023 Johannesburg expansion summit but deferred membership until after its own presidential election — a careful sequencing that ensured the decision would not become a campaign issue.26 The accession made Indonesia the first Southeast Asian nation to hold full BRICS membership, and Indonesian Foreign Minister Sugiono explicitly framed it as "an embodiment of Indonesia's independent and active foreign policy" — a deliberate invocation of bebas-aktif to reassure interlocutors that BRICS membership was not a strategic pivot toward China and Russia.27

The most striking aspect of Indonesia's BRICS strategy was its parallel move. Simultaneously with BRICS accession, the Prabowo government applied for membership in the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD), the club of predominantly wealthy democracies led by the United States, European Union, and Japan. Pursuing BRICS and OECD membership simultaneously is the clearest possible expression of multi-alignment: Indonesia explicitly refuses to choose between the Global South economic solidarity platform and the Western institutional framework. Whether this dual-track approach represents genuine strategic coherence — maximizing access to both institutional networks — or a form of calculated ambiguity that ultimately satisfies neither, is itself contested among Indonesian foreign policy scholars.28

Islam, Palestine, and the Islamic World

Indonesia is home to the world's largest Muslim population — approximately 240 million people, roughly 87 percent of a national population exceeding 280 million — and this demographic reality shapes its foreign policy in ways that are real, complex, and frequently misunderstood outside the country. Indonesia is emphatically not an "Islamic state": Pancasila, the national ideology, defines the republic on the basis of five principles (belief in God, humanitarian values, unity, deliberative democracy, and social justice) without specifying Islam, and the country's constitutional structure is secular. Yet no Indonesian government can afford to be perceived as indifferent to the Palestinian cause or to the suffering of Muslim minorities elsewhere, and the result is a foreign policy that maintains strong Islamic solidarity on certain issues while refusing to translate that solidarity into military alignment with any Islamic grouping.

Indonesia does not maintain diplomatic relations with Israel and has not done so since independence. The historical roots of this position include a meaningful early debt: the Grand Mufti of Jerusalem, Amin al-Husseini, helped persuade Arab League members to recognize Indonesian independence in 1945 and lobbied against British logistical support for Dutch forces during the National Revolution; nationalist gratitude for this support crystallized into a principled refusal to recognize a state seen as displacing the Palestinian people.29 Indonesia formally recognized the Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO) in 1974 and recognized the State of Palestine in 1988 following the PLO's Algiers Declaration of independence.30

Successive Indonesian governments have contributed UN peacekeeping troops to Lebanon (UNIFIL) and the Sinai, established a field hospital in Gaza under President Jokowi, maintained an Honorary Consulate in Ramallah, and repeatedly voted at the UN General Assembly and Human Rights Council for resolutions supporting Palestinian rights. After Hamas's October 7, 2023, attacks and Israel's subsequent military campaign in Gaza, Indonesia was among the most vocal states condemning Israeli actions before international bodies. Siti Mutiah Setiawati of Universitas Gadjah Mada has argued that Indonesian engagement with the Palestine issue represents an interplay of "affective solidarity" grounded in Islamic identity and "strategic ambivalence" shaped by the non-alignment doctrine — a combination that produces strong rhetorical support and humanitarian commitment alongside consistent refusal of military entanglement.31

In February 2026, President Prabowo announced plans to deploy between 5,000 and 8,000 troops to Gaza as part of an envisioned international stabilization force — the strongest military commitment to the Palestinian cause in Indonesian history, though the modalities of such a deployment under existing UN frameworks remained to be negotiated.32 At the 51st OIC Council of Foreign Ministers in Istanbul in June 2025, Indonesian Foreign Minister Sugiono called on OIC members to adopt "a firmer and more assertive stance in defending international law" against Israeli violations, condemning the expansion of West Bank settlements and the obstruction of humanitarian aid.33

Yet Indonesia's Palestinian solidarity has clearly defined limits when it comes to military alignment. In 2015, when Saudi Arabia convened an "Islamic Military Alliance" under the banner of combating terrorism and security threats in the Muslim world, Indonesia declined to participate, judging that membership would contradict the non-aligned doctrine and potentially draw it into conflicts not of its own choosing.34 The distinction between moral solidarity and military alignment is a constant thread: Jakarta speaks forcefully for Palestinian rights in every multilateral forum while ensuring that the voice does not become a commitment to a particular coalition defined by religious identity rather than by Indonesia's own strategic judgment.

West Papua and Pacific Diplomacy

No issue in Indonesian foreign policy carries a heavier diplomatic burden than West Papua — the western half of the island of New Guinea, incorporated into Indonesia in 1963 following a UN-supervised transfer from Dutch administration and legitimized by the "Act of Free Choice" of 1969, in which a selected group of approximately 1,025 Papuan representatives voted unanimously for integration under circumstances that critics then and since have characterized as coerced and unrepresentative. (The full history of the Papua issue, including the armed insurgency and human rights situation, is treated in Chapter 42; this chapter focuses on its external dimensions.)

The fundamental tension in Pacific diplomacy is ethnic. The indigenous Melanesian people of Papua share ethnicity, culture, and deep historical ties with the peoples of Papua New Guinea, Fiji, Solomon Islands, and Vanuatu — the core members of the Melanesian Spearhead Group (MSG), founded as a political gathering in 1986 and formalized as an intergovernmental organization with a full charter in 2007, headquartered in Port Vila, Vanuatu.35 The United Liberation Movement for West Papua (ULMWP), representing the main pro-independence political coalition, has actively sought recognition and support from Pacific island states, framing the Papuan struggle as a decolonization matter and an expression of Melanesian solidarity.

Indonesia's response has combined strategic engagement with firm sovereignty assertion. In June 2015, Indonesia was granted associate membership of the MSG — a significant diplomatic achievement that gave Jakarta a formal voice in the organization and a platform from which to lobby against pro-independence positions from within. Indonesia justified its application for MSG membership by pointing to approximately 11 million Melanesian Indonesians living in five eastern provinces, arguing that Indonesia itself is a Melanesian nation in part and therefore has legitimate standing in the grouping.36 The ULMWP received observer status at the same 2015 meeting rather than full membership, a compromise that gave the independence movement a foot in the door of Pacific regionalism while falling short of formal recognition; ULMWP secretary-general Octavianus Mote acknowledged the outcome cautiously, noting that "a door has opened to us."37

The Pacific dimension of the Papua issue continues to generate periodic friction. Vanuatu and, at times, Solomon Islands have raised West Papua at the UN Human Rights Council and General Assembly, prompting Indonesian counter-mobilization through its Pacific diplomatic network. Fiji remained a more consistent supporter of Indonesian sovereignty arguments. The overall dynamic is one in which Melanesian solidarity creates legitimate international space for concerns about Papuan rights, while ASEAN solidarity and bilateral economic relationships generally prevent these concerns from crystallizing into the kind of sustained international pressure that accompanied East Timor's decolonization process in the 1990s — a comparison that Indonesian policy-makers are acutely aware of and determined to prevent repeating.

Melanesian Spearhead Group (MSG) — An intergovernmental organization comprising the four Melanesian sovereign states (Fiji, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, Vanuatu) and the Kanak and Socialist National Liberation Front of New Caledonia, formalized in 2007. Indonesia joined as an associate member in 2015, citing its eleven million Melanesian citizens in eastern provinces.

Indonesia's Trajectory: Centrality, Constraint, and the Question of Choice

At the start of the third decade of the twenty-first century, Indonesia occupies an international position that would have seemed improbable in 1998, when the financial crisis reduced it to IMF dependency and the streets of Jakarta were burning. It holds the G20 chair in rotation. It is a full member of both BRICS and the ASEAN Chair cycle — the only state in both groupings. It has treaty-level security commitments with Australia and a Major Defence Cooperation Partnership with the United States, while simultaneously being China's most important Southeast Asian economic partner. It hosts the world's largest Muslim population while sustaining a secular republican constitution. It is a democracy of 280 million people — the world's third-largest — in a region where democratic governance is not universal. None of these attributes is accidental: each reflects decades of deliberate diplomatic investment grounded in the bebas-aktif doctrine.

This position is precisely Indonesia's strategic aspiration — being important enough that no great power can afford to ignore it, yet independent enough that no great power can take it for granted. The ASEAN Outlook on the Indo-Pacific articulates this aspiration institutionally: ASEAN, with Indonesia as its natural center of gravity, should be the hub around which great-power engagement in the region is organized, not the passive arena over which great powers compete. Indonesia's bebas-aktif is not merely a bilateral posture but a multilateral architecture project.

The limitations of this vision are real and honestly debated. ASEAN's consensus requirement prevents unified responses to Chinese assertiveness in the South China Sea; Cambodia's and Laos's deep integration into China's economic orbit has repeatedly blocked the kind of coordinated ASEAN message that Indonesia would prefer. Indonesia's own economic dependence on China — as trading partner, BRI investor, and destination for its critical mineral exports — constrains how forcefully it can push back on issues like the Natuna EEZ. The bebas-aktif doctrine, formulated for a bipolar Cold War world, faces structural pressures in a world where the relevant challenge is not bloc alignment but deep economic interdependence combined with great-power maritime competition in Indonesia's own backyard.

The BRICS-and-OECD dual candidacy illustrates the tension most sharply. Some Indonesian scholars see simultaneous pursuit of both institutions as a masterclass in multi-alignment that extracts maximum benefit from both networks without committing to either; others warn that it risks signaling incoherence to both sides, or that the institutional commitments implicit in OECD membership — on governance standards, rule of law, labor rights — may prove more constraining than Indonesian policy-makers currently anticipate. The question of whether Indonesia's non-alignment is a sustainable long-term strategic culture or a transitional posture awaiting harder choices under greater US-China polarization remains genuinely open in Indonesian academic and policy debate — as it has, in different forms, since Hatta first formulated it in 1948.

What is not in doubt is Indonesia's trajectory as a consequential state. Its demographic scale, strategic geography astride the primary sea lanes of the Indo-Pacific, economic weight among the ten largest economies globally by purchasing power parity, and historical claim to have hosted the intellectual origins of the post-colonial international order at Bandung all give Indonesia a structural claim to global relevance that its leaders are increasingly determined to exercise. How it navigates the pressures that come with that relevance — from China's nine-dash line to the G20 table to the Pacific island forum where West Papua continues to be raised — will shape not only Indonesia's future but the future of the regional order in which all Southeast Asians live.

Summary

Indonesia's foreign policy has been shaped since 1948 by the bebas-aktif ("free and active") doctrine formulated by Vice-President Hatta: independence from great-power bloc alignment combined with active engagement in international affairs. This doctrine found its most dramatic early expression at the 1955 Bandung Conference, which brought together twenty-nine Asian and African governments and laid the intellectual groundwork for the Non-Aligned Movement. Institutionally, bebas-aktif was expressed through Indonesia's co-founding of ASEAN in 1967 and its long-standing promotion of "ASEAN centrality" as a framework for managing great-power relations in Southeast Asia. In the contemporary era, Indonesia navigates deep economic integration with China — through BRI infrastructure investment, nickel processing, and trade — alongside recurring maritime tensions over the North Natuna Sea, where China's nine-dash line overlaps Indonesia's EEZ. It maintains practical security partnerships with the United States, Australia (including the 2026 Jakarta Treaty), and Japan without accepting formal alliance membership. It held the G20 presidency in 2022 and joined BRICS in January 2025 while simultaneously applying for OECD membership — a dual-track expression of multi-alignment. Indonesia advocates strongly for Palestinian rights through the OIC and UN, and manages Pacific diplomatic pressure over West Papua through its associate membership in the Melanesian Spearhead Group. The central question is whether bebas-aktif can remain a coherent strategic culture as US-China competition intensifies and demands for hard choices increase.

Review & Discussion Questions

43.1 Mohammad Hatta described Indonesia's foreign policy as "bebas-aktif" — "free and active." In what specific ways is this different from classical neutrality? What obligations does the "active" element impose that neutrality does not?
43.2 The 1955 Bandung Conference is remembered as a founding moment of post-colonial solidarity, yet the "spirit of Bandung" had visibly weakened within fifteen years. What structural factors undermined the solidarity it produced, and does this weaken or strengthen its long-term historical significance?
43.3 Indonesia describes itself as ASEAN's "natural leader," yet ASEAN makes decisions by consensus and formally treats all members as equals. How does Indonesia exercise leadership within an organization whose rules formally prevent it? What are the costs of this indirect approach?
43.4 Indonesia insists it has no territorial dispute with China, yet Chinese Coast Guard vessels periodically operate inside Indonesia's EEZ near the Natuna Islands. Is Indonesia's formal position logically consistent? What are the diplomatic consequences of maintaining this distinction?
43.5 Indonesia deepened its security partnerships with the United States, Australia, and Japan during the same period it was joining BRICS and deepening economic ties with China. Is this multi-alignment a coherent strategy, or does it carry long-term risks of being trusted by neither side?
43.6 Indonesia's support for Palestinian rights is framed as a constitutional obligation rooted in anti-colonialism rather than as an expression of Islamic identity. How convincing is this framing, and how does it complicate or facilitate Indonesian diplomacy with non-Muslim states?
43.7 Indonesia joined the Melanesian Spearhead Group as an associate member in 2015, arguing that it is itself a Melanesian nation in part. How does this argument function diplomatically, and what are its implications for Indonesia's sovereignty claims over Papua?
43.8 Compare Indonesia's international position in 1955 (hosting Bandung) with its position in 2022 (hosting the G20). What has changed, and what structural continuities persist in how it relates to great powers and to the developing world?
