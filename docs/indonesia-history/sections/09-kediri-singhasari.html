Learning Objectives

  • Explain why the political centre of gravity in Java shifted eastward after the tenth century, and what the "Pralaya" catastrophe meant for Javanese dynastic continuity.
  • Describe Airlangga's reign, his patronage of Old Javanese literature, and the circumstances of his division of the kingdom into Kediri and Janggala.
  • Characterise the literary golden age of Kediri, with particular attention to the kakawin genre and the Bharatayuddha of 1157.
  • Analyse the founding narrative of Singhasari as told in the Pararaton — distinguishing its mythic register from what inscriptions and Chinese sources can verify.
  • Trace the Singhasari dynasty through its chain of violent successions to Kertanagara's expansionist reign.
  • Explain Kertanagara's Tantric religious programme, his Pamalayu expedition, and his confrontation with Kublai Khan's Yuan dynasty.
  • Account for how the Mongol punitive expedition of 1293 inadvertently enabled Raden Wijaya to found Majapahit.
  • Identify the principal East Javanese candi of the Singhasari period and describe how their architectural and sculptural style differed from Central Javanese precedents.

In the middle of the eleventh century the political heart of Java moved east. For three centuries, power had anchored itself in the fertile volcanic plains of Central Java, where the Sailendra and Mataram dynasties built Borobudur and Prambanan and patronised a brilliant Sanskrit-influenced culture. A catastrophic attack on the Javanese court around 1006, tradition and inscription together call it the Pralaya — the "destruction" — wiped out the old ruling house and left the young prince Airlangga alone in the forest. What emerged from that rupture was a new political world centred on the Brantas River valley of East Java: first the twin kingdoms of Kediri and Janggala, then the ambitious Singhasari, and finally the trigger-event that would beget the greatest of all the classical Javanese empires. The story of this chapter runs from the ashes of the Mataram court to the moment, in late 1293, when a Javanese aristocrat named Raden Wijaya outwitted the most powerful military force in Asia and planted his banner at a place called Majapahit — the Bitter Maja Fruit.

The Pralaya and the Restoration of Java

Airlangga — the name means something close to "he who leapt across the water" — was born around 1002 CE to an unusual dynastic union: his father was of the Balinese Warmadewa line, his mother was Princess Mahendradatta of the Javanese Ishana dynasty.1 As was common in the stratified world of Indianised Southeast Asian courts, he was sent as a teenager to be educated at the court of his maternal uncle, King Dharmawangsa of Java's Mataram kingdom. There, around 1006, he was about to be married when catastrophe struck.

The attack on the Mataram court is recorded obliquely in several copper-plate inscriptions and in the literary tradition but its exact nature remains debated among scholars. The Nagarakretagama, composed more than three centuries later, calls the event the "Pralaya" — a cosmological term for annihilation — and sources attribute it to a vassal king named Wurawari from the region of Lwaram, possibly acting in concert with, or encouraged by, the rival maritime power of Srivijaya in Sumatra, whose trade interests were threatened by Mataram's growing ambitions.2 Dharmawangsa and most of his court were killed. Airlangga, perhaps through the speed of his escape, survived. He fled into the forest and lived as a Hindu ascetic for several years, gathering around him a small group of loyal followers.

The recovery was gradual. By 1019 CE, Airlangga had begun to rally support from local lords and to reunify the fragmented territories of the former Mataram realm. He founded the kingdom of Kahuripan, eventually controlling a broad territory of East Java stretching from Pasuruan westward toward Madiun.3 His copper-plate inscriptions — the Calcutta and Leiden inscriptions are among the most cited — are the primary documentary sources for his reign, and they present him as a restorer of order, a protector of the realm, and a ruler who bridged Hindu and Buddhist devotion.

Airlangga was also a patron of literature. Under his reign, the court poet Mpu Kanwa composed the Kakawin Arjunawiwaha (c. 1030 CE), the "Marriage of Arjuna," which retells an episode from the Mahabharata in which the Pandava hero Arjuna undergoes austere meditation to win divine weapons, defeats a demon king, and receives a celestial bride.4 The poem is the first great kakawin of the East Javanese period and one of the earliest surviving works of any Old Javanese literature. Its allegorical layers — Arjuna as the model king, temptation overcome through asceticism, the king as protector of cosmic order — clearly map onto Airlangga's own self-presentation as a ruler who had endured loss and returned to restore righteous governance. The Arjunawiwaha would be performed in wayang shadow-puppet theatre for centuries and preserved in Balinese manuscript traditions long after Java itself had moved on.5

Kakawin — long narrative poems composed in Old Javanese (also called Kawi), structured in metres derived from Sanskrit prosody. Each stanza has four lines with strictly fixed syllable patterns. Kakawin drew their plots from Hindu mythology — particularly the Mahabharata and Ramayana — but relocated the action within a Javanese landscape and cosmology, and served as royal political allegory as much as literary entertainment. They are invaluable sources for the social and ceremonial life of the classical Javanese courts.

Late in his reign, facing the problem of succession, Airlangga made a decision that would shape Javanese history for the next two centuries. Unable or unwilling to choose between two competing sons — the chronicled tradition also mentions that his designated heir, a princess named Sanggramawijaya, chose the life of a hermit over the throne — Airlangga divided his kingdom into two roughly equal halves, Janggala (or Jenggala) in the northeast and Panjalu, which would become known as Kediri, in the west centred on the Brantas valley city of Daha.6 The precise date of the division is debated: some sources give 1042, others 1045, and the Marimbong Inscription of 1264 provides the clearest documentary indication of the boundary. Airlangga himself abdicated and died, around 1049, in the ascetic retreat he had chosen; he was deified posthumously as Vishnu on Garuda, and a celebrated eleventh-century statue of that image survives at the Belahan spring-shrine near Pasuruan.7

The division into Janggala and Kediri did not produce the peaceful equilibrium Airlangga may have intended. The two kingdoms competed for dominance across the following century. Janggala gradually faded from the historical record: the Javanese king Kameswara of Kediri (r. c. 1116–1136) appears in inscriptions as having incorporated Janggala's territory, most likely through a dynastic marriage to a Janggala princess, and the dual-kingdom arrangement effectively ended at that point.8 It was under Kediri that the literary flowering that defines this era reached its zenith.

Kediri: The Literary Kingdom of East Java

The Kediri kingdom (also known as Panjalu or Kadiri) endured from the mid-eleventh century until 1222. Its capital sat in the fertile Brantas River valley, near the site of the modern city of Kediri in East Java. The kingdom sustained itself through intensive rice agriculture — the Brantas valley is among the most productive agricultural zones in the archipelago — supplemented by an increasingly significant role in the spice trade, with Kediri's merchants and tributaries extending their reach south and east toward the Maluku Islands.9 Chinese diplomatic and commercial records are among the most important sources for Kediri's external relations: the Ling-wai-tai-ta of 1178, compiled by Chou K'u-fei, and the Chu-fan-chi of c. 1200 by Chou Ju-kua both describe the kingdom's governance and trade connections, noting that it controlled spice routes through the Java Sea and engaged regularly with Indian and Chinese merchants.10

The royal line of Kediri is partially reconstructed from inscriptions: scholars identify a sequence of kings including Śrī Samaravijaya (c. 1042–1051), Jayavarsha (c. 1104–1115), Kameswara (c. 1116–1136), Jayabhaya (c. 1135–1159), and a further series concluding with Kertajaya (r. c. 1194–1222).11 Of these, Jayabhaya's reign stands out as the kingdom's cultural pinnacle. His inscriptions portray him as a king of unambiguous military success who reunited the divided realm and compared himself, without apparent irony, to the legendary Pandava hero Yudhishthira — a comparison whose literary resonance was deepened by the great work his court produced.

The Bharatayuddha and the Kakawin Golden Age

The centrepiece of Kediri's cultural legacy is the Kakawin Bhāratayuddha, an Old Javanese epic adaptation of the climactic eighteen-day war between the Pandavas and Kauravas from the Sanskrit Mahabharata. The work was begun by the court poet Mpu Sedah under Jayabhaya's patronage and completed, after Sedah's death or inability to finish, by his colleague Mpu Panuluh; the composition date inscribed in the text corresponds to 6 November 1157 CE.12 With some hundred manuscripts catalogued in libraries across the world, the Bharatayuddha is the most-copied surviving Old Javanese work and a foundational text for both Javanese and Balinese literary culture.13

The poem is not a simple translation. Javanese literary culture at its best was adaptive rather than imitative: the Bharatayuddha reshapes the Sanskrit original in significant ways, giving greater depth and autonomy to secondary characters, relocating scenes within an unmistakably Javanese landscape imagination, and aligning the outcome of the war with the political self-understanding of Jayabhaya's court. The comparison between Jayabhaya-as-Yudhishthira and his Pandava counterpart was not merely decorative flattery; it was a statement of cosmological legitimacy, positioning the Kediri king as the rightful heir to a cycle of royal virtue.14

Mpu Panuluh, who completed the Bharatayuddha, also composed two other important kakawin: the Gatotkacasraya and the Hariwangsa. The literary ecosystem of the Kediri court extended further: the Smaradahana ("The Burning of Kama") by Mpu Dharmaja, another Kediri-period kakawin, retells the myth of the god Kama being incinerated by Shiva's third eye, a narrative whose themes of divine passion and destruction resonated with the Shaivite religious orientation of the court.15 To these works must be added the Sumanasantaka ("Death of Sumanasa") and a series of prose narrative cycles — the Panji cycle, stories of the prince Panji and his beloved Candra Kirana — whose origins are tied to the Kediri-Janggala cultural milieu and which spread across Southeast Asia as far as Thailand, Cambodia, and the Malay world.16

Panji cycle — a genre of prose narrative and theatrical performance originating in the Kediri-Janggala cultural world of East Java (c. eleventh to thirteenth centuries), centring on the loves and adventures of the prince Panji (also known as Inu Kertapati) and his consort Candra Kirana. The Panji cycle spread throughout the Southeast Asian mainland and Malay world, becoming one of the most widely diffused narrative traditions of the region.

The Decline of Kediri

The Kediri kingdom's final ruler, Kertajaya (r. c. 1194–1222), appears in sources as a king whose claims to divine status exceeded acceptable limits. The Nagarakretagama and later Javanese tradition accuse him of claiming worship from the Brahmin priestly class — a reversal of the expected order in which the king honoured the priests who performed his sacral rites — and the Brahmins, alarmed by this pretension, sought a protector outside the Kediri court.17 They found one in Ken Arok, the ambitious military leader who controlled the sub-kingdom of Tumapel to the east. In 1222, at the Battle of Ganter, Ken Arok's forces defeated and apparently killed Kertajaya, ending the Kediri kingdom's two-century run and inaugurating the Singhasari dynasty.18

The Pararaton Chronicle and the Legend of Ken Arok

No account of the founding of Singhasari can avoid confronting its principal source: the Pararaton, or "Book of Kings." The text is a relatively short chronicle of 32 folio pages (roughly 1,126 lines), composed in Old Javanese (Kawi) and surviving in manuscripts whose oldest colophon is dated 1522 Saka — equivalent to 1600 CE. This means it was compiled at least three centuries after the events it purports to describe.19 The work covers the reigns of the kings of Singhasari (1222–1292) and Majapahit, but nearly half its total length is devoted to the mythologised career of Singhasari's founder, Ken Arok. Understanding what the Pararaton can and cannot tell us is essential to understanding this period.

The Narrative of Ken Arok

In the Pararaton's account, Ken Arok's origin is supernatural. He was fathered by the god Brahma upon a peasant woman, and at birth was placed in a graveyard — his body, effulgent with divine light, attracted the attention of a passing thief named Ki Lembong, who adopted him and taught him the arts of the road.20 A wandering spiritual teacher, Mpu Lohgawe, later recognised him as "an avatar of Vishnu" and directed his energies toward courtly life. Ken Arok became an attendant at the court of Tunggul Ametung, the ruler of Tumapel, a sub-region under Kediri's sovereignty.

The pivot of the narrative arrives when Ken Arok observed Tunggul Ametung's wife, Ken Dedes, alighting from a litter and saw a divine radiance emanating from her — a sign, he understood, that she would bear a royal dynasty.21 Determined to possess both her and the throne, he commissioned the master smith Mpu Gandring to forge a magical kris (keris dagger). When the weapon was not yet complete — its purification rituals unfinished — Ken Arok killed the smith in his impatience. Mpu Gandring, dying, cursed the kris: it would kill seven of Ken Arok's own descendants. Ken Arok then used the weapon to murder Tunggul Ametung, cleverly arranging for suspicion to fall on another man by lending the weapon to a third party who was thus also murdered. He married the widowed Ken Dedes, rallied local support, and in 1222 met and defeated King Kertajaya of Kediri at the Battle of Ganter.22

The Curse of the Kris and the Pararaton's Historical Register
The Dutch scholar C. C. Berg argued in the mid-twentieth century that texts like the Pararaton were entirely ahistorical — not records of the past but exercises in magical thinking, intended not to report events but to shape future royal destiny. Berg's position was an extreme one and has not prevailed among specialists. The majority of modern scholars accept a nuanced position: the Pararaton's prelude, with its divine parentage and supernatural kris, clearly operates in the register of royal mythology, making historically verifiable claims very difficult to extract. But the later portions of the text, dealing with Singhasari's internal succession conflicts and Majapahit's founding, contain enough correspondence with copper-plate inscriptions and Chinese records to suggest a core of historical memory, however dressed in legendary elaboration.23 The appropriate scholarly stance is neither Berg's outright dismissal nor credulous acceptance, but careful triangulation against other source types.

Historically, Ken Arok is attested through the convergence of the Pararaton, the early Majapahit inscriptions that acknowledge him as the dynasty's progenitor (the "Rajasa" ancestral line), and the general coherence of the 1222 date with Kediri's documented end.24 His name itself — the word arok has connotations of disturbance or confusion in Javanese — may be an epithet rather than a birth name, as would be appropriate for a founder celebrated for disrupting the existing order. Ken Dedes, his wife, appears to be historically attested; a notable inscription places a "Mahisa-champaka" (an associate of the Singhasari line) in a context consistent with the Pararaton's account of Ken Arok's family connections.

The Singhasari Dynasty: The Curse Plays Out

Ken Arok ruled as the founder-king of what the Pararaton calls the Rajasa (or Tumapel-Singhasari) dynasty from 1222 until his assassination in 1227, reportedly killed by his own stepson Anusapati, who used the same cursed kris — just as Mpu Gandring had foretold.25 Anusapati was Tunggul Ametung's biological son, and the Pararaton frames his act as revenge for his father's murder. This began a succession of regicides that the chronicle presents as the working-out of the kris's curse through seven generations.

Anusapati reigned from 1227 to 1248, when he was in turn killed by Panji Tohjaya, Ken Arok's son by another wife. Tohjaya's own reign lasted only months before a revolt led by the sons of Anusapati and a prince named Mahisa Wonga Teleng overthrew him. The resulting power-sharing arrangement placed two men on the throne simultaneously: Vishnuwardhana-Narasimhamurthy, sometimes described as twin brothers or co-regents, who ruled jointly from 1248 to 1268.26 The political instability embedded in Singhasari's founding mythology — and reflected in its historical reality — gave way only with the accession of the last and most consequential Singhasari king.

Vishnuwardhana died in 1268 and was deified as a Shiva-Buddha synthesis, commemorated at Candi Jago — a monument whose layered syncretic iconography reflects precisely this kind of royal apotheosis. His death brought his son, Kertanagara, to the throne, and Kertanagara would transform what had been a regionally significant East Javanese kingdom into something with far wider maritime ambitions.

Kertanagara: God-King and Archipelagic Visionary

Kertanagara (r. 1268–1292) is the central figure of the Singhasari story, the ruler who both brought the kingdom to its greatest power and, through the enemies he made and the military commitments he scattered abroad, left it fatally vulnerable. He was the first Javanese ruler to be accorded the title dewaprabu — god-king — and his religious programme was the most intellectually ambitious of any Javanese ruler before Hayam Wuruk of Majapahit.27

Tantric Religion and Royal Ideology

Kertanagara's religious identity was emphatically syncretic, specifically oriented toward a Tantric synthesis of Shaivism and Vajrayana Buddhism in which the king presented himself as a living embodiment of both Shiva and the Buddha — the deity-form known as Shiva-Buddha or, in certain Tantric contexts, Bhairava.28 This was not a merely ceremonial position. Kertanagara is reported in later sources to have engaged in Tantric practices — ritual feasting, the drinking of wine, meditation associated with wrathful deity-forms — that shocked or alarmed some of his subjects and that the hostile Pararaton uses to critique his reign. The Nagarakretagama, composed decades later under Majapahit patronage and broadly sympathetic to the Singhasari lineage, tends to present Kertanagara's religious intensity as mystical kingship rather than transgression.29

The contrast between these two sources is instructive. The Pararaton's account of Kertanagara, written long after the events and composed in a tradition that traced Majapahit's legitimacy directly through him, is paradoxically critical — it attributes his downfall partly to religious excess and court mismanagement. The Nagarakretagama of Mpu Prapanca (1365), a court poem celebrating the glory of Majapahit under Hayam Wuruk, offers retrospective praise and draws heavily on Singhasari as the empire's illustrious ancestor. Neither text is neutral historical reportage, and scholarship consistently notes the need to read them against each other and against the epigraphic record.30

Expansion: Bali, Sumatra, and the Pamalayu Expedition

Kertanagara's most significant geopolitical initiative was the Pamalayu expedition. In 1275 — the date is given in the Padang Roco inscription — Kertanagara dispatched a military and diplomatic expedition to the Melayu kingdom in the Dharmasraya region of eastern Sumatra, presumably with the aim of subordinating or neutralising Melayu as a rival power and of extending Javanese influence over the critical Strait of Malacca trade routes.31 The Pamalayu mission was not simply a military conquest; it also had a pronounced religious dimension. A bronze statue of the bodhisattva Amoghapasa was sent along with the expedition, and the Padang Roco inscription of 1286 records that it was received by the Melayu ruler Tribhuwanaraja with expressions of devotion and gratitude — a gift of sacred Buddhist sculpture that functioned simultaneously as a statement of Singhasari's spiritual prestige and political reach.32

The political consequences of the Pamalayu expedition would outlast Kertanagara himself. When the expedition force returned to Java after his death in 1292, it brought with it two Melayu princesses — Dara Petak and Dara Jingga — whose subsequent marriages into the Majapahit royal house would play significant roles in the new empire's succession history. Dara Petak married Raden Wijaya, the Majapahit founder, and bore the second Majapahit king, Jayanegara.33

Kertanagara also subdued Bali in 1284, extending Singhasari's direct influence to an island that would become the great preserver of Java's Hindu-Buddhist cultural inheritance after Islam's arrival on the mainland. His reign thus represented the most concerted attempt yet by any Javanese ruler to assert hegemony across the wider archipelago — a vision that Majapahit's Gajah Mada would later claim to have fulfilled, and that the modern Indonesian state would invoke as a legitimising historical precedent through the concept of Nusantara.

The Confrontation with Kublai Khan

The expanding world of the late thirteenth century brought Kertanagara into collision with the most powerful empire on earth. In 1289, ambassadors from the Yuan dynasty of Kublai Khan arrived at the Singhasari court demanding tribute and the formal submission of Java to the universal sovereign of the Mongol empire. Kertanagara's response was spectacular in its defiance. According to Chinese and Javanese sources, he had the ambassadors disfigured — accounts vary between branding their faces, cutting their ears, and other mutilations — and sent them home.34 The outrage this caused at the Yuan court made a punitive naval expedition not just likely but politically necessary for Kublai Khan.

The humiliation of the Yuan envoys has been read by historians in several ways. It may have been a calculated provocation — Kertanagara possibly understood that his Pamalayu expedition's control of the Straits gave him defensive strategic depth. It may equally have been a failure of judgment about the Yuan dynasty's capacity to project military power across the sea. Whatever the reasoning, the decision to mutilate Kublai Khan's ambassadors set in motion a chain of events that Kertanagara did not live to see resolved.

The Fall of Singhasari and the Mongol Arrival

In 1292, while his best military forces were committed to distant operations — troops were deployed in Sumatra, in Bali, and to suppress a rebellion in the western part of the island — Kertanagara was caught fatally exposed by an internal revolt. Jayakatwang, the ruler of the vassal territory of Kediri (a descendant of the old Kediri royal line who had been incorporated into the Singhasari system), launched a two-pronged attack on the Singhasari capital at Tumapel. The attack succeeded. Kertanagara was killed — the Pararaton describes him as having been slain during a Tantric religious feast, though the account cannot be independently verified — in May or June 1292.35 Singhasari ceased to exist, and Jayakatwang re-established a Kediri-based kingdom, completing a dynastic circle that had been broken seven decades earlier.

Among those who escaped the coup was Raden Wijaya, a prince of the Singhasari royal house who had married four daughters of Kertanagara and thus had a legitimate claim to the succession. Wijaya fled eastward to Madura, where he found protection with the regional governor Arya Wiraraja. The two men forged a pragmatic alliance: Wiraraja negotiated Wijaya's formal submission to Jayakatwang in exchange for being granted control of the Tarik forest region on the Brantas delta, ostensibly to clear it for settlement. Wijaya accepted these terms and bided his time.36

The Yuan fleet arrived off the coast of Java in late 1292 or early 1293, comprising somewhere between 20,000 and 30,000 soldiers aboard 500 to 1,000 ships — figures from the Yuan Shi (Official History of the Yuan Dynasty), which is one of the primary Chinese sources for the invasion and which, though prone to official exaggeration, is the most contemporaneous documentary record available.37 The expedition found a Java in political chaos. The target of their punitive mission, Kertanagara, was already dead. The ruler who had killed him, Jayakatwang, now occupied the Javanese throne. Raden Wijaya — who had every reason to want Jayakatwang destroyed — met the Yuan commanders and offered an alliance.

Raden Wijaya and the Birth of Majapahit

The story of how Raden Wijaya turned the Mongol invasion to his advantage is one of the most consequential acts of strategic deception in Southeast Asian history. His alliance with the Yuan forces was genuine enough in tactical terms: in the spring of 1293, the joint Javanese-Mongol army attacked Jayakatwang's Kediri realm, defeated it in engagements around the capital Daha, and Jayakatwang was captured. He was subsequently executed.38 The Yuan commanders were now in full expectation of receiving tribute and formal submission from their Javanese ally.

Wijaya then took leave of the Yuan forces, explaining that he needed to return to his base to prepare the tributary gifts. Instead, once safely clear of the main Mongol camp, he mobilised his own troops and Arya Wiraraja's forces and launched a coordinated surprise attack on the Yuan army as it began preparations for departure. The attack was devastating: estimates from the Yuan Shi suggest losses of 12,000 to 18,000 dead.39 The surviving Yuan forces, weakened by tropical diseases, the unfamiliar climate, and now a catastrophic ambush, retreated to their ships and departed in late 1293 before the monsoon season could trap them further.

Wijaya's coronation as the first king of Majapahit took place on the fifteenth day of the month of Kārttika in the Javanese Saka year 1215, which scholars calculate as 10 November 1293.40 The capital was established at Tarik, the forest settlement on the Brantas delta that Arya Wiraraja had negotiated for him. In Javanese tradition, the name Majapahit — maja being a bitter-fruited tree (Aegle marmelos) — is explained by the bitter taste of the fruit Wijaya encountered when clearing the forest. Whether this etymology is literal or legend, the name adhered.41

Majapahit — the great Hindu-Buddhist empire of East Java (1293–c. 1527), founded by Raden Wijaya after his defeat of the Yuan Chinese expedition force in 1293. Majapahit reached its classical apogee under King Hayam Wuruk (r. 1350–1389) and his chief minister Gajah Mada, claiming suzerainty over much of the archipelago. It is treated in Chapter 9.

The Mongol invasion thus produced an outcome the opposite of what Kublai Khan intended. Rather than bringing Java to heel, it provided a Javanese prince with the military muscle — and the legitimating narrative of having expelled a foreign enemy — needed to found the greatest of all the classical Javanese empires. The irony was not lost on later Javanese historical memory. Raden Wijaya's betrayal of his Mongol allies is not presented in Javanese tradition as treachery but as cunning statecraft — the resourcefulness of a ruler who turned an impossible situation into a founding moment.

East Javanese Temple Art: The Singhasari Candi

The three principal surviving temple complexes of the Singhasari period — Candi Kidal, Candi Jago, and Candi Singhasari itself — embody the kingdom's distinctive religious and artistic programme and illustrate a stylistic evolution away from the Central Javanese temple tradition that had produced Borobudur and Prambanan.

Central vs. East Javanese Architectural Style

The shift from Central to East Javanese temple-building involved changes in material, proportion, and iconographic programme. Central Javanese temples (those of the eighth to tenth centuries) were typically constructed of volcanic andesite stone, tended toward bulky, broad proportions with clearly articulated horizontal registers, and employed naturalistically carved reliefs with high projection — the Jataka scenes at Borobudur being the supreme example. East Javanese temples (post-1000 CE) evolved a more slender, vertically elongated profile; their roofs tend to have smoother, more integrated lines; and their relief carving adopted a more stylised, flattened aesthetic that scholars often describe as "wayang-like" — resembling the two-dimensional formal conventions of shadow-puppet performance.42

The primary building material of later East Javanese temples was often red brick rather than andesite — brick being more abundant in the lowland river valleys where the Singhasari and Majapahit capitals were located — though the three main Singhasari temples are notable exceptions, having been built in andesite, which links them stylistically to the earlier Central Javanese tradition even as their proportions and iconography mark them as East Javanese.43

Candi Kidal

Built around 1248, Candi Kidal is widely regarded as the most elegant of the Singhasari-period temples and the one that best captures the transitional quality of the era's art. Located in Rejokidal village, Tumpang district, approximately 20 kilometres east of Malang, it was constructed as a mortuary commemoration of King Anusapati — the second Singhasari ruler, killed in 1248 — who was deified here as Shiva.44 The structure rises in three tiers from a raised platform, with the characteristic verticality of the East Javanese style expressed in the diminishing mass of the upper tiers. The base is decorated with three celebrated Kala-faced medallion panels — stone faces of the time-devouring deity — alternating with depictions of Garuda, the divine bird of Vishnu, in the episodes of his mother's liberation from serpent servitude. These Garuda panels at Candi Kidal are among the finest examples of narrative relief carving from the period.45

Candi Jago

Candi Jago (from Jajaghu, "the majestic") was built as a mortuary temple for King Vishnuwardhana, who died in 1268 and was deified here in a syncretic form — as both Shiva and the Bodhisattva Amoghasiddhi, reflecting the Shiva-Buddha synthesis that would characterise the late Singhasari and early Majapahit royal religious programme.46 The temple, located near Tumpang (approximately 22 kilometres from Malang), is distinguished by the extraordinary quantity and variety of its carved reliefs, which crowd every terrace surface. The narratives depicted — drawn from the Kunjarakarna, Parthayajna, Arjunavivaha, and Krishnayana texts — are rendered in the mature East Javanese wayang-relief style: figures that are simultaneously graceful and angular, with the flattened, profile-facing conventions of shadow-puppet art rather than the rounded, frontal naturalism of Central Javanese predecessors.47

Candi Singhasari

The temple complex of Candi Singhasari, situated in the Singosari district of Malang Regency approximately 10 kilometres north of Malang city, was intended as the primary memorial for Kertanagara himself — the last and most ambitious of the Singhasari kings.48 The site's importance is confirmed by both the Nagarakretagama and the Gajah Mada inscription of 1351, and the temple is understood as the probable location of the original Singhasari royal court. Notably, the structure was never fully completed: the upper portions remain unfinished, the decorative carving interrupted — a reflection, perhaps, of the catastrophic political rupture of 1292 that killed Kertanagara and swept away his dynasty before his monument could be completed.

What does survive at Candi Singhasari is imposing: a pair of enormous dvarapala guardian figures flanking the entrance — among the largest surviving guardian statues in Java — and a statue identified as Shiva as Agastya or Batara Guru in the lower chamber. The dvarapala figures exemplify the East Javanese sculptural aesthetic noted by modern art historians: where Central Javanese guardian figures tend toward a chubby, round-faced, almost comic ferocity, the East Javanese version is muscular, elongated, and more genuinely threatening in aspect.49

Together, these three temples — Kidal, Jago, and Singhasari — constitute the architectural legacy of a dynasty whose political history was violent and condensed but whose artistic programme was extraordinarily sophisticated. Each temple functioned as a royal candi in the specific East Javanese sense: not merely a place of worship but a monument of royal apotheosis, where the deceased king was merged with a deity and made accessible to his descendants and subjects as a divine ancestor. This theology of royal deification, expressed in stone, would be inherited and elaborated on an even grander scale by Majapahit.

Chronicle and History: Reading the Sources

The history of Kediri and Singhasari rests on a more precarious documentary foundation than the student might expect for a period only seven centuries past. The challenge is not absence of sources but the nature of those that exist: two major Javanese-language literary chronicles composed well after the events they describe, a corpus of copper-plate inscriptions that are contemporary but highly formulaic and politically motivated, and a body of Chinese records that are contemporaneous for some questions (trade, tribute relations) but limited for internal Javanese politics.

The Pararaton

The Pararaton, as noted above, dates in its surviving form to around 1600 — three centuries after Singhasari and nearly four centuries after Ken Arok. It is relatively short (32 folios), anonymous, and written in a literary register that freely blends mythological and chronicle modes. The first half is dominated by the Ken Arok legend, which operates in the idiom of foundation myths shared across many cultures: the hero of divine or ambiguous birth, exile, supernatural helpers, a magical weapon, the acquisition of a royal wife, and the violent establishment of a new order.50 To read this as straightforward biography is methodologically indefensible. To dismiss it entirely — as Berg proposed — ignores the substantial correspondences between the Pararaton's later sections and other source types.

Where the Pararaton is most historically valuable is in its succession data: the sequence of kings, the approximate dates (which scholars cross-check against inscriptions), and the internal political dynamics of the Singhasari court. Even here, the "curse of the kris" narrative gives the text's author a ready-made framework for explaining each regicide that could be applied retrospectively without historical accuracy — the structural neatness of a series of seven deaths fulfilling a prophecy is suspicious precisely because it is neat.

The Nagarakretagama

The Nagarakretagama, composed by the court poet Mpu Prapanca in 1365, is the other foundational Javanese source for this period. It is a kakawin — a formal Old Javanese poem — written as a eulogy (puja sastra) to the reigning Majapahit king Hayam Wuruk. Cantos 41–43 provide a retrospective account of the Singhasari dynasty, presenting Kertanagara in particular as a towering figure of royal achievement and religious wisdom.51 The Nagarakretagama is generally considered more reliable than the Pararaton on matters of religious culture and royal ceremonial because it was composed closer to the events (though still decades after Singhasari's end) and by a court poet with access to temple records and the living traditions of royal ritual. But it too is a partisan document: its function is to glorify Majapahit by celebrating its ancestors, and critical events that reflect badly on the dynasty tend to receive little treatment.

Inscriptions and Chinese Sources

Copper-plate inscriptions from the Kediri and Singhasari periods provide the firmest documentary anchors. Royal inscriptions typically record land grants, tax exemptions, and the consecration of religious foundations; they name the reigning king and often provide regnal year dates. The Talan, Jaring, Kamulan, and Padlegan inscriptions are among those used to establish the Kediri ruler sequence; Singhasari-period inscriptions are rarer but include the Mula-Malurung inscription of 1177 Saka (1255 CE), which names Vishnuwardhana and gives significant genealogical information about the Rajasa dynasty.52

Chinese sources, particularly the Yuan Shi (compiled 1370) and its account of the 1293 expedition, provide a cross-cultural perspective on Java's external relations and on the Mongol invasion. The Yuan Shi's account of the invasion is detailed but reflects a Yuan court perspective that seeks to explain an embarrassing military failure — making claims about the scale of the expedition force, and the treachery of Wijaya, that serve their own narrative purposes. Scholars use these records in conjunction with the Javanese chronicles rather than accepting either set in isolation.53

The overall picture that emerges from triangulating these sources is one of genuine historical complexity: a period of intense political creativity and cultural efflorescence that left deep marks on Javanese, Balinese, and broader Southeast Asian civilisation, but whose reconstruction demands honest acknowledgment of what we know, what we infer, and what remains buried in the silences between the texts.

Pararaton — Old Javanese chronicle (in Kawi) covering the kings of Singhasari and Majapahit, surviving in manuscripts dated c. 1600 CE. Nearly half its content is devoted to the mythologised founding narrative of Ken Arok. Its historical reliability is strongest for institutional and genealogical data in its later sections; its origin legends operate in a literary-mythological register that should not be read as biography.
Nagarakretagama — a kakawin (Old Javanese court poem) composed by Mpu Prapanca in 1365 CE as a eulogy to Majapahit king Hayam Wuruk. It provides retrospective accounts of the Singhasari dynasty and detailed descriptions of Majapahit's territory, capital, and ceremonial life. Considered a primary source for Majapahit's apogee but read with awareness of its panegyric purpose.

Summary

The history of Eastern Java from the eleventh to the thirteenth century traces a compressed arc from cultural efflorescence to violent political transformation. Airlangga rebuilt the Javanese state after the Pralaya catastrophe, divided his kingdom between Kediri and Janggala, and patronised the first great works of East Javanese literature — including the Arjunawiwaha of Mpu Kanwa. Under Kediri, and above all during the reign of King Jayabhaya, the kakawin tradition reached its zenith with the Bharatayuddha of 1157 and a constellation of other works that shaped Javanese literary culture for centuries. Kediri fell in 1222 to Ken Arok, whose founding narrative — as told in the late-composed Pararaton chronicle — blends supernatural legend with a core of historical reality that modern scholarship reads carefully against inscriptional and Chinese evidence.

The Singhasari dynasty that Ken Arok founded was distinguished by a chain of violent successions that the Pararaton frames as the working-out of a smith's curse. It reached its most expansionist expression under Kertanagara (1268–1292), whose Tantric religious programme, Pamalayu expedition to Sumatra, and defiant mutilation of Kublai Khan's envoys made him the most internationally consequential Javanese ruler of his era — and also exposed his kingdom fatally. Killed by Jayakatwang's coup in 1292, Kertanagara did not live to see the Yuan fleet arrive. His son-in-law Raden Wijaya turned that invasion to his advantage: allying with the Mongols to destroy Jayakatwang, then ambushing his allies and founding, in November 1293, the Majapahit empire. The Singhasari period's architectural legacy — Candi Kidal, Candi Jago, and the unfinished Candi Singhasari — embodies its distinctive synthesis of East Javanese stylistic innovation with the inherited tradition of Hindu-Buddhist royal apotheosis.

Review & Discussion Questions

8.1 Why did Airlangga divide his kingdom between Kediri and Janggala rather than leaving it intact? What does this decision reveal about the nature of dynastic power and succession in the Hindu-Buddhist Javanese political tradition?
8.2 The kakawin literature of the Kediri period adapted Sanskrit epics such as the Mahabharata for a Javanese audience. What was gained and what was changed in this process of adaptation? How does the Bharatayuddha's association with King Jayabhaya illustrate the political functions of court literature?
8.3 Compare the historical value of the Pararaton and the Nagarakretagama as sources for the Singhasari period. What questions can each answer, and where does each have significant limitations?
8.4 What does the "curse of the kris" narrative in the Pararaton suggest about how Javanese political culture understood the relationship between royal violence, fate, and legitimacy? Is there a comparable device in other world historical or literary traditions?
8.5 Assess Kertanagara's Pamalayu expedition and his confrontation with the Yuan dynasty. Was his strategy coherent? What does it reveal about how Javanese rulers of this period understood the political geography of the archipelago?
8.6 How did the Mongol invasion of 1293 — a punitive expedition that failed its stated objective — end up benefiting the Javanese state? What does this suggest about the role of unintended consequences in historical causation?
8.7 East Javanese temple art is often described as stylistically distinct from Central Javanese precedents. In what specific ways did the Singhasari-period candi differ from temples like Borobudur and Prambanan, and what cultural or religious factors might account for these differences?
8.8 Ken Arok is described in the Pararaton as both a criminal and a god-descended hero. How should historians approach founding figures who are inseparable from mythological narratives? Is it possible — or desirable — to separate the "real" Ken Arok from the legendary one?
