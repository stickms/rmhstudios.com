Learning Objectives

  • Trace the four constitutional amendments of 1999–2002 and explain how they restructured Indonesia's political system — introducing direct presidential elections, the DPD, term limits, and the removal of appointed military seats.
  • Evaluate the abolition of dwifungsi and the practical limits of military reform, distinguishing formal prohibition from substantive change.
  • Analyze Indonesia's "Big Bang" decentralization — what Laws 22 and 25 of 1999 devolved, to whom, and why the results were both promising and problematic.
  • Describe the emergence of a free press and a pluralist party system after 1998, and explain why some scholars regard that party system as institutionally fragile.
  • Understand the establishment of the KPK (2002–2003), its early record, and the attacks it subsequently faced.
  • Reconstruct the 1999 East Timor independence referendum — its conduct, the militia violence that followed, and the INTERFET intervention.
  • Explain the 2005 Helsinki MoU ending the Aceh insurgency, including the role of the December 2004 tsunami in creating space for a settlement.
  • Weigh Reformasi as both a genuine democratic breakthrough and an unfinished project, appreciating the scholarly debates about oligarchic persistence and institutional quality.

On the morning of 21 May 1998, Suharto resigned from the presidency he had held for thirty-two years, and Indonesia entered an era it called Reformasi — reform. The word carried enormous weight: it named not only a political transition but a promise that the predatory structures of the New Order would be dismantled and replaced by accountable, democratic governance. Over the next seven years, Indonesia's leaders and citizens made extraordinary progress on that promise. The 1945 Constitution was transformed by four rounds of amendment; direct presidential elections replaced backroom selection; the military was formally stripped of its appointed legislative seats and its doctrine of social-political supremacy; nearly three hundred districts and municipalities received sweeping new governing powers; a free press burst into life; the Corruption Eradication Commission was created; and two long-running conflicts — in East Timor and in Aceh — reached negotiated or enforced endings. The chapters that follow deal with the individual reform-era presidents and with Indonesia's subsequent trajectory; this chapter concerns the institutional architecture of the transformation itself, asking both what was built and what remained unfinished.

Rewriting the Constitution: Amendments, 1999–2002

Suharto had governed for three decades under the 1945 Constitution — a short, deliberately vague document that concentrated power in the president and in a largely appointed legislature, the People's Consultative Assembly (Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat, MPR). The constitution had been designed in the revolutionary year of Indonesian independence for emergency conditions, and it had been used by both Sukarno and Suharto to justify authoritarian rule. After 1998, there was near-universal agreement that the document needed fundamental revision, though also a significant reluctance — rooted in the bitter ideological battles of the 1950s — to scrap it entirely and start afresh. "Any suggestions to replace it with a completely new constitution," the political scientist Dewi Fortuna Anwar has written, "were overwhelmingly rejected" because too many actors feared that reopening foundational questions would reignite unresolved debates about the place of Islam in the state.1

The MPR therefore amended the existing document in four separate sessions between 1999 and 2002, completing the package on 10 August 2002 and producing, in the words of constitutional law scholars Susi Dwi Harijanti and Tim Lindsey, "a radically revised and newly liberal democratic political system."2 The reforms were structural and far-reaching. First, and most symbolically charged, the president was henceforth to be chosen by direct popular election rather than by MPR vote — Indonesia would elect its head of state as citizens, not through an assembly dominated by appointed delegates. Second, a new upper chamber, the Dewan Perwakilan Daerah (DPD, or Regional Representative Council), was created to give the provinces and districts a voice in national legislation. Third, presidential terms were capped at two five-year terms, foreclosing the indefinite tenure that had allowed both Sukarno and Suharto to entrench themselves. Fourth, and arguably the most consequential institutional step, the MPR's composition was reformed to consist entirely of elected members, thereby ending the system of appointed "functional group" seats through which the military and police had for decades held guaranteed representation in the national legislature without seeking a popular mandate.3

The amendments also incorporated an expanded chapter on human rights, giving constitutional status to rights that had been systematically violated under the New Order, and they provided an explicit constitutional foundation for regional autonomy — anchoring in the country's supreme law the decentralization that was already being legislated separately. A new Constitutional Court was established to adjudicate disputes about the meaning of the amended text; by June 2004 it had already received 465 election-related cases and had struck down one provision — a bar on people with family connections to communism from standing for election — as unconstitutional.4

The DPD, however, embodied one of the amendments' structural compromises. It was created to represent territorial interests, and its 128 members (four from each province) are elected by popular vote — but the constitution gave it only the power to submit bills to the lower house, the Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat (DPR), and to offer recommendations. It cannot independently legislate.5 Critics argued that this made the DPD closer to a consultative appendage than a genuine upper chamber, producing an asymmetric bicameralism that satisfied regional demands rhetorically but left actual legislative power concentrated in the DPR. Constitutional scholar Andrew Ellis described the overall amendment package as a "'good enough' and consistent" political achievement — real reform, negotiated under intense democratic pressure, but constrained by the political interests in play during the transition.6

DPD (Dewan Perwakilan Daerah) — the Regional Representative Council, created by the 2002 constitutional amendment as an upper chamber representing Indonesia's provinces. Unlike a full senate, the DPD can submit legislation but cannot independently pass it, making Indonesia's bicameralism structurally asymmetric.

The Retreat from the Barracks? Military Reform and the End of Dwifungsi

No institution was more central to the New Order's political economy than the armed forces. Under the doctrine of dwifungsi — "dual function" — the military (Tentara Nasional Indonesia, TNI) claimed not only a defense role but a permanent socio-political role in governance, justifying the presence of military officers in parliament, the cabinet, regional governorships, state enterprises, and every layer of the civilian bureaucracy. This doctrine was not merely ideological; it was economically productive for the officer corps, sustaining an elaborate network of military-owned businesses, foundations, and cooperatives that supplemented the official defense budget. Reformasi targeted both the political and the economic dimensions of dwifungsi.

The clearest institutional victory was the separation of the police from the military. Under Suharto, the Indonesian National Police (Kepolisian Negara Republik Indonesia, POLRI) had been organizationally part of ABRI (the combined armed forces). MPR Decree No. VI/2000 formally separated the two institutions, making POLRI an independent body reporting directly to the president — a reform that removed the military from domestic law-enforcement and established a civilian police chain of command, however imperfect in practice.7 The doctrine of dwifungsi was formally abolished; active military officers were prohibited from holding civilian positions without resigning their commissions; and the constitutional amendments removed the military's appointed seats from the DPR.

Yet the substance of reform lagged far behind its formal contours. Scholars Abdil Mughis Mudhoffir and Rafiqa Qurrata Ayun have argued bluntly that "the military has never completely returned to the barracks and security sector reform has stagnated."8 Officers continued to occupy civilian bureaucratic positions through a variety of legal workarounds; political parties and regional governments retained close ties to retired military figures; and the military's commercial networks proved resistant to divestment. The 2004 TNI Law (No. 34/2004) explicitly required the government to take over all military-owned businesses within five years — by October 2009 — but a Human Rights Watch investigation documented that this deadline passed without meaningful action. As of 2007, TNI operated businesses generating approximately US$28.5 million annually through foundations and cooperatives, with total assets valued at around US$235 million.9 Major holdings included PT. Kartika Airlines (Army), PT. Admiral Lines (Navy), and timber concessions; a presidential decree issued just five days before the 2009 deadline created only an oversight team rather than transferring any ownership.10

This gap between formal prohibition and substantive enforcement reflected a political reality: the reform-era governments depended on TNI cooperation to manage regional conflicts and maintain order, and none was willing to risk a full confrontation with the officer corps over the business question. The result was a compromise that civil society organizations — Propatria, KontraS, Imparsial — called inadequate.11 Formal dwifungsi had ended; its practical habits had not. The military's role in the post-Reformasi era remained a subject of ongoing contestation, surfacing again in 2025 when a revised TNI Law renewed debate about civilian-military boundaries — a sign, as Mudhoffir and Ayun note, that the reform agenda of 1998–2004 had left the underlying structural question unresolved.

Dwifungsi — "Dual function": the New Order doctrine by which the Indonesian armed forces claimed permanent socio-political as well as defense roles, justifying military representation in the legislature, cabinet, and civilian bureaucracy. Formally abolished under Reformasi, though full institutional separation proved difficult to enforce in practice.

The Big Bang: Decentralization and Its Discontents

If the constitutional amendments were the most visible act of political restructuring, the decentralization laws were arguably the most sweeping. Indonesia under Suharto had been one of the most highly centralized states in the developing world; the central government in Jakarta controlled budgets, appointed regional governors, and retained effective veto power over local decision-making in some of the world's largest and most geographically dispersed communities. After 1998, the pressure for regional autonomy — amplified by fears that East Timor's trajectory might be replicated in Aceh, West Papua, and elsewhere — produced a radical reversal. Laws No. 22 and No. 25 of 1999, together constituting what economists and political scientists labelled the "Big Bang" of fiscal decentralization, transferred enormous governing powers from Jakarta to the district (kabupaten) and municipality (kota) level, taking effect on 1 January 2001.12

The choice to devolve to districts rather than to provinces was politically deliberate. Habibie's government calculated that empowering the larger, more historically assertive provincial governments — Aceh, West Papua, Riau — risked strengthening separatist movements. Districts were numerous, smaller, and less likely to sustain a secessionist critical mass. Law 22 accordingly gave district assemblies the right to legislate and to oversee executive functions across a wide range of policy areas — health, education, infrastructure, licensing — while bypassing the provincial level. Law 25 transferred corresponding fiscal powers, including control over certain revenue streams and natural resource royalties, though the central government retained the largest share of tax revenues.13

Early economic assessments were cautiously optimistic. James Alm, Robert Aten, and Roy Bahl, writing in the Bulletin of Indonesian Economic Studies in 2001, acknowledged the enormity of the undertaking — "few countries have undertaken a more radical transformation of their fiscal systems in so short a time" — while identifying serious implementation risks including mismatched revenue and expenditure assignments, weak local administrative capacity, and inadequate accountability mechanisms.14 These risks materialized. Thomas Pepinsky and Maria Wihardja, in a rigorous econometric study published in the Journal of East Asian Studies in 2011, found that Indonesian decentralization had "no discernible effect on the country's national-level economic performance," concluding that three structural impediments — extreme heterogeneity in local resource endowments, factor immobility, and "endogenous deterioration of local governance institutions" — had prevented the theorized efficiency gains from competition or democratic accountability from accruing.15

The political consequences were equally mixed. On the positive side, decentralization created genuine local democratic life where none had existed: popularly elected district heads (bupati and walikota), local assemblies with real budgetary powers, and competition among localities for investment and services. In regions long neglected by the centralizing New Order, communities gained the ability to set local priorities for the first time. But the reforms also multiplied the arenas for corruption and patronage. Before decentralization, Indonesia had 292 districts; by 2003, that number had grown to 434 as local elites exploited the new system to carve out additional administrative units — a process scholars call pemekaran (proliferation) — each generating its own patronage networks, civil service positions, and budget allocations.16 Scholars Vedi Hadiz and Richard Robison characterized the primary effect of decentralization as shifting "the locus of predatory behavior downward, allowing for the multiplication of sites for corruption and informal deal making, rather than their elimination."17

Researcher Beche Mamma concluded in a systematic evaluation that Indonesian decentralization was "much more of an administrative decentralization rather than a fiscal decentralization" — the central government continued to control the vast share of revenues required for meaningful local self-governance.18 Anwar, reflecting from the inside of policymaking, acknowledged that decentralization had also produced unintended consequences including environmental degradation (as district heads issued logging and mining permits without effective oversight), ethnic violence linked to local political competition, and "shadow states" dominated by local strongmen who had adapted the New Order's patronage logic to the new democratic form. These assessments do not negate the significance of the reform — the alternative of indefinite Jakartacentric centralism was untenable and dangerous — but they establish that the "Big Bang" produced mixed and contested results that continue to be debated in Indonesia's political economy literature.

Pemekaran — the proliferation of new administrative units (districts and municipalities) through division of existing ones, accelerated by Indonesia's post-1999 decentralization. The number of districts rose from 292 before 2001 to over 430 by 2003, creating new patronage opportunities alongside new local governments.

A Free Press and a Plural Party System

Among the most immediately visible transformations of the Reformasi era was the explosion of press freedom and political pluralism. The New Order had enforced a claustrophobic information environment: the minister of information could and did revoke publishing licenses, and the party system had been reduced — by legal compulsion — to three: Golkar (the regime party), the PPP (for Islamic political forces), and the PDI (for nationalists). After May 1998, Habibie's caretaker government lifted restrictions with remarkable speed. Political prisoners were released, opposition parties were legalized, and a new press law replaced the New Order's restrictive framework with one that eliminated ministerial censorship. Within months Indonesia had developed what Anwar called "a lively press, one of the freest in Asia."19

The party system diversified equally rapidly. For the June 1999 elections — the first free national elections since 1955 — forty-eight parties competed for national legislative seats, a proliferation that reflected both genuine political diversity and the ease with which new organizations could be registered. Voter turnout approached ninety percent. The elections produced a fragmented result: the Indonesian Democratic Party of Struggle (Partai Demokrasi Indonesia-Perjuangan, PDI-P), led by Sukarno's daughter Megawati Sukarnoputri, won the largest share (33.75 percent), followed by Golkar (22.44 percent), which survived the transition by rapidly reinventing itself as a reform-compatible organization. Islamic parties — PKB, PPP, PAN — competed vigorously but no single Islamic political force dominated.20 The subsequent April 2004 elections saw twenty-four parties contest roughly 16,000 legislative seats at national, provincial, and district levels, with more than 140 million voters going to the polls — and for the first time, voters chose the president directly, electing Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono (SBY) over Megawati in a run-off in September 2004.21

Yet from early on, scholars identified structural weaknesses in this democratic party system. The shift from closed-list to open-list proportional representation incentivized individual vote-buying over party-building; parties became vehicles for elite deal-making rather than programmatic organizations with deep roots in civil society. Kristiyanto, Arinanto, and Ghafur, writing in 2023, documented a dramatic collapse in party identification: from approximately 86 percent of voters who identified with a party in 1999 to a mere 6.8 percent by 2022 — a fall that suggests the initial pluralism was organized enthusiasm rather than durable institutional loyalty.22 This institutional shallowness would become increasingly significant in the following decade, as parties converged on a catch-all, transactional model that prioritized coalition formation and business access over ideological differentiation.

The Fight Against Corruption: The KPK

Corruption had been the organizing principle of the New Order's political economy: Suharto's family and associates had accumulated vast wealth through monopolies, contracts, and kickbacks, while the practice of informal payment permeated every layer of the bureaucracy, judiciary, police, and military. The challenge of post-1998 Indonesia was not simply to prosecute Suharto — an effort that produced decades of inconclusive legal proceedings — but to build institutions capable of enforcing anti-corruption norms systemically, against powerful actors who had every reason to resist them.

The principal institutional response was the Corruption Eradication Commission (Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi, KPK), created under Law No. 30 of 2002 (enacted December 2002) and becoming fully operational in late 2003 under President Megawati Sukarnoputri. The law was drafted by a Ministry of Justice steering committee led by Romli Atmasasmita, with technical support from the Asian Development Bank, in the context of an IMF conditional bailout that had made anti-corruption enforcement a formal requirement.23 The KPK was given powers that deliberately exceeded those of the existing attorney general's office and national police — powers widely recognized as having been corrupted themselves. These included the authority to conduct wiretapping without prior judicial approval, to freeze financial transactions, to impose travel bans, and to detain high-ranking officials. Cases handled by the KPK were tried in a specialized anti-corruption court (Pengadilan Tindak Pidana Korupsi, Tipikor), modeled on the Philippines' Sandiganbayan, which heard KPK prosecutions exclusively.24

The results were striking. In its first thirteen years of operation, the KPK maintained what observers described as an "astonishing 100 percent conviction rate" across dozens of prosecuted cases, targeting ministers, governors, members of parliament, supreme court justices, and police generals — actors who had previously enjoyed near-total impunity.25 Indonesia's score on Transparency International's Corruption Perception Index improved from 122nd in 2003 to 88th in 2015, representing a meaningful if incomplete improvement. By 2007, surveys showed the KPK had become Indonesia's most trusted public institution — a remarkable standing for a body created in the shadow of one of the world's most comprehensively corrupt states.26

The KPK's success made it a target. In 2009, two of its deputy chairmen — Bibit Samad Rianto and Chandra Hamzah — were arrested on disputed charges widely seen as an attempt by police and prosecutors to neutralize the commission. The Constitutional Court released recordings that appeared to corroborate allegations of a conspiracy; President Yudhoyono's advisory "Team of Eight" recommended dismissal of the case; and a massive public mobilization — the #SaveKPK moment — forced the government to back down. The episode demonstrated both the genuine popular investment in the commission's independence and the intensity of elite opposition to effective anti-corruption enforcement.27 The KPK's subsequent trajectory — including its effective weakening by a 2019 parliamentary revision passed in twelve days without consulting the commission, which introduced advance approval requirements for wiretaps and converted its staff to civil servant status — falls largely in the story of later administrations treated in subsequent chapters. But the pattern was already visible in the Reformasi era: the KPK was built, tested, survived its first assault, and became a permanent site of contest between reformist and patrimonial impulses in Indonesian democracy.

East Timor, 1999: Referendum, Violence, and Independence

The most internationally consequential episode of the early Reformasi period was not a domestic constitutional question but a decision by the Habibie government to offer the people of East Timor a choice that no Indonesian government had previously been willing to contemplate: a direct vote on whether to accept a special autonomy arrangement within Indonesia or to seek independence. The offer was extraordinary. Indonesia had invaded the Portuguese colony in December 1975 and formally annexed it in 1976; the subsequent occupation had been marked by mass killings, famine, and systematic human rights abuses documented by the UN and human rights organizations, killing an estimated 100,000–180,000 East Timorese through violence, starvation, and disease across two decades. The Reformasi political context — an Indonesia attempting to reposition itself as a democratic state committed to human rights — and Habibie's own political calculations combined to produce a referendum offer that surprised even his cabinet.

The United Nations Mission in East Timor (UNAMET) administered the ballot, which was scheduled for August 1999. The security environment was immediately threatening: Indonesian military units and their allied militia organizations — numbering some 30,000 fighters in fourteen groups, including Aitarak, Besi Merah Putih, and Mahidi — began a campaign of intimidation and violence against pro-independence communities and UNAMET staff. Attacks at the Catholic Diocese of Dili and at Liquiça, where dozens were killed, demonstrated the willingness of pro-integration forces to use lethal force. Despite the violence, an overwhelming 98.6 percent of registered East Timorese voters cast ballots on 30 August 1999. On 4 September, UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan announced the result: 78.5 percent had voted for independence.28

The announcement triggered a catastrophe. In the days following the result, militia forces — acting alongside elements of the Indonesian Army (TNI) under the overall command of General Wiranto — launched what scholars and human rights investigators have characterized as a "scorched earth" operation across East Timor. Approximately 1,400 civilians were killed; 80 percent of Dili's infrastructure was destroyed; and more than 220,000 people — the majority of the territory's population — were forcibly displaced, many driven at gunpoint into camps in Indonesian West Timor under deplorable conditions.29 The Catholic Diocese of Dili was burned; the International Committee of the Red Cross compound was attacked; and UNAMET staff sheltered thousands of fleeing civilians before being themselves endangered.

International pressure mounted rapidly. On 15 September 1999, the UN Security Council adopted Resolution 1264, authorizing a multinational force to restore peace and security. The International Force East Timor (INTERFET) entered the territory on 20 September 1999, commanded by Australian Major General Peter Cosgrove and drawn from twenty-two nations — Australia providing some 5,500 of the peak deployment of roughly 11,500 troops, New Zealand contributing 1,200.30 Indonesia's acceptance of the force — however reluctant — marked a significant concession; the last Indonesian troops departed by 31 October 1999. East Timor transitioned through a UN transitional administration to formal independence on 20 May 2002, becoming the first new sovereign state of the twenty-first century as Timor-Leste.

The East Timor episode had lasting consequences for Indonesia's international reputation and its domestic politics. It raised questions about command accountability — General Wiranto faced a UN-mandated inquiry into his role, though he was never successfully prosecuted — and it intensified demands within Indonesia for civilian control over the military and for accountability mechanisms that could reach senior commanders. For the peoples of Aceh and West Papua, watching from within Indonesia, the East Timor outcome carried an ambiguous message: that sustained resistance to Jakarta could produce a referendum, but also that the path to that point had been extraordinarily violent.

INTERFET — the International Force East Timor, a UN Security Council–authorized multinational force that entered East Timor on 20 September 1999 following the post-referendum violence. Commanded by Australian Major General Peter Cosgrove, INTERFET restored order and preceded a UN transitional administration leading to full independence in 2002.

The Aceh Peace: Tsunami, Talks, and the Helsinki MoU

The conflict in Aceh was older, more entrenched, and more structurally embedded in Indonesian sovereignty than the East Timor situation. The Gerakan Aceh Merdeka (GAM, the Free Aceh Movement), founded on 4 December 1976 by Hasan di Tiro with an initial force of just twenty-five fighters, had by the late 1990s grown into a substantial insurgency of between 15,000 and 27,000 combatants, drawing on deep Acehnese grievances about Jakarta's exploitation of the province's oil and gas wealth, the brutality of military operations dating to the 1989–1998 "Military Operations Zone" (Daerah Operasi Militer, DOM), and a historic sense of Acehnese distinctiveness rooted in centuries of independence and the longest anticolonial resistance in Indonesian history.31 Across the full course of the GAM insurgency, approximately 15,000 people died — overwhelmingly Acehnese civilians caught between a brutal military counter-insurgency and a disciplined but also violent armed movement.

Previous attempts to negotiate a settlement had failed. A "Humanitarian Pause" in 2000 and a more formal Cessation of Hostilities Agreement (COHA) signed in Geneva in 2002 both collapsed, the former amid continuing violence, the latter when the Indonesian military launched a full-scale offensive in May 2003 that killed or captured large numbers of GAM fighters and drove the leadership back to Sweden. The offensive reduced GAM's military strength by approximately fifty percent, but it could not extinguish the movement, and the cost in civilian casualties, displacement, and international criticism produced a "costly stalemate" that left both sides looking for an exit.32

The catastrophe of 26 December 2004 altered the political geometry. The Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami killed between 130,000 and 170,000 people in Aceh — roughly a third of the province's fatalities from the whole tsunami event — destroyed entire coastal cities including Banda Aceh and Meulaboh, and presented both the Indonesian government and GAM with an overwhelming humanitarian emergency that superseded their military contest. GAM declared a unilateral ceasefire and offered to assist relief efforts. The Indonesian government, for the first time, opened Aceh to a substantial international humanitarian presence — a decision driven by necessity that also, irreversibly, ended the province's information blackout. Former Finnish President Martti Ahtisaari, acting through his Crisis Management Initiative (CMI), seized the opening. His assistant Juha Christensen had been pursuing backchannel contacts since before the tsunami; the disaster provided both the urgency and the political cover to bring the parties formally to the table.33

Five rounds of talks in Helsinki — in January, February, April, May, and July 2005 — produced the Memorandum of Understanding signed on 15 August 2005. The Indonesian delegation was led by Minister of Law and Human Rights Hamid Awaluddin; the GAM delegation was led by Malik Mahmud. The MoU's seventy-one articles across six sections provided for comprehensive self-governance for Aceh in all matters except foreign relations, external defense, national security, monetary and fiscal policy, justice, and religious freedom — an autonomy architecture that went well beyond existing decentralization law. Crucially, and without precedent in Indonesian constitutional practice, Aceh was permitted to establish local political parties, enabling former GAM members to compete in elections without joining national parties.34

The Aceh Monitoring Mission (AMM) — a joint EU-ASEAN force of more than 300 monitors — oversaw implementation. GAM decommissioned its weapons; Indonesian military and police forces were drawn down to agreed levels; political prisoners were released; and the AMM's mandate expired on 15 December 2006 following the successful conduct of provincial elections. In those December 2006 elections, former GAM commander Irwandi Yusuf won the provincial governorship — a symbolic capstone to the peace process that demonstrated the MoU's transformation of armed separatists into electoral competitors. The settlement has held. Aceh has remained part of Indonesia while exercising substantially greater autonomy than other provinces, including through Sharia-based local regulations, though human rights organizations have noted that accountability for the abuses of the DOM era — on both sides — has been minimal.35

The Helsinki MoU is widely regarded as one of the most successful peace processes of the post-Cold War era, notable for its speed (six months of talks), its comprehensiveness (covering security, governance, economics, and political participation in a single document), and the durability of its outcomes. Its success was possible because both sides had genuine incentives — the tsunami had demonstrated that continued conflict was unconscionable, and the stalemate had convinced military planners that victory was unattainable. Ahtisaari's ability to build confidence between delegations that had spent three years trying to kill each other, and Hamid Awaluddin's authority within the Indonesian government, were equally essential. The process also benefited from the Yudhoyono government's political interest in demonstrating that Reformasi Indonesia could resolve its conflicts through negotiation rather than force.

GAM (Gerakan Aceh Merdeka) — the Free Aceh Movement, an insurgent organization founded in 1976 that fought for an independent Acehnese state. GAM signed the 2005 Helsinki MoU, disarmed, and transformed into a political movement; former GAM commanders have subsequently won elections in the province.

Reformasi: Success, Stagnation, and Unfinished Business

How should the Reformasi era be assessed? A generation after 1998, the Indonesian political science literature contains a genuine and productive debate, not a settled verdict.

The case for Reformasi as a significant democratic success is substantial and should not be understated. Indonesia in 1999 became a democracy — not a quasi-democracy or a formal democracy with a single dominant party, but a genuinely competitive system with free elections, a free press, multiple viable parties, civilian supremacy (formally) over the military, an independent judiciary (however imperfect), an independent anti-corruption commission, and institutionalized mechanisms for peaceful transfers of power. It is the world's third-largest democracy by population and, as of the early 2000s, the world's largest Muslim-majority democracy — a fact of enormous global significance given anxieties about the compatibility of Islam and democratic governance. The constitutional architecture built between 1999 and 2004 has proven resilient: elections have been held on schedule, incumbents have lost and accepted defeats, and no military coup has interrupted the democratic sequence.36 The peace settlements in East Timor and Aceh removed two of the New Order's most intractable conflict legacies. Press freedom, for all its vulnerabilities to commercial concentration and political pressure, represents a transformation from the censored information environment of the Suharto years.

Against this, a strand of critical scholarship — associated especially with Vedi Hadiz, Richard Robison, and their collaborators — argues that what Indonesia achieved after 1998 was better characterized as a reorganization of power within an existing elite than as a fundamental break. In their analysis, the New Order had produced a political economy organized around oligarchic networks of military, bureaucratic, and business interests; Reformasi did not displace these networks but allowed them to adapt to democratic forms. Golkar survived and thrived. Business oligarchs moved into party politics. Former military commanders won elected offices. Corruption did not decline; it decentralized. The rules of competition changed, but the players and their fundamental interests remained.37 This "oligarchic democracy" thesis has been widely debated but never fully refuted; it captures something real about the persistence of patrimonial relationships even within functioning democratic institutions.

A related concern is what scholars have called the institutional fragility of the new party system. Party identification collapsed from the heady enthusiasm of 1999 to single digits by the 2020s; parties function less as programmatic organizations than as electoral vehicles for individual politicians and elite factions.38 The open-list proportional representation system, combined with the absence of state funding for political parties at a meaningful scale, produced a permanent incentive for vote-buying and patronage distribution — what Indonesian commentators call politik uang ("money politics") — at every level of the electoral system. Democracy had taken root, but its roots were shallow: voters participated enthusiastically, but their relationship with the party system was transactional rather than programmatic.

Dewi Fortuna Anwar, reflecting as an insider who participated in the early reform process, offers a nuanced assessment that acknowledges both the achievement and its limits: "Consolidating and institutionalising democracy is still very much a work in progress."39 The haste of the early transition period — Habibie's government passed over two hundred laws in seventeen months — meant that many were poorly drafted, internally inconsistent, or inadequately resourced for implementation. The sequencing problem was acute: new institutions were established before the personnel, legal culture, and accountability mechanisms necessary for them to function effectively were in place. The Constitutional Court operated admirably from the outset; the KPK built an impressive record; but decentralization produced mixed governance outcomes and the military reform remained incomplete.

One way to frame the debate is in terms of transition versus consolidation. Reformasi accomplished the transition from authoritarianism to competitive democracy with remarkable speed and limited violence — in a country of 200 million people with no prior democratic tradition beyond the brief parliamentary experiments of the 1950s, this was an extraordinary achievement. Consolidation — the embedding of democratic norms, practices, and expectations so deeply that backsliding becomes structurally difficult — proved far more challenging. The KPK's assault by political elites in 2009 and again in 2019; the military's slow retreat from civilian spheres; the persistence of money politics; and the fragility of party institutionalization all signal that Reformasi had opened a democratic era without yet closing the question of what kind of democracy Indonesia would ultimately become. The succeeding chapters trace how that question has continued to be answered, and contested, under the reform-era presidents and their successors.

Scholarly Debate: "Oligarchic Democracy"? The most influential critical account of post-Suharto Indonesia holds that Reformasi produced a "reorganization of power" rather than its displacement — oligarchic networks adapted to electoral competition rather than being swept away by it. Vedi Hadiz and Richard Robison's Reorganising Power in Indonesia (2004) is the foundational text of this position. Critics counter that the thesis underestimates the extent of genuine political pluralism and the constraints democratic accountability places on elite behavior over time. The debate remains unresolved and active in the literature.

Summary

The post-1998 Reformasi era produced a structural transformation of Indonesian governance whose depth is sometimes underestimated. Four rounds of constitutional amendment (1999–2002) replaced an authoritarian presidential system with one featuring direct elections, a new regional chamber (DPD), term limits, and military exclusion from the legislature. The formal doctrine of dwifungsi was abolished and POLRI separated from TNI, though full military withdrawal from civilian life remained incomplete. Laws 22 and 25 of 1999 launched a "Big Bang" decentralization, transferring powers to districts and municipalities — creating local democratic life but also new arenas for corruption and patronage. Forty-eight parties contested the 1999 elections; a free press took root; and the KPK, established under Law 30/2002, achieved a 100 percent conviction rate in its early years and became Indonesia's most trusted institution. The 1999 East Timor referendum — in which 78.5 percent voted for independence — was followed by catastrophic militia and TNI violence before INTERFET restored order and independence was secured. The 2005 Helsinki MoU, mediated by Martti Ahtisaari and catalyzed by the 2004 tsunami, ended the Aceh insurgency through a settlement providing extensive autonomy and local political parties. The scholarly assessment of Reformasi balances genuine democratic achievement against persistent oligarchic adaptation, institutional fragility, and the gap between formal reform and substantive change — a balance that defines the unfinished project of Indonesian democracy.

37.1 Why did the post-Suharto reformers choose to amend the 1945 Constitution four times rather than draft an entirely new constitution? What does this decision reveal about the political constraints of the transition period?
37.2 The DPD was created as a regional chamber but given limited legislative powers. Compare it with a fully empowered senate. What might Indonesia have gained and risked from a stronger upper chamber?
37.3 Evaluate the claim that Indonesia's decentralization shifted the locus of corruption downward rather than reducing it. What evidence supports this view, and what might complicate or challenge it?
37.4 Why did Indonesia's party identification collapse from approximately 86 percent in 1999 to single digits by 2022? What does this trajectory suggest about the relationship between democratic elections and democratic consolidation?
37.5 The East Timor crisis and the Aceh peace settlement had very different outcomes. What factors explain why the Indonesian government's approach to these two territorial conflicts diverged so sharply?
37.6 How did the December 2004 tsunami create conditions for the Helsinki peace process? What does this suggest about the relationship between humanitarian catastrophe and political negotiation?
37.7 Assess the "oligarchic democracy" thesis of Hadiz and Robison. In what ways does the post-Suharto evidence support their argument? In what ways does it challenge it?
37.8 The KPK achieved a 100 percent conviction rate in its early years but faced repeated political attacks. Why might an effective anti-corruption commission generate such intense elite opposition in a democracy?
