Learning Objectives

  • Analyse the Banda Islands massacre of 1621 as a formative act of VOC monopoly violence, and evaluate the historiographic debate over whether it constitutes genocide.
  • Describe the perkenier plantation system installed after the massacre and explain how it relied on enslaved labour to sustain the nutmeg monopoly.
  • Explain the logic of the hongi-tochten extirpation expeditions in the Moluccas and assess their human costs.
  • Account for the VOC's military entanglement in the Sultanate of Gowa (Makassar) and in the successive Javanese civil wars of the late seventeenth and early eighteenth centuries.
  • Trace the causes and aftermath of the 1740 Chinese massacre of Batavia and its connection to structural changes in the VOC's Asian economy.
  • Identify the intertwined factors — corruption, military overstretch, financial mismanagement, and external war — that brought about the VOC's bankruptcy and dissolution in 1799.

For nearly two centuries the Dutch East India Company (Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie, or VOC) wielded a violence that went far beyond the protection of trade. Beginning with the annihilation of the Bandanese people in 1621 and ending with a bankruptcy engineered partly by its own corruption, the company's arc in the Indonesian archipelago is one of the most consequential — and most debated — episodes of early modern colonialism. What the VOC built through massacre, plantation slavery, and war it lost through institutional decay, financial recklessness, and the storms of the Atlantic world. This chapter traces that arc from its most brutal peak to its ignominious end, following the spice islands, the ports of Sulawesi, the courts of Java, the riverbanks of Batavia, and the counting houses of Amsterdam.

Monopoly by Other Means

The VOC's charter of 1602 gave it an extraordinary instrument: the right, on behalf of the Dutch Republic, to make treaties, wage war, and establish fortified posts throughout the Indian Ocean world. What distinguished the company from its Portuguese predecessor or English rival was not merely commercial ambition but a readiness — indeed, a systematic disposition — to use overwhelming military force to establish and enforce exclusive trading relationships. The Heeren XVII (the "Lords Seventeen" who governed the company from Amsterdam) were clear that profit required monopoly, and monopoly in the spice trade required the elimination of all competition, whether from rival Europeans, from Asian merchants who could trade freely, or from the indigenous producers themselves.1

Jan Pieterszoon Coen, who became Governor-General of the VOC's Asian operations in 1618, was the man who most completely embodied this logic. Coen was not simply a zealous official; he was an architect. He had argued before the Heeren XVII that the company could never be profitable without planting colonial settlements of Dutch subjects in Asia — a model of territorial domination rather than trading post. He saw indigenous autonomy as an obstacle to be removed, not an interest to be accommodated. His letters to the Heeren XVII are striking for their cold instrumental reasoning: if the Bandanese would not submit to exclusive contracts on Dutch terms, they would have to be replaced with people who would.2 That formulation — the replacement of a recalcitrant population — was not a figure of speech.

To understand why the spice islands became the site of the VOC's most extreme violence, one must grasp the economics of the trade. Nutmeg and mace, produced nowhere in the world except the tiny Banda archipelago — a cluster of ten islands in the Banda Sea, the largest no more than twelve kilometres across — commanded extraordinary prices in European markets as flavourings, preservatives, and supposed medicines. Cloves were produced almost exclusively on a handful of islands in the northern Moluccas. The VOC's strategy was simple: monopolise the source, control the price, and pocket the difference. Any indigenous trade that evaded VOC middlemen was not merely a lost profit; it was, in the company's view, an act of war against the monopoly itself. It was this logic, pressed to its ultimate conclusion, that produced the massacre of 1621.3

The Banda Massacre, 1621

The Banda Islands before the VOC were home to a society of perhaps thirteen thousand to fifteen thousand people — estimates by historians Vincent Loth and Charles Corn — organised around a merchant-aristocracy known as the orang kaya (literally "rich men"), who governed inter-island trade and maintained a loose confederation among the islands.3 The Bandanese had traded nutmeg with Javanese, Malay, Indian, and Chinese merchants for centuries before Europeans arrived, and they resisted — sometimes violently — the VOC's insistence on an exclusive contract that would hand all their produce to the company at company-set prices. Earlier VOC attempts to force submission had been resisted or evaded; a 1609 negotiation had ended with the killing of the VOC's Admiral Verhoef.2

Orang kaya — Literally "rich men" in Malay: the merchant-aristocratic leaders of Bandanese society who governed interisland trade and negotiated (and resisted) with the VOC. Their execution in May 1621 was the decisive act in the destruction of Bandanese political society.

In October 1620, Coen wrote to the Heeren XVII that "to adequately deal with this matter, it is necessary to once and again subjugate the islands and repopulate them with different inhabitants."4 The intent was unambiguous. In February and March 1621 he sailed into the Banda Sea with a force the PALA Project's reconstruction estimates at approximately two thousand two hundred military personnel — around one thousand six hundred and fifty-five Dutch soldiers, two hundred and fifty settled-garrison troops, one hundred rōnin (Japanese mercenary samurai), and nearly three hundred Javanese auxiliaries, carried on nineteen ships.5 The main assault on Lontor island — the largest in the archipelago — came on 11–12 March 1621. VOC forces and their allies swept across the island, burning villages and driving the population into the forests and up the volcanic slopes, where many jumped from the cliffs rather than surrender.

On 8 May 1621, forty-four to forty-eight orang kaya who had agreed to negotiate were instead arrested, tried summarily, and executed. Their bodies were quartered; the heads were mounted on bamboo stakes as a display of VOC authority.5 Coen himself reported that "about 2,500 died of hunger and misery or by the sword" — a figure historians regard as a probable undercount. Peter V. Lape, analysing the archaeological and demographic evidence, estimates that roughly ninety per cent of the total pre-conquest population was killed, enslaved, or forcibly deported.6 Loth's estimate, based on colonial records, suggests that by 1681 the indigenous Bandanese population had declined to approximately one hundred individuals — from a starting point of at least thirteen thousand.3

The question of how to characterise these events has been debated by historians. Amitav Ghosh, writing in 2021, places the Banda massacre at the origin point of a global history of extractive capitalism — treating it as an archetype of the dispossession of indigenous peoples in service of commodity production.7 More formally, Frank Dhont, contributing to the Cambridge World History of Genocide (2023), categorises the VOC's actions in the Banda Islands as genocide, pointing to the deliberate targeting of a population for elimination as a precondition for economic exploitation.8 Tristan Mostert's 2023 Leiden University doctoral research on the clove islands of the northern Moluccas employs the same framing for the VOC's later campaigns on West Seram.13 Other historians have been more cautious about anachronistic application of a term coined in the twentieth century, preferring to describe the events as an extraordinary act of colonial violence driven by commercial imperatives. Wim van Lent, Gazi Islam, and Imran Chowdhury have analysed the Banda conquest through the lens of corporate capitalism, arguing that the VOC combined legitimating discourse — claims to treaty rights and retaliation for Bandanese "treachery" — with systematic violence to enable accumulation; they describe the result as "civilized dispossession."4 What is not in dispute, across all these interpretive frameworks, is that the indigenous population of the Banda Islands was effectively destroyed as a social and political entity, and that this destruction was the deliberate precondition for the establishment of the company's most profitable monopoly.

The wider Banda Sea remained contested. The island of Run, held by English traders, was a persistent irritant to the Dutch monopoly; its formal cession to the VOC was only secured by the Treaty of Breda in 1667, in which England surrendered its last Banda foothold in exchange for Dutch recognition of New Amsterdam (New York).1 The Amboyna incident of 1623 — the execution by the VOC of ten English East India Company employees on charges of conspiracy — deepened the breach between the two companies and effectively drove the English out of the Spice Islands, leaving the VOC without a European rival in the eastern archipelago for the next century and a half.

The Perkenier System: Plantation Slavery in the Nutmeg Islands

The VOC did not leave the Banda Islands empty. Coen's plan was explicit: destroy the existing population, import enslaved labour, and install Dutch-managed plantations to continue producing the nutmeg that had made the islands valuable in the first place. By 1628, the Westfries Museum's PALA Project has documented, the company had surveyed and divided Banda's arable land into sixty-eight equal units called perken (singular perk): thirty-three on Lontor, thirty-one on Ay island, and three on Banda Neira. Each perk was assigned to a manager — a perkenier — typically a former VOC employee, and was stocked by the company with twenty-five enslaved workers.9

Perkenier / perk — The plantation system the VOC installed in the Banda Islands after 1621. Each perk (estate unit) was managed by a perkenier, usually a former VOC employee. Perkeniers were obligated to sell all nutmeg and mace to the VOC at company-set prices and to purchase supplies exclusively from VOC stores — making them simultaneously private entrepreneurs and company dependants. The system survived until 1873.

The perkenier system was a peculiar hybrid. The managers held nominal private rights to their estates but were thoroughly enmeshed in company control: they were compelled to sell all nutmeg and mace to the VOC at prices fixed by the Heeren XVII, and to buy food, tools, and imported goods exclusively from VOC warehouses at company-set prices. Their profit margin was the sliver between those two controlled rates.10 Enslaved workers were sourced from across the Asian world — from Siau (North Sulawesi), Sri Lanka, India, the Malay peninsula, the Cape Colony, Java, and Makassar — because the Bandanese themselves had been nearly exterminated.9 By 1694, the PALA Project's records show approximately one thousand nine hundred enslaved workers on the perken, though the company estimated that two thousand five hundred were needed to operate the plantations at full capacity; the shortfall was partly a consequence of catastrophic mortality rates. In Banda Neira alone, three hundred and seventy-five people died in 1638; across the archipelago, seven hundred and seventy-seven enslaved workers died in 1693.9

The perkenier system has attracted considerable scholarly attention as an early example of what Phillip Winn has described as "one of very few historical situations where Asian slaves worked on European-owned farms" — a configuration that anticipated the plantation economies of the Atlantic world.11 The economic historian J.L. van Zanden has noted the role of the Banda revenues in sustaining Dutch merchant capital through much of the seventeenth century.12 The VOC managed to control global nutmeg prices effectively only from around 1680, after years of strategic stockpiling and auction manipulation. The monopoly was not definitively broken until 1778, when French botanists succeeded in cultivating nutmeg in Mauritius — a development that eventually opened global production and destroyed the geographic exclusivity on which the entire violent architecture of the perkenier system rested.20

Cloves, Hongi-Tochten, and the Extirpation System

Nutmeg was only one of the VOC's two great Moluccan monopolies. Cloves, grown primarily on the islands of Ternate, Tidore, Bacan, and the Ambon Islands (Amboina), were equally precious to European consumers and equally targeted for exclusive company control. The clove trade, however, presented a different challenge from nutmeg: the islands capable of growing cloves were more numerous and spread across a larger area, and the indigenous rulers of Ternate and Tidore had cultivated long-distance trading relationships with Makassar, Java, and beyond that the VOC found impossible to eliminate simply by treaty.

The VOC's response was the hongi-tochten — annual extirpation expeditions, named from the Ternatan word for "armada," which deployed fleets of kora-kora (Moluccan war canoes) to enforce the clove monopoly by fire and sword.13 Under Governor Philip Lucasz (1618–1631), the VOC intercepted foreign merchant vessels and coerced indigenous rulers into handing over contraband cloves. Under his successor Artus Gijsels (1631–1634), the policy became more explicitly destructive: houses were burnt, edible crops destroyed, and clove trees felled in any village found to be trading with non-Dutch merchants.13 The logic was the same as at Banda — replace a trading society that could not be controlled with a captive labour system that could — but the scale of destruction in the clove islands unfolded more slowly and more diffusely.

Hongi-tochten — Annual VOC extirpation expeditions in the Moluccas, conducted by fleets of kora-kora (Maluku war canoes) supplemented by Dutch soldiers. The purpose was to destroy clove trees outside authorised VOC-licensed areas and to punish villages trading with non-Dutch merchants. The name derives from the Ternatan word for "armada." The expeditions were launched continuously from the 1620s through the eighteenth century.

The western peninsula of Ceram (Seram) — known as Hoamoal — proved a persistent centre of resistance: it was attacked from 1625 onward, endured a major military campaign from 1651 to 1655, and was finally subdued with what Mostert characterises as the systematic destruction of its society. By 1656 the VOC had achieved effective colonial control of the Ambon Islands, giving it the administrative platform from which to launch the annual hongi fleets.13

The human costs of the extirpation system were enormous and are only now receiving full scholarly attention. Matthias van Rossum's 2019 study of labour regimes within the VOC empire documented that the Ambon region alone may have contributed "more than two million days of work" in corvée labour obligations during the 1690s — with the annual hongi expeditions demanding the conscription of thousands of Ambonese rowers and warriors for the company's war fleets.14 Van Rossum's analysis describes the VOC as an "amphibious monster" that combined merchant, military, and governmental functions simultaneously, operating multiple labour regimes — slave, corvée, contract, and casual wage — across the same geographic space. The population of the clove islands declined sharply during the seventeenth century, as disease, famine consequent on crop destruction, and direct violence reduced communities that had sustained themselves through trade to impoverished and coerced dependants of the company.

The Conquest of Makassar and the Treaty of Bongaya

While the VOC consolidated its grip on the nutmeg and clove islands, the greatest remaining challenge to its eastern archipelago strategy was the port-city of Makassar, capital of the Sultanate of Gowa on the southwestern tip of Sulawesi. Makassar was, by the mid-seventeenth century, one of the largest and most cosmopolitan trading cities in Southeast Asia. Its rulers — particularly Sultan Hasanuddin, the sixteenth Sultan of Gowa, who reigned from 1653 to 1669 — had deliberately kept the port open to all comers, including English, Portuguese, Danish, and Malay merchants whose presence directly undermined the VOC's attempt to monopolise regional commodity flows.15 "The sea is free," Hasanuddin reportedly told the Dutch; it was a principle the VOC could not tolerate.

The deeper military context included the Sultan of Ternate's alignment with Makassar and the deployment of Gowa troops against VOC allies in the northern Moluccas, which the company interpreted as an act of war. In October 1666 the VOC formally declared war on Gowa. On 24 November 1666, a fleet of twenty-one ships carrying approximately one thousand eight hundred and sixty men under Admiral Cornelis Speelman departed for Sulawesi.15 Speelman's campaign was shaped decisively by an alliance with Arung Palakka, the exiled Bugis leader of the Bone kingdom, whose people had long resented Gowa's hegemony over South Sulawesi. Leonard Andaya's authoritative study of this period argues that the VOC victory was not simply a Dutch triumph but a Bugis revenge: Arung Palakka's warriors provided the manpower that made Speelman's campaign viable, and the Bugis leader used the alliance to dismantle Gowa's dominance of the peninsula.15

The war lasted through 1667 and into 1669. After a series of naval engagements and the progressive reduction of Gowa's coastal fortifications, Hasanuddin signed the Treaty of Bongaya on 18 November 1667. The treaty's thirty articles were a comprehensive dismemberment of Gowa's sovereignty: all non-Dutch Europeans were expelled from Makassar; a Dutch monopoly was established over the port's trade; Gowa was reduced to vassal status; and Hasanuddin was required to demolish all fortresses except his own residence at Samboepo. The strategic stronghold at Jum Pandan was ceded to the Dutch, who converted it into Fort Rotterdam — named by Speelman after his birthplace in the Netherlands — and turned it into the VOC's administrative headquarters for Sulawesi. Gowa was also required to pay reparations of two hundred and fifty thousand rijksdaalders over five seasons.16

Hasanuddin did not accept the settlement passively. Within months of signing, he resumed hostilities, and the VOC fought on until Gowa's strongest fortress fell on 12 June 1669. Hasanuddin abdicated and died exactly one year later, on 12 June 1670. News of the original treaty's signing had been celebrated in Batavia in March 1668.16 The conquest of Makassar effectively closed the last major free port of the eastern archipelago: after 1667, no European rival and no Indonesian state had the military capacity to challenge the VOC's monopoly system in the waters east of Java. Arung Palakka became the dominant political figure of South Sulawesi, governing as a VOC client until his death in 1696, and the Bugis — having helped destroy Gowa — became some of the most important maritime actors in the eighteenth-century archipelago, sometimes as VOC allies and sometimes as the very free traders the company sought to suppress.

Entanglement in Java: The Mataram Succession Wars

The VOC's engagement with the Islamic Mataram Sultanate of Java differed fundamentally from its approach to the spice islands. In the Moluccas, the company sought to destroy or replace indigenous societies; in Java, where the agricultural hinterland was too vast and the population too dense for such a strategy, the company inserted itself into existing political structures as a power-broker, moneylender, and kingmaker. The result was a century-long entanglement that drained the company's resources while progressively dismembering Mataram's sovereignty, leaving Java's inland rulers as clients of the VOC and its northern coast as a VOC economic zone.1

The first major crisis came with the Trunajaya rebellion of 1674–1681. A Madurese prince, Trunajaya, allied with Makassarese fighters who had fled Speelman's conquest of Gowa to raise a broad anti-Mataram coalition. In 1677 his forces captured and burned Plered, the Mataram capital, prompting the flight and death of the long-reigning but widely despised Amangkurat I. The new king, Amangkurat II, appealed to the VOC for military assistance. Speelman again led the campaign, now with Arung Palakka's Bugis troops as allies. Trunajaya's capital at Kediri fell in November 1678; the rebel leader was captured in December 1679 and executed by Amangkurat II himself in 1680.1 The VOC presented its bill. By 1682, Mataram's accumulated debt to the company exceeded one and a half million reals; in lieu of cash, Amangkurat II ceded the revenues of the northern coast harbour towns, transferred the Priangan highlands and the port of Semarang to VOC control, and granted the company legal jurisdiction over non-Javanese residents of the kingdom.17

The pattern deepened through successive wars. The First Javanese War of Succession (1703–1708) broke out after Amangkurat II's death, when a rival claimant — Pangeran Puger, who took the throne name Pakubuwono I — sought VOC support against the legitimate heir Amangkurat III. The company backed Pakubuwono I, whose 1705 agreement with the VOC was among the most sweeping surrenders of sovereignty in the entire period. Under its terms, Mataram ceded to the VOC an annual tribute of approximately thirteen hundred metric tons of rice and granted the company exclusive monopolies over opium and imported textiles, rendering what had been one of Java's most powerful monarchies effectively a VOC tributary state. Ricklefs has described Pakubuwono I as "the first genuine puppet of the Dutch."19

Two further wars of succession — the Second (1719–1723) and Third (1746–1755) — followed the same structural logic: rival Javanese princes competed for the throne, each seeking VOC military support and each paying for it with territorial and economic concessions. By the Treaty of Salatiga in 1757, which ended the Third Javanese War of Succession, the once-unified Mataram had been permanently divided into four realms: the Sultanate of Yogyakarta, the Sunanate of Surakarta (Solo), and two subsidiary principalities. The VOC controlled the entire northern Java coast — the region the Dutch called the Pasisir — along with the revenues of its ports, the coffee production of the Priangan, and effective sovereignty over most of Java's external relations.18 What the VOC gained militarily from these wars, however, was more than offset by what it spent. The costs of each intervention — in ships, men, munitions, and the salaries of an expanding administrative apparatus — added to a balance sheet that was already straining by the early eighteenth century.

The period of the Mataram wars also saw the emergence of two figures who became emblematic of the difficulties the VOC faced in Java. The fugitive Makassarese warrior Surapati, sheltered by the anti-Dutch faction at the Mataram court in the 1680s, killed the VOC captain François Tack in 1686 and spent twenty years leading resistance in East Java before his death in battle in 1706. His career illustrates how the VOC's enemies from one theatre of operations — Makassar — migrated to another, carrying their resistance with them across the archipelago. The company's Java interventions were thus not merely a Javanese political story but the continuation of a broader regional struggle against VOC monopoly that the conquest of Makassar had displaced rather than resolved.1

The Chinese Massacre of Batavia, 1740

In the early decades of the eighteenth century, the Chinese community of Batavia had grown to become the indispensable economic foundation of the VOC's Asian capital. Leonard Blussé's influential 1981 study traced the rise of Batavia as what he called a "Chinese colonial town": from its founding by Coen in 1619, the city depended on Chinese merchants, artisans, and labourers to function as a port and processing centre. By the 1730s the Chinese population of Batavia and its immediate hinterland — the area beyond the city walls where tens of thousands worked in sugar mills, market gardens, and construction — was estimated at between ten thousand and fifteen thousand people within the city and many thousands more outside it.21

The crisis of 1740 emerged from a convergence of economic and political pressures. The global price of sugar had roughly halved between 1720 and 1740, destroying the profitability of the sugar mills in Batavia's hinterland and throwing thousands of Chinese workers into unemployment and poverty. The number of operating sugar mills around Batavia fell from one hundred and thirty-one in 1710 to sixty-six by 1750.22 At the same time, the VOC was attempting to reduce the Chinese population through a policy of deporting "surplus" Chinese — those without employment or residence permits — to Ceylon and the Cape Colony as forced labourers. Governor-General Adriaan Valckenier's decree of 25 July 1740 extended deportation to all Chinese regarded as "suspicious," and corrupt VOC officials used the threat of deportation as leverage for extortion, demanding bribes to stay removal orders.23

Katy Ward's research on forced migration within the VOC empire has shown that the deportation of Chinese from Batavia was part of a broader pattern of the company moving dependent labour populations across its Asian network to address perceived surpluses and shortages, with little regard for the human consequences.24 The Chinese community responded to Valckenier's decrees with rising alarm; by September 1740, rumours circulating inside the walls predicted that the VOC intended to kill all the Chinese rather than deport them. Armed groups of unemployed Chinese sugar workers began gathering in the hinterland.

On 7 October 1740, Chinese workers attacked and killed approximately fifty Dutch soldiers at Meister Cornelis (now Jatinegara) and Tanah Abang. The following day, Dutch and VOC allied forces repelled an assault on the city itself, and the colonial government issued a decree offering a reward of two ducats for every Chinese head brought to the authorities.25 What followed was a massacre. Over the nights of 9 and 10 October, VOC troops and armed European and Eurasian residents moved through the Chinese quarter — the pecinan along the Besar River — killing indiscriminately. "Pregnant and nursing women, children, and trembling old men fell on the sword," a contemporary Dutch account recorded.25 The widely cited death toll, drawn from Thomas Stamford Raffles's later History of Java (1830), is approximately ten thousand ethnic Chinese killed; between six hundred and seven hundred Chinese-owned houses were burned. Modern Indonesian historians generally accept a figure in the range of five thousand to ten thousand. The Batavia city council eventually called for the killings to cease on 22 October, offering amnesty to all but the identified leaders of the uprising.

Governor-General Valckenier was arrested in December 1740 by his own council — not for the massacre, which the VOC in Amsterdam eventually chose to overlook, but for abuses of power against Dutch rivals within the administration. He was convicted in 1744 and died in prison on 20 June 1751 before his sentence could be executed.25 Blussé's structural analysis of the massacre situates it within the longer transformation of Batavia's economic role: as the VOC shifted from Asian "country trade" to direct Europe-Asia shipping in the late seventeenth century, the Chinese middlemen whose commercial network had made Batavia prosperous became structurally superfluous and politically expendable.21 A.R.T. Kemasang, writing from a political-economic perspective in 1982, argued that the massacre served to clear the way for the VOC's own plantation agriculture in the Batavia hinterland — that the destruction of independent Chinese enterprise was the "curtain raiser" for the Dutch plantation economy that would follow under the Cultivation System in the nineteenth century.22 M.C. Ricklefs's careful archival study of "the crisis of 1740–1 in Java" has mapped the events in finer grain, tracing the roles of rival factions within the VOC council and the impact on Javanese political alignments.23

All surviving Chinese in Batavia were subsequently confined to the pecinan — the district outside the city walls that is today's Glodok neighbourhood of Jakarta. Many Chinese refugees fled to the Javanese interior, joining forces with Pakubuwono II of Mataram, who had initially cooperated with the VOC but now saw an opportunity to exploit the chaos. The resulting conflict — sometimes called the Java War of 1741–1743, distinct from the later Java War of 1825–1830 under Diponegoro — spread across the northern Java coast to Semarang, Surabaya, and Gresik, requiring a substantial VOC military response before Pakubuwono II, calculating that the Dutch would prevail, switched sides again and agreed to further territorial concessions. The massacre and its aftermath illustrated with brutal clarity how the VOC's extractive regime generated the instabilities that in turn demanded expensive military responses — a cycle that was by the mid-eighteenth century eating the company alive.18

Corruption, Debt, and Dissolution: The VOC's Long Decline

The VOC that entered the eighteenth century was, on paper, the largest commercial enterprise in the world. Its stock capitalisation had reached a peak of approximately seventy-eight million Dutch guilders; its dividend record was the envy of European investors; its Asian network stretched from Nagasaki and Guangzhou through Batavia to Ceylon and the Cape. But the revenues that fed those dividends were increasingly illusory, and the structural weaknesses of the company's model were becoming impossible to conceal. Femme Gaastra, the Leiden economic historian who has produced the most comprehensive account of the company's financial history, identifies the mid-seventeenth century as the turning point: after about 1650, military expenditure began to grow faster than commercial income, and the company moved from being a profit-generating enterprise to a revenue-extracting territorial administration that could sustain itself only through increasingly coercive mechanisms.26

The scale of the company's military overstretch is captured in a single statistic: annual military expenditure rose from approximately four hundred thousand guilders in 1609 to more than twelve million guilders by 1766, an increase that entirely outpaced the growth of commercial revenues from spice sales, coffee (which had become a major VOC export from Java's Priangan highlands), and textiles.27 The Mataram succession wars, the suppression of the 1740 aftermath, and the maintenance of a permanent garrison and bureaucracy in Batavia, along with dozens of smaller posts across the archipelago, made the VOC a state — but a state whose subjects produced revenues that flowed primarily to shareholders in Amsterdam rather than being reinvested in the infrastructure of the territories it governed.

Institutional corruption compounded the financial difficulty. The company had always suffered from the principal-agent problem inherent in running a global enterprise in the age of sail — agents operating thousands of miles from their principals, months away from scrutiny, with enormous discretionary authority and relatively modest salaries. By the early eighteenth century, corruption had become systemic rather than occasional. The Global Anticorruption Blog's historical survey describes a VOC in which three forms of malfeasance had become normalised: embezzlement of company goods, nepotism in appointments, and illegal private trading — the very practice the company had been designed to monopolise. Governor-General Joan van Hoorn, who served from 1704 to 1709, returned to the Netherlands with an estimated ten million guilders in private wealth, against an official annual salary of fourteen thousand guilders — a disproportion that astonished even contemporaries accustomed to colonial enrichment.27 As the eighteenth century progressed, the directorate of the Heeren XVII was increasingly composed of Amsterdam patricians whose interest in the Indies was financial rather than commercial — men who voted dividends rather than reinvesting in fleet modernisation or administrative reform. Dividends continued to be paid at levels that exceeded actual earnings from at least 1690 to 1760, with the gap covered by secret loans from the Bank of Amsterdam — loans that ultimately impaired the credibility of that institution as well.29 The company's nickname in the later eighteenth century — Vergaan Onder Corruptie, "perished under corruption," a bitter inversion of the initials VOC — was a piece of popular satire that carried a great deal of truth.29

By 1780 the company's net capital position — the difference between its assets in Asia and Europe and its accumulated liabilities — had been steadily eroding. Mansell Upham's reconstruction of the VOC's balance sheet suggests that between 1730 and 1780 the company drew down its Asian liquid capital by approximately four million guilders and its European capital by twenty million, reducing total net assets to roughly sixty-two million guilders — far below what would have been needed to cover its outstanding obligations. Then came the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War (1780–1784).

The war arose from Dutch commercial neutrality during the American Revolutionary War — the Netherlands had been supplying materials to France and American colonists, and Britain used this as a pretext for a declaration of war in December 1780. The Dutch Republic was wholly unprepared: it possessed only twenty ships of the line against the Royal Navy's vastly superior force, and its merchant fleet was overwhelmingly vulnerable. Within the first weeks of hostilities, more than two hundred Dutch merchant vessels, carrying cargo estimated at fifteen million guilders, were seized by British warships, while three hundred more were bottled up in foreign ports.28 The VOC's Sumatran pepper posts and other Indian Ocean assets were attacked; Nagapatnam on the Coromandel Coast was permanently ceded to Britain by the subsequent peace. The Treaty of Paris (1784) granted the British East India Company free navigation rights in the Moluccas — a devastating concession that punctured the geographic exclusivity of the VOC's spice monopoly from a different direction entirely.28 The war's total damage to the VOC has been estimated at approximately forty-three million guilders — enough to wipe out the entire net capital that had survived two generations of corrosive dividends and institutional rot.28

The company never recovered. French plants smuggled from the Banda Islands were already producing nutmeg in Mauritius; cloves had been transplanted to Penang and the Indian Ocean islands; the European market for Indonesian spices was being supplied increasingly by non-Dutch routes. Coffee and indigo from Java remained significant, but the commercial rationale for a monopoly company with a quasi-governmental apparatus — sustaining garrisons, courts, and bureaucracies across the archipelago — had collapsed. In 1796, with French armies in the Netherlands and the old Dutch Republic replaced by the French-aligned Batavian Republic, the Heeren XVII were sacked and management of the VOC was handed to a government-appointed Committee for Affairs Relating to East India Trade and Possessions. The company's charter was allowed to expire on 31 December 1799.30

The territories, assets, and debts of the VOC were assumed by the Batavian Republic. Those debts — accumulated over a century of military overreach, dividend fraud, and systematic corruption — amounted to approximately one hundred and fifty million guilders, a burden that the Batavian Republic, itself already staggering under total liabilities approaching seven hundred and sixty million guilders from all sources, could barely absorb.30 The VOC's dissolution did not bring the colonial enterprise in the Indonesian archipelago to an end; it transferred it to the Dutch state. The transition from company to crown — the subject of the following chapter — was less a transformation than a relabelling, at least in the short term. What changed was the legal and institutional form of colonial authority; what did not change, for the peoples of Java, the Moluccas, and Sulawesi, was the extractive character of that authority. The debts, the garrisons, the forced-labour plantation systems, and the coercive trading relationships all passed seamlessly from one Dutch institution to the next.

Vergaan Onder Corruptie — "Perished under corruption": a popular eighteenth-century inversion of the VOC's initials, capturing the widespread perception that the company's decline was driven as much by institutional rot — embezzlement, nepotism, illegal private trade — as by external competition or military defeat.
Historiographic debate: the balance between structural and contingent explanations for VOC decline. Historians have emphasised different factors in the VOC's collapse. Femme Gaastra's financial history stresses the long-run unsustainability of dividend policy relative to earnings. Blussé's urban history emphasises the transformation of Batavia from an Asian entrepôt into a European colonial capital, which undermined the commercial intelligence network on which the company's Asian trade had depended. Others, including van Rossum, stress the labour costs and social destabilisation generated by the company's coercive labour regimes. External shocks — the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War, the spread of spice cultivation beyond Dutch control — were critical but not sufficient causes; a financially healthy company might have survived them. The consensus in recent scholarship is that VOC decline was over-determined: structural, institutional, and contingent factors reinforced one another in a cascade that the company's sclerotic governance was unable to interrupt.

Summary

Between 1621 and 1799, the VOC transformed the Indonesian archipelago through a combination of organised violence, plantation slavery, maritime coercion, and political manipulation. The Banda Islands massacre of 1621 destroyed an indigenous society to establish a nutmeg monopoly maintained by enslaved labour on sixty-eight company-supervised plantations. Across the Moluccas, annual hongi extirpation expeditions burned clove trees and conscripted corvée labour to enforce a clove monopoly secured by the subjugation of the Ambon Islands by 1656. The conquest of Makassar and the Treaty of Bongaya (1667) closed the last major free port of the eastern archipelago, with the Bugis alliance of Arung Palakka providing the decisive military advantage. In Java, the VOC inserted itself into Mataram's succession crises as a kingmaker and moneylender, extracting territorial and commercial concessions that progressively dismembered Javanese sovereignty. The Chinese massacre of Batavia in 1740 — driven by economic collapse in the sugar sector and a climate of persecution and fear — killed as many as ten thousand people and triggered a further Java-wide conflict. Through all of this, the company's institutional fabric was rotting: military expenditure outpaced revenues, corruption became systemic, dividends were paid out of borrowed capital, and the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War (1780–1784) delivered the financial blow from which the company could not recover. Its charter expired on 31 December 1799, and its territories and debts passed to the Dutch state — inaugurating the colonial state era examined in the next chapter.

Review & Discussion Questions

17.1 Jan Pieterszoon Coen wrote in 1620 that the Banda Islands should be "repopulated with different inhabitants." What does this statement reveal about the VOC's conception of property, sovereignty, and indigenous rights? How does it compare to other colonial land-clearing episodes you have studied?
17.2 Some historians characterise the 1621 Banda massacre as genocide; others prefer terms like "colonial atrocity" or "forced displacement." What are the historical and ethical stakes of the terminology? Does the distinction matter for how we assess the VOC's responsibility?
17.3 The perkenier system created a form of plantation agriculture staffed by enslaved workers from multiple regions of Asia. How did it resemble, and how did it differ from, the plantation economies of the Atlantic world that developed during the same period?
17.4 The VOC's conquest of Makassar was enabled significantly by the Bugis leader Arung Palakka. What does his alliance with Speelman reveal about the limits of a purely "colonial vs. indigenous" framing of VOC expansion? How did local political rivalries shape the outcomes of VOC campaigns?
17.5 Trace the pattern of VOC involvement in the Javanese succession wars. Why were successive Mataram rulers willing to invite the VOC's military assistance despite the crushing concessions it required? What structural features of Javanese politics made this dynamic so persistent?
17.6 Leonard Blussé has argued that the 1740 Chinese massacre was connected to structural changes in Batavia's economic role, not simply to anti-Chinese racism or one governor's cruelty. Evaluate this structural interpretation. Does it exculpate individual decision-makers?
17.7 Historians identify multiple factors in the VOC's decline: structural (over-extension, loss of intra-Asian trade), institutional (corruption, poor governance), and contingent (the Fourth Anglo-Dutch War). How would you weight these factors? Is the "Vergaan Onder Corruptie" label fair?
17.8 The VOC's dissolution in 1799 transferred its territories and debts to the Dutch state rather than restoring them to indigenous sovereignty. What does this continuity suggest about the relationship between chartered company colonialism and state colonialism? Does the distinction matter?
