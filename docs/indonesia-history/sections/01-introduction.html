Indonesia is at once one of the largest nations on earth and one of the youngest ideas. The land is ancient — the islands have been inhabited by humans and their ancestors for more than a million years — but the notion that these thousands of islands, hundreds of peoples, and dozens of languages constitute a single country is barely more than a century old. To study Indonesian history is to hold both facts at once: a deep past stretching back to Homo erectus, and a national identity deliberately invented, argued over, and fought for in living memory.

A nation that had to be imagined

The word "Indonesia" was not coined by Indonesians, and at first it had nothing to do with a nation. It was manufactured by European scholars in the mid-nineteenth century. In 1850, in the Journal of the Indian Archipelago and Eastern Asia, the English ethnographer George Windsor Earl proposed terms — "Indunesians" and his own preferred "Malayunesians" — for the peoples of the "Indian Archipelago." His colleague James Richardson Logan, writing in the same journal, took up the alternative "Indonesia" as a geographical synonym for that archipelago.1 The name combined the Latin Indus ("Indies") with the Greek nēsos ("island") — the "island Indies."2 For decades it remained an academic convenience; the German ethnologist Adolf Bastian helped spread it through his multi-volume Indonesien (1884–1894).3

Only in the twentieth century did "Indonesia" become a political claim. Nationalist students and exiles seized on the term precisely because it was not "Netherlands East Indies" — it named a country that did not yet exist but might. Suwardi Suryaningrat (later Ki Hajar Dewantara) used it when he founded an Indonesisch Pers-bureau in the Netherlands in 1913, and the name acquired decisive weight on 28 October 1928, when young activists swore the Sumpah Pemuda — the Youth Pledge — to "one motherland, one nation, one language: Indonesia."4 The historian R. E. Elson has argued that the "idea of Indonesia" had to be conceived before the nation could be built, and that its trajectory from a foreign coinage to a unifying ideal is itself one of the central stories of modern Indonesian history.5

Sumpah Pemuda — the 1928 "Youth Pledge," in which nationalist youth congresses affirmed a single Indonesian homeland, nation, and language. It is conventionally treated as the moment "Indonesia" became an irreversible political idea.

The shape of the land

The country these nationalists imagined is staggering in extent. Indonesia is the world's largest archipelagic state, scattered across more than 17,000 islands — by the Indonesian government's count — of which roughly 920 are permanently inhabited.6 It straddles the equator for some 5,000 kilometres between the Indian and Pacific oceans, and between the landmasses of mainland Asia and Australia, a position that has made it a crossroads of trade, religion, and migration for two thousand years. Its territory of about 1.9 million square kilometres ranks fourteenth in the world by land area, but its maritime expanse is far larger.

This geography is not a neutral backdrop; it is an active force in the story. Volcanic soils made Java one of the most fertile and densely populated places on earth, concentrating power and people; the monsoon winds set the rhythm of maritime trade; and the sea, more than dividing the islands, connected them, so that the archipelago's early states were often thalassocracies — powers built on control of water rather than land.7 We examine this physical setting in detail in Chapter 1.

Unity in diversity

If geography is the first fact of Indonesian history, diversity is the second. Indonesia is home to more than 280 million people, making it the fourth most populous country in the world, and the nation with the largest Muslim population anywhere — about seven in eight Indonesians are Muslim.8 Yet it is far from homogeneous: its people speak some seven hundred living languages, belong to hundreds of ethnic groups, and practise Islam, Protestant and Catholic Christianity, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, and a range of local faiths.9 The relief map above contains, in effect, hundreds of distinct societies.

The state's response to this diversity is captured in its motto, Bhinneka Tunggal Ika — usually translated "Unity in Diversity." The phrase is Old Javanese, drawn from the fourteenth-century poem Sutasoma by the Majapahit-era poet Mpu Tantular, where it originally expressed the reconcilability of Buddhism and Hinduism.10 That a modern secular republic took its motto from a medieval Javanese Buddhist text is itself emblematic: Indonesia continually reaches back into the deep archipelagic past to authorize its modern unity. The central tension of this book — how a single nation has been imagined, governed, and contested across such a varied space — runs from the classical kingdoms straight through to the politics of the present day.

How this book is organized

The book proceeds chronologically through nine parts and then turns thematic in a tenth. Part I sets the physical and prehistoric stage; Part II covers the Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms of Srivijaya, Mataram, and Majapahit; Part III, the coming of Islam and the sultanates; Parts IV–V, the Portuguese, the Dutch East India Company, and the colonial state; Parts VI–VII, the national awakening, the Japanese occupation, and the revolution; Parts VIII–IX, Sukarno's republic, Suharto's New Order, and the democratic Reformasi era down to the 2024 election. Part X then revisits the whole sweep through culture, religion, economy, diaspora, gender, environment, and historiography. Each chapter opens with objectives and closes with a summary, review questions, and full source notes.

Summary

"Indonesia" began as a nineteenth-century scholarly coinage and became, in the twentieth century, the name of a nation that had to be deliberately imagined into being. That nation occupies the world's largest archipelago — over 17,000 islands spanning two oceans — and contains extraordinary human, linguistic, and religious diversity, including the world's largest Muslim population. The defining problem of Indonesian history, expressed in the motto Bhinneka Tunggal Ika, is how unity has been built and contested across this immense and varied space — a problem this book traces from deep prehistory to the present.
