These tables are selective reference aids, not exhaustive king-lists. Regnal dates for the early Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms are approximate and frequently contested: they are reconstructed from scattered inscriptions, foreign accounts, and later court chronicles rather than from continuous records, and scholars disagree on many of them. Dates marked "c." are scholarly estimates rather than securely attested years. The sultanate, colonial, and republican tables rest on firmer ground, but the sultanate and Company sections are deliberately limited to the most significant rulers and administrators. Where a kingdom left no royal genealogy at all — most acutely Srivijaya — the gaps below are real and unresolved in the scholarship.

Srivijaya (Sumatra, c. 7th–13th Century CE)

Srivijaya left no native chronicle, no royal genealogy, and no continuous dynastic list; it was not even recognised as a distinct historical entity until George Cœdès identified the name in Sumatran inscriptions in 1918. The names below survive only as isolated attestations — a single stone, a Chinese embassy log, or a foreign victory inscription. Long stretches of the record are entirely blank, most notably a gap of roughly 128 years (c. 860–988 CE) for which no ruler's name can be recovered.

Sailendra & Medang (Central Javanese Mataram) Kingdom (c. 730s–947 CE)

Two intertwined lineages ruled Central Java: the Buddhist Sailendra and the Shaivite Sanjaya (Isyana) line of the Medang kingdom. Their precise relationship is genuinely contested — some historians posit two coexisting dynasties, others a single line with shifting religious identities. Only a handful of dates have firm inscriptional anchors (Sanjaya 732, Panangkaran 778, Balitung's accession 10 May 898, Wawa 928, Sindok 929); the rest are estimates.

Singhasari (East Java, 1222–1292)

Founded by Ken Arok after his defeat of King Kertajaya of Kadiri at the Battle of Ganter (1222). All five rulers belong to the Rajasa dynasty, and the kingdom's history — much of it derived from the Pararaton chronicle — is a sequence of usurpations and revenge killings ending with Kertanagara's murder.

Majapahit (East Java, c. 1293–c. 1527)

Founded by Raden Wijaya, a Singhasari prince who turned the Mongol expeditionary force against the usurper Jayakatwang and then expelled the Mongols. The empire peaked under Hayam Wuruk and his prime minister Gajah Mada, then fragmented after 1389 into a long, poorly documented decline. The "Brawijaya" numbering of the later kings is a later Javanese tradition, not contemporaneous, and the final extinction date is variously given between 1478 and 1527.

The office of Governor-General was created by the Dutch East India Company (VOC) in 1610, with supreme authority over Company operations in Asia from Batavia. This is a selection of the most consequential; the VOC was dissolved on 1 January 1800 and its territories passed to the Dutch state.
