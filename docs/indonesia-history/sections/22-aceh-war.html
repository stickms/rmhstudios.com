Learning Objectives

  • Explain how the Anglo-Dutch Sumatra Treaty of 1871 removed British protection over the Acehnese Sultanate and precipitated the Dutch invasion of 1873.
  • Trace the three main phases of the Aceh War — the failed conventional campaigns, the ulama-led guerrilla struggle, and the brutal "Aceh method" pacification — and explain why each phase gave way to the next.
  • Analyze the roles of Teuku Umar, Cut Nyak Dhien, and the ulama leadership in sustaining four decades of resistance.
  • Assess the dual role of Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje as both a pioneering Islamicist and the intellectual architect of a counterinsurgency strategy that exploited divisions within Acehnese society.
  • Place the Aceh War within the wider series of campaigns in Lombok, Bali, South Sulawesi, and Borneo that completed the territorial unification of the Netherlands East Indies by approximately 1910–1914.
  • Engage critically with historiographical debates over whether the Aceh War constituted genocide, and trace how the war's memory shaped both Dutch colonial culture and Acehnese political identity.

No episode in the long history of Dutch colonialism in Southeast Asia was more protracted, more costly, or more brutal than the war against the Acehnese Sultanate in northern Sumatra. For more than four decades — from the first disastrous expedition of 1873 to the last embers of organized resistance in 1914 — the Royal Netherlands East Indies Army fought a people who refused, with tenacity that astonished their adversaries, to submit. When the account was finally reckoned, somewhere between 50,000 and 60,000 Acehnese had died of violence and disease; more than 37,000 Dutch and allied soldiers had perished — most of them from cholera; and an ancient sultanate had been reduced to a colonial residency. The Aceh War was not simply a colonial campaign: it was a holy war, a crucible of Islamic identity, and a proving-ground for a counterinsurgency theory that would reverberate through the twentieth century. The story of how it began, how it transformed, and how the Dutch eventually prevailed illuminates the violence at the foundations of the modern Indonesian state.

The Sultanate at Bay: Aceh Before 1873

The Aceh Sultanate occupied the northern tip of Sumatra, commanding the western entrance to the Strait of Malacca — the narrow passage through which all maritime traffic between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea was funneled. This geographical position had made Aceh a power of the first order in the early modern world. Under Sultan Iskandar Muda (r. 1607–1636), the sultanate reached the height of its prestige: its war fleet threatened Portuguese Malacca; its port drew merchants from Arabia, India, Persia, and China; and its revenues rested on the world's richest pepper-producing hinterland.1 The French scholar Denys Lombard, in his foundational study of the sultanate, documented how Acehnese pepper dominated the spice routes from Southeast Asia to the Middle East and Europe throughout the seventeenth century, giving the sultanate both the wealth to sustain independence and a strategic importance that major powers could not ignore.2 Aceh was, for much of this period, the preeminent Islamic polity in the eastern archipelago, its ulama trading ideas and texts with scholars in Mecca, Cairo, and the Haramain.

By the nineteenth century Aceh's territorial reach had contracted and its central authority had weakened through succession struggles and Dutch encroachment on neighboring states. Yet the sultanate remained commercially significant: it was producing roughly a third of the world's pepper supply at mid-century, and its position at the northern Strait of Malacca acquired renewed strategic weight after the opening of the Suez Canal in 1869 transformed that passage into the primary highway between Europe and Asia. The Dutch, who had slowly consolidated control over much of the Indonesian archipelago since the dissolution of the VOC in 1799 (discussed in Chapters 18–19), had long eyed Aceh but were restrained by the 1824 Anglo-Dutch Treaty, which implicitly recognized British interests in northern Sumatra and prohibited either power from acquiring territory there at the other's expense. For decades this arrangement preserved Acehnese independence as an unintended consequence of Anglo-Dutch rivalry.3

Acehnese rulers understood their vulnerability and pursued active diplomacy. The sultans cultivated relationships with the Ottoman Empire — the traditional head of the Sunni Islamic world — and with the United States, whose merchant ships called regularly at Acehnese ports. In the early 1870s, as Dutch ambitions became clearer, the Acehnese court made approaches to both powers seeking recognition or some form of protection — approaches that came to nothing but revealed the sultanate's awareness that its survival depended on breaking the closed diplomatic world the Dutch were constructing around it.4 The historian Anthony Reid, in his authoritative study of the tripartite contest among the Dutch, the British, and the Acehnese, has shown that Acehnese diplomacy was sophisticated and well-informed and came agonizingly close to winning at least British restraint on Dutch expansion.5

The Road to War: The Sumatra Treaty of 1871 and the Invasions

The diplomatic knot was cut not by any Acehnese action but by a colonial land swap half a world away. In 1870–71, the Netherlands and Britain negotiated a package of three treaties to resolve outstanding disputes in West Africa and Southeast Asia. The most consequential for Aceh was the Sumatra Treaty, signed in The Hague on 2 November 1871: Britain received equal commercial rights in the Dutch-controlled territory of Siak in Sumatra; the Dutch received British acquiescence to Dutch expansion in Aceh — effectively nullifying the 1824 prohibition that had protected Acehnese independence for nearly half a century.6 The Suez Canal's opening two years earlier was the geopolitical engine of the deal: Britain, now focused on securing the route to India, was willing to formalize spheres of influence in Southeast Asia in exchange for concessions elsewhere. The full text of the Sumatra Treaty declared that Britain recognized "full Dutch influence over the Aceh Sultanate."7

Armed with this diplomatic license, the Dutch moved quickly. A pretext was supplied by piracy allegations and reports that Acehnese authorities were negotiating independent commercial treaties with foreign powers — precisely the kind of autonomous diplomatic behavior that the Dutch, having now claimed Aceh as their sphere, could not tolerate. In March 1873, Major General J.H.R. Köhler led a force of approximately 3,000 troops to Banda Aceh, the capital. The expedition was a catastrophe. The Acehnese had fortified their positions with earthworks and artillery, and their resistance was fierce and coordinated. Köhler himself was shot and killed on 6 April 1873, and the Dutch, having suffered nearly a hundred combat dead and with their forces further depleted by cholera, withdrew in humiliation.8 The defeat stunned the Netherlands and set the terms for everything that followed: this would not be a quick conquest.

A second, far larger expedition was dispatched in November 1873. General Jan van Swieten commanded a force eventually exceeding 8,500 troops, with substantial artillery and naval support. By January 1874 the Dutch had stormed the Grand Mosque and the sultan's kraton at Banda Aceh. Sultan Mahmud Syah, who had contracted cholera, abandoned the capital and retreated to the interior, where he died shortly afterward. Van Swieten declared victory and the Dutch proclaimed a protectorate over Aceh. But the war had barely begun.9 The sultanate's power was not concentrated in its capital: authority was distributed among dozens of local chiefs — the uleebalang, hereditary adat rulers of their own districts — and among the ulama, Islamic scholars whose spiritual authority transcended any geographical boundary. When the kraton fell, the web of Acehnese power simply dispersed into the interior, and the Dutch found themselves holding a fortified coastal enclave ringed by a territory that refused to submit.

For the next seven years the pattern repeated: Dutch columns probed the interior, took casualties, and withdrew. Cholera killed more Dutch soldiers than Acehnese fighters did. The colonial army constructed a ring of military posts around Banda Aceh — the so-called "ring of steel" — but every post became a target and every resupply convoy an opportunity for ambush. By 1877, casualties had become so severe and the cost of the war so crippling to Dutch finances that Batavia shifted to a policy of containment rather than conquest, pulling back from active offensive operations while maintaining coastal positions. This approach, which the Dutch called the "concentration system" (concentratiestelsel), was widely recognized even at the time as an admission of failure.

A War Without End: Holy War and Guerrilla Resistance, 1881–1898

The Dutch containment policy, rather than ending the war, transformed it. Withdrawal from the interior removed any check on a new and far more dangerous form of resistance: one organized not by the sultanate's secular hierarchy but by the ulama — the Islamic religious scholars — who reframed the entire conflict as a perang sabil, literally "war in the path of God," a form of defensive jihad against the infidel invader.10 This theological reorientation was not improvised but drew on deep roots in Acehnese Islamic culture. The key text was the Hikayat Perang Sabil (Ballad of the Holy War), composed by Teuku Chik Pante Kulu and deployed by ulama as a mobilizing war-cry: recited nightly in the prayer houses (meunasah) of Acehnese villages, it promised paradise to those who died fighting the Dutch and damnation to those who collaborated or submitted.11

Perang sabil — Literally "war in the path of God"; a form of defensive jihad fought against non-Muslim invaders. In the Acehnese context, the perang sabil ideology transformed the Aceh War from a political-military conflict into a struggle for Islamic identity, sustained by the Hikayat Perang Sabil text and the authority of the ulama leadership.

The anthropologist-historian James Siegel, in his landmark study of Acehnese society, argued that the perang sabil tradition was not an external imposition on Acehnese identity but a deep expression of it: the ulama's authority derived from mastery of the Islamic textual tradition, and in a time of invasion that mastery became military leadership.12 The dominant figure of this phase was Teungku Chik di Tiro, an ulama from the Pidie coast who became the most effective guerrilla commander of the 1880s. His forces practiced a classic insurgent strategy: avoiding pitched battles with superior Dutch firepower, striking at isolated outposts and supply lines, and melting back into villages whose populations provided food, shelter, and intelligence.

The Dutch responded with tactics that would become notorious: burning villages suspected of sheltering fighters, destroying rice stores, cutting irrigation channels, and killing livestock — what the historian Emmanuel Kreike has analyzed as the systematic destruction of the agricultural environment that sustained resistance, with effects that killed "tens and perhaps even hundreds of thousands" beyond those directly killed in combat.13 The environmental dimension of the war — the deliberate impoverishment of the Acehnese agricultural base — would become central to later historiographical debates about whether the conflict amounted to genocide.

A distinctive and deeply unsettling feature of the guerrilla phase was the phenomenon the Dutch called Atjèh-moord (Aceh-murder): individual Acehnese, often young men, would arm themselves with a rencong (Acehnese dagger) and charge Dutch soldiers, seeking martyrdom in a form of religiously motivated self-sacrifice that the Hikayat Perang Sabil explicitly sanctioned as the highest expression of holy war. These attacks, which could not be suppressed by conventional military means, made Dutch service in Aceh uniquely terrifying and persuaded some analysts that a political and sociological solution was needed — not merely a military one. Into that analytical gap stepped the figure who would, in the view of many historians, decisively shape the war's eventual outcome.

Teuku Umar and Cut Nyak Dhien: The Human Face of Resistance

Among the secular Acehnese leaders of the resistance, Teuku Umar stands apart for the sheer drama of his career. Born in 1854 in Meulaboh on Aceh's west coast, Umar was an uleebalang — a hereditary adat chief — who, in the chaotic conditions of the war, built a private army of considerable size and became the most powerful single military figure on the Acehnese side. As early as 1883 he had made contact with the Dutch, beginning a years-long double game in which he offered cooperation while retaining his armed forces and independence. In January 1894 the Dutch rewarded him with the title "Johan Pahlawan" (Hero of the War) and command of 250 armed men; by 1896 they had supplied him with weapons and ammunition to use against other Acehnese resistance fighters.14

Then, on 30 March 1896, Teuku Umar disappeared into the jungle — taking with him 800 weapons, 25,000 rounds of ammunition, 500 kilograms of gunpowder, 400 soldiers, and approximately $18,000 in Dutch colonial funds.15 The Dutch recorded the event, with characteristic understatement, as "Het verraad van Teukoe Oemar" — "The Treachery of Teuku Umar." In Acehnese memory it was a masterstroke of resistance. For the next three years Umar used the weapons the Dutch had supplied him with against Dutch forces, inflicting significant casualties. He was eventually tracked down and killed in a Dutch surprise attack at Meulaboh on 11 February 1899, at approximately forty-four years of age. The career of Teuku Umar illustrates both the flexibility of Acehnese resistance — capable of adapting to any tactical opportunity — and the fundamental unreliability, from a Dutch perspective, of any accommodation with the Acehnese uleebalang who had not been thoroughly integrated into the colonial administrative structure.

Teuku Umar's second wife, Cut Nyak Dhien — who had become his partner in resistance after her first husband, Teuku Ibrahim Lamnga, was killed in battle in 1878 — continued fighting after Umar's death with a ferocity that made her legendary in her own time.16 Born around 1848 into an uleebalang family in Aceh Besar, she led a guerrilla force through the Acehnese interior, maintaining resistance despite deteriorating health (a progressive illness, possibly diabetes, was robbing her of sight and mobility) and dwindling supplies. Her small army was finally destroyed around 1901 — a subordinate named Pang Laot revealed her location to the Dutch — and she was captured and exiled to Sumedang in West Java.17 In exile she taught Qur'anic studies and was regarded with reverence by the local Sundanese population, who called her Ibu Perbu (Queen Mother). She died in Sumedang on 6 November 1908. Proclaimed a National Hero of Indonesia by President Sukarno in 1964, her story has been told in film, textbooks, and commemorations, embodying both the ferocity of Acehnese resistance and the human cost it exacted from those who sustained it.

Scholar and Strategist: Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje and the "Aceh Method"

The man who provided the Dutch with the intellectual framework for finally winning the Aceh War was Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje — born 8 February 1857 in Oosterhout, Netherlands, died 26 June 1936 in Leiden — one of the nineteenth century's most brilliant and most controversial Orientalists. Before he turned his attention to Aceh, his career had already taken him to remarkable places. His 1880 Leiden doctoral dissertation examined Meccan pilgrimage ritual; in 1884–1885 he spent seven months in Mecca itself, under the assumed Muslim name "Abdul Ghaffar," producing the two-volume Mekka (1888–89) — the first comprehensive Western ethnography of life in the holy city, the first photographs ever made of the Kaaba's interior, and the first-ever audio recording of Qur'anic recitation.18 He had, in the course of this extraordinary sojourn, acquired a depth of Islamic learning that few Western scholars could match, and he had developed an analytical understanding of how Islamic social networks functioned — knowledge that the colonial state in Batavia would soon find directly applicable.

Snouck arrived in the Dutch East Indies in 1889 and traveled to Aceh in 1891–1892, where he spent more than a year in close contact with Acehnese society. The product of this fieldwork was De Atjèhers (The Acehnese), published in two volumes in 1893–94 and translated into English by A.W.S. O'Sullivan in 1906.19 This work remains the most detailed ethnography of Acehnese social structure produced in the colonial era, covering kinship, adat law, Islamic practice, and the dynamics of resistance. But it was not merely a scholarly contribution: it was simultaneously a policy document. The central analytical insight Snouck drew from his fieldwork was a distinction between two fundamentally different elite formations whose interests the Dutch could exploit.

Uleebalang (ulèëbalang) — Hereditary Acehnese adat chiefs who held territorial authority at the village and district level. Their power derived from customary law and land control rather than from Islamic learning. The uleebalang had existing commercial relationships with outside traders and were potentially susceptible to pragmatic accommodation with the Dutch colonial state.
Ulama (in Acehnese: teungku) — Islamic scholars whose authority derived entirely from religious learning and the moral economy of the Islamic community. The ulama controlled the meunasah (prayer houses) and religious schools, transmitted the Hikayat Perang Sabil, and formed the backbone of armed resistance once the sultanate's secular power was broken. In Snouck's analysis, they were the irreconcilable enemies of Dutch colonial rule and had to be physically destroyed.

The policy recommendations Snouck developed from this analysis — articulated in a series of confidential reports to the colonial government from 1892 onward and elaborated in De Atjèhers — were precise and ruthless. First, the Dutch should strengthen the uleebalang by making them reliable administrative intermediaries: respect their adat authority, pay them a colonial salary, and use them to administer their districts within the Dutch framework. Second, the ulama, as the backbone of armed resistance, should be subjected to relentless military pressure: their authority rested on the success of the jihad, and if the jihad was visibly failing — if their fighters were being killed, their villages burned, and their social network disrupted — their authority would collapse. Third, ordinary Islamic religious practice (prayer, fasting, pilgrimage) should be left unmolested: the Dutch should distinguish between "political Islam" (ulama-led holy war) and "spiritual Islam" (ordinary piety), suppressing the former while tolerating the latter, so as not to drive the pragmatic uleebalang into the ulama's camp.20

This analysis transformed the Aceh War from a blunt military exercise into a precision instrument of social engineering. The historian Harry Benda, in his classic study of Indonesian Islam under the Japanese occupation, argued that Snouck's ulama-uleebalang framework shaped Dutch policy toward Islam throughout the Netherlands East Indies for decades after the war — creating an enduring colonial-bureaucratic tradition of distinguishing "political Islam" (dangerous, to be suppressed) from "spiritual Islam" (acceptable, to be tolerated) that would have profound and lasting consequences for the nationalist movement of the twentieth century.21

Snouck Hurgronje's dual legacy has become the subject of increasingly intense scholarly scrutiny. The 2022 Brill volume Scholarship in Action: Essays on the Life and Work of Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje, edited by Léon Buskens and Jan Just Witkam and freely available as open access, brings together essays grappling with how a scholar of genuine erudition — and, in his later career, one who advocated in 1923 for "vigorous constitutional reform" and democratic representation for Indonesians — could also have been the intellectual architect of a counterinsurgency that killed tens of thousands and has been characterized by some scholars as genocidal.22 The military-anthropology scholar Montgomery McFate, writing in the journal Orbis, placed Snouck's Aceh work in the tradition of what she calls "useful knowledge" — scholarship deployed for military purposes — and argued that the "Aceh method" he developed anticipates the core tenets of modern population-centric counterinsurgency doctrine.23 In Dutch popular memory Snouck remains contested: admired in academic circles, regarded with moral gravity by scholars of colonial violence, and largely unknown to the broader public that has yet to fully reckon with the scale of what was done in Aceh in his name.

Pacification and Its Price: Van Heutsz, the Marechaussee, and the War's Endgame, 1898–1914

By 1898 the colonial government had found its instrument: Joannes Benedictus van Heutsz, a career military officer who had served in Aceh since the 1870s and was appointed Military Governor of Aceh with a mandate to end the war. Van Heutsz operated in close partnership with Snouck Hurgronje — and, increasingly, with his lieutenant Hendrikus Colijn, later to become a Dutch Prime Minister — translating the scholar's sociological analysis into a systematic military program. His principal tool was the Korps Marechaussee te voet, an elite mobile infantry corps founded on 2 April 1890 and specifically designed for irregular warfare.24 Where conventional Dutch infantry columns moved slowly through the jungle and telegraphed their movements to local informants, the Marechaussee — organized in small, rapid brigades of European officers commanding native Indonesian and African soldiers, armed with carbine-length Mannlicher rifles and klewang short swords — employed offensive surprise tactics, intensive jungle patrols, and a speed of movement through terrain that denied the Acehnese their usual advantage of advance warning.

Korps Marechaussee te voet — The Royal Netherlands East Indies Army's elite mobile infantry corps, founded in 1890 and deployed primarily in Aceh. Its soldiers carried a light Mannlicher carbine and a klewang (short sword), optimizing speed and surprise. Its doctrine of relentless offensive pursuit and close combat was the military expression of the "Aceh method" counterinsurgency strategy.

Under Van Heutsz and Snouck's joint strategy, the Dutch simultaneously pursued the ulama leadership with mobile columns and built up cooperative uleebalang as colonial administrators. Major ulama commanders were systematically tracked and killed. Cooperative uleebalang received Dutch salaries, administrative authority, and military protection. The economic structures that had sustained resistance — the pepper trade, the rice cultivation of the Aceh River valley, the networks of Islamic schools that served as recruitment and communication hubs — were penetrated and monitored. By 1903, Sultan Muhammad Daud had been persuaded to surrender, and the major secular commanders had submitted or been eliminated.

Yet the ulama resistance persisted in the interior highlands. In 1904, Colonel G.C.E. van Daalen led a punitive expedition into the Gayo and Alas highlands — a rugged, densely forested region that had never been under effective Dutch control. Van Daalen's methods admitted no ambiguity. His column destroyed each village that offered resistance, killing its inhabitants with a thoroughness that shocked even some Dutch observers. The most notorious single engagement was the massacre at the village of Kuta Reh on 14 June 1904: Dutch forces stormed an Acehnese community in the Alas valley defended by approximately 75 fighters armed with outdated muzzle-loading firearms behind an earthen wall. In approximately ninety minutes of fighting, 561 Acehnese were killed — 313 men, 189 women, and 59 children. Two Dutch soldiers died.25 The disproportion was recorded in the account of Adjutant J.C.J. Kempees, a Dutch officer present at the engagement, who noted with apparent satisfaction that "the effect of the fire was tremendous — each bullet made several hits in these close ranks." For Van Daalen's entire 1904 highlands campaign, at least 2,922 Acehnese were killed, of whom 1,149 were women and children. Dutch losses were 26.26

The Kuta Reh massacre provoked outrage in the Netherlands when Kempees's account circulated — the political tradition that had produced Multatuli's Max Havelaar and a generation of Ethical Policy reformers still possessed a voice. But Van Daalen was promoted rather than prosecuted, and Van Heutsz was elevated to Governor-General of the Netherlands East Indies in 1904, rewarded for his "pacification." The political career of Hendrikus Colijn, who had served under Van Heutsz in Aceh and who would eventually lead five Dutch governments in the 1920s and 1930s, exemplifies the career paths that the Aceh War opened within the Dutch imperial system — and the degree to which colonial violence was normalized, even celebrated, in the metropolitan power.

Organized resistance in the Acehnese lowlands had been substantially suppressed by approximately 1907. The Gayo and Alas highlands remained an active theater until 1912–1914, when the last significant ulama commanders were killed or surrendered. The war that had begun with Köhler's death in 1873 wound down after four decades — the longest, costliest, and by any humane measure the most devastating colonial war in Indonesian history.

Completing the Map: Lombok, Bali, Sulawesi, and the Territorial Unification of the Netherlands East Indies

The Aceh War was the most protracted and costly campaign of Dutch expansion, but it was not the only one. Between roughly 1880 and 1910, the Dutch waged a series of campaigns across the archipelago that transformed the Netherlands East Indies from a patchwork of controlled territories, client states, and nominally sovereign kingdoms into something approaching a unified colonial state with boundaries roughly corresponding to those of modern Indonesia. These campaigns shared a common military instrument — the Royal Netherlands East Indies Army (KNIL), reorganized and professionalized in the wake of the Aceh experience — and a common legal framework: the Korte Verklaring, or "Short Declaration," a standardized treaty by which indigenous rulers acknowledged full Dutch sovereignty and pledged loyalty to the Governor-General.

Korte Verklaring (Short Declaration) — A standardized submission treaty imposed on Indonesian rulers by the Dutch from the 1890s onward. Unlike earlier treaties that negotiated specific rights and obligations, the Short Declaration required rulers to recognize Dutch sovereignty unconditionally and pledge loyalty to the Governor-General — the legal instrument by which the Netherlands East Indies was juridically unified.

The conquest of Lombok in 1894 illustrates how Dutch expansion could begin as an internal dispute and rapidly become an annexation. The expedition was dispatched ostensibly in response to Sasak (Muslim) appeals for relief from Balinese Karangasem overlordship. The initial force was ambushed at the Mayura Water Palace on 25 August 1894, losing more than 500 soldiers, sailors, and laborers — including General van Ham — in a disaster comparable in immediate scale to the first Aceh expedition. A reinforced assault in November killed approximately 2,000 Balinese defenders at a cost of 166 Dutch dead. The Raja of Lombok was exiled to Batavia, where he died in May 1895.27 The conquest of Lombok was significant not only militarily but also materially: the Dutch seizure of the royal treasury helped finance subsequent operations across the archipelago.

The Dutch campaigns in Bali culminated in two events that made colonial violence visible to the world in a way that Aceh's grinding guerrilla war largely had not. On 14–20 September 1906, an expeditionary force under Major General M.B. Rost van Tonningen landed at Sanur and marched on Denpasar. At the palace of the Raja of Badung, the Dutch were met not by armed resistance but by a puputan — a Balinese ritual mass sortie in which the ruler, his court, priests, wives, and followers dressed in white cremation garments, armed themselves with sacred kris daggers and ceremonial spears, and marched out to face certain death rather than submit to capture.28 The priest stabbed the Raja; the court members began killing each other and themselves; as Dutch soldiers opened fire the spectacle became a massacre. "At least 1,000 people in all" were killed in the Badung events of September 1906.29

Puputan — A Balinese concept of ritual mass death in battle: a final charge to certain death by a ruler and court against an overwhelming enemy, preserving martial honor rather than submitting to capture. The 1906 Badung and 1908 Klungkung puputans were witnessed by foreign journalists and became internationally reported scandals exposing the violence of Dutch colonial expansion.

Two years later, on 18 April 1908, a smaller but equally dramatic puputan ended resistance at Klungkung, the last independent Balinese kingdom. The Dewa Agung Jambe led approximately 200 followers in a final sortie; he was shot by Dutch troops when his ritual kris "failed to fulfill the desired outcome"; his six wives then immediately performed puputan, and the procession of followers followed.30 With Klungkung's fall, the entire island of Bali passed under Dutch administration. The American scholar Robert Pringle has analyzed the puputan as "a last desperate attack" rather than passive suicide — Balinese warriors understanding themselves as choosing an honorable death in battle over the dishonor of submission, a reading that complicates the image of passive victims even as it underscores the disproportionate outcome of Dutch firepower against ceremonial weapons.31

The puputans of 1906 and 1908 attracted international newspaper coverage at a moment when the Dutch Ethical Policy (proclaimed 1901, discussed in Chapter 22) had committed the colonial state to a more humane form of governance. The contrast between professed ethical intent and the spectacle of hundreds of Balinese court members shot in ritual mass sortie was not lost on Dutch observers at home, sharpening a domestic debate about the moral costs of empire that would grow throughout the early twentieth century.

In South Sulawesi, the dominant kingdoms of Bone and Gowa, which had long alternated accommodation and resistance toward Dutch power, were finally subdued in 1905 under Governor-General Van Heutsz's "Peace Policy." When Bone's king La Pawawoi Karaeng Segeri refused Dutch ultimatums in July 1905, Dutch forces attacked; his son Baso Abdul Hamid was killed in battle in November; and La Pawawoi himself was captured and exiled to Bandung, where he died in 1911. The king of Gowa died while fleeing Dutch troops — reportedly falling into a ravine. The royal regalia of both kingdoms — including the sacred sword Sundanga and the chain Tanisamang — were seized by the Dutch, and Bone and Gowa were divided into seven administrative districts by 1911.32 Similar campaigns in Borneo (Kalimantan), Maluku, and Nusa Tenggara extended Dutch administrative control across regions where it had previously been nominal or nonexistent.

The cumulative effect of these campaigns was, by approximately 1910–1914, the territorial consolidation of the Netherlands East Indies into something approaching the borders of modern Indonesia. What had been an archipelago of hundreds of polities — some directly administered, some loosely controlled, some essentially independent — became a single colonial administrative entity. The instruments by which this unity was imposed — the Korte Verklaring, the network of Dutch resident officers, the military tram line built in Aceh, the telegraph networks — connected this immense territory into a recognizable administrative whole. It was a unity forged in violence, and its contradictions would fuel the nationalist movement of the generation that followed.

Casualties, Memory, and the Genocide Debate

The human cost of the Aceh War defies easy summary. Adrian Vickers, in his History of Modern Indonesia, provides the most widely cited scholarly estimate: "during the entire Aceh war, 50,000 to 60,000 Acehnese died from violence and disease, approximately 2,000 European and allied indigenous soldiers were killed in combat, and more than 35,000 soldiers and laborers died from disease" — overwhelmingly cholera.33 At least 10,000 Acehnese fled as refugees to Malaya. The wider human cost, including those who died of hunger and displacement caused by the systematic destruction of Acehnese agricultural infrastructure — fields, irrigation works, rice stores, fruit trees, livestock — may have been considerably higher; some estimates place the total Acehnese death toll at close to 100,000.

Whether these deaths constituted genocide is a question that scholars have recently examined with new rigor. Emmanuel Kreike, writing in the Journal of Genocide Research in 2012, argued that the Dutch campaign in Aceh constituted "indirect genocide" through the deliberate destruction of the environmental infrastructure that sustained Acehnese communities.34 Drawing on Dutch military journals from the 1870s and 1880s, Kreike showed that the scorched-earth destruction of the Aceh River delta's agricultural base — including the demolition of the complex irrigation systems that made intensive rice cultivation possible — was not collateral damage but deliberate policy designed to starve resistance of its material base. Most provocatively, he noted that Dutch contemporaries themselves used the term volkerenmoord (peoples-murder, roughly equivalent to genocide) to describe what their own government was doing, sparking a domestic debate that was subsequently suppressed as the "pacification" narrative triumphed. The same issue of the journal included a companion article situating Aceh within broader patterns of mass violence in colonial Indonesia.35

The genocide question remains contested and open. Critics of the characterization note that "genocide" in international law requires intent to destroy a group as such — not merely the high casualties that result from counterinsurgency warfare, however brutal. Defenders of the "indirect genocide" framing point to the deliberate targeting of the civilian agricultural base and the documented disproportion of casualties, of which the Kuta Reh engagement — 561 Acehnese versus 2 Dutch dead — is only the most extreme example. The debate has moral and historiographical stakes that should not be foreclosed by either the language of "pacification" that sustained colonial mythology or by an insufficiently precise application of legal categories.

The Aceh War left deep and lasting marks on both Dutch and Acehnese memory. In the Netherlands, the Van Heutsz Monument in Amsterdam — erected in 1935 to honor the "Pacificator of Aceh" — was bombed by activists in September 1984, a reminder that the war's moral reckoning remained unfinished half a century after the colonial period's formal end. In Aceh, the memory of the perang sabil and the national heroes who died in it — Teuku Umar, Cut Nyak Dhien, Teungku Chik di Tiro — was woven into the Indonesian national mythology after independence: figures who had resisted Dutch colonialism became proof of an Acehnese tradition of anti-colonial resistance that the new republic could claim as its own. Yet this incorporation of Acehnese resistance into Indonesian national identity created its own tensions. The same pride in distinctive Acehnese religious and political identity that had sustained four decades of resistance against the Dutch would fuel the post-independence Darul Islam rebellion of the 1950s and then the long GAM (Gerakan Aceh Merdeka, Free Aceh Movement) separatist insurgency from the 1970s to 2005 — both driven, at least in part, by the sense that Jakarta's centralized postcolonial state was simply a new form of external domination. The 2005 Helsinki peace agreement, which brought the GAM conflict to an end and gave Aceh special autonomy including the right to implement Islamic law, represented a belated recognition that the Acehnese Islamic identity that Snouck Hurgronje had tried to split and tame a century earlier had survived the colonial experience intact and demanded acknowledgment in the post-colonial order.

Summary

The Aceh War (1873–1914), the longest and most costly campaign in Dutch colonial history, arose from the 1871 Sumatra Treaty's removal of British diplomatic protection over the Acehnese Sultanate. What began as a conventional invasion rapidly became a forty-year insurgency sustained by the ulama's transformation of the conflict into a perang sabil — a holy war mobilized through the Hikayat Perang Sabil and embodied in resistance leaders like Teuku Umar and Cut Nyak Dhien. The Dutch finally gained the upper hand after 1898 through the "Aceh method" devised by the Islamicist Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje: co-opting the hereditary uleebalang chiefs while deploying the mobile Marechaussee corps to destroy the ulama leadership and its social base. Van Daalen's 1904 highland campaign — including the Kuta Reh massacre of 561 Acehnese villagers — marked the war's most brutal phase. The Aceh War formed part of a broader wave of Dutch conquest — encompassing Lombok (1894), Bali (1906, 1908), South Sulawesi (1905), and Borneo — that completed the territorial unification of the Netherlands East Indies into roughly modern Indonesia's borders. Historians continue to debate whether the systematic destruction of Acehnese agricultural infrastructure constitutes genocide. The war's memory shaped both Dutch colonial culture and Acehnese political identity, contributing to Aceh's post-independence demands for autonomy and the 2005 Helsinki peace settlement.

Review & Discussion Questions

21.1 The 1871 Sumatra Treaty was presented as resolving Anglo-Dutch disputes primarily concerning West Africa. Why did a colonial exchange thousands of miles from Aceh effectively determine the fate of the Acehnese Sultanate? What does this reveal about the structure of nineteenth-century imperial diplomacy?
21.2 How did the transformation of the Aceh War from a conventional military conflict into a perang sabil (holy war) change both the nature of Acehnese resistance and the challenges facing the Dutch colonial army? Why were purely military solutions insufficient?
21.3 Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje has been described as both a pioneering scholar of Islam and an architect of colonial atrocity. How should we evaluate a figure whose intellectual legacy and political legacy are so sharply at odds? Does scholarly achievement mitigate complicity in violence?
21.4 Snouck Hurgronje's strategy rested on exploiting the distinction between the ulama and the uleebalang within Acehnese society. What were the long-term consequences of this strategy for Acehnese social structure and for Dutch colonial policy toward Islam throughout the Netherlands East Indies?
21.5 The Kuta Reh massacre (1904) killed 561 Acehnese, including 189 women and 59 children, at a cost of 2 Dutch soldiers. Van Daalen was promoted for his performance. What does this outcome reveal about the values and accountability structures of the late colonial state?
21.6 The Balinese puputans of 1906 and 1908 attracted international newspaper coverage in ways that the Aceh War's atrocities largely did not. What might explain this difference in visibility, and what were the consequences of that difference for Dutch colonial policy and for the Ethical Policy's credibility?
21.7 Emmanuel Kreike has argued that the Aceh War constituted "indirect genocide" through the systematic destruction of Acehnese agricultural infrastructure. Evaluate this claim. What is the strongest evidence for it? What are the strongest counterarguments? Does the legal definition of genocide need to be adapted to address colonial-era warfare of this kind?
21.8 By 1914, the Netherlands East Indies had been territorially unified largely through conquest. In what sense does this violent unification represent both the foundation and a fundamental contradiction of the Indonesian national identity that would emerge in the following decades? How do the post-independence Acehnese insurgencies (1950s Darul Islam; 1970s–2005 GAM) complicate the standard nationalist narrative?
