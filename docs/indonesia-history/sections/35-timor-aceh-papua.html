Learning Objectives

  • Explain the circumstances of Portugal's decolonization of East Timor, Fretilin's independence declaration, and Indonesia's decision to invade in December 1975.
  • Assess the scale of human suffering during the Indonesian occupation of East Timor, including the contested death-toll estimates and the significance of the 1991 Santa Cruz massacre.
  • Understand the origins and character of the Free Aceh Movement (GAM), the role of natural-resource grievances, and the violence of the DOM "military operations zone" period.
  • Analyse the contested legitimacy of the 1969 "Act of Free Choice" in Papua, and explain how the Freeport mine and transmigration policy shaped Papuan politics under the New Order.
  • Evaluate how these three conflicts reveal the internal tensions of the New Order state and its ambitions of territorial integration.

At its widest extent the New Order presided over a republic of remarkable geographic ambition: a nation that stretched from Sabang in northernmost Aceh to the border of Papua New Guinea, encompassing territories whose incorporation into the Indonesian state remained deeply contested. Three of these territories — East Timor on the island of Timor's eastern tip, the fiercely independent Acehnese province at the western end of Sumatra, and the vast western half of the island of New Guinea administered as Irian Jaya — became the sites of prolonged, sometimes devastating, conflict between Suharto's centralising state and populations that refused full absorption into its order. Their stories are distinct in character, origin, and trajectory, yet they share a common structure: the collision between a military-dominated government's drive for territorial integrity and local identities rooted in history, culture, religion, and the unequal distribution of natural-resource wealth. Any honest reckoning with the New Order requires confronting what happened in these outer provinces — the atrocities committed, the lives lost, and the debates, many still unresolved, about how those losses should be named and judged.

East Timor: A Colony Adrift

East Timor had been a Portuguese colonial possession since the sixteenth century, governed from Dili as a sleepy administrative outpost on the lesser Sunda chain. Portugal's Carnation Revolution of April 1974 — the military coup that ended the Salazar-Caetano dictatorship — transformed the colony's political landscape almost overnight. Lisbon's new reformist government committed itself to decolonization across its empire, and a brief, heady season of political mobilisation followed in East Timor. Three major parties emerged: the Frente Revolucionária de Timor-Leste Independente (Fretilin), which advocated prompt full independence and drew on broadly left-nationalist ideas; the Timorese Democratic Union (UDT), which favoured a federative arrangement retaining ties with Portugal; and the Timorese Popular Democratic Association (Apodeti), which called for integration with Indonesia.1

Jakarta observed these developments with acute anxiety. Suharto's inner circle, shaped by Cold War anti-communism and determined to consolidate Indonesia's archipelagic claims, feared an independent East Timor as a potential "Cuba on our doorstep" — a phrase that circulated among Indonesian generals and in American diplomatic cables — and began covert operations to destabilise Fretilin well before the end of 1974.2 In August 1975 a brief and bloody internal civil war between Fretilin and UDT ended in Fretilin's military victory, but the fighting convinced both Jakarta and Washington that the territory was in crisis. Fretilin moved to consolidate its control and, on 28 November 1975, declared the República Democrática de Timor-Leste — the Democratic Republic of East Timor — with Francisco Xavier do Amaral as president and Nicolau dos Reis Lobato as prime minister. The declaration went unrecognised by Portugal, the United Nations, and the world's major powers.3

Fretilin — Frente Revolucionária de Timor-Leste Independente, the principal independence movement of East Timor, founded 1974; dominated the short-lived 1975 provisional government and led the resistance throughout the Indonesian occupation.

Operation Seroja and the Annexation

Nine days after Fretilin's independence declaration, on the morning of 7 December 1975, Indonesian naval vessels shelled Dili while paratroopers and assault forces came ashore. Operation Seroja (Operasi Seroja, "Lotus") — the largest military operation Indonesia had ever mounted — deployed an estimated ten thousand troops in its initial phase, and the numbers grew substantially in the months that followed.4 Indonesia justified the invasion as a response to appeals for integration by the UDT, Apodeti, and smaller parties, who had been induced to sign the "Balibo Declaration" in November; but subsequent scholarship has consistently identified this as a manufactured pretext for an operation long planned in Jakarta and tacitly endorsed by Indonesia's most powerful international partner.

The smoking gun of outside complicity emerged decades later in documents released by the National Security Archive at George Washington University. On 6 December 1975 — the evening before the full invasion — President Gerald Ford and Secretary of State Henry Kissinger met Suharto in Jakarta. The declassified transcript records Suharto seeking assurance: "We want your understanding if we deem it necessary to take rapid or drastic action." Ford replied without hesitation: "We will understand and will not press you on the issue. We understand the problems you have and the intentions you have." Kissinger added that "it is important that whatever you do succeeds quickly," and promised to manage congressional reaction once the delegation was home.5 The United States subsequently supplied Indonesia with the bulk of its weapons — M-16 rifles, OV-10 Bronco aircraft, and later armoured vehicles — for use in the occupation, a fact that bedevilled American foreign policy for decades.

The UN Security Council and General Assembly both condemned the invasion in resolutions passed in December 1975, calling on Indonesia to withdraw immediately; Indonesia ignored them, and the resolutions were never enforced.6 On 17 July 1976 Indonesia formally annexed East Timor as its twenty-seventh province, which it named Timor Timur ("East Timor" in Indonesian). Australia became the only Western country ultimately to grant de jure recognition to the annexation, a position shaped in large part by negotiations over the Timor Gap — the oil-and-gas-rich seabed between the two countries — formalised in the 1989 Timor Gap Treaty.7

Occupation, Resistance, and the Great Famine

The early phase of the occupation was marked by extreme violence. Indonesian troops entering Dili executed suspected Fretilin members and sympathisers on the spot; reports reaching Australia and Portugal in early 1976 spoke of mass killings and the summary execution of prisoners. In March 1977 the former Australian consul to Portuguese Timor, James Dunn, published a report based on interviews with East Timorese refugees that estimated between 50,000 and 100,000 civilians had been killed since the December invasion — extraordinary numbers for a population then around 600,000–700,000.8

The deadliest phase of the occupation, however, was not direct killing but engineered famine. Between 1977 and 1979 the Indonesian military conducted a campaign of "encirclement and annihilation" against Fretilin and its civilian support base in the mountains. Aerial bombardment, including the use of napalm, destroyed crops and drove populations from their gardens; ground forces prevented civilians from cultivating new plots. By late 1979 an estimated 300,000 to 370,000 East Timorese — the majority of the surviving population — had passed through Indonesian-run detention camps where food, medical care, and sanitation were wholly inadequate. An International Committee of the Red Cross envoy who visited the territory in 1979 found malnutrition rates he described as "as bad as Biafra."9

The Commission for Reception, Truth and Reconciliation in East Timor (Comissão de Acolhimento, Verdade e Reconciliação de Timor-Leste, CAVR), established by the United Nations after independence and reporting in its landmark 2005 document Chega! ("Stop!"), incorporated statistical analysis by the Human Rights Data Analysis Group (HRDAG) drawing on nearly 8,000 testimonies, a census of public graveyards, and a retrospective mortality survey of approximately 1,400 households. The analysis estimated that at least 102,800 people (± 11,000) died as a result of the conflict between 1974 and 1999 — of whom approximately 17,600 to 19,600 were killed or disappeared, and approximately 84,200 (+11,000/−3,600) died from hunger and illness in excess of peacetime mortality rates, with a possible maximum for the latter category approaching 183,000.10 Total conflict-related deaths from all causes thus lay in a plausible range of roughly 90,800 to 202,600. These numbers, set against a pre-occupation population of under 700,000, represent a demographic catastrophe by any measure; historians differ on whether the appropriate legal category is "genocide," "crimes against humanity," or both, but the severity is not in dispute.11

From Resistance to International Outcry

Organised armed resistance survived despite the ferocity of the encirclement campaigns. The guerrilla force Falintil (Forças Armadas de Libertação Nacional de Timor-Leste) — originally Fretilin's armed wing — continued to operate in the mountains under a succession of commanders. By the early 1980s a new figure had emerged at the centre of resistance: Kay Rala Xanana Gusmão, a poet, guerrilla strategist, and charismatic leader who proved extraordinarily capable of keeping the movement alive through devastating military pressure.12 In 1988 Xanana broadened the resistance coalition by founding the Conselho Nacional da Resistência Maubere (CNRM — National Council of Maubere Resistance), explicitly designed to transcend Fretilin's party identity and unify Catholic clergy, students, clandestine urban networks, and Falintil fighters in a single non-partisan front.

The external face of the East Timorese cause was carried principally by José Ramos-Horta, who had been in exile since the 1975 invasion and worked tirelessly at the United Nations and in international diplomatic circuits to keep the question of East Timor before the world. The Vatican connection — East Timor was overwhelmingly Catholic; the Church had resisted total incorporation into the Indonesian state's Pancasila framework and sheltered civilians in its buildings — gave the cause moral authority. Bishop Carlos Felipe Ximenes Belo, the apostolic administrator of Dili from 1983, repeatedly appealed to the United Nations and to the Holy See over the conditions of the occupation, writing to the UN Secretary-General in 1989 that "we are dying as a people and as a nation."13

Indonesia sought to manage international perceptions through controlled visits and development programmes, pointing to roads, schools, and clinics built in the province. In 1989 Pope John Paul II visited Dili — a visit that ended in extraordinary scenes of young East Timorese prostrating themselves before him weeping, waving Fretilin flags, and beseeching international attention. The moment captured on television illustrated the deep identification of Catholicism with East Timorese national identity against the Muslim-majority Indonesian state.

The Santa Cruz Massacre, November 1991

The event that decisively shifted international opinion came on 12 November 1991. Several thousand East Timorese — estimates range from 2,000 to 5,000 — joined a procession from the Motael Church in Dili to Santa Cruz cemetery to place flowers on the grave of Sebastião Gomes, a young independence activist killed by Indonesian forces two weeks earlier. As the crowd gathered at the cemetery, Indonesian troops opened fire at close range, then moved through the cemetery continuing to shoot, beat, and bayonet those trying to flee or hide among the graves. Contemporary documentation suggests at least 250 people were killed that day, with many more wounded and an unknown number subsequently dying in military custody or "disappearing" after the attack.14

What made the Santa Cruz massacre a watershed was its documentation. A young British filmmaker, Christopher Wenner (known as Max Stahl), had entered East Timor clandestinely with a video camera and filmed the troops opening fire. Under interrogation for nine hours afterward, he concealed two rolls of film by burying them in a grave; they were later smuggled to Australia and eventually broadcast internationally as part of the ITV documentary In Cold Blood: The Massacre of East Timor in January 1992.15 Two American journalists, Amy Goodman and Allan Nairn, who had attended the procession were beaten by Indonesian soldiers: Nairn's skull was fractured. Their testimony, combined with Stahl's footage, created the first undeniable visual record of Indonesian military violence in the territory and produced an international outcry that Indonesia's diplomatic damage-control efforts could not contain.

The Indonesian government's initial response — dismissing the event as a "provocation" by Fretilin infiltrators, releasing wildly lower death tolls (initially claiming 19 dead), and court-martialling two junior officers as scapegoats — only deepened international scepticism. A fact-finding mission by the International Commission of Jurists, which travelled to the territory in early 1992, reported that the evidence clearly pointed to premeditated military action against a peaceful procession.16 The United States Congress, responding to the footage, suspended most military training assistance to Indonesia for the first time.

The Nobel Prize and the Road to 1999

The Santa Cruz massacre accelerated the internationalisation of the East Timor cause. In the years that followed, solidarity networks multiplied — East Timor Action Network in the United States, the East Timor Ireland Solidarity Campaign, Tapol in the United Kingdom — and the issue became a recurring embarrassment in Indonesia's relations with Europe, Australia, and North America. Xanana Gusmão's capture on 20 November 1992, in a tunnel beneath a house in Lahane on the outskirts of Dili, removed the guerrilla resistance's most charismatic leader but paradoxically raised his international profile. Sentenced in May 1993 to life imprisonment (later commuted to twenty years), held first in Cipinang prison in Jakarta, he became a cause célèbre comparable to Nelson Mandela: a political prisoner whose continuing detention symbolised the injustice of the whole occupation.17

The culminating symbolic moment of the pre-independence period came on 11 October 1996, when the Norwegian Nobel Committee awarded the Nobel Peace Prize jointly to Bishop Carlos Felipe Ximenes Belo and José Ramos-Horta, citing their work "towards a just and peaceful solution to the conflict in East Timor." The committee's citation spoke explicitly of the "sustained violations of human rights" committed in the territory; awarding the prize to Belo — still resident in Dili and subject to Indonesian authority — was an extraordinary act of international recognition.18 In his Nobel lecture, delivered in Oslo on 10 December 1996, Ramos-Horta stated: "I accept this prize on behalf of the people of East Timor, who for centuries have suffered and resisted." Belo, who spoke with characteristic simplicity, described himself as "the voice of the voiceless people of East Timor."

The award enraged Jakarta but catalysed the final phase of international pressure. When the Asian financial crisis toppled Suharto in May 1998, his successor B.J. Habibie — in a move that surprised his own cabinet — offered East Timor the choice between "special autonomy" within Indonesia and independence. The offer was driven by a complex mix of Habibie's personal religious convictions, a desire to reset Indonesia's international image, and genuine uncertainty about the costs of continued occupation. A UN-supervised referendum on 30 August 1999 produced an overwhelming vote — 78.5 per cent — for independence. The Indonesian military and its proxy militias responded with a violent campaign of destruction before an international peacekeeping force (INTERFET) arrived; the eventual result was independence, and on 20 May 2002 Timor-Leste became a sovereign state. These final events lie properly in the story of the post-Suharto era, but their roots reach deep into the New Order's quarter-century of occupation.19

Aceh: Oil, Gas, and the Roots of Rebellion

Aceh's relationship with central authority had been contentious long before Suharto. The province at the northwestern tip of Sumatra had a powerful historical memory of independence: it had been the last major Indonesian polity to succumb to Dutch conquest, resisting from 1873 until well into the twentieth century in what became one of the longest and costliest colonial wars in Dutch imperial history (treated in Chapter 21). After independence, Acehnese leaders grew frustrated with Jakarta's dominance; Teuku Daud Beureueh, the senior religious-military figure who had mobilised Acehnese for the revolution, led a Darul Islam rebellion from 1953 to 1962, seeking an Islamic state. The rebellion was eventually resolved through negotiation, with Aceh granted a degree of autonomy in religious affairs — but the underlying tension between Acehnese identity and Javanese-dominated central power was never resolved.20

The New Order discovery of vast natural-gas reserves in Aceh transformed the province's political economy — and its grievances. In 1971 the American oil company Mobil Corporation found natural gas at the Arun field near Lhokseumawe, in the North Aceh district. Production began in 1975, and the Arun liquefied natural gas (LNG) plant, built in partnership with Pertamina, came on stream in 1978 as one of the world's largest LNG facilities. At its peak in the early 1990s the Arun field produced approximately one-quarter of Mobil Corporation's global revenue and was described as "the most lucrative LNG operation of the twentieth century."21 Yet the economic windfall was almost entirely captured by the central government in Jakarta. Aceh received a trivial fraction of the revenues from its own soil; infrastructure investment in the province remained modest; and local people bore the environmental costs — water contamination, forced relocations, chemical leaks — while Javanese state contractors and Jakarta bureaucrats reaped the rewards.

GAM (Gerakan Aceh Merdeka) — the Free Aceh Movement, founded in December 1976 by Hasan Muhammad di Tiro; the primary Acehnese secessionist organisation, which fought Indonesian military and paramilitary forces intermittently until the 2005 Helsinki peace agreement.

It was in this context of natural-resource exploitation that Hasan Muhammad di Tiro — a descendant of legendary Acehnese resistance leaders, educated in the United States, and later a Swedish citizen — founded the Gerakan Aceh Merdeka (GAM — Free Aceh Movement) in the jungles of Pidie district on 4 December 1976, issuing a declaration of independence that drew explicitly on the Acehnese Sultanate's historical sovereignty. GAM's initial manifesto framed the conflict not as a domestic Indonesian affair but as a continuation of anticolonial struggle against what it called "Javanese neocolonialism," alleging that "Acheh, Sumatra has been producing a revenue of over 15 billion US dollars yearly for the Javanese neocolonialists, which they used totally for the benefit of Java and the Javanese."22

The movement's early phase was quickly suppressed. Indonesian military operations in 1979 crushed the first GAM uprising, and di Tiro fled to Sweden, where he remained in exile for nearly three decades, running the movement's international political operations from Stockholm and later Kuala Lumpur. GAM re-emerged in a more dangerous form in the late 1980s, after a cadre of fighters returned from training in Libya, better armed and tactically more capable. The military response was swift and overwhelming.

The DOM: Military Operations Zone and Its Human Cost

In May 1989 the Indonesian government formally designated Aceh as a Daerah Operasi Militer (DOM — Military Operations Zone), a legal status that granted the military extraordinary powers and effectively suspended civilian oversight. The DOM period lasted until August 1998 — nine years — and represented the most intensive counterinsurgency campaign the New Order had conducted on Indonesian soil outside East Timor. Historian Kirsten Schulze, in her detailed study of GAM's organisational anatomy, characterises the DOM as a strategy that deliberately targeted the civilian population rather than GAM fighters, aiming to drain the "sea" in which the guerrilla "fish" swam, using Maoist vocabulary that the Indonesian military had made its own.23

The scale of documented abuse was severe. Amnesty International's landmark 1993 investigation, titled "Shock Therapy: Restoring Order in Aceh" — the military's own term for its methods — documented approximately 2,000 civilians unlawfully killed and more than 1,000 detained incommunicado during the DOM period; rape and torture were systematic. Human Rights Watch researchers who visited Aceh in early 1999 confirmed that bodies had been found mutilated along roadsides and in rivers, and that "almost every family in the three districts of Aceh where military operations were concentrated was affected." The most conservative verified accounting documented 871 people killed outright, 387 missing later found dead, and more than 500 classified as "disappeared" — never found.24

The violence generated no accountability under the New Order. Officers responsible for the worst abuses were promoted rather than prosecuted. Moderate Acehnese civil society leaders later told Human Rights Watch that the failure to pursue even a single prosecution after 1998 was a catastrophic missed opportunity: "One trial of an officer for abuses during the DOM period could have halted the referendum movement in its tracks."25 Instead, the absence of accountability confirmed, for many Acehnese, that the New Order state was structurally incapable of justice — a perception that drove the dramatic post-1998 resurgence of secessionist sentiment and eventually forced the democratic government to seek a negotiated settlement in Helsinki.

The Arun gas wealth continued to fuel grievances. Throughout the DOM period, the Arun field remained among the most productive LNG operations in the world, and the revenues flowed almost exclusively to Jakarta. Aceh's population experienced a bitter paradox: one of the world's most valuable natural-resource complexes sat in their province, foreign engineers and executives lived in secure compounds, and Pertamina and Mobil (later ExxonMobil) made immense profits — while the local government remained one of the poorer in Indonesia, schools were underfunded, and the military conducting the DOM was partly financed by arrangements that allowed units to extract informal revenues from the gas companies themselves.26 This combination of resource extraction, military violence, and revenue denial produced in Aceh a depth of anti-Jakarta sentiment that survived the New Order and ultimately necessitated the comprehensive peace process of 2005.

Papua: The "Act of No Choice"

The incorporation of the western half of New Guinea into the Indonesian republic followed a different trajectory from both East Timor and Aceh — one that began not with direct military invasion but with a carefully managed international legal process that left a wound on Papuan political identity that has never closed. The territory, known successively as Dutch New Guinea, West Irian, Irian Jaya, and Papua, had been excluded from the 1949 transfer of sovereignty from the Netherlands to the Republic of Indonesia; the Dutch had announced their intention to prepare the Papuan population for eventual self-determination, a position Indonesian nationalism categorically rejected as a colonialist stratagem to prevent full sovereignty over the former colonial territory.27

The dispute was resolved — or rather deferred — by the New York Agreement of 15 August 1962, in which the Netherlands, under significant American diplomatic pressure driven by Cold War anxieties about Sukarno's neutralism, agreed to transfer the territory to a United Nations Temporary Executive Authority (UNTEA) that would administer it for a minimum of seven months before handing over to Indonesia. The Agreement stipulated that an "act of free choice" in accordance with "international practice" would be conducted before the end of 1969, allowing the Papuan people to determine their political future. UNTEA transferred the territory to Indonesia precisely on the minimum date — 1 May 1963 — having done virtually nothing to prepare Papuans for self-governance or to organise the promised plebiscite.28

The long-promised act of self-determination finally occurred between 14 July and 2 August 1969, under the Indonesian administration that had by then been in place for six years. What took place bore little resemblance to what the New York Agreement had promised. Rather than a universal adult suffrage vote, the Indonesian government — over the protests of the UN representative, Bolivian diplomat Fernando Ortiz-Sanz — substituted a system of "musyawarah" (deliberation) conducted by 1,025 hand-picked "people's representatives" chosen by the Indonesian military. These individuals were gathered in eight locations across the territory, presented with prepared texts affirming integration with Indonesia, and required to declare their assent — publicly, in the presence of Indonesian soldiers. All 1,025 voted unanimously in favour of integration.29

Scholarly consensus on the "Act of Free Choice": Historian John Saltford, whose archival study remains the most detailed examination of UN conduct during the transition, concludes that Ortiz-Sanz's own team estimated that "95 per cent of Papuans" actually supported independence, a figure that stood in grotesque contrast to the orchestrated unanimous vote. Ortiz-Sanz's final report to the UN stated only that "an act of free choice has taken place in accordance with Indonesian practice" — conspicuously declining to certify compliance with international practice, as the New York Agreement had required. Declassified American diplomatic cables show that US officials privately suspected "Indonesia could not have won a fair vote" but judged the territory marginal to US strategic interests.30

The UN General Assembly noted the result in Resolution 2504 (XXIV) "without giving an opinion whether it complied with the authorising New York Agreement" — an act of diplomatic non-endorsement that Papuan independence advocates have cited ever since as evidence that the international community recognised but refused to challenge the act's illegitimacy. The event has been widely referred to in the Papuan independence movement as the "Act of No Choice," and it remains the central legitimating argument for those who claim that the incorporation of West Papua into Indonesia was achieved without the consent of its people.31

The OPM, Freeport, and the Transformation of Papua

Armed resistance to Indonesian rule in western New Guinea predated the Act of Free Choice. The Organisasi Papua Merdeka (OPM — Free Papua Movement) had been founded in the early 1960s and launched its first armed action against Indonesian military posts in 1965, when fighters using bows, arrows, and spears attacked an army post at Erambu village in Manokwari district, killing two soldiers and a government employee.32 The OPM was never militarily capable of challenging Indonesian control in any sustained way — the disparity between the two sides in weapons and organisation was simply too great — but its existence, and the military campaigns it provoked, generated a steady stream of human rights abuses against the broader Papuan civilian population. Under the New Order, any expression of Papuan cultural or political identity could attract the designation "OPM sympathiser," exposing ordinary people to arrest, torture, and extrajudicial killing.

The New Order's economic transformation of Papua centred above all on the Freeport mine. As early as April 1967 — just months after the New Order's consolidation — Suharto's government signed a contract with the American company Freeport Sulphur (later Freeport-McMoRan) granting it rights over 250,000 acres of the Papuan highlands under a "Contract of Work" that gave the company authority to take land, timber, water, and other natural resources on a tax-free basis and to "resettle indigenous inhabitants while providing reasonable compensation."33 Freeport was the first foreign company to receive investment rights under the New Order's 1967 Foreign Investment Law, and its close relationship with the Suharto government — including direct ties to Suharto's family through share arrangements — became emblematic of New Order crony capitalism.

The true scale of the Freeport concession only became apparent after 1988, when exploratory drilling confirmed that the Grasberg mountain in the central highlands contained one of the world's largest known deposits of gold and copper. The Grasberg mine, developed in the 1990s, eventually became the largest gold mine and lowest-extraction-cost copper mine in the world; between 1992 and 1998 alone, Freeport provided the Indonesian government with $1.27 billion in dividends, royalties, and corporate taxes.34 The mine accounted for roughly half of Papua's gross domestic product and was the single largest taxpayer in Indonesia for much of the 1990s.

The human rights dimensions of the Freeport mine under the New Order were serious and documented. The Indonesian military maintained a substantial garrison in the area — ostensibly to protect the company's operations but also functioning as a revenue source, since Freeport made payments to military units that provided security. Human rights organisations documented multiple incidents of killings, disappearances, and arbitrary arrests of Papuans in the vicinity of the mine's operations, including incidents in 1995 and 1996 that drew international attention.35 The Amungme and Kamoro peoples, whose ancestral lands lay within the mine's concession area, received minimal compensation for the destruction of their sacred mountains and river systems; protests were met with force.

Alongside the mine, the New Order's transmigration programme fundamentally altered the demographic composition of Papua. The programme — transferring landless Javanese, Balinese, and Sumatran peasants to the Outer Islands — had operated since the colonial period, but it was massively accelerated under Suharto as both a development strategy and a mechanism of national integration. For Papua, historian Jim Elmslie estimates that by 1995 approximately 200,000 government-sponsored transmigrants and a further 100,000 unassisted migrants had settled in the province, with the pace of arrival accelerating through the 1984–1989 "super-programme" phase that resettled millions from Java across the Outer Islands.36 Papuan independence advocates characterised transmigration as demographic engineering designed to make Papuans a minority in their own land; Indonesian officials argued it was standard development policy applied consistently across the archipelago.

Transmigration (transmigrasi) — the Indonesian government programme, greatly expanded under the New Order, of relocating landless farmers and other settlers from densely populated islands (primarily Java and Bali) to less densely settled outer provinces; it produced significant demographic change in Papua, Kalimantan, and other receiving provinces.

The statistical reality by the end of the Suharto era was contested but significant: Papuans of Melanesian origin had become, in certain urban areas and coastal districts, a minority in their own province. Whether this amounted to what some scholars have called "slow-motion genocide" — using the language of settler colonialism — or represented the ordinary demographic consequences of development policy remains a deeply contested scholarly and political question. What is not contested is that the combination of land dispossession, demographic displacement, military violence, and the extraction of mineral wealth without proportionate local benefit generated a depth of Papuan alienation from the Indonesian state that the New Order never successfully addressed and that its successors would inherit as one of the most intractable issues of the Reformasi era.37

Three Territories, One System

Placed alongside each other, the three conflicts illuminate the structural logic of the New Order's approach to peripheral territories. In each case the state's primary instruments were military force and economic extraction, with limited tolerance for cultural distinctiveness that might nourish political autonomy. In each case revenues from locally produced resources — Acehnese natural gas, Papuan copper and gold, the eventual Timor Gap oil — flowed predominantly to the centre, while the costs of extraction and the burdens of military occupation fell on local populations. In each case the New Order's legal and ideological framework — the doctrine of Dwifungsi, which placed the military in a socio-political as well as defence role, and the constitutional requirement of territorial integrity (Negara Kesatuan Republik Indonesia, NKRI) — provided justification for suppressing political dissent regardless of its legitimacy.38

The differences between the three cases are nonetheless significant. East Timor was a colonial acquisition made under the New Order itself, without any prior legal connection to the Indonesian state, and its resistance drew on a distinct Portuguese-language Catholic identity that had no parallel elsewhere in the archipelago. International law was clearly applicable — the UN Security Council had called for Indonesian withdrawal — and international pressure, though slow to build, eventually proved decisive. Aceh's situation was formally that of an internal Indonesian province with a recognised, if contested, history within the republic; the GAM's secessionist claims rested on Acehnese distinctiveness and natural-resource grievance rather than any external legal status. Papua occupied an intermediate position: its incorporation rested on the New York Agreement and a plebiscite that international law had at least nominally supervised, but whose legitimacy was almost universally doubted by scholars and Papuan political actors alike.

These distinctions shaped the international trajectories of the three conflicts. East Timor's path to a UN referendum in 1999 was at least partly enabled by its anomalous legal status. Aceh's conflict was resolved through the bilateral Helsinki peace process of 2005, brokered by the Crisis Management Initiative and facilitated by the context of the 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami. Papua, lacking the external legal leverage of East Timor and the negotiated settlement of Aceh, remained the most intractable, carrying the unresolved legitimacy question of 1969 into the post-New Order democratic era and beyond.

Dwifungsi — "Dual Function," the New Order doctrine that assigned the Indonesian Armed Forces (ABRI) a socio-political role alongside defence, justifying military representation in civilian government, regional administration, and the legislature, and underpinning the military's capacity to conduct counterinsurgency operations without civilian oversight.

Summary

The New Order's integration of East Timor, Aceh, and Papua produced three of the most serious human rights crises in late twentieth-century Southeast Asia. In East Timor, Indonesia's December 1975 invasion — conducted with tacit American approval — was followed by a twenty-four-year occupation in which the CAVR estimated at least 102,800 deaths, including a devastating famine period from 1977 to 1979; the 1991 Santa Cruz massacre and the 1996 Nobel Peace Prize to Belo and Ramos-Horta internationalised a cause that ultimately ended with the 1999 independence referendum. In Aceh, the 1976 founding of GAM by Hasan di Tiro expressed grievances rooted in the extraction of Arun gas revenues without local benefit; the 1989–1998 DOM period brought systematic violence that Amnesty International documented as approximately 2,000 unlawful killings, with hundreds more disappeared. In Papua, the contested 1969 "Act of Free Choice" — in which 1,025 hand-picked representatives unanimously voted for integration under conditions of documented coercion — left a legitimacy deficit that Freeport's mineral extraction and the New Order's transmigration programme deepened through demographic change and resource dispossession. Taken together, the three crises reveal the limits of a developmentalist authoritarian state whose drive for territorial and economic integration outran its capacity for inclusive governance.

34.7 Indonesia's transmigration programme in Papua is characterised by some scholars as a form of settler colonialism and by others as a standard development tool applied across the archipelago. What criteria would you use to evaluate these competing characterisations?
34.8 Compare the international trajectories of East Timor, Aceh, and Papua: why did East Timor achieve independence, Aceh a peace agreement, and Papua neither, despite all three being sites of serious human rights violations?
