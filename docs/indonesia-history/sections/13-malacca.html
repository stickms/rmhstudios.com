Learning Objectives

  • Trace the founding of Malacca around 1400 by the fugitive prince Parameswara, and weigh the conflicting Malay, Chinese, and Portuguese accounts of its origins.
  • Explain how a small fishing settlement on the Straits became the dominant entrepôt of maritime Southeast Asia within a single generation.
  • Assess the role of Ming China and the Zheng He voyages in protecting and elevating Malacca against its rivals.
  • Understand how and when Malacca adopted Islam, and how the sultanate became an engine for the religion's spread across the archipelago.
  • Use the Sejarah Melayu (Malay Annals) critically as a historical source, alongside Tomé Pires' Suma Oriental.
  • Describe the Malay model of kingship and the rise of Malay as a regional lingua franca.
  • Evaluate the 1511 Portuguese conquest and its consequences for the archipelago's trade order.

For roughly one hundred years, between about 1400 and 1511, a city on the marshy western shore of the Malay Peninsula governed the commercial life of maritime Southeast Asia. Malacca was not large by the standards of Java's rice kingdoms, and it stood on poor soil that grew almost no food. Yet it commanded the narrow sea-lane through which the trade of China, India, the Indonesian archipelago, and the Islamic west had to pass, and it made of that position an empire of the market. Malacca was at once a fugitive prince's refuge, a Chinese protectorate, a Muslim sultanate, and the most cosmopolitan port in the fifteenth-century world. Although the city sits on the peninsula rather than on any Indonesian island, no history of the archipelago can omit it: Malacca set the template for the Malay sultanate, carried Islam to the coasts of Sumatra, Java, and Borneo, and spread the Malay language as the common tongue of island trade. Its fall to the Portuguese in 1511 did not end that world so much as scatter it.

A fugitive prince and the founding of Malacca

The conventional story of Malacca's birth begins with flight. According to the Sejarah Melayu and the Portuguese chroniclers alike, a prince named Parameswara — claimed in Malay tradition as a descendant of the old royal line of Srivijaya — ruled at Singapura (Singapore) or Palembang in the late fourteenth century, was driven out by the rival power of Majapahit or Ayudhya, and fled north up the peninsula. Pausing at the mouth of a river, the story goes, he watched one of his hunting dogs cornered and kicked into the water by a tiny mouse-deer (pelanduk); taking the underdog's defiance as a favourable omen, he founded a city on the spot and named it after the melaka tree under which he rested.1 The etymological tale is almost certainly a later embellishment, but the outline of a displaced Srivijayan-descended prince establishing a new port around 1400 is broadly accepted.2

Almost every concrete detail, however, is contested, and the modern reader must hold the sources apart. The exact date of the foundation has been debated for more than a century. As Peter Borschberg has shown, the question generated a sharp exchange between the French orientalist Gabriel Ferrand and the Dutch scholar Gerret Pieter Rouffaer between 1918 and 1921, and the echoes of that dispute persist; some sources even preserve a claim, reported by the Portuguese writer Gaspar Correia, that a port existed at the site well before Parameswara's arrival.3 Dates between roughly 1390 and 1403 are all defensible, and the round figure of "about 1400" is best treated as a convenient marker rather than a fact.

The identity of the founder is equally vexed. The Malay and Portuguese traditions call him Parameswara — a Sanskrit royal title meaning "supreme lord" rather than a personal name — while the Chinese records of the Ming court, which are the earliest datable witnesses to Malacca's existence, give a ruler whom they transcribe as Pai-li-mi-su-la and later, after his conversion to Islam, as Megat Iskandar Shah. The great Malay historian R. O. Winstedt argued, comparing the Malay annals with Chinese, Portuguese, and Islamic genealogies, that Parameswara and the early Muslim king were one and the same man under two names, his pre- and post-conversion identities, and that the founder died in 1424.4 Other scholars have read the same evidence as describing two or three successive rulers. Wang Gungwu, working closely from the Ming veritable records (the Shih-lu), reconstructed a chronology in which Parameswara rose to prominence through Palembang, Singapore, and Muar in the last two decades of the fourteenth century, founded Malacca in the first years of the fifteenth, and died in 1424, succeeded by a Sri Maharaja who reigned until 1444.5 The disagreement is not trivial pedantry: it bears directly on when Malacca became Muslim, a problem we return to below. What matters for the larger story is that a prince claiming the prestige of the vanished Srivijayan thalassocracy planted, around 1400, a new settlement at the strategic heart of the Straits.

Entrepôt — a port that thrives not by producing goods itself but by gathering, storing, and redistributing the merchandise of others. Malacca, which grew almost no food and made few manufactures, was the classic Southeast Asian entrepôt, living entirely on the through-trade of the Straits.

The geography of advantage: rise of the Straits emporium

Malacca's spectacular rise — from fishing village to dominant emporium within a generation — is best explained by geography working together with politics. The Straits of Malacca form the maritime funnel between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea, the unavoidable corridor along which the monsoon-driven trade of Asia had moved for a thousand years. A port placed near the narrowest part of that channel, with a sheltered harbour and a hill from which to watch the sea-lane, could oblige passing ships to call, to revictual, and to trade. This was the same logic that had earlier sustained Srivijaya from Palembang; Malacca inherited the role.6

The timing of the winds reinforced the site. Ships from the west — from India, the Bay of Bengal, and the Arab and Persian worlds — sailed east on the southwest monsoon; ships from China and the eastern archipelago came on the northeast monsoon. The two systems met, with a seasonal lag, in the latitudes of the Straits, so that a port there became the natural place to wait, to warehouse cargo, and to exchange one ocean's goods for the other's.7 Malacca offered the additional asset of a ruling house willing to make trade easy: light and predictable duties, a secure anchorage policed against the piracy that plagued the Straits, weights and measures that merchants could trust, and a legal order — eventually codified in the Undang-Undang Melaka, the Laws of Malacca — that gave foreign traders confidence.

Historians have refined the older picture of Malacca as a single, static "emporium." Kenneth R. Hall has argued that the fifteenth-century ports of the Straits region are better understood as nodes in a network of marketplaces, drawing on long-established local and regional trading systems rather than springing up from nothing; Malacca succeeded by capturing and concentrating flows that already existed.8 The city's hinterland produced little, but its harbour aggregated the tin of the peninsula, the gold of Sumatra, the pepper of the western archipelago, and above all the cloves, nutmeg, and mace of the distant Moluccas, exchanging them for Indian textiles and Chinese porcelain and silk. The orang laut — the "sea people" of the Straits, whose boats had served the rulers of Srivijaya — gave the new state a maritime militia that could both protect friendly shipping and compel the unfriendly to call at Malacca rather than slip past.9

Orang laut — literally "sea people," the boat-dwelling maritime communities of the Straits and Riau archipelago. Their loyalty to the ruler furnished Srivijaya and then Malacca with naval power, pilotage, and the means to channel passing trade into the royal port.

Ming China and the Zheng He voyages

Malacca's rise cannot be separated from the extraordinary intervention of Ming China in Southeast Asian waters during the early fifteenth century. The Yongle emperor, who seized the throne in 1402, launched a series of vast state-sponsored maritime expeditions under the eunuch admiral Zheng He — seven voyages between 1405 and 1433 that carried tens of thousands of men in fleets of hundreds of ships across the Indian Ocean as far as Arabia and East Africa. Malacca lay directly on their route, and the relationship between the new port and the new emperor proved decisive for both.

Diplomatic contact opened almost at once. Wang Gungwu's close study of the Ming records shows that relations between China and Malacca were established in 1403–05, when a Chinese envoy reached the port and Parameswara responded by sending tribute; the Ming Shih-lu is in fact the earliest surviving documentary record of Malacca's existence.10 In 1405 the Yongle emperor formally recognised the ruler as a king and the settlement as a state, and Parameswara himself is said to have travelled to the Ming court — an act of homage that brought, in return, the protection of the most powerful empire in the world.11 That protection mattered enormously to a fragile new port hemmed in by two predatory neighbours: Majapahit in Java to the south and the Thai kingdom of Ayudhya (Siam) to the north, both of which claimed overlordship of the peninsula and exacted tribute from its ports.

The Chinese fleets did more than confer prestige. Geoff Wade, in an influential reassessment of the voyages, has argued that they should be understood not as peaceful gestures of Confucian goodwill but as a militarised projection of Ming power — a form of "proto maritime colonialism" — in which the fleets intervened in local conflicts, installed and removed rulers, and established forward bases. Malacca, Wade argues, served as a fortified depot for the treasure fleets, with Chinese garrisons and stockades, and it was this military umbrella that allowed the port to defy the tribute claims of Ayudhya and to consolidate its grip on the Straits.12 Wade's reading is not universally accepted; other scholars stress the genuinely diplomatic and commercial dimensions of the voyages. Tansen Sen, surveying the expeditions' wider impact on the Indian Ocean, agrees that they introduced a new, state-directed and sometimes coercive commercial presence into the region, and suggests that the networks and knowledge they generated may even have eased the later entry of European powers.13 Whatever the balance of motives, the consequence for Malacca was clear: under the shelter of the Ming, a peripheral port became, within two decades, the central market of the Straits. When the voyages were abruptly discontinued after 1433 and China withdrew its fleets, Malacca was already strong enough to stand on its own — and turned increasingly toward the Muslim trading world of the Indian Ocean.

The coming of Islam to Malacca

Islam had been present on the northern coast of Sumatra since at least the thirteenth century, when a Muslim ruler was established at Samudra Pasai — a polity whose example, marriage alliances, and merchants would draw Malacca into the faith.14 Exactly when and how Malacca's own rulers became Muslim is, once again, a matter of genuine scholarly dispute, and the textbook reader should be wary of the tidy dates often quoted. The sources are, in the words of one specialist, "meagre, scattered, and often contradictory."15

The older account, drawn from a literal reading of the Sejarah Melayu, has a later ruler — often given as Sultan Muhammad Shah or his successor — converting after a dream in which the Prophet Muhammad appeared, taught him the confession of faith, and foretold the arrival of a ship from Jeddah bearing a teacher. Christopher Wake, in a careful source-critical study, dismantled the simple version. He argued that the confusion of names and reign-lengths in the annals conceals a smaller number of rulers than tradition supposes, and that the decisive conversion of the ruling house is best dated to around 1414, in the reign of the king the Chinese called Megat Iskandar Shah — that is, very possibly the founder Parameswara himself, or his immediate successor, taking a Muslim name and the title Shah.16 On this reading the adoption of Islam was closely bound up with Malacca's commercial reorientation: as the Ming withdrew and the trade of the Muslim Indian Ocean grew, embracing Islam aligned the dynasty with the Gujarati, Tamil Muslim, Persian, and Arab merchants on whom its prosperity depended, and with the example of Pasai.

How active a part the rulers themselves played has been debated. An older view, associated with the colonial scholar J. M. Gullick, treated conversion as a gradual, almost passive seepage of belief from foreign merchants into the population. A. C. Milner challenged this, arguing that the Malay rulers were the prime movers: they adopted Islam deliberately, took the title sultan, and used the religion to magnify their own sovereignty, so that conversion spread downward from the court rather than upward from the bazaar.17 The new faith arrived, moreover, in a form already shaped for absorption. Much of the Islam that reached the archipelago was carried by Sufi teachers and mystics whose cosmopolitan, accommodating piety could coexist with older beliefs; the presence of Persian settlers and Sufi holy men in the fifteenth-century archipelago helped Islam take root without demanding a wholesale rupture with the past.18

Whatever its precise timing, the conversion of Malacca had consequences out of all proportion to a single dynasty's piety. As the dominant port and the most prestigious court of the region, Malacca became the great disseminator of Islam through the archipelago. Its merchants carried the faith along the trade routes; its princesses married into other ruling houses; and its prestige made conversion attractive to rulers who wished to share in its commercial network and its glamour. Over the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries Islam spread from the Malacca orbit to the pepper ports of Sumatra, to Demak and the north coast of Java, to Brunei and the coasts of Borneo, and toward the spice islands of the east. The chapter that follows traces those sultanates in detail; here it is enough to note that Malacca was their common point of reference and, in many cases, the immediate source of their faith.

Reading the Sejarah Melayu: a source and its uses

Our knowledge of Malacca from the inside rests above all on the Sejarah Melayu, the "Malay Annals" — a courtly chronicle of the Malacca dynasty and its origins that is at once the supreme literary monument of classical Malay and a treacherous historical source. Composed and recompiled at the later Malay courts of Johor in the early seventeenth century, after Malacca itself had fallen, the Sejarah Melayu survives in numerous manuscript recensions. The oldest, known as Raffles MS 18, is dated to 1612, though its narrative material is probably eighty years or more older; the standard English version remains C. C. Brown's translation, first published in the Journal of the Malayan Branch of the Royal Asiatic Society in 1952 and later as a monograph.19

The work must be read as literature with a purpose, not as a chronicle in the modern sense. Its aim was to glorify the Malacca-Johor dynasty, to anchor its legitimacy in a genealogy reaching back through a miraculous origin at Bukit Si-Guntang in Sumatra to Alexander the Great (Iskandar Zulkarnain) and the kings of Srivijaya, and to articulate the proper relationship between ruler and subject.20 Its chronology is unreliable, its kings are sometimes composites, and its episodes are shaped by didactic and dramatic needs. A recent study by Tan Zi Hao reads the famous account of the fall of Singapura — the episode of the unjustly impaled woman Tun Jana Khatib and the loyal-but-doomed warrior, which precedes the founding of Malacca — as a piece of politically motivated literature designed to justify violence and dynastic transfer, rather than as a record of events.21 To use the Sejarah Melayu responsibly, historians triangulate it against external evidence: the Ming records, which supply firm dates and the Chinese view; and the early Portuguese accounts — the commentaries attributed to Afonso de Albuquerque, the Book of Duarte Barbosa, and the Suma Oriental of Tomé Pires — which describe Malacca as eyewitnesses saw it.22 Where these independent strands agree, we can be reasonably confident; where the annals stand alone, we read them as the court wished to be remembered.

Sejarah Melayu — the "Malay Annals," a court chronicle of the Malacca sultanate compiled at Johor in the early seventeenth century. A masterpiece of classical Malay literature and a key source for the sultanate, it is nonetheless a legitimising, genealogical text whose chronology must be checked against Chinese and Portuguese evidence.

The cosmopolitan emporium: Tomé Pires and the merchant communities

The most vivid surviving portrait of Malacca at its height comes from a man who arrived just after its fall. Tomé Pires, a Portuguese apothecary, lived in the conquered city from 1512 and wrote the Suma Oriental, a survey of the trade of the East completed around 1515; it was edited and translated in the twentieth century by Armando Cortesão for the Hakluyt Society.23 Pires described a port of astonishing diversity. Modern scholarship, drawing on his account and others, has reckoned that Malacca was home to merchant communities speaking on the order of eighty-four languages, making it, in one recent assessment, "arguably the most cosmopolitan city of the fifteenth- century world."24 Gujaratis and other Indian Muslims, Tamils, Bengalis, Persians, Arabs, Armenians, Javanese, Sumatrans, Bugis, Luzon islanders, Chinese, Ryukyuans, and Burmese all traded and lodged there, alongside the Malay establishment.

The genius of Malacca's administration lay in accommodating this Babel without dissolving into chaos. The port was governed for commercial purposes by a college of shahbandars — harbourmasters, each responsible for a defined group of nationalities. One shahbandar oversaw the Gujaratis; another, the merchants of the rest of India, Pegu, and Pasai; a third, those of the archipelago to the east; a fourth, the Chinese and traders of the Far East. Each shahbandar received the ships of his charges, assigned them warehouses, set them before the bendahara, the chief minister, and adjudicated their disputes. The result was a regime in which a foreign trader knew to whom he answered, found his customs and language respected, and could expect even-handed treatment under the Laws of Malacca.25

It was the strategic value of this market that produced the most famous line ever written about Malacca. Tomé Pires concluded that the city's command of the choke-point between the oceans made it the hinge of all Asian commerce — declaring that "whoever is lord of Malacca has his hand on the throat of Venice," because control of the port meant control of the spice trade on which the wealth of the Mediterranean ultimately depended.26 The remark, written for a Portuguese crown that had just seized the city, was also a strategic prospectus; but it captured a real truth about the fifteenth-century world economy. The cloves, nutmeg, and mace that commanded fortunes in Cairo, Venice, and Lisbon passed, almost all of them, through Malacca's warehouses. Anthony Reid has placed this fifteenth- and sixteenth-century boom within a wider "age of commerce" in Southeast Asia, an era of swelling international trade, urban growth, and rising demand for the region's spices and forest products — a boom of which Malacca was both the symbol and the principal beneficiary.27

Shahbandar — a harbourmaster in the ports of the Indian-Ocean and Southeast-Asian trading world. At Malacca a college of four shahbandars each managed a group of foreign merchant nationalities, allotting them warehouses and settling their disputes — the institutional key to the port's cosmopolitan order.

The Malay model: kingship, court, and a common tongue

Malacca did more than dominate trade; it crystallised a model of kingship and culture that later Malay states would imitate for centuries. At the apex stood the ruler, the raja and, after the conversion, sultan, whose authority was theorised through the concept of daulat — a sacral, almost supernatural sovereign power inhering in the legitimate ruler. To betray the ruler was derhaka, treason against the cosmic order, an offence that daulat would itself punish; the Sejarah Melayu repeatedly dramatises the fatal consequences that befall those who lift a hand against a king.28 Islam did not displace this older Indic-Malay conception of sacred kingship so much as reinforce it, the ruler now appearing as the shadow of God upon earth while also remaining the bearer of daulat; the sharī'a took its place alongside, rather than wholly supplanting, the customary law of adat.29

Beneath the sultan, Malacca developed a graded officialdom that became the standard pattern of Malay statecraft. Pre-eminent was the bendahara, a grand vizier or chief minister who combined administrative, judicial, and military authority and who, in the person of the celebrated Tun Perak, effectively ran the state through several reigns.30 Below him stood the temenggung (in charge of security and the port police), the laksamana (admiral), and the shahbandars already described. This hierarchy of titles, the court ceremonial that surrounded it, and the ideal of the loyal subject and the just-but-absolute ruler were exported wholesale to the successor sultanates of Johor, Perak, Pahang, and beyond, so that "Malayness" itself came to be defined in part by adherence to this Malacca-derived model of kingship and custom.31

The most enduring of Malacca's legacies was linguistic. The form of Malay used in the markets and court of the sultanate spread along the trade routes as a lingua franca — a contact language that merchants of every origin could use to deal with one another. Malay was relatively easy to learn, carried the prestige of the dominant port, and was unencumbered by the rigid status-marking of Javanese; it became, in effect, the business language of the archipelago, written in the Arabic-derived Jawi script that arrived with Islam.32 From this trade Malay would descend, through many channels and centuries, the "bazaar Malay" of the region's ports and ultimately the national languages of modern Indonesia and Malaysia.33 When the framers of Indonesian nationalism chose Malay — not the majority Javanese — as the basis for the national language at the Youth Pledge of 1928, they were building, knowingly or not, on a status that Malacca had helped to establish five centuries earlier.

Daulat — the sacral sovereign power believed to inhere in a legitimate Malay ruler. Its counterpart was derhaka, treason; the ideology of daulat made loyalty to the ruler a cosmic as well as a political duty and underpinned the Malacca model of kingship.
Bendahara — the chief minister or grand vizier of a Malay sultanate, combining administrative, judicial, and military authority. At Malacca the bendahara (notably Tun Perak) was the most powerful officer of state after the sultan himself.

1511: the Portuguese conquest and its aftermath

Malacca's wealth and its strategic command of the Straits made it the obvious prize for the Portuguese, who had rounded the Cape of Good Hope and were driving eastward in search of the spice trade at its source. A first Portuguese embassy under Diogo Lopes de Sequeira reached Malacca in 1509 but ended in violence and the seizure of several Portuguese, giving the crown both a grievance and a pretext. In 1511 Afonso de Albuquerque, the architect of Portugal's eastern empire, sailed from Goa with a fleet of some eighteen ships and roughly a thousand to twelve hundred fighting men and, after weeks of negotiation and assault, stormed the city. Despite the defenders' war-elephants and cannon, Malacca fell; the last sultan, Mahmud Shah, withdrew up-country and then to the islands, founding the line of exile that would become the Johor sultanate.34

The Portuguese moved at once to entrench themselves. Albuquerque set his men to building a great stone fortress, A Famosa, begun in 1512, partly from the rubble of the city's mosques and tombs; its gatehouse, the Porta de Santiago, still stands. Graham Irwin's study of the fort traces how it dominated the settlement for nearly three centuries, withstanding repeated sieges and changing hands only twice — to the Dutch in 1641 and the British in 1807 — before being largely demolished.35 The Portuguese governed through a compromise with what they found, retaining indigenous offices such as the bendahara and the shahbandars to manage the Asian merchant communities, even as endemic corruption and the absence of systematic accounting hollowed out the administration from within.36

The deeper consequence of 1511 was not that the Portuguese inherited Malacca's empire but that they could not. Sanjay Subrahmanyam has emphasised how thinly stretched and how dependent on Asian commerce the Portuguese Estado da Índia always was; capturing a city was one thing, capturing a network another.37 Muslim merchants, unwilling to trade under Christian masters who taxed them heavily and periodically harassed them, simply diverted their custom elsewhere. New Muslim emporia rose to absorb the redirected trade — Aceh at the northern tip of Sumatra, Banten and the ports of the Java Sea, Brunei, Patani, and the exiled court at Johor — so that the very conquest meant to monopolise the spice trade instead dispersed it across a more fragmented and more thoroughly Islamic archipelago. The Portuguese held the fortress; the commerce of the Straits flowed around it. Malacca itself declined into a garrison town, its cosmopolitan glory gone, though its mixed Portuguese-Asian community survives to this day in the endangered Malacca-Portuguese Creole, the "last vital variety" of Southeast Asian Creole Portuguese.38

Summary

Around 1400 a displaced prince of Srivijayan descent, Parameswara, founded Malacca at the strategic heart of the Straits, though the sources — Malay, Chinese, and Portuguese — disagree sharply on the date, the founder's identity, and the timing of his line's conversion to Islam. Geography and politics together made the port the dominant entrepôt of maritime Asia: the monsoon winds met in its latitudes, the orang laut policed its trade, and the protection of Ming China and the Zheng He voyages shielded it from Majapahit and Ayudhya during its fragile youth. The ruling house adopted Islam, probably around 1414, and Malacca became the great disseminator of the faith and of the Malay language across the archipelago. Its court produced the model of Malay kingship — daulat, the bendahara, the graded officialdom — later imitated everywhere, and the Sejarah Melayu, a literary masterpiece that historians must read critically against Chinese records and the eyewitness Suma Oriental of Tomé Pires, who described a port speaking dozens of languages and called it the hand on the throat of Venice. The Portuguese conquest of 1511 captured the city but not its trade, which scattered to a constellation of new Muslim sultanates and left the archipelago more fragmented, and more Islamic, than before.

12.1 The Malay, Chinese, and Portuguese sources disagree about Malacca's founding date and its founder's identity. How should a historian weigh these different kinds of evidence, and why might the date of the dynasty's conversion to Islam depend on resolving the question of how many early kings there were?
12.2 Malacca produced almost no food and few goods of its own. Explain, using the concept of the entrepôt, how it nonetheless became the richest port in the region.
12.3 Geoff Wade describes the Zheng He voyages as a kind of "proto maritime colonialism," while others stress their diplomatic and commercial character. What is at stake in this debate for our understanding of Malacca's rise?
12.4 Why is the Sejarah Melayu both indispensable and unreliable as a source? Give a concrete example of how it might be checked against independent evidence.
12.5 What did the institution of the shahbandars reveal about how Malacca managed its extraordinarily diverse merchant population? Could a port today learn anything from it?
12.6 Scholars disagree about whether Malacca's rulers actively drove the adoption of Islam or whether the faith seeped in passively through foreign merchants. Which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and what evidence would settle the question?
12.7 How did the Malacca model of kingship — daulat, derhaka, the bendahara — shape later Malay states and the very idea of "Malayness"?
12.8 The Portuguese captured Malacca in 1511 but failed to capture its trade. Explain why, and describe what happened to the commerce of the Straits afterward.
12.9 In what sense was Malacca's spread of the Malay language a precondition for the choice of Malay as the basis of modern Indonesian in 1928?
