  • Analyze the post-1998 conservative turn in Indonesian Islam, including the proliferation of sharia-influenced local regulations (Perda Syariah), the Front Pembela Islam (FPI), and the blasphemy law as upheld in 2010.

  • Document the specific pressures faced by Ahmadiyya, Shia, Christian, and indigenous (penghayat) communities, including the landmark Constitutional Court ruling of November 2017.
  • Assess competing interpretations of Indonesia's pluralist future, weighing the roles of NU, Muhammadiyah, Islamist parties, civil society monitors, and electoral dynamics.

Indonesia is not only the world's most populous Muslim-majority country — with more than 235 million Muslims among a national population of 270 million, a share of roughly 87 percent — it is also one of the most internally diverse: a nation in which Islam itself fractures along reformist, traditionalist, and Islamist lines, in which five other state-recognized religions maintain deep historical roots, and in which tens of millions of citizens observe indigenous belief systems the state has only recently begun to acknowledge.1 The Reformasi period that began in 1998 simultaneously expanded democratic space and unleashed competitive religious mobilization, producing a paradox that scholars have labored to explain: a constitutional democracy whose founding ideology, Pancasila, enshrines religious pluralism, yet whose legal code sanctions a blasphemy law weaponized against minority believers, whose regional governments have enacted hundreds of Islamic morality regulations, and whose most dramatic electoral moment in a generation — the prosecution and imprisonment of a Christian governor on blasphemy charges — was precipitated by mass rallies organized under the banner of Islam. Understanding this paradox requires close attention to institutions, legal frameworks, social movements, and the layered histories of communities that have coexisted, accommodated, and periodically clashed across the Indonesian archipelago.

Indonesia's Religious Landscape: Demographics, Recognition, and Pancasila

The 2020 national census conducted by Badan Pusat Statistik (BPS–Statistics Indonesia) recorded a total population of 270.2 million, of whom 236.5 million (86.83 percent) identified as Muslim. Protestants constituted approximately 7.5 percent of the population, Catholics 3.1 percent, Hindus 1.7 percent, Buddhists 0.7 percent, and Confucians a small fraction under 0.1 percent.1 These proportions represent a modest but measurable shift from the 2000 census, in which Muslims made up 88.2 percent of the population; the slight decline reflects both differential birth rates and an increase in Christian adherents, particularly in eastern Indonesia, Papua, and Kalimantan. Hinduism remains concentrated overwhelmingly in Bali, whose preservation of Balinese Hinduism — a synthesis of Shaivite, Buddhist, and animist elements unique to the island — represents one of the most culturally distinctive religious traditions in Asia.

The formal architecture of religious recognition in Indonesia dates to the administration of Sukarno. Presidential Decree PNPS No. 1 of 1965, framed primarily as a blasphemy law, defined a list of six agama (religions) receiving state recognition: Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, and — later formally restored by Abdurrahman Wahid's government — Confucianism. Recognition carries substantial practical consequences: only adherents of recognized religions may list their faith on the national identity card (KTP), may legally marry under their religious law, and are entitled to state-funded religious instruction in public schools. The effect is to create a two-tier system in which recognized religions receive institutional support and legal protection, while adherents of indigenous belief traditions, minority Islamic movements, and unrecognized faiths occupy a legally precarious position.2

The foundational pluralist framework within which these six religions formally coexist is provided by Pancasila, the five-principle state ideology promulgated by Sukarno in June 1945. Its first and most theologically charged principle — Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa, "Belief in the One Almighty God" — was formulated as a deliberately capacious formulation that could accommodate monotheistic, henotheistic, and even broadly theistic cosmologies. The United States Commission on International Religious Freedom has noted that Pancasila "provides a constitutional framework for religious pluralism" but simultaneously "limits religious freedom by designating only six official religions."3 The sociologist Robert W. Hefner, in his foundational study Civil Islam (2000), argued that this framework reflects the outcome of a genuine historical contest between Muslim reformists who sought a pluralist democratic republic and those who pressed for an explicit Islamic state: "The founding generation of Indonesian Muslim leaders," Hefner wrote, "made a remarkable choice — they repudiated the goal of an Islamic state, mobilized religiously ecumenical support, and championed democratic ideals."2 That choice, embodied in Pancasila, has defined — and periodically strained — the Indonesian polity ever since.

The political scientist Jeremy Menchik, drawing on archival documents, ethnographic observation, and original survey data for his Cambridge University Press study Islam and Democracy in Indonesia (2016), introduced the concept of "godly nationalism" to explain the particular form of tolerance that major Indonesian Muslim organizations actually practice: not a liberal individualist pluralism that protects the rights of each citizen to worship as they please, but rather a communitarian tolerance that acknowledges the legitimacy of recognized religious communities while maintaining a clear distinction between the sanctioned and the deviant.4 This distinction, Menchik argues, is not hypocrisy but a coherent normative framework — one with significant implications for how Ahmadiyah, Shia, and penghayat communities have been treated.

The Two Great Mass Organizations: Nahdlatul Ulama and Muhammadiyah

No account of Indonesian Islam can proceed far without engaging the two organizational giants that have shaped the country's religious landscape for more than a century: Nahdlatul Ulama (NU), the "Revival of Religious Scholars," and Muhammadiyah. Together they claim membership rolls representing a substantial fraction of the country's Muslim population, operate overlapping networks of schools, hospitals, and social welfare institutions, and have exercised decisive influence over Indonesian politics, theology, and civil society from the colonial period to the present day.5

Muhammadiyah was founded first, in 1912 in Yogyakarta, by the religious scholar Ahmad Dahlan. Dahlan had been influenced by the reformist ideas circulating in Cairo and elsewhere in the Islamic world, which called for the purification of Muslim practice from what reformists identified as syncretic accretions and superstition, and for a return to direct engagement with the Quran and hadith through independent reasoning (ijtihad) rather than deference to classical legal tradition (taqlid). Muhammadiyah thus positioned itself as a modernist, reformist organization — not primarily political in the sense of seeking state power, but socially transformative through its extensive program of schools, clinics, and welfare institutions. By the early twenty-first century, Muhammadiyah managed thousands of schools and dozens of universities, making it one of the largest private educational networks in the world, alongside some 450 hospitals and clinics. Hilman Latief and Haedar Nashir, in their examination of Muhammadiyah's evolution from 2000 to 2020 published in the Journal of Current Southeast Asian Affairs, documented the organization's increasing international engagement — articulating a vision of "Islamic cosmopolitanism" that combined commitment to ijtihad with active participation in global civil society — while stressing that it has never abandoned its foundational modernist theological commitments.6 By 2008, Muhammadiyah's membership was estimated at between 29 and 35 million, largely urban and middle-class in composition.

Nahdlatul Ulama was founded fourteen years later, on January 31, 1926, in Surabaya, East Java, by a coalition of traditionalist ulama led by the revered scholar Kiai Hasyim Asy'ari. Its founding was in part a reaction against the same modernist pressures that Muhammadiyah represented, and against the Wahhabi reformism that had recently triumphed in the Arabian Peninsula when Ibn Saud's forces captured the Hejaz in 1924–25, threatening traditional pilgrimage practices and Islamic cultural patrimony. NU explicitly championed the Sunni traditionalist tradition — adherence to the four classical schools of jurisprudence, veneration of saints and graves, observance of tahlilan (communal prayers for the dead), and integration of Javanese and broader Nusantara cultural practices into Islamic life. This orientation, sometimes caricatured as syncretic by modernist critics, underpins what NU intellectuals in the early twenty-first century have systematized under the concept of Islam Nusantara — "Archipelago Islam" — a framework that presents the historically developed synthesis of Islamic teaching with local wisdom, cultural plurality, and civil tolerance as a model not just for Indonesia but for the broader Muslim world.5

Islam Nusantara — a theological and civilizational concept articulated primarily by Nahdlatul Ulama, referring to the distinctively Indonesian (Nusantara = "Archipelago") form of Islam that has developed over six centuries through the synthesis of Islamic teaching with local cultural traditions, emphasizing tolerance, pluralism, and contextual legal reasoning. NU insists it is not a new sect or legal school but a historically grounded methodology. The concept has become influential internationally as a counterweight to Salafi and Islamist interpretations.

NU's organizational scale is extraordinary. Credible estimates of its membership and affiliated constituency range from 60 million to over 90 million, placing it among the world's largest religious organizations of any kind.4 Its organizational reach extends from village-level Islamic boarding schools (pesantren) — the traditional institution of Sunni Islamic learning that NU regards as the heartbeat of its educational system — to hospitals, universities, youth organizations, and a national women's wing (Fatayat NU and Muslimat NU) with memberships in the tens of millions. NU's political influence has been exercised both indirectly — through its moral authority over the Islamic discourse — and directly, most prominently during the presidency of Abdurrahman Wahid (known as Gus Dur), a towering NU intellectual who served as Indonesia's fourth president from 1999 to 2001. Wahid's presidency, however brief and turbulent, embodied the NU's pluralist commitments: he restored Confucianism as an official religion, opposed the anti-Ahmadiyah and anti-communist legislation, and promoted inter-faith dialogue as a governing principle.

The relationship between NU and Muhammadiyah is marked by long-standing theological tension — over the permissibility of traditional practices, the authority of classical scholarship, and attitudes toward Sufi orders — but also by fundamental convergence on the question of the Indonesian state. Both organizations have historically rejected the goal of an Islamic state or the imposition of sharia law through constitutional means, a position that has placed them at odds with Islamist political movements since the 1940s. The political scientist Thomas Power and others have described this convergence as constitutive of Indonesian "mainstream" Islam, distinguishing it both from the Salafi-jihadi fringe and from the Islamist political parties that emerged after 1998.5

The Conservative Turn: Sharia Bylaws, the FPI, and the Blasphemy Law

The political liberalization that followed Suharto's fall in May 1998 — known as Reformasi — produced an immediate opening for forces that had been suppressed or constrained under the New Order's authoritarian management of religion. The simultaneous decentralization of governmental authority through the 1999 Regional Autonomy Laws created hundreds of new points of decision-making power at the district and provincial levels, many of which became arenas for competitive Islamist mobilization. The result, documented extensively in the edited volume by Greg Fealy and Sally White, Expressing Islam: Religious Life and Politics in Indonesia (ISEAS, 2008), was a complex and often contradictory landscape in which Indonesia's centuries-old pluralist practices found themselves contested by new actors equipped with new organizational capacities and new technologies of mobilization.5

One of the most extensively analyzed manifestations of this conservative turn was the proliferation of Perda Syariah — regional regulations with an Islamic content. Robin Bush, in a chapter in Fealy and White's volume, conducted a systematic analysis of regional bylaws enacted between 1999 and 2007, identifying approximately 78 regulations across 52 of Indonesia's 441 regencies and municipalities that fell under this rubric.8 Bush found that 45 percent of all Perda Syariah regulations fell into the first category of "Islamic observance" requirements — obligatory Quranic literacy tests for students, requirements that civil servants demonstrate Islamic knowledge, and similar measures. Of the remaining 55 percent, 67 percent concerned Islamic religious duties and dress codes, while 33 percent addressed what Bush classified as "social" regulations targeting gambling, prostitution, and the consumption of alcohol. Geographically, Perda Syariah was concentrated in West Sumatra (where reformist Islamic traditions are strong), South Kalimantan, Banten, and parts of West Java. Bush's analysis concluded that while the phenomenon was real and had significant local effects, it was "anomalous" rather than hegemonic at the national level — a product of specific regional political calculations rather than an inevitable expression of Muslim majority will.

The Martin van Bruinessen-edited volume Contemporary Developments in Indonesian Islam: Explaining the "Conservative Turn" (ISEAS, 2013) provided a broader framework for understanding the ideological shifts driving these legislative changes. Van Bruinessen and his contributors identified a cluster of interconnected trends: the rapid expansion of Salafi networks financed by Gulf funding, the growth of a new conservative Muslim middle class, the increased influence of the Majelis Ulama Indonesia (MUI — Indonesian Council of Ulama) as a source of religiously authoritative guidance, and the weakening of the formerly dominant traditions of NU and Muhammadiyah among urban, educated youth.7 The Council's influence was amplified by a series of controversial fatwas: in 2005, the MUI issued sweeping condemnations of liberalism, pluralism, and secularism in Islam, as well as declaring Ahmadiyah to be outside the Islamic fold — a theological determination with immediate and severe practical consequences for that community.

The most visible street-level expression of post-Reformasi Islamist mobilization was the Front Pembela Islam (FPI — Islamic Defenders Front), founded in 1998 in Betawi, Jakarta, by Habib Muhammad Rizieq Shihab. The FPI combined the language of Islamic moral defense with vigilante tactics: organized raids on establishments selling alcohol, nightclubs, and other venues deemed un-Islamic, attacks on Christian churches and minority Muslim gatherings, and large-scale street demonstrations that exploited democratic freedoms to threaten and intimidate. The organization attracted recruits from Jakarta's urban poor and underemployed youth, operating as what social scientists have described as a hybrid of religious vigilantism and clientelist patronage network. The FPI's activities intersected periodically with the interests of political actors and military figures who used the organization as a tool of pressure while denying official connection. It was formally banned by joint ministerial decree on December 30, 2020 — a decision attributed to its failure to renew its organizational registration, but coming amid a sharpening confrontation between FPI leader Rizieq (who had returned from three years in Saudi Arabia in November 2020) and the Jokowi government, following the death of six FPI members in a confrontation with police surveillance teams.7

Underlying both Perda Syariah legislation and FPI vigilantism was the continuing architecture of PNPS No. 1 of 1965 — the blasphemy law. A formal constitutional challenge to the law's validity was submitted to the Constitutional Court in 2009 by a coalition of petitioners including prominent human rights organizations. On April 19, 2010, the Court rejected the challenge in an 8-to-1 decision, holding that the law did not violate the constitution and was necessary to maintain public order and religious harmony. The lone dissenter, Justice Maria Farida Indrati, argued that the law's vagueness created unacceptable opportunities for persecution of minority believers. Melissa Crouch's landmark analysis of the decision in the Asian Journal of Comparative Law argued that the ruling validated a deeply problematic legal instrument: by upholding a law that gave the state power to define and prohibit "deviant" religious interpretations within recognized faiths, the Court had institutionalized an architecture of religious control that subjected minority communities — particularly those like Ahmadiyah whose theological positions were already politically disfavored — to continuous threat of prosecution.10 The Library of Congress's documentation of the ruling confirmed that the decision preserved maximum prison penalties of up to five years for blasphemy offenses, with no provision for intent or proportionality.11

Perda Syariah — regional government bylaws (peraturan daerah, or perda) containing Islamic content, enacted by district and provincial governments following the decentralization laws of 1999. Robin Bush's 2008 analysis identified approximately 78 such regulations across 52 Indonesian districts enacted between 1999 and 2007, covering matters ranging from obligatory Quran literacy to dress codes and prohibition of alcohol and gambling. Scholars debate whether they represent a systemic Islamization of governance or region-specific political responses to competitive electoral environments.

Aceh's Special Autonomy and the Full Implementation of Sharia

The province of Aceh at the northwestern tip of Sumatra represents a special and analytically distinct case within Indonesia's religious landscape. Long known as the "Veranda of Mecca" (serambi Mekkah) for its historically high levels of Islamic piety and its role as the primary point of entry for Islam into the Indonesian archipelago, Aceh was the site of a prolonged separatist insurgency by the Free Aceh Movement (GAM) from 1976 until the 2005 Helsinki Peace Agreement. The Indonesian government's strategy for managing the Aceh conflict included a significant religious concession: the 1999 Special Status Law (Law 44/1999) and the 2001 Special Autonomy Law (Law 18/2001), later superseded by the Law on the Governing of Aceh (Law 11/2006), gave Aceh the right to implement Islamic law across a range of public life domains — a right no other Indonesian province possesses.

R. Michael Feener's Oxford University Press monograph Shariʿa and Social Engineering: The Implementation of Islamic Law in Contemporary Aceh, Indonesia (2013) provides the most thorough scholarly examination of how this autonomy has been exercised. Feener's central argument is that the Acehnese state sharia apparatus should be understood primarily as a "project of social engineering" — an attempt by Acehnese political and religious elites to use the regulatory power of Islamic law to transform social behavior, reduce corruption, strengthen public morality, and build a distinctively Islamic form of governance, rather than as a straightforward expression of popular religiosity or traditional Islamic jurisprudence.9 The key instruments of implementation are the Qanun — Islamic bylaws covering dress, gambling, alcohol, and unlawful proximity (khalwat) between unrelated members of the opposite sex — and the Wilayatul Hisbah (WH), the Islamic morality police empowered to enforce these regulations and refer violators to the sharia court. Punishments under the Qanun include caning (cambuk), which has been publicly administered on multiple occasions and attracted international criticism.

Feener's analysis of implementation reveals a gap between the aspirations of Aceh's sharia architects and the practical realities of governance. The Wilayatul Hisbah was chronically underfunded, poorly trained, and inconsistently deployed. Enforcement was selective and frequently correlated with the political utility of prosecutions rather than with the prevalence of offenses. Women bore a disproportionate share of enforcement, as the regulations on dress and khalwat were applied primarily to female behavior while male patrons of gambling establishments and informal alcohol sales went largely unsanctioned. Feener's conclusion — that Aceh's sharia experiment has been "more successful as a symbol than as a program" — reflects a broader scholarly judgment that the implementation of Islamic law through state administrative apparatus involves fundamental tensions between the aspirational language of religious law and the mundane interests of bureaucratic governance.9 The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, which killed an estimated 130,000 Acehnese and devastated the province's infrastructure, also disrupted the sharia program's implementation in ways from which it never fully recovered, even as some advocates argued that the tsunami represented divine judgment requiring heightened religious vigilance.

The Ahok Affair and the 212 Movement: Identity Politics in the Electoral Arena

No episode in contemporary Indonesian history has done more to expose the tensions between the country's pluralist constitutional framework and the mobilizing power of religious identity politics than the prosecution of Basuki Tjahaja Purnama, known universally as "Ahok," for blasphemy in 2016–17. Ahok — a Chinese-Indonesian Christian who had become Jakarta's governor in November 2014 after serving as deputy to Joko Widodo (Jokowi) — was a polarizing figure before the blasphemy controversy: his blunt governing style, his ethnic and religious identity, and his determination to clean up corruption in the capital all generated both enthusiastic support and deep resentment. In September 2016, as he faced re-election in a crowded gubernatorial race, he delivered a speech on the Thousand Islands in which he cited Quran verse 5:51 (Al-Maidah) — a verse commonly translated as cautioning Muslims against taking Jews and Christians as protectors or allies — and told a Moslem audience they should not be deceived by those who used this verse to argue they should not vote for him.

The reaction was swift and severe. A selectively edited video of the speech circulated on social media, stripped of contextual framing, and was rapidly characterized by Islamist critics as blasphemy against the Quran. The Majelis Ulama Indonesia (MUI) issued a fatwa on October 11, 2016 affirming that Ahok had committed blasphemy; significantly, the fatwa was signed by MUI chairman Ma'ruf Amin — at the time also a senior figure in Nahdlatul Ulama, a relationship that illustrated the complex alignments of Indonesia's Islamic institutional landscape. What followed was a series of mass demonstrations without precedent in Indonesia's post-Reformasi history. The first major rally, on November 4, 2016, drew hundreds of thousands of participants to Jakarta; a second rally on November 25 mobilized further crowds; and on December 2, 2016 — the rally that gave the movement its popular name, "Aksi 212" (the 212 Action, after the date 2/12) — an estimated 200,000 to over a million demonstrators gathered at the National Monument (Monas) in central Jakarta for combined Friday prayers and political protest, in an event that organizers framed as Aksi Bela Islam (Action in Defense of Islam).

Sidney Jones of the Institute for Policy Analysis of Conflict (IPAC), in her authoritative analysis After Ahok: The Islamist Agenda in Indonesia (IPAC Report No. 44, 2018), characterized the 212 movement as a coalition with heterogeneous composition and divergent agendas. The FPI provided street mobilization capacity; Salafi networks organized through the Indonesian Ulema Forum contributed ideological coherence; and Islamist political parties including the Prosperous Justice Party (PKS) offered organizational infrastructure.13 Jones identified two principal strategic orientations within the Islamist coalition: one, represented by figures like Bachtiar Nasir, oriented toward long-term social transformation through education and media penetration; the other, represented by the FPI's Rizieq Shihab, oriented toward immediate political gains through street pressure. Jones's central conclusion — that "Islamists have shown they can mobilise huge numbers of demonstrators if they can persuade the mainstream that their faith is under attack" — identified the key innovation of the 212 episode: the successful reframing of a gubernatorial electoral contest as a religious emergency requiring popular defense.

The legal proceedings against Ahok were expedited by what critics described as political pressure. He was formally charged with blasphemy in November 2016, tried before a South Jakarta district court in early 2017, and convicted on May 9, 2017 — two days after losing the second round of the gubernatorial election to Anies Baswedan, who had explicitly courted Islamic conservative support throughout the campaign. The court sentenced Ahok to two years of imprisonment, a sentence that exceeded the one-year suspended term prosecutors had requested, in a ruling observers widely characterized as reflecting the court's sensitivity to mass political pressure. Ahok served his sentence and was released in January 2019. International human rights organizations, including Human Rights Watch, condemned both the prosecution and the sentence as a capitulation of the justice system to religious mob pressure.12

The reverberations of the 212 affair extended far beyond Ahok's individual fate. Jones documented how the Islamist coalition that formed around the blasphemy case subsequently attempted to reshape the 2019 presidential election, mobilizing opposition to Joko Widodo under accusations of insufficient Islamic credentials. Jokowi responded by selecting Ma'ruf Amin — the same senior NU figure who had signed the MUI blasphemy fatwa against Ahok — as his running mate, in a calculated appeal to Islamic legitimacy that illustrated how thoroughly the 212 episode had altered the calculus of electoral politics. Jokowi won re-election, but the episode demonstrated that even an incumbent president with a strong economic record felt compelled to make significant symbolic concessions to religious conservative constituencies.13

Terrorism, Counterterrorism, and De-radicalization

The most lethal expression of Islamist radicalism in post-Reformasi Indonesia was the emergence of Jemaah Islamiyah (JI), a transnational jihadist network founded on January 1, 1993 in Malaysia by Abdullah Sungkar and Abu Bakar Ba'asyir following their flight from Indonesian security services. JI was structured as a hierarchical organization divided into four territorial units (Mantiqi), spanning Indonesia, Malaysia, Singapore, the Philippines, and Australia, with training facilities in Afghanistan and direct connections to Al-Qaeda. The International Crisis Group, in its foundational analysis of the network published in December 2002, documented JI's organizational structure, its financing through commercial enterprises and charitable organizations, and its operational doctrine of "strategic patience" — building a network over years before initiating spectacular violence designed to polarize Muslim and non-Muslim populations and precipitate an Islamic revolutionary situation.14

On October 12, 2002, JI operatives detonated three coordinated bombs at tourist venues in Kuta, Bali — in the Sari Club, Paddy's Irish Bar, and near the U.S. Consulate. The attacks killed 202 people, including 88 Australians and numerous other foreign nationals, making them the deadliest terrorist attack in Indonesian history and the largest single casualty event attributable to Al-Qaeda's regional network outside the United States. The Indonesian government, which had previously been reluctant to acknowledge the existence of JI on its soil, was compelled by the scale of the atrocity to launch an aggressive counterterrorism response. Detachment 88 (Densus 88) — an elite counterterrorism unit trained and equipped with American and Australian support — was established within the Indonesian National Police in 2003 and rapidly became the primary instrument of JI's operational dismemberment. Over the following years, Densus 88 killed or captured the key operational planners of the Bali bombings, including Amrozi, Ali Ghufron, and Imam Samudra (all executed in November 2008), and progressively dismantled JI's command structure. The National Counterterrorism Agency (BNPT) was established in 2010 to coordinate counterterrorism across government agencies and to oversee deradicalization programs.

JI's organizational resilience nonetheless surprised many analysts. As the network's operational capacity was degraded, it underwent an internal ideological revision, pivoting away from spectacular attacks toward a long-term strategy of social penetration: establishing schools, businesses, and community organizations, and positioning members within civil society institutions, government offices, and even the military. RSIS analysts documented this shift in 2022, noting that JI's capacity for social infiltration posed a different kind of challenge from its previous operational terrorism — one less amenable to the arrest-and-prosecution responses that had proved effective against violent cells, but potentially more corrosive to democratic institutions over the long term.14 On June 30, 2024, JI's leadership formally dissolved the organization — an extraordinary development attributed to years of sustained counterterrorism pressure and to an internal theological revision that concluded that the organization's jihadi strategy was producing harm to Indonesian Muslims without advancing its stated goals.

A second and distinct threat emerged in the mid-2010s with the establishment of Jamaah Ansharut Daulah (JAD), formed in 2015 as an umbrella coalition of at least 24 Indonesian groups pledging allegiance to the Islamic State (IS) caliphate under Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi. JAD, under the ideological leadership of Oman Rochman (also known as Aman Abdurrahman), was responsible for nearly all significant terrorist attacks in Indonesia between 2016 and 2018, including the January 2016 attack near Sarinah shopping district in Jakarta (two killed, twenty-five injured) and the devastating May 2018 triple church bombings in Surabaya that killed at least thirteen people and wounded forty. The South Jakarta District Court banned JAD as a proscribed organization in July 2018. The UN Security Council designated JAD as a terrorist entity linked to IS.

The effectiveness of Indonesia's deradicalization programs has been disputed by independent analysts. The Institute for Policy Analysis of Conflict, in a 2020 report documenting patterns of recidivism among released terrorism convicts, found that 94 of 825 individuals released between 2002 and May 2020 had committed second terrorism offenses — a recidivism rate of approximately 11.4 percent.15 IPAC concluded that the state's deradicalization programs were "largely ineffective," primarily because they focused on individuals who had already autonomously decided to disengage from extremism, while failing to reach those still committed to jihadist ideology. The organization of deradicalization around dialogue and material incentives, without sustained theological counter-engagement, was identified as a structural weakness.

Pressures on Religious Minorities: Ahmadiyah, Shia, Christians, and Penghayat

The formal pluralism of the Pancasila framework has not consistently shielded Indonesia's religious minorities from legal discrimination, mob violence, and state-facilitated persecution. Human Rights Watch's landmark 2013 report In Religion's Name: Abuses against Religious Minorities in Indonesia, based on research conducted across ten provinces and 115 interviews between 2011 and 2012, documented a systematic pattern in which state actors — police, local government officials, and court systems — either directly participated in discrimination against minority communities or failed to provide protection against private violence.16

The Ahmadiyah community, estimated at between 200,000 and 400,000 members in Indonesia, has faced the most sustained official persecution of any religious minority. The Ahmadiyah movement, founded in Punjab in 1889 by Mirza Ghulam Ahmad, holds beliefs about prophecy that mainstream Sunni scholars regard as incompatible with Islamic orthodoxy. The MUI issued its first fatwa against Ahmadiyah in 1980, declaring it outside the bounds of Islam; a renewed fatwa in 2005 intensified official pressure. On June 9, 2008, three government ministers signed the Joint Ministerial Decree (SKB) on Ahmadiyah, ordering the community to "stop spreading interpretations and activities which deviate from the principal teachings of Islam" and threatening up to five years imprisonment for violations. Human Rights Watch called on the government to reverse the decree, noting that it followed a June 1, 2008 attack by more than 500 Islamist militants on approximately 100 Ahmadi supporters at Jakarta's National Monument in which more than sixty people were injured, with police present but largely passive.17 The 2008 decree produced a cascade of provincial and district-level bans: HRW documented that since the decree, at least 50 Ahmadiyah mosques had been vandalized, 36 mosques and gathering places had been forcibly closed, and three Ahmadiyah followers had been killed in mob violence. Reported attacks against Ahmadiyah rose from three in 2006 to 114 by 2011.16

Indonesia's Shia Muslim community — estimated at between 2 and 2.5 million, concentrated in East Java, Madura, South Kalimantan, and Jakarta — has also faced intensifying hostility. The MUI issued a 1984 caution against Shia teachings that, while not a formal fatwa of heresy, effectively flagged Shia as religiously suspect. The most violent episode in the contemporary period occurred in August 2012 in Sampang regency on the island of Madura, where hundreds of Sunni militants burned approximately fifty Shia homes and killed one man while police officers stood by; an earlier December 2011 attack on the same community had displaced approximately 500 residents. The Sampang district court subsequently convicted Shia cleric Tajul Muluk — not the attackers — on blasphemy charges for his religious teachings, sentencing him to two years (later raised to four years on appeal). An estimated 165 Shia community members, including 48 children, were still living in an inadequate sports complex in Sampang years later, unable to return to their burned homes. The U.S. State Department's 2017 religious freedom report noted that local authorities consistently failed to facilitate the community's return, reflecting a pattern of official complicity in religious displacement.18

Christian communities, constituting approximately 10 percent of Indonesia's population, face a different but related pattern of pressure centered primarily on the construction and operation of houses of worship. The 2006 Joint Ministerial Regulation No. 9/2006 governing the construction of houses of worship requires, among other conditions, that at least 90 signatures of local residents from outside the petitioning congregation be obtained before a building permit can be issued — a requirement that effectively grants majority communities a veto over minority religious construction. The most internationally prominent case was that of the Gereja Kristen Indonesia (GKI) Yasmin congregation in Bogor, which obtained a building permit in 2006, had it revoked in 2008 following resistance from local Muslim residents who alleged forged signatures, and won a series of court victories culminating in a 2010 Supreme Court order that the permit be reinstated — an order the Bogor city government defied for over a decade. As Alamsyah Djafar and A'an Suryana documented in their 2023 ISEAS Perspective analysis, the congregation held services on Jakarta sidewalks and outside the presidential palace for years as a form of protest, and the dispute was only resolved in 2023 through a relocation compromise that critics, including Djafar and Suryana themselves, characterized as fundamentally inadequate: "This approach of applying the majoritarian principle is not ideal, as it infringes on the rights of minorities to practise their faiths."20 HRW data indicate that between 1945 and 2005, 978 construction disputes involving minority houses of worship were documented in Indonesia, with more than half occurring after the democratic transition of 1998.

The most positive development for religious minorities in the Reformasi era came on November 7, 2017, when the Constitutional Court issued its ruling in Case No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016. The case challenged two provisions of the Population Administration Law that required citizens either to list one of the six recognized religions on their national identity card or to leave the religion column blank. The Constitutional Court ruled that these provisions violated the constitutional rights of adherents of indigenous belief systems (penghayat kepercayaan) — an umbrella category covering an estimated 12 to 20 million Indonesians who practice various traditional and syncretic belief systems, particularly in Java, Kalimantan, and Papua. The Court ordered the Civil Registration Office to print "Penghayat Kepercayaan" in the religion column on the KTP for those who requested it. The Library of Congress's Global Legal Monitor, which documented the ruling, reported that the Ministry of Home Affairs received 138,791 requests from individuals wishing to register as penghayat within days of the decision.19 Implementation remained uneven, with local civil registration offices frequently unfamiliar with the new requirements, but the ruling represented a significant judicial acknowledgment that Indonesia's pluralist constitution extended beyond its six officially recognized religions.

Penghayat kepercayaan — Indonesian term for "practitioners of belief," referring to adherents of indigenous, traditional, and syncretic belief systems outside the six state-recognized religions. An estimated 12–20 million Indonesians practice various forms of penghayat kepercayaan, particularly in Java (Kejawen), Kalimantan, and Papua. Before the Constitutional Court's November 2017 ruling in Case No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016, penghayat adherents were required to leave the religion column of their national identity card blank, denying them access to services tied to religious registration and exposing them to social discrimination.

Pluralism under Pressure: Debates, Elections, and Future Trajectories

The tensions documented in the preceding sections — between Pancasila's pluralist aspirations and the discriminatory architecture of the blasphemy law; between the moderation of NU and Muhammadiyah and the mobilizing power of Islamist street politics; between constitutional guarantees of religious freedom and the practical vulnerability of minorities — constitute the central fault lines of contemporary Indonesian religious politics. Scholars and analysts disagree significantly about the long-term trajectory of these tensions.

The Setara Institute, one of Indonesia's most prominent civil society organizations monitoring religious freedom, has published annual reports on religious intolerance incidents since 2008. Its data documents significant year-to-year variation in the incidence of violations: the organization recorded 333 incidents in 2022, declining to 230 in 2023, 260 in 2024, and 221 in 2025, with approximately half in each year attributable to state actors including police, local governments, and courts rather than to private vigilante groups.22 The Wahid Foundation, established by the daughter of former President Abdurrahman Wahid, has conducted parallel monitoring and periodically published tolerance indices assessing public attitudes toward religious others across Indonesian provinces. These datasets provide a quantitative complement to the qualitative case studies, suggesting that while religious intolerance remains endemic and structurally enabled by law, it has not uniformly worsened over the post-Reformasi period and fluctuates substantially with political context.

The electoral landscape has become an increasingly critical arena for religious politics. The 2016–17 Ahok episode demonstrated the potential of religious mobilization to displace an otherwise popular incumbent executive, and subsequent elections have been deeply marked by the question of which candidates and parties can credibly claim Islamic legitimacy. The 2019 presidential election saw Jokowi win re-election partly by co-opting the Islamic establishment through his VP selection, while Prabowo Subianto — his challenger, a former special forces general with a controversial human rights record — cultivated the Islamist political parties that had formed the core of the 212 coalition. The 2024 presidential election, in which Prabowo won outright in the first round with over 58 percent of the vote, taking Jokowi's eldest son Gibran Rakabuming Raka as his running mate, introduced a new dynamic: the Islamist coalition that had opposed Jokowi in 2019 splintered, with significant elements backing Prabowo while others supported rival candidates. The U.S. State Department's 2023 religious freedom report noted that the political instrumentalization of religion remained a significant structural feature of Indonesian electoral competition, with candidates routinely competing for endorsements from religious organizations and fatwas from the MUI.21

The role of NU and Muhammadiyah in this landscape is ambivalent and contested. Both organizations have formally maintained their historic positions opposing an Islamic state and supporting Pancasila democracy, and both contain significant constituencies committed to genuine religious pluralism. NU under the leadership of Yahya Cholil Staquf (Gus Yahya), who became the organization's general secretary and subsequently its chairman, has pursued an ambitious international agenda — articulating Islam Nusantara as a counter-model to global Salafism and engaging with Jewish, Christian, and other religious communities in direct dialogue. But NU's internal diversity includes conservative factions that share much of the MUI's suspicion of minorities, and the organization's signing of the anti-Ahok fatwa by one of its most senior leaders illustrated the limits of institutional moderation when electoral calculation intervenes. Muhammadiyah, despite its modernist orientation, has similarly produced significant constituencies that support stricter Islamic regulation in public life and that have been reluctant to defend minority rights when doing so conflicts with intra-Muslim solidarity.

The scholarly debate over Indonesia's pluralist future broadly divides between optimists who emphasize the structural resilience of Pancasila, the organizational depth of NU and Muhammadiyah as moderating forces, and the demonstrated capacity of the democratic system to contain Islamist political ambitions, and pessimists who point to the progressive erosion of legal protections for minorities, the normalization of blasphemy prosecutions as electoral weapons, and the structural inability of the state to enforce court orders protecting minority rights against majority community resistance. Robert Hefner, surveying the landscape from the perspective of two decades of research, has argued that Indonesia's "civil Islam" — the tradition of pluralist Muslim politics represented by the founding generation of NU and Muhammadiyah leaders — remains alive but embattled, requiring active civic engagement and institutional investment to survive the populist religious pressures of the democratic era.2 Human Rights Watch, reviewing developments through 2024, noted that significant legal reforms to religious freedom remained stalled, but that judicial decisions like the 2017 penghayat ruling offered evidence of the constitutional system's capacity for self-correction when organized civil society pressed its claims through legal channels.23

The comparison with other large Muslim-majority democracies is instructive, if necessarily imperfect. Turkey's trajectory from Kemalist secularism toward Islamist governance under the AKP has been cited both as a warning of what "democratic erosion through religious mobilization" can look like and as a reminder that the trajectory is not inevitable. Pakistan's experience with blasphemy prosecutions as instruments of social control offers a sobering parallel to Indonesia's own blasphemy law dynamics. Conversely, Tunisia's post-Arab Spring democratic experiment — until its reversal in 2021 — provided evidence that Muslim-majority societies can sustain pluralist constitutionalism under pressure. Indonesia's scale, diversity, federal structure, and the organizational depth of its moderate Islamic institutions continue to distinguish it analytically from most comparanda. Whether those distinguishing features prove sufficient to sustain a pluralist democratic republic in which all religious communities — recognized and unrecognized, majority and minority — enjoy genuine equality before the law remains, as of the mid-2020s, an open question.

212 movement (Aksi Bela Islam) — the series of mass demonstrations organized in Jakarta between November 2016 and December 2, 2016 (the "212" date), primarily by a coalition of Islamist groups including the Front Pembela Islam, demanding the blasphemy prosecution of Jakarta Governor Basuki Tjahaja Purnama (Ahok). The December 2 rally at Monas drew between 200,000 and over a million participants. Sidney Jones (IPAC) described the movement as demonstrating Islamists' capacity to mobilize mass action "when they can persuade the mainstream that their faith is under attack." The movement's political infrastructure was subsequently deployed in the 2019 presidential election and became a reference point for debates about the limits of Indonesia's pluralist democratic consensus.
Densus 88 (Detachment 88) — Indonesia's elite national police counterterrorism unit, established in 2003 following the 2002 Bali bombings with technical and financial support from the United States and Australia. Densus 88 was primarily responsible for dismantling Jemaah Islamiyah's operational network through targeted arrests and, in some cases, lethal operations against bomb makers and planners. Its interrogation methods and some lethal operations have attracted human rights concerns, while its success in reducing large-scale attacks has been widely credited by counterterrorism analysts. The unit evolved its approach from conventional law enforcement to include deradicalization dialogue programs, establishing the Rumah Wasathiyah (House of Moderation) jointly with former JI members in 2025.

Chapter Summary

Indonesia's position as the world's most populous Muslim-majority country does not produce a monolithic Islamic politics: rather, it generates a field of contestation among traditionalist, modernist, conservative, and Islamist tendencies, all operating within the constitutional framework of Pancasila and its six recognized religions. The two great mass organizations, Nahdlatul Ulama and Muhammadiyah, represent the organizational backbone of moderate Indonesian Islam, with combined constituencies of over 100 million members and deep roots in education, healthcare, and social welfare. NU's concept of Islam Nusantara — the historically grounded synthesis of Islamic teaching with archipelago cultural pluralism — has emerged as Indonesia's most significant contribution to contemporary global Islamic thought.

The post-Reformasi period has simultaneously expanded democratic freedoms and enabled conservative religious mobilization, producing sharia-influenced regional regulations across dozens of Indonesian districts, the growth and eventual banning of the vigilante FPI, and the maintenance of a blasphemy law upheld by the Constitutional Court in 2010 that systematically disadvantages religious minorities. Aceh's full sharia implementation, analyzed by R. Michael Feener as a project of "social engineering" that has achieved more symbolic than programmatic success, represents the furthest extension of this conservative impulse within Indonesia's federal structure. The 2016–17 Ahok affair — in which a Chinese-Christian governor was prosecuted for blasphemy and imprisoned following massive Islamist demonstrations — demonstrated the electoral potency of religious identity politics and reshaped Indonesian democratic competition.

Religious minorities — Ahmadiyah, Shia, Christians, and penghayat — have all experienced significant pressures ranging from mob violence to legal discrimination, state complicity in displacement, and denial of building permits and identity registration. The November 2017 Constitutional Court ruling recognizing penghayat belief on identity cards represented a significant judicial corrective, but implementation gaps persist. Indonesia's pluralist future depends on the continued organizational vitality of NU, Muhammadiyah, and civil society monitors like the Setara Institute and Wahid Foundation, on the capacity of the constitutional system to enforce minority rights against majoritarian pressure, and on the degree to which electoral competition can be insulated from the weaponization of religious identity.
