This glossary brings together specialist terms from across all chapters of the book. Foreign-language words — Javanese, Malay, Dutch, Sanskrit, Arabic, and others — are italicised on their first appearance in each chapter and defined here in their full context. Institutional abbreviations, colonial-administrative vocabulary, and key proper nouns are included alongside cultural and religious vocabulary. Where a term is treated at length in a specific chapter that is noted in the definition.

A Note on Spelling and Transliteration

This book follows modern Indonesian and standard scholarly English spellings throughout. Older colonial-era Dutch orthography appears only in direct quotations from primary sources and is glossed on its first occurrence.

Personal and dynastic names. Sukarno and Suharto replace the colonial-era spellings Soekarno and Soeharto; readers will encounter the older forms in primary sources and some older secondary works. The maritime empire of Sumatra is spelled Srivijaya (not Sriwijaya or Srividjaja). The Central Javanese dynasty is Sailendra. Kingdom names follow standard modern usage: Majapahit, Singhasari (not Singosari), Kediri, Tarumanagara, and Kutai. The great temple complexes are Borobudur and Prambanan; the Majapahit capital site is Trowulan.

Place names. City names: Yogyakarta (informally Yogya), Surakarta (informally Solo), and Batavia for the colonial capital down to 1942, after which Jakarta is used. Ethnic groups named in the text follow standard modern forms: Javanese, Sundanese, Balinese, Minangkabau, Buginese, Acehnese, Batak, Dayak, Papuans.

Mataram disambiguation. Two separate polities share this name: the Hindu-Buddhist Mataram kingdom of central Java (c. 8th–10th centuries CE) and the Islamic Mataram Sultanate (c. 1587–1755). The text always disambiguates.

Nusantara disambiguation. Nusantara designates (a) the classical Malay concept of the archipelagic world of maritime Southeast Asia and (b) the new national capital under construction in East Kalimantan (authorized by law in 2022). Context always distinguishes the two uses.

Colonial entity vs. trading company. The Dutch East Indies and the Netherlands East Indies refer to the colonial state administered by the Dutch Crown; the VOC (Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie) refers specifically to the chartered trading company that preceded and partly overlapped with formal colonial rule (1602–1799).

Dates. All dates use the Common Era conventions: CE and BCE. Approximate dates are signalled by c. (from Latin circa), e.g., c. 800 CE. Centuries are written out in full: the seventh century.

Foreign terms. Javanese, Malay, Dutch, Sanskrit, Arabic, and other non-English terms are italicised on their first use in each chapter. In this glossary the headwords are given in roman type for ease of scanning; their foreign-language status is noted in the definition where relevant.

Glossary of Terms

212 movement
The series of mass demonstrations in Jakarta in November–December 2016, culminating in the 2 December rally at Monas (Aksi Bela Islam III, "Aksi 212"), organized primarily by Islamist groups to demand blasphemy prosecution of Jakarta Governor Ahok; one of the largest public demonstrations in Indonesian history, drawing an estimated 500,000–700,000 participants.
abangan
In Clifford Geertz's typology, nominally Muslim Javanese whose religious life blends Islamic observance with animist and Hindu-Buddhist elements, centred on the communal feast (slametan); contrasted with santri. Subsequent scholars, including Mark Woodward and Merle Ricklefs, have critiqued the category as a normative external label rather than a stable self-ascription.
ABRI
Angkatan Bersenjata Republik Indonesia — the Armed Forces of the Republic of Indonesia as constituted under the New Order, comprising army, navy, air force, and police; renamed Tentara Nasional Indonesia (TNI) in 1999 when the police (POLRI) were separated as part of Reformasi reforms.
adat
Customary law and tradition of local communities across the archipelago; coexisted with and was often in tension with Islamic law and colonial statute. Under Dutch colonial administration adat was codified selectively by ethnographers and applied through native courts.
Adat Aceh
The legal code of the Sultanate of Aceh, systematized under Sultan Iskandar Muda (r. 1607–1636), blending customary (adat) and Islamic jurisprudence and defining the respective powers of sultan, uleebalang chiefs, and ulama.
adipati
Javanese title for a regional governor or duke appointed by the central court; Sultan Agung of Mataram installed adipati across his expanded realm in the early seventeenth century. Roughly equivalent to the Makassarese title karaeng.
Age of Commerce
Anthony Reid's term for the period c. 1450–1680 in Southeast Asia, characterized by booming maritime trade, urban growth, Islamic and Christian conversion, gunpowder technology, and the dominance of trade-based city-states.
Agrarische Wet
(Dutch: Agrarian Law, 1870) Legislation that introduced the domein verklaring (domain declaration) declaring land not demonstrably under documented private or communal use to be state domain available for long-term lease, and opened the Netherlands East Indies to private European plantation capital. Together with the Suikerwet (Sugar Law, 1870) it inaugurated the Liberal Period of private enterprise.
agricultural involution
Clifford Geertz's concept (Agricultural Involution, 1963) for the process by which Javanese wet-rice agriculture absorbed a growing population through increasing social complexity and labour intensity rather than through technological change or capital accumulation — a condition of "shared poverty" Geertz attributed partly to colonial pressures. The thesis has been substantially critiqued and largely superseded empirically but remains an influential analytical framework.
Ahmadiyya
A Muslim religious movement founded in Punjab (British India) by Mirza Ghulam Ahmad in 1889; its theological position on prophethood is regarded by mainstream Sunni scholars as incompatible with Islamic orthodoxy. Approximately 200,000–400,000 adherents in Indonesia; subject to a 2008 Joint Ministerial Decree restricting activities and to periodic mob violence.
Algemeene Studieclub
General Study Club, a discussion group founded by Sukarno and colleagues in Bandung c. 1925–26; its journal Soeloeh Indonesia Moeda published Sukarno's 1926 synthesis essay "Nationalism, Islam and Marxism"; the club was reconstituted as the PNI in 1927.
AMM (Aceh Monitoring Mission)
Joint EU–ASEAN monitoring force of over 300 observers that oversaw implementation of the 2005 Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding between the Indonesian government and GAM; mandate expired December 2006 following successful Aceh provincial elections.
Angkatan '45
The "Generation of '45," the cohort of Indonesian writers who emerged around independence, characterized by individualism, free verse, and a rejection of Balai Pustaka conventions; led by the poet Chairil Anwar (1922–1949).
ANRI
Arsip Nasional Republik Indonesia (National Archives of the Republic of Indonesia, Jakarta) — the principal repository for Indonesian state records, including large volumes of colonial-era documents transferred from the Netherlands.
Asas tunggal
(Indonesian: "sole foundation") The New Order requirement, enacted in Law No. 8 of 1985, that all Indonesian social and political organisations adopt Pancasila as their only ideological basis, displacing Islamic or other ideological foundations; resisted especially by Islamic organisations.
ASEAN centrality
The principle that ASEAN and its associated frameworks should serve as the primary platforms through which great powers engage Southeast Asia, preventing any single power from dominating the region; a cornerstone of Indonesian and regional foreign policy since the 1990s.
Atjèh-moord
(Dutch: "Aceh murder") The term used by Dutch colonial forces for individual Acehnese suicide attacks against Dutch soldiers, religiously motivated by the perang sabil doctrine; a distinctive and deeply unsettling feature of the guerrilla phase of the Aceh War (post-c. 1881).
Babad Diponegoro
Javanese autobiographical chronicle dictated by Prince Diponegoro during his exile in Manado, 1831–1832; approximately 1,150 pages in Javanese verse in pegon (Arabic) script. The most important primary source for the Java War from the Javanese perspective; inscribed as a UNESCO Memory of the World document in 2013.
babad
A Javanese court chronicle, typically composed in verse (tembang), recounting dynastic history as a blend of genealogy, mythology, cosmological legitimation, and occasional documentary detail. The most influential is the Babad Tanah Jawi ("Chronicle of the Land of Java").
Bahasa Indonesia
The standardized national language of Indonesia, declared the "language of unity" in the 1928 Sumpah Pemuda; based on an elevated form of trade Malay (Melayu dagang), chosen over Javanese for its supraethnic, cross-archipelago reach. Subsequently developed by the Indonesian state into a full literary and administrative language.
Balai Pustaka
The Dutch colonial government's official publishing house (founded 1917 as Commissie voor de Volkslectuur), simultaneously a censorship mechanism — approving only texts deemed politically safe — and the institutional incubator of modern Indonesian-language prose fiction.
Baperki
Badan Permusyawaratan Kewarganegaraan Indonesia (Consultative Body on Indonesian Citizenship), founded 1954; the main Chinese-Indonesian political organisation under Sukarno, advocating integration as full citizens rather than assimilation; banned and destroyed after the October 1965 coup.
Bapak Pembangunan
(Indonesian: "Father of Development") Honorary title conferred on Suharto by the MPR in 1982; the focal point of a personality cult centred on developmental achievement and national stability, a counterpoint to Sukarno's revolutionary charisma.
batik
Wax-resist textile dyeing technique applied to cotton or silk, in which hot wax is drawn with a canting pen (or stamped with a cap block) to resist the dye; the resulting pattern is revealed when wax is removed. Central to Javanese ceremonial life from birth to death rites; UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2009.
batig slot
(Dutch: "credit balance" / "favourable balance") The annual surplus of colonial revenues over colonial expenditures remitted from the Netherlands East Indies treasury to the Dutch state. Between 1831 and 1877 the cumulative batig slot totalled approximately 823 million guilders; at its peak in the 1850s it financed roughly half of Dutch government expenditure and funded the construction of Dutch railways. The concept became central to the Ethical Policy debate.
Batavia
Fortified Dutch colonial city founded 30 May 1619 by Jan Pieterszoon Coen on the ruins of the Javanese port of Jayakarta; served as the VOC's Asian headquarters and later the capital of the Netherlands East Indies until renamed Jakarta under Japanese occupation in 1942.
bebas-aktif
(Indonesian: "free and active") Indonesia's foundational foreign policy doctrine — non-alignment with great-power blocs combined with proactive international engagement — first articulated by Vice-President Mohammad Hatta in 1948 and formalized in the 1950s; remains the official basis of Indonesian diplomacy.
Belt and Road Initiative (BRI)
President Xi Jinping's global infrastructure investment strategy (launched 2013), under which China funds large-scale transportation, energy, digital, and manufacturing projects in partner countries, often through state-backed loans; a major frame for Sino-Indonesian economic relations from the 2010s.
bendahara
The chief minister or grand vizier of a Malay sultanate, combining administrative, judicial, and military authority; at Malacca the bendahara was the most powerful officer of state after the sultan (the most celebrated being Tun Perak, r. c. 1456–1498).
benteng stelsel
(Dutch: "fortress system") Counterinsurgency strategy adopted by General Hendrik Merkus de Kock from 1827 during the Java War: a dense network of small fortified posts positioned close enough for mutual support, linked by roads, with mobile columns operating between them to deny guerrillas territory, supply, and civilian cover. Applied subsequently in Aceh and other colonial campaigns.
Benteng programme
Indonesia's first affirmative-action economic initiative (early 1950s), reserving import licences and preferential credit for indigenous (pribumi) entrepreneurs to displace Dutch and Chinese commercial dominance; largely a vehicle for corruption and terminated as a policy failure by the late 1950s.
Berkeley Mafia
Informal name for the cohort of US-trained Indonesian economists — led by Widjojo Nitisastro and including Ali Wardhana, Emil Salim, and others — who served as Suharto's chief economic advisers from 1966, pursuing orthodox stabilisation, market-opening, and foreign-investment-friendly policies through Bappenas and the Ministry of Finance.
bewindhebbers
The managing directors of the VOC (between 60 and 76 in total), drawn from each of the company's six chambers; the Heeren XVII were elected from among them.
BIMAS
Bimbingan Massal (Mass Guidance) — the New Order's national rice intensification programme, distributing high-yielding seed varieties (notably IR-8 and later IR-36), subsidised fertiliser, pesticides, and supervised credit to farmers from the late 1960s; credited with enabling Indonesian rice self-sufficiency by 1984.
Binnenlands Bestuur
(Dutch: "Interior Administration") The Dutch colonial civil service that operated in parallel with the indigenous Pangreh Praja; staffed by European officials ranging from resident (residency level) down to controller (sub-district level).
BKKBN
Badan Koordinasi Keluarga Berencana Nasional — the National Family Planning Coordinating Board, established 1970; administered Indonesia's Keluarga Berencana programme and achieved one of the fastest fertility transitions in the developing world.
blasphemy law
Presidential Decree PNPS No. 1/1965 on the Prevention of Blasphemy and Abuse of Religions, prohibiting "deviant interpretations" of the six state-recognized religions; carries a maximum five-year prison sentence; upheld by the Constitutional Court in April 2010. Used to prosecute adherents of the Ahmadiyya, minority Muslims, and the Jakarta Governor Basuki Tjahaja Purnama (Ahok) in 2016–17.
BLBI
Bantuan Likuiditas Bank Indonesia (Bank Indonesia Liquidity Support) — emergency central-bank liquidity injections made to failing commercial banks after the November 1997 bank closures; became one of Indonesia's largest financial fraud scandals as funds were diverted by bank owners, totalling over Rp 140 trillion in losses.
blusukan
(Indonesian) An impromptu, unannounced inspection visit by a public official to an ordinary neighbourhood, market, or workplace; popularised as a governing style by Joko Widodo first as mayor of Solo, then as Jakarta governor, signalling accessibility and rejection of bureaucratic formality.
Boven Digul
Dutch colonial internment camp established in 1927 on the Digul River in the malaria-infested lowlands of southwestern Dutch New Guinea (now Papua, Indonesia); held approximately 1,300 political prisoners following the PKI-led uprisings of 1926–27, and later nationalist leaders including Hatta and Sjahrir (1934–36); a major instrument of colonial political repression through 1942.
BPUPK
Badan Penyelidik Usaha Persiapan Kemerdekaan (Investigating Committee for the Preparation of Independence) — the body convened by the Japanese military authorities on Java from 29 May 1945 to design the constitutional framework for Indonesian independence; its deliberations produced the first formulations of Pancasila and the draft 1945 Constitution.
BPS
Badan Pusat Statistik — Statistics Indonesia, the national statistical agency responsible for the census, household surveys, and national economic, demographic, and social data.
BRICS
Intergovernmental grouping originally comprising Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa (joined 2010); expanded at the 2023 Johannesburg summit to include several additional members; Indonesia joined as a full member in January 2025, becoming the first Southeast Asian country in the bloc.
Budi Utomo
(Javanese: "Noble Endeavour") Javanese cultural and social organisation founded at STOVIA medical school, Batavia, on 20 May 1908; conventionally treated as the symbolic start of the Indonesian national awakening, though its membership was initially limited to Javanese priyayi.
BULOG
Badan Urusan Logistik (State Logistics Agency) — the state body responsible for procuring, storing, and distributing strategic food commodities, especially rice; bought rice from farmers at a guaranteed floor price and sold to urban consumers at a subsidised ceiling price, thereby stabilising food prices under the New Order.
Bundo Kanduang
(Minangkabau: "real mother") A mature Minangkabau woman who holds ceremonial authority, custodianship of lineage property, and recognized communal voice; symbolizes the female centre of Minangkabau matrilineal society.
bupati
The Javanese "regent" who headed a regency (kabupaten) as the highest rank in the indigenous colonial administrative hierarchy (Pangreh Praja); typically hereditary in practice under Dutch rule; served as the critical intermediary between the Dutch resident and the Javanese population.
cabang atas
(Malay/Dutch colonial usage: "upper branch") The hereditary peranakan Chinese elite appointed as Chinese officers (kapitein, luitenant, majoor der Chinezen) in the colonial Indies; administered tax-farming contracts and brokered between Dutch capital and indigenous producers.
candi
Hindu-Buddhist temple monument of Java; the term derives from the death goddess Candika (an aspect of Durga). The great examples — Borobudur (c. 800 CE) and Prambanan (c. 850 CE) — date to the eighth and ninth centuries; hundreds of smaller candi dot the Javanese landscape.
cartaz
(Portuguese) A sea-pass issued by Portuguese port authorities to Asian merchant vessels, authorizing transit in Indian Ocean waters without interference; ships without a valid cartaz faced seizure. The primary enforcement mechanism of the Estado da India's maritime toll system.
casados
(Portuguese: "married men") The class of Portuguese settlers in Asia who had taken local wives with official encouragement; their Eurasian households conducted inter-Asian trade networks that extended Portuguese commercial reach far beyond what Crown ships could achieve. Their descendants formed creole communities across the Indian Ocean.
CAVR
Comissão de Acolhimento, Verdade e Reconciliação (Commission for Reception, Truth and Reconciliation) in East Timor; its 2005 final report, Chega! ("Stop!"), estimated at least 102,800 conflict-related deaths during the Indonesian occupation (1974–1999) and documented systematic human rights violations.
company-state
Scholarly term (associated with Adam Clulow, Philip J. Stern, and others) for early modern chartered trading companies — especially the VOC and the English East India Company — that exercised quasi-sovereign powers including war-making, treaty-making, coinage, and capital punishment, constituting an alternative sovereign formation alongside the emerging nation-state.
cukong
(from Hokkien: "boss") An ethnic-Chinese businessman who provides capital, commercial expertise, and management to a politically connected military officer or government official, receiving in return licences, concessions, and political protection. A structural role central to the New Order political economy, not merely a pejorative.
Cultuurgebied
(Dutch: "cultivation belt") The plantation zone of the east coast of Sumatra — principally the Deli residency of modern North Sumatra — premier tobacco-growing region from the 1860s and the primary site of the contract coolie labour system after 1870.
cultuurprocenten
(Dutch: "cultivation percentages") Commission payments made to Javanese priyayi officials under the Cultivation System, proportional to the volume of government export crops they extracted from their districts; structurally incentivised overextraction.
Cultuurstelsel
(Dutch: "Cultivation System"; Indonesian: tanam paksa, "forced planting") The colonial agricultural-taxation regime introduced on Java in 1830 under Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch, requiring villages to dedicate approximately one-fifth of their arable land (or 66 days of annual labour) to government-specified export crops — sugar, coffee, indigo — in lieu of a cash land tax. Dismantled between 1863 and 1870 following Dutch liberal agitation. Generated massive revenues for the Dutch treasury while causing periodic famine and severe hardship on Java.
dalang
The master puppeteer and sole performer of a wayang kulit performance, responsible for voicing all characters, narrating the story, cueing the gamelan orchestra, and sustaining performances from dusk to dawn. A dalang is as much philosopher and priest as entertainer.
Danantara
Daya Anagata Nusantara, a sovereign wealth fund established by the Prabowo government in February 2025 to consolidate and manage assets of Indonesian state-owned enterprises; initial estimated assets under management approximately US$900 billion.
dangdut
Indonesia's most widely popular contemporary music genre, crystallized in the early 1970s from Malay orkes melayu, North Indian film music, and rock; associated with urban migrant workers; became increasingly Islamicised through the career of Rhoma Irama from the late 1970s.
Dar al-Islam
(Arabic: "the abode of Islam") The lands governed under Muslim rule; the Moroccan traveller Ibn Battuta regarded the Sumatran port of Samudra Pasai as its easternmost edge when he visited in 1345.
daulat
The sacral, quasi-supernatural sovereign power believed to inhere in a legitimate Malay ruler; its violation through treason (derhaka) was understood as cosmically dangerous, making loyalty to the ruler a religious and cosmic duty as well as a political one.
Demokrasi Terpimpin
(Indonesian: "Guided Democracy") The political system proclaimed by Sukarno following his decree of 5 July 1959 dissolving the Konstituante and restoring the 1945 Constitution; concentrated executive power in the president and governed through the informal coalition of the army, the PKI, and Sukarno himself. Lasted until 1966–67.
demographic dividend
The period in a country's development when the working-age population (roughly 15–64 years) is large relative to dependants (children and elderly), creating favourable conditions for accelerated economic growth if accompanied by productive employment, education, and health investment. Indonesia's dividend is expected to peak in the 2030s.
Densus 88
Detasemen Khusus 88 (Special Detachment 88) — Indonesia's elite National Police counterterrorism unit, established in June 2003 following the 2002 Bali bombings with US and Australian assistance; named for the 88 Australian victims of those bombings; primary instrument for dismantling the Jemaah Islamiyah network.
derhaka
(Malay) Treason or rebellion against a legitimate Malay ruler, conceived not merely as a political crime but as a violation of the cosmic order that the ruler's daulat would itself punish.
Dharma Wanita
(Indonesian: "Women's Duty/Service") New Order state organisation established in 1974 for wives of male civil servants; its internal hierarchy mirrored the civil-service rank of members' husbands; a central instrument of State Ibuism and the appendage status assigned to women under New Order gender ideology.
Discipline clause (disiplin partai)
The rule adopted by the Fifth Central Sarekat Islam Congress in October 1921 requiring SI members to hold no other party membership, effectively expelling PKI members from the central organisation. A pivotal rupture in Indonesian political history that split Islamic and communist wings of the nationalist movement.
DOM
Daerah Operasi Militer (Military Operations Zone) — a legal designation conferring extraordinary counterinsurgency powers on the Indonesian military and suspending civilian oversight; applied to Aceh from 1989 to 1998, during which period extensive human rights abuses were documented.
domein verklaring
(Dutch: "domain declaration") The clause in the 1870 Agrarische Wet declaring all land not demonstrably under documented private or communal use to be state domain, available for lease to private European plantation companies for up to 75 years; dispossessed many village communities of communally used forests and upland fields.
DPD (Dewan Perwakilan Daerah)
Regional Representative Council, created by the 2002 constitutional amendment; 128 elected members (four per province); empowered to submit legislation relating to regional affairs but unable to independently pass bills — Indonesia's asymmetric upper chamber.
DPR (Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat)
People's Representative Council — Indonesia's lower house of parliament; under the amended 1945 Constitution, directly elected by proportional representation; the primary legislative body.
dual economy
J.H. Boeke's concept (1910 dissertation; 1953 book) of colonial Indonesia as containing two mutually incompatible economic systems: a Western capitalist sector (plantations, mining, trading houses) governed by classical economic laws, and a pre-capitalistic indigenous sector operating under different social logic. Critics including Benjamin Higgins (1956) argued the divide was structural and technological rather than cultural.
Dwifungsi
(Indonesian: "dual function") New Order doctrine formalising the Indonesian military's simultaneous roles in national defence and sociopolitical management of the state; institutionalised through guaranteed military seats in parliament and the bureaucracy; formally abolished under Reformasi.
Dwikora
(Indonesian: "People's Dual Command") Sukarno's May 1964 mobilisation proclamation escalating the Konfrontasi against Malaysia, calling for Indonesian volunteers to "crush Malaysia" (Ganyang Malaysia).
emporium (port polity)
A coastal state whose political power and revenue rested on brokering and taxing seaborne trade rather than on agricultural production or territorial control. The dominant political form in the Indonesian archipelago from Srivijaya (7th–11th centuries) through the sultanate era; termed thalassocracy when naval power was central.
Empat Serangkai
(Javanese: "four-in-a-cluster") The four Indonesian nationalist leaders who headed the Japanese-sponsored Putera organisation (1943–44): Sukarno, Mohammad Hatta, Ki Hajar Dewantara, and Kyai Haji Mas Mansur.
entrepôt
A port that thrives by gathering, warehousing, and redistributing the goods of others rather than producing its own staple export; the classic Southeast Asian example was Malacca (c. 1400–1511), subsequently Batavia under the VOC.
ereschuld
(Dutch: "debt of honour") Concept advanced by Conrad van Deventer in his 1899 essay "Een Eereschuld," arguing the Netherlands owed the Indies compensation for the wealth extracted through the batig slot; underpinned the Ethical Policy of 1901.
Estado da India
(Portuguese: "State of India") The Portuguese Crown's maritime empire in Asia, formally administered from Goa from 1505 to 1961; centred on a network of fortified trading posts (feitorias) from East Africa to Japan, combining licensed trade (cartaz fees) with naval coercion; displaced from most of the Indonesian archipelago by the VOC after 1605.
Ethische Politiek
(Dutch: "Ethical Policy") Colonial development programme proclaimed by Queen Wilhelmina in her throne speech of September 1901, committing the colonial state to three pillars — irrigatie (irrigation), emigratie (transmigration), and onderwijs (education) — for indigenous welfare. Its expansion of Western schooling created the educated elite that challenged colonial rule.
extirpatie
(Dutch: "extirpation") VOC policy of systematically destroying clove and nutmeg trees on non-designated islands to enforce its spice monopoly and concentrate production on Ambon and the Banda Islands; devastated Maluku communities and drove the annual hongi-tochten expeditions.
Falintil
Forças Armadas de Libertação Nacional de Timor-Leste (Armed Forces of the National Liberation of East Timor) — the armed wing of the East Timorese resistance, originally Fretilin's military branch; led by Xanana Gusmão from the early 1980s; demobilised after independence and replaced by the Timor-Leste Defence Force (F-FDTL).
feitoria
(Portuguese: "factory") A Portuguese fortified trading post in an Asian or African port, staffed by a Crown-appointed feitor (factor) who collected customs duties, issued trade licences, and enforced the cartaz system; the commercial node of the Estado da India network.
FOLU Net Sink
Indonesia's Forestry and Other Land Use Net Sink 2030 target, formalised under Presidential Regulation No. 98/2021, aiming for net negative greenhouse-gas emissions of 140 MtCO₂e from the land-use sector by 2030 as part of Indonesia's nationally determined contribution under the Paris Agreement.
Fretilin
Frente Revolucionária de Timor-Leste Independente (Revolutionary Front for an Independent East Timor) — the principal East Timorese independence movement, founded in 1974; declared a short-lived provisional government in November 1975; led the armed and diplomatic resistance throughout Indonesian occupation (1975–1999); remains a major political party in independent Timor-Leste.
GAM (Gerakan Aceh Merdeka)
Free Aceh Movement, founded 4 December 1976 by Hasan Muhammad di Tiro; fought an intermittent insurgency for Acehnese independence against Indonesian forces. Signed the Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding on 15 August 2005, accepting self-government within Indonesia, and transformed into the political party Partai Aceh.
gamelan
Traditional Indonesian bronze percussion orchestra, consisting primarily of gongs, metallophones (saron, gender), and drums, supplemented by flute (suling), bowed fiddle (rebab), and zither (siter). Central to Javanese and Balinese court ceremony, wayang performance, and ritual life. UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2021.
gelijkstelling
(Dutch: "equalisation") The legal mechanism by which an indigenous person in the Dutch East Indies could petition to be reclassified under European civil law, typically granted on grounds of Christian conversion or adoption of a European lifestyle; placed the individual in the "European" legal category for civil matters.
Gerindra
Partai Gerakan Indonesia Raya (Greater Indonesia Movement Party), the nationalist political party founded by Prabowo Subianto in 2008; through Gerindra Prabowo contested the 2014, 2019, and 2024 presidential elections, winning the last.
Gerwani
Gerakan Wanita Indonesia (Indonesian Women's Movement), founded 1950; by the mid-1960s the largest women's organisation outside the communist bloc; banned and destroyed after October 1965, its members targeted by fabricated military propaganda accusing them of mutilating the generals killed on the night of 30 September–1 October.
Golkar
Golongan Karya ("Functional Groups") — the New Order's electoral vehicle, winning between 62 and 75 percent of the vote in all six general elections from 1971 to 1997; technically a "social organisation" rather than a political party; has continued as a party under Reformasi.
gunseikan
(Japanese: "military administrator") Chief of the Japanese military administration in an occupied territory; in Java the post was held by the Sixteenth Army commander and the military administration governed through the gunseikan and his apparatus.
Hari Ibu
(Indonesian: "Mother's Day") National holiday observed on 22 December, established to commemorate the First Indonesian Women's Congress convened in Yogyakarta from 22 to 25 December 1928, which founded the national women's movement.
Hari Kebangkitan Nasional
(Indonesian: "National Awakening Day") National holiday observed on 20 May, marking the 1908 founding of Budi Utomo; the date was deliberately invoked by student demonstrators on 20 May 1998, two days before Suharto's resignation.
Hari Pahlawan
(Indonesian: "Heroes' Day") National holiday observed on 10 November, commemorating the Battle of Surabaya (1945), in which Indonesian fighters — overwhelmingly poorly armed youth — held off British and Indian troops for three weeks; a symbol of the revolution's capacity for mass armed resistance.
Heeren XVII (Heren XVII)
(Dutch: "Lords Seventeen") The central governing board of the VOC, drawn from the bewindhebbers of the company's six chambers; Amsterdam held 8 of 17 seats, Zeeland 4, the smaller chambers 1 each, and the 17th seat rotated. Met two or three times per year to set company policy.
hikayat
Classical Malay prose narrative genre encompassing heroic romances, royal chronicles, and miracle tales; the pre-eminent literary form of the Malay-Islamic maritime world. The oldest surviving Malay chronicle is the Hikayat Raja-Raja Pasai.
Hikayat Perang Sabil
(Acehnese: "Ballad of the Holy War") Acehnese-language text composed by Teuku Chik Pante Kulu; deployed by ulama as a mobilising nightly recitation in village prayer houses (meunasah) throughout the Aceh War; promised paradise to fighters and damnation to collaborators.
hilirisasi
(Indonesian: "downstreaming") Government policy of prohibiting or taxing exports of raw mineral commodities to force foreign investors to establish domestic processing industries and capture value-added rents within Indonesia; most prominently applied to nickel ore (export ban from January 2020) and bauxite (June 2023).
HKBP
Huria Kristen Batak Protestan (Batak Christian Protestant Church), founded from the Rhenish Mission work of Ludwig Ingwer Nommensen in the Toba Batak highlands of North Sumatra from the 1860s; one of the world's largest Lutheran churches with approximately four million members.
hongi-tochten
(Dutch, from Ternatan hongi: "armada") Annual VOC extirpation expeditions in the Moluccas, using kora-kora war canoes to destroy clove trees on non-designated islands and punish trade with non-Dutch merchants; a recurring instrument of terror enforcing the spice monopoly.
HPH
Hak Pengusahaan Hutan (Forestry Exploitation Rights) — a logging concession right granted by Indonesia's Ministry of Forestry over a defined forest area; under the New Order, HPH concessions were allocated to politically connected firms, driving large-scale deforestation across Kalimantan and Sumatra.
ICMI
Ikatan Cendekiawan Muslim se-Indonesia (All-Indonesia Association of Muslim Intellectuals), founded 7 December 1990 under B.J. Habibie's chairmanship with Suharto's endorsement; the main organisational vehicle of the late New Order's strategic accommodation of political Islam after 1990.
ijtihad
(Arabic) Independent scholarly interpretation of Islamic scripture, exercising individual reasoning rather than merely following established legal rulings (taqlid); central to Muhammadiyah's modernist Islamic vision and a recurring fault-line between reformist and traditionalist Indonesian Muslims.
IJEPA
Indonesia–Japan Economic Partnership Agreement (signed 2007, in force 2008) — a bilateral free-trade and investment framework covering goods, services, energy, intellectual property, and customs cooperation; Japan's first bilateral EPA with a Southeast Asian country.
ikat
Textile technique in which yarn bundles are bound and resist-dyed before weaving, creating patterns in the finished cloth; includes warp-ikat, weft-ikat, and the extremely rare double-ikat (geringsing), produced only in Tenganan Pegringsingan, Bali.
IKN
Ibu Kota Nusantara (Nusantara Capital City) — the formal official designation of Indonesia's new national capital under construction in the Penajam Paser Utara and Kutai Kartanegara regencies of East Kalimantan, authorised by Law No. 3/2022. See also Nusantara.
Indische Partij
("Indies Party") The first political organisation in the Netherlands East Indies to declare independence as its explicit goal, founded 25 December 1912 by E.F.E. Douwes Dekker (Setiabudi), Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo, and Soewardi Soerjaningrat (Ki Hajar Dewantara); banned by the colonial government in 1913 and its founders exiled.
Indonesia Menggugat
(Indonesian: "Indonesia Accuses") Sukarno's defence oration at his 1930 Bandung trial, a five-part indictment of imperialism drawing on Hobson, Lenin, and Luxemburg; one of the most widely circulated nationalist texts of the colonial era.
Indonesia Raya
(Indonesian: "Great Indonesia") The national anthem of Indonesia, composed by Wage Rudolf Supratman c. 1924 and premiered on violin at the Second Youth Congress on 28 October 1928; banned by Dutch authorities; proclaimed the national anthem on 17 August 1945.
Indonesianisasi
(Indonesian: "Indonesianization") The political and intellectual project of transferring control of Indonesian history-writing from Dutch and Western institutions to Indonesian scholars and institutions, advanced especially from the 1970s by historians including Sartono Kartodirdjo.
Inlanders
(Dutch: "Natives") The colonial legal designation for the indigenous majority of the Netherlands East Indies in the tripartite racial hierarchy (Europeans / Vreemde Oosterlingen / Inlanders); subject to adat (customary law) and native courts; could not hold freehold land.
INTERFET
International Force East Timor — the UN Security Council–authorised multinational force (UNSC Resolution 1264) that entered East Timor on 20 September 1999 following the wave of militia violence after the independence referendum; commanded by Australian Major-General Peter Cosgrove; 22 contributing nations.
intra-Asian trade
The VOC's network of trade routes connecting Asian markets with one another — for example, Bengal textiles exchanged for Japanese silver, or spices exchanged for Indian cotton goods — distinct from the direct Europe–Asia carrying trade; a major and often overlooked source of VOC profits.
involution
See agricultural involution.
IPTN
Industri Pesawat Terbang Nurtanio (later Nusantara) — Indonesia's state aircraft manufacturer, established 1976 under B.J. Habibie's direction as part of the New Order's strategic-industries programme; produced the N-250 turboprop prototype before the IMF's 1998 austerity conditions forced the programme's closure.
ISDV
Indische Sociaal-Democratische Vereeniging (Indies Social-Democratic Association), founded by Henk Sneevliet in Surabaya in May 1914; precursor of the PKI; used the "bloc within" tactic of joining Sarekat Islam to radicalise it from the inside.
Islam Nusantara
(Indonesian: "Archipelago Islam") A theological and civilisational concept articulated by Nahdlatul Ulama, describing the historically developed synthesis of Islamic teaching with Indonesian local cultural traditions, emphasising tolerance, pluralism, and contextual legal reasoning. Not a new school of jurisprudence but a methodology of cultural integration; formally endorsed at the 33rd NU Congress in 2015.
Islamization
The long, uneven, and regionally variable process by which Islam spread through the Indonesian archipelago, beginning along northern Sumatra's coasts by the late thirteenth century and reaching most lowland areas by the late seventeenth; woven into local society, law, and culture in diverse ways rather than replacing prior religious systems wholesale.
ITE Law (UU ITE)
Undang-Undang Informasi dan Transaksi Elektronik — Indonesia's Electronic Information and Transactions Law (2008, amended 2016 and 2023), whose broadly drafted criminal provisions against "defamatory" or "offensive" online content have been used extensively against journalists, activists, government critics, and religious minorities.
Jakarta Charter (Piagam Jakarta)
A document drawn up by Indonesian nationalist leaders on 22 June 1945 containing the Pancasila principles plus a seven-word clause obligating Indonesian Muslims to observe Islamic law (dengan kewajiban menjalankan syariat Islam bagi pemeluk-pemeluknya). The seven words were removed from the final 1945 Constitution on 18 August 1945 in a last-minute compromise; their removal defined Indonesia as a non-theocratic state and the demand for their restoration became the central contested issue in later Konstituante debates.
Jamaah Ansharut Daulah
Indonesian umbrella organisation of at least 24 extremist groups pledging allegiance to the Islamic State (IS/ISIS), formed in 2015 under the ideological leadership of Oman Rochman; responsible for the January 2016 Jakarta attack and the May 2018 Surabaya church bombings; banned by South Jakarta District Court in July 2018.
Jawa Hokokai
(Japanese: "Java Service Association") Tightly controlled Japanese occupation mass organisation established from January 1944 to replace Putera; more directly supervised by the military administration and focused on labour mobilisation and war-support activities.
Jayakarta
Javanese port town (also written Jacatra) on the Ciliwung River, a vassal of the Sultanate of Banten, which Jan Pieterszoon Coen burned in May 1619 to found the VOC headquarters city of Batavia.
Jemaah Islamiyah
Al-Qaeda-linked jihadist network formally established in January 1993 in Malaysia by Abdullah Sungkar and Abu Bakar Ba'asyir; responsible for the October 2002 Bali bombings (202 killed), the 2003 Marriott bombing, and other attacks across Southeast Asia; its network was dismantled by Densus 88 over more than a decade; the organisation formally announced dissolution on 30 June 2024.
jilbab
(Indonesian) The Islamic headscarf (hijab); banned under Suharto in state schools and government offices, permitted from c. 1991, and increasingly mandated by regional Perda Syariah regulations in the post-Reformasi era.
joglo
Traditional Javanese aristocratic house form, defined by four central pillars (soko guru) supporting a soaring inner roof (brunjung); the interior is organised from a public veranda (pendapa) through an intermediary space (pringgitan) to the private inner hall (dalem).
jong
A large Javanese or Malay sailing vessel used in the inter-island spice trade before the seventeenth century; outrigger-free, with distinctive canted sails; could carry several hundred tonnes of cargo. Largely displaced by European vessels after the Portuguese conquest of Malacca (1511).
jugun ianfu
(Japanese: "comfort women") The Japanese military's euphemism for women and girls — predominantly Indonesian, Korean, and Chinese — subjected to military sexual slavery in the Japanese-occupied Indies; estimates of Indonesian victims range into the tens of thousands.
kakawin
Long narrative poems composed in Old Javanese (Kawi) using Sanskrit quantitative metres (kavya prosody), written at the courts of Java and Bali from approximately the ninth to the sixteenth centuries; includes the Nagarakretagama (1365) and the Kakawin Sutasoma, source of the national motto Bhinneka Tunggal Ika.
kapitan (Kapitan Cina)
Colonial-era Chinese community headman appointed by the VOC and later Dutch colonial authorities to mediate between the government and the Chinese community, collect taxes, and maintain order; the first in Batavia was Souw Beng Kong (1619); the institution created a hereditary peranakan elite (cabang atas).
karaeng
Makassarese noble or chiefly title, roughly equivalent to Javanese adipati; became a prominent marker of Makassarese diaspora identity after the VOC's Makassar War (1666–69) drove Makassarese migrants across the archipelago.
kebatinan
(Javanese, from batin: "inner") A family of Javanese spiritual practices centred on cultivating inner refinement (rasa) and alignment with the cosmic order, drawing on animist, Hindu-Buddhist, and Sufi elements; categorized by the Indonesian state as kepercayaan (indigenous belief) rather than agama (religion) until a 2017 Constitutional Court ruling.
Kejawen
The syncretic spiritual and cultural tradition of Central Javanese court society, blending pre-Islamic Hindu-Buddhist concepts of divine kingship with Islamic piety; characteristic of the Mataram and successor kratons of Yogyakarta and Surakarta.
Keluarga Berencana (KB)
(Indonesian: "Family Planning") Indonesia's national family planning programme, administered by BKKBN; promoted the two-child ideal (Dua Anak Cukup, "Two is Enough") and achieved one of the fastest fertility transitions in the developing world, reducing the total fertility rate from approximately 5.6 (early 1970s) to 2.6 (mid-1990s).
Kenpeitai
(Japanese: "military police corps") The Japanese military police, which in occupied Indonesia ruled through arbitrary arrest, torture, and execution; responsible for the surveillance and suppression of nationalist activities during the occupation (1942–45).
Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa
(Indonesian: "Belief in the One Almighty God") The first principle of Pancasila, adopted in the final form of the 1945 Constitution on 18 August 1945 after the removal of the seven-word Jakarta Charter clause; deliberately formulated to accommodate all monotheistic and non-monotheistic Indonesian religious traditions simultaneously.
KITLV
Koninklijk Instituut voor Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde (Royal Netherlands Institute for Southeast Asian and Caribbean Studies), founded in Leiden in 1851; the principal institutional home for Dutch scholarship on Indonesia for over a century and a half; publishes the journal Bijdragen tot de Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde.
KKN
Korupsi, Kolusi, Nepotisme (Corruption, Collusion, Nepotism) — the triad of systemic abuses that characterised the Suharto regime's political economy; became the central slogan of the 1997–98 student movement and the defining critique of New Order governance.
KKR
Komisi Kebenaran dan Rekonsiliasi — Indonesia's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, authorised by Law No. 27 of 2004 to investigate past human rights violations; struck down by the Constitutional Court in December 2006 because its reparations provisions required victims to consent to perpetrators' amnesties.
KNIL
Koninklijk Nederlands Indisch Leger (Royal Netherlands Indies Army), the professional colonial military force of the Netherlands East Indies, established by royal decree in September 1814 and dissolved in 1950; comprised European officers, European contract soldiers, and indigenous recruits, with Ambonese and Menadonese soldiers from Christian eastern Indonesia increasingly prominent.
koeli / coolie
(Dutch colonial term, from Tamil kuli) A contract migrant labourer, typically Chinese or Javanese, recruited to work on Outer Island plantations under the Coolie Ordinance of 1880 and its associated poenale sanctie (penal sanction); a system of indentured labour characterised by violence and high mortality.
kolong
(Bangka-Belitung Malay) The shallow ponds left in exhausted alluvial tin-mine workings after the ore-bearing soil has been hydraulically sluiced; now a dominant landscape feature of the Bangka-Belitung islands after three centuries of extraction by Chinese workers and later industrial dredges.
Komnas HAM
Komisi Nasional Hak Asasi Manusia (National Human Rights Commission), established by Presidential Decree in 1993; investigated the Trisakti shootings, the May 1998 riots, and subsequently the 1965–66 killings and other historical violations.
Komnas Perempuan
National Commission on Violence Against Women (Komisi Nasional Anti Kekerasan terhadap Perempuan), established by Presidential Decree No. 181/1998 directly in response to the documented sexual violence against ethnic-Chinese women during the May 1998 riots.
Konfrontasi
(Indonesian: "Confrontation") Sukarno's 1963–66 policy of armed "confrontation" opposing the formation of the Federation of Malaysia, framed as anti-imperialist opposition to a "British neo-colonial project"; ended by the New Order government as part of normalising international relations.
Kongres Perempuan Indonesia
Indonesian Women's Congress; first convened 22–25 December 1928 in Yogyakarta, bringing together women's organisations from across the colony to establish the national women's movement and the PPPI umbrella organisation; the date is commemorated as Hari Ibu.
Konstituante
The Constituent Assembly elected in the December 1955 general elections (520 seats) to draft a permanent constitution; deadlocked over the fundamental question of whether the state's ideological basis should be Pancasila or Islam; dissolved by Sukarno's presidential decree of 5 July 1959.
Kopkamtib
Komando Operasi Pemulihan Keamanan dan Ketertiban (Operational Command for the Restoration of Security and Order) — the New Order's primary extra-legal security instrument (1965–1988), with authority to detain, interrogate, and "rehabilitate" political opponents without judicial oversight; replaced by Bakorstanas in 1988.
kora-kora
Maluku war canoes used in the annual hongi extirpation expeditions; crewed by conscripted Ambonese rowers performing corvée obligations; designed for speed in the shallow reef-strewn waters of the Moluccas.
Korps Marechaussee te voet
(Dutch: "Foot Gendarmerie Corps") Elite Royal Netherlands East Indies Army mobile infantry corps, founded 2 April 1890; armed with carbine-length Mannlicher rifle and short klewang sword; employed offensive surprise tactics in jungle counterinsurgency in Aceh under Generals Van Heutsz and Colijn.
Korte Verklaring
(Dutch: "Short Declaration") Standardised Dutch submission treaty imposed on Indonesian rulers from the 1890s onward, requiring unconditional acknowledgment of Dutch sovereignty in a brief, unambiguous text; the principal legal instrument by which the Netherlands East Indies was juridically unified under direct Dutch authority.
Kostrad
Komando Cadangan Strategis Angkatan Darat (Army Strategic Reserve Command) — the army's principal mobile combat force; in May 1998 commanded by Lieutenant-General Prabowo Subianto; Kostrad units played a decisive role in securing Jakarta during Suharto's final days.
KPK
Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi (Corruption Eradication Commission) — Indonesia's independent anti-corruption body, established by Law No. 30/2002 and operational from late 2003; given powers exceeding ordinary prosecutors including warrantless wiretapping; achieved a near-100% conviction rate in its early years. Institutional independence significantly weakened by Law No. 19/2019, which placed it under executive oversight.
kraton
A Javanese royal palace complex and court, housing the ruler, his family, retainers, and the ritual objects legitimising his rule; the great kratons of Yogyakarta (est. 1755) and Surakarta (est. 1745) remain centres of Javanese high culture.
KUHP
Kitab Undang-Undang Hukum Pidana (Criminal Code) — Indonesia's penal code; the revised KUHP passed by the DPR in December 2022 replaces the colonial-era Dutch penal law that had been in force since 1918 and enters into force in January 2026. Contested provisions include presidential-insult articles, an expanded blasphemy clause, and the criminalisation of consensual extramarital sex.
landrente
(Dutch: "land rent") The land-tax system introduced by Governor-General Raffles (1813) and retained in modified form by the Dutch; levied a cash proportion of annual crop value on Javanese cultivators in lieu of forced deliveries or labour obligations; a foundational fiscal instrument of colonial administration.
laskar
(Malay/Indonesian: "militia") Irregular armed units that proliferated during the National Revolution (1945–49), ranging from disciplined pemuda battalions and ideologically motivated groups to neighbourhood defence forces and criminal networks; their relationship with the Republican army was often tense.
LIPI
Lembaga Ilmu Pengetahuan Indonesia (Indonesian Institute of Sciences), founded 1956; the state body overseeing scientific and social-science research in Indonesia, including history; its independence was constrained during the New Order. Merged into BRIN (National Research and Innovation Agency) in 2021.
lontar
A palm-leaf manuscript made from dried strips of the Borassus (lontar) palm, incised with a stylus and rubbed with lamp-black; the principal vehicle for Old Javanese and Balinese literary, religious, and administrative texts, surviving in thousands of examples in Balinese temple libraries.
lurah
A Javanese village headman who served as the lowest-level administrative intermediary between colonial authorities and the peasant population; under the New Order, the lurah was incorporated into the state apparatus as a salaried government official.
madhhab
(Arabic: "school of law") One of the four classical Sunni schools of Islamic jurisprudence; the Shafi'i madhhab, dominant in Yemen and the Malabar Coast of India, prevailed in early Sumatran Islam and remains the dominant legal school in Indonesia.
Madiun Affair (Peristiwa Madiun)
The September–October 1948 uprising in which PKI-affiliated forces in Madiun, East Java, clashed with the Republican army; suppressed by the Siliwangi Division; a pivotal turning point whose interpretation — spontaneous revolt vs. PKI coup attempt vs. Sukarno provocation — has been fiercely debated across subsequent political regimes.
mahasiswa
(Indonesian: "university student/students") In political discourse, refers to the organised student movement that has historically intervened at national crises — 1966 (against Sukarno), 1974 (Malari), 1978 (anti-Suharto), and 1998 (Reformasi); a recurrent actor in Indonesian political transitions.
MANIPOL-USDEK
The official state ideology of Guided Democracy: MANIPOL = Manifesto Politik (Sukarno's 1959 Independence Day address); USDEK = five principles: UUD 1945, Indonesian Socialism (Sosialisme Indonesia), Guided Democracy (Demokrasi Terpimpin), Guided Economy (Ekonomi Terpimpin), Indonesian Identity (Kepribadian Indonesia). Mandatory study in all schools and for civil servants.
Marhaenisme
(Marhaenism) The political ideology developed by Sukarno, adapting Marxist class analysis to Indonesian conditions; the marhaen — the impoverished small peasant owner who owns his own tools but remains poor due to the structural conditions of colonialism — represented Indonesia's colonial masses and defined the social base of the independence movement.
Massa mengambang
(Indonesian: "floating mass") The New Order policy, formalised in Government Regulation No. 5/1973, treating rural Indonesians as requiring insulation from partisan politics; restricted opposition parties to the subdistrict level while Golkar retained unrestricted village-level access.
Masyumi
Majelis Syuro Muslimin Indonesia (Council of Indonesian Muslim Associations) — the major modernist Islamic political party of the parliamentary era (1945–60); strong in the Outer Islands, urban educated Muslims, and Chinese-minority towns; banned by Sukarno in 1960 following the leaders' involvement in the PRRI rebellion.
Melanesian Spearhead Group (MSG)
Intergovernmental organisation of four Melanesian states (Fiji, Papua New Guinea, Solomon Islands, Vanuatu) plus the New Caledonia independence movement FLNKS; formalized in 2007; Indonesia joined as an associate member in 2015, partly to manage Papuan independence lobbying within the forum.
meunasah
(Acehnese) Village prayer house in Aceh, serving simultaneously as community gathering place, Islamic school, and residential dormitory for unmarried men; during the Aceh War, the site of nightly recitations of the Hikayat Perang Sabil.
Monoloyalitas
(Indonesian: "single loyalty") New Order requirement codified in Government Regulation No. 6/1970 that all civil servants channel their political activity exclusively through Golkar and enrol in the state-sponsored civil servants' organisation KORPRI, effectively prohibiting government employees from joining opposition parties.
MPR
Majelis Permusyawaratan Rakyat (People's Consultative Assembly) — under the original 1945 Constitution, the supreme legislative body above the DPR; elected the president and vice-president before direct presidential elections began in 2004; significantly reduced in power by the 1999–2002 constitutional amendments.
mudaraba
(Arabic: muḍāraba) Islamic commercial partnership contract in which one party supplies capital (rabb al-māl) and another supplies labour or expertise (muḍārib); profits are shared by prior agreement, losses borne by the capital provider alone. The mudaraba enabled long-distance Indian Ocean trade across cultural, religious, and linguistic barriers.
multi-alignment
Contemporary term for a foreign policy strategy that deepens relationships with multiple great powers simultaneously without committing to any single alliance or bloc; Indonesia's approach under Presidents Jokowi and Prabowo, extending the historical bebas-aktif doctrine into a more actively managed great-power balancing.
Muhammadiyah
Indonesia's second-largest Islamic organisation, founded in Yogyakarta on 18 November 1912 by Kyai Haji Ahmad Dahlan; modernist-reformist orientation, promoting scripturally purified Islamic practice through ijtihad and condemning syncretic accretions; manages thousands of schools, universities, hospitals, and welfare institutions; estimated membership 29–35 million.
Multatuli
Pen name of Eduard Douwes Dekker (1820–1887), from Latin multa tuli ("I have suffered much"); Dutch colonial official turned critic and author of Max Havelaar (1860), the semi-autobiographical novel that exposed Cultivation System abuses and became a landmark of both Dutch literature and colonial critique.
nagari
(Minangkabau) The village republic — the fundamental political and social unit of Minangkabau society in the West Sumatran highlands; governed by adat through councils of penghulu (clan chiefs); the conflict between Padri reformers and the nagari adat system was the internal dimension of the Padri War.
Nahdlatul Ulama (NU)
(Arabic/Indonesian: "Revival of the Religious Scholars") Indonesia's largest Islamic organisation, founded 31 January 1926 in East Java by Kyai Haji Hasyim Asy'ari and others; traditionalist Sunni following the Shafi'i legal school; defends classical Ash'ari theology and the pesantren network; champions Islam Nusantara and Indonesian pluralism; estimated membership 60–90 million.
Nanyang
(Chinese: "Southern Seas") The term used by Chinese emigrants, traders, and sojourners to refer to maritime Southeast Asia — their principal zone of migration, commerce, and settlement from at least the Song dynasty.
NASAKOM
Sukarno's ideological formula combining Nasionalisme (nationalism), Agama (religion), and Komunisme (communism) — intended to unite secular nationalists, Islamic organisations, and the PKI within the coalition of Guided Democracy; contested by both Islamic and army leaders.
NICA
Netherlands Indies Civil Administration — the Dutch colonial administrative body assembled in exile in Australia during the Japanese occupation, which followed Allied forces into Indonesia from late 1945 intending to restore Netherlands sovereignty; encountered immediate armed resistance from Republican forces.
nine-dash line
China's claimed maritime boundary in the South China Sea, encompassing approximately 90 percent of that sea; found to have no basis in international law by the Permanent Court of Arbitration in 2016 (Philippines v. China). Relevant to Indonesia as the line overlaps with Indonesia's exclusive economic zone north of the Natuna Islands.
NHM
Nederlandsche Handel-Maatschappij (Netherlands Trading Company) — a Dutch Crown enterprise established in 1824 at King William I's initiative, granted a monopoly on the shipping and sale of Javanese government produce in Europe until 1872; a key mechanism for transferring colonial surplus to the Dutch economy.
NKK/BKK
Normalisasi Kehidupan Kampus / Badan Koordinasi Kemahasiswaan (Normalization of Campus Life / Student Coordination Board) — Suharto's 1978–79 regulations banning autonomous student political organisations on university campuses, fragmenting the student movement that had challenged him in 1977–78 and driving political organising underground.
non-alignment
Foreign-policy orientation refusing formal alignment with either Cold War superpower bloc; advocated by Sukarno, Nehru, Nasser, and others; institutionalised at the Bandung Conference (April 1955) and the founding of the Non-Aligned Movement (Belgrade, September 1961). See also bebas-aktif.
Non-Aligned Movement (NAM)
International forum founded in Belgrade in September 1961, grouping states that declined alignment with either NATO or the Warsaw Pact; grew directly from the 1955 Bandung Conference; Indonesia was a founding member and has played a prominent role throughout its history.
noncooperatie
(Dutch: "non-cooperation") The strategy adopted by Sukarno's PNI and the Perhimpoenan Indonesia of refusing to participate in Dutch colonial advisory councils or institutions as a matter of nationalist principle; contrasted with the cooperative strategy of Budi Utomo and the PSII.
Nusantara
(Malay/Old Javanese: "outer islands" or "island world") (a) The classical concept denoting the wider archipelagic world of maritime Southeast Asia, used from the Majapahit period onwards; (b) the name chosen for Indonesia's new national capital under construction in East Kalimantan (authorized 2022), deliberately invoking the classical resonance to signal national unity. The two usages must always be distinguished in context. See also IKN.
nyai
(Javanese/Malay) An Indonesian or Chinese woman who cohabited with a European man in the colonial Indies as domestic manager, housekeeper, and sexual companion; tacitly sanctioned by colonial authorities; the woman had no formal legal standing and her children's status depended on formal paternal acknowledgment. A central figure in colonial society, analysed by Frances Gouda and Ann Stoler.
OPM (Organisasi Papua Merdeka)
Free Papua Movement, founded in the early 1960s; the principal armed resistance organisation opposing Indonesian rule in West Papua/Irian Jaya; its military wing is now the TPNPB. Designated a terrorist organisation by Indonesia in 2021.
Operasi Seroja
(Indonesian: "Operation Lotus") The Indonesian military's codename for the December 1975 invasion of East Timor, launched two days after Fretilin's unilateral declaration of independence; the largest military operation in Indonesian history to that date, involving all three service branches.
orang kaya
(Malay: "rich person") The mercantile and noble elite of port-city sultanates — Banten, Makassar, the Maluku spice islands — who governed inter-island trade networks and served as the key intermediaries between sultan and trading population; also the Bandanese patrician leaders who initially negotiated and ultimately resisted the VOC.
orang laut
(Malay: "sea people") The boat-dwelling maritime communities of the Straits of Malacca and the Riau-Lingga Archipelago, who lived and worked on the water; their loyalty — secured through gift exchange and patronage — furnished Srivijaya and Malacca with naval power and the capacity to direct trade to the royal port.
Orde Baru
(Indonesian: "New Order") Suharto's self-designation for his regime (1966–1998), coined in explicit contrast with Sukarno's "Old Order" (Orde Lama); defined by developmental ideology, authoritarian stability, and anti-communism.
P4
Pedoman Penghayatan dan Pengamalan Pancasila (Guide to Experiencing and Practising Pancasila) — the mandatory state indoctrination programme established by MPR Resolution No. 2/1978, requiring rote memorisation of 36 formulaic Pancasila precepts by all civil servants, university students, and eventually schoolchildren; abolished under Reformasi.
Padri
The Wahhabi-influenced Islamic reformist movement in the Minangkabau highlands of West Sumatra, active c. 1803–1837, and its members. Etymology disputed — possibly from the Acehnese port Pedir/Pidie, a pilgrimage embarkation point, or from Portuguese padre. Padri adherents sought to impose strict Sharia and eliminate matrilineal adat customs, triggering the Padri War.
Padroado
(Portuguese: "patronage") The system of royal patronage by which the Portuguese Crown held the right to nominate Catholic clergy and establish churches in its overseas territories, granted by a series of papal bulls from the 1450s; made the Portuguese Crown the official instrument of Catholic mission across the Indian Ocean rim.
Pan-Islamism
The political idea that the world's Muslim communities share a common religious identity that should translate into political solidarity, especially against imperial powers; a key reference point in the ideological debates of the early Indonesian nationalist movement, particularly within Sarekat Islam and the Muhammadiyah.
Panembahan
(Javanese: "revered lord") Honorific title below the rank of Sultan, meaning approximately "lord" or "the one revered by all"; used by Senapati, the founder of the Islamic Mataram state, before later Mataram rulers adopted the full title Sultan.
Pancasila
(Sanskrit: "five principles") The five founding philosophical principles of the Indonesian state, first articulated by Sukarno on 1 June 1945: (1) belief in the one Almighty God (Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa); (2) just and civilised humanity (Kemanusiaan yang adil dan beradab); (3) the unity of Indonesia (Persatuan Indonesia); (4) democracy guided by deliberation among representatives (Kerakyatan yang dipimpin oleh hikmat kebijaksanaan dalam permusyawaratan/perwakilan); and (5) social justice for all Indonesians (Keadilan sosial bagi seluruh rakyat Indonesia).
Pangreh Praja
(Javanese/Indonesian: "ruler of the realm") The indigenous Javanese civil service, hierarchically structured as bupati (regent, c. 70 posts), wedana (district chief, c. 400), and asisten wedana (sub-district chief, c. 1,200); operated under Dutch Binnenlands Bestuur supervision; staffed by the priyayi aristocracy.
Papia Kristang
(Creole Portuguese: "speak Christian") A Portuguese-based creole language developed by the Eurasian casado communities of Malacca after the Portuguese conquest of 1511; the only extant Portuguese-derived creole in Southeast Asia, still spoken in Melaka's "Portuguese Settlement."
participanten
(Dutch: "participants/shareholders") Ordinary investors in the VOC, as distinct from the bewindhebbers (managing directors); liable only to their paid-in capital; the first publicly traded joint-stock shareholders in history.
passenstelsel
(Dutch: "pass system") The colonial Netherlands East Indies regulation requiring Chinese (Vreemde Oosterlingen) and indigenous Indonesians (Inlanders) to carry written authorisation when leaving their designated residential areas or travelling between residencies; formally abolished in 1919 for Chinese in most areas.
PDI-P
Partai Demokrasi Indonesia – Perjuangan (Indonesian Democratic Party of Struggle), the party of Megawati Sukarnoputri, founded 1999 from a split in the original PDI; Indonesia's largest secular nationalist party; provided the political vehicle for Joko Widodo's 2014 and 2019 presidential victories.
peatland
A wetland ecosystem in which partially decomposed plant matter accumulates over millennia under waterlogged, oxygen-poor conditions; Indonesian peatlands in Kalimantan and Sumatra represent the world's largest tropical peat deposits, storing enormous quantities of carbon and, when drained for agriculture, becoming highly susceptible to catastrophic fire.
pecinan
(Malay/Indonesian) The designated Chinese residential and commercial quarter (Chinatown) in colonial Indies cities, created or formalised in the aftermath of the 1740 Batavia massacre and enforced by the wijkenstelsel until 1919. The Glodok district of Jakarta (Batavia) remains the most famous.
pembangunan
(Indonesian: "development") The New Order's central ideological concept, which transformed a contested policy goal into an unquestionable national telos — growth as the regime's primary legitimating claim; analysed by Ariel Heryanto as an ideology that silenced political alternatives by naturalising economic technocracy.
pemekaran
(Indonesian: "blossoming/proliferation") The creation of new administrative districts through subdivision of existing ones, accelerated by Indonesia's 1999 decentralization laws; the number of districts and cities rose from 292 in 1999 to over 500 by 2014, driven partly by local elites seeking new patronage resources.
pemuda
(Indonesian: "youth") The radicalized young Indonesians who came of age under the Japanese occupation and became the driving force of the 1945 revolution; pemuda groups kidnapped Sukarno and Hatta on 16 August 1945 to force the independence proclamation, and subsequently fought the Dutch in the National Revolution.
Pendidikan Nasional Indonesia
(Indonesian National Education, also "New PNI" or PNI-baru) The cadre-education organisation led by Hatta and Sjahrir from 1931, after Sukarno's imprisonment; emphasised intellectual preparation of democratic citizens over mass mobilisation, in deliberate contrast to Sukarno's style.
penghayat kepercayaan
(Indonesian: "indigenous belief practitioners") Adherents of non-recognized spiritual traditions, indigenous religions, and syncretic belief systems outside the six state-recognized religions (agama); estimated at 12–20 million Indonesians; denied full civil recognition, including the ability to record their beliefs on national ID cards, until a Constitutional Court ruling in November 2017 (Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016).
Pepera
Penentuan Pendapat Rakyat ("Determination of the People's Opinion" / "Act of Free Choice") — the Indonesian government's name for the 1969 plebiscite in West Papua/West Irian, conducted over July–August 1969; 1,025 hand-picked Papuan representatives — rather than the universal vote originally proposed — voted unanimously for integration with Indonesia; widely condemned by international scholars as a sham. Endorsed by the UN General Assembly in Resolution 2504 (1969) in disputed circumstances.
Perda Syariah
(Indonesian: "Sharia regional regulations") Regional government bylaws (peraturan daerah) incorporating Islamic content, enacted by district and provincial governments using the expanded autonomy granted by Indonesia's 1999 decentralization laws; approximately 78 such regulations documented 1999–2007 covering Islamic observance, dress codes, and prohibition of alcohol and gambling.
pergerakan
(Indonesian: "the movement") The collective term for the Indonesian nationalist movement of the early twentieth century, encompassing its Islamic, communist, secular-nationalist, and cultural tendencies within a shared anti-colonial project.
perk
(Dutch: "plot/garden") The equal-sized plantation unit into which the VOC divided the Banda Islands after the 1621 conquest, each allocated to a perkenier; 68 perken were established across the islands of Lontor, Ay, and Banda Neira by 1628.
peranakan
(Malay: "locally born") Ethnic Chinese who had settled for several generations in the Indonesian archipelago and adopted substantial elements of local (Javanese or Malay) language, dress, cuisine, and ceremony, while retaining Chinese surnames and some ceremonial practices; distinguished from totok (recently arrived) Chinese.
Perdjoeangan Kita
(Indonesian: "Our Struggle") Sutan Sjahrir's 1945 political manifesto arguing that Indonesia's ethnic and religious diversity required a secular, democratic national identity; published in Dutch as Indonesische overpeinzingen; influential in shaping the Republic's early diplomatic strategy and Sjahrir's approach to the revolution.
Perhimpoenan Indonesia
(Indonesian: "Indonesian Union") The Indonesian students' association in the Netherlands, evolving from the Indische Vereeniging (1908) through the Indonesische Vereeniging (1922) to the Perhimpoenan Indonesia (1924); under Hatta's chairmanship it pioneered the use of "Indonesia" and "Indonesia Merdeka" (Free Indonesia) as political slogans.
perkenier
(Dutch) The manager of a perk (Banda Islands nutmeg plantation), typically a former VOC soldier or employee assigned an estate and a quota of enslaved workers after the 1621 conquest, obligated to sell all produce exclusively to the VOC at company-fixed prices.
perang sabil
(from Arabic sabilillah, "in the path of God") A declaration of holy war framing armed resistance against non-Muslim invaders as religious duty and promising spiritual reward for fighters and martyrdom for the fallen. Prince Diponegoro declared perang sabil in 1825; the concept recurred in the Acehnese resistance from the 1870s through the early twentieth century.
Pers Pancasila
(Indonesian: "Pancasila Press") The official doctrine governing all Indonesian print and broadcast media under the New Order, requiring journalists to subordinate factual objectivity to national unity and development goals; also called "developmental journalism" (jurnalisme pembangunan); enforced through licensing requirements and the threat of closure.
Pertamina
Perusahaan Pertambangan Minyak dan Gas Bumi Negara — the Indonesian state oil and gas company, created in 1968 by merging earlier state petroleum bodies; its near-collapse in 1975 with over US$10 billion in debt under Director Ibnu Sutowo was the New Order's first major institutional crisis.
pesantren
(Javanese/Indonesian: "place of the santri") Traditional Islamic boarding school in which students (santri) live with and study under a senior Islamic scholar (kyai); the organisational heartbeat of Nahdlatul Ulama and the Indonesian traditionalist Islamic network; numbering over 27,000 institutions across the archipelago.
PETA (Pembela Tanah Air)
(Indonesian: "Defenders of the Homeland") The Indonesian-officered volunteer defence corps established by the Japanese military authorities on Java from October 1943 and on Bali from January 1944; trained a generation of pemuda officers who became the cadre of the Republican army. A PETA battalion mutiny in Blitar (February 1945) foreshadowed the revolution.
Petrus (penembakan misterius)
(Indonesian: "mysterious shootings") The Suharto government's extrajudicial killing campaign against alleged criminals and gangsters, 1982–85; Suharto acknowledged authorising the killings as "shock therapy" in his 1988 autobiography; the Constitutional Court's Komnas HAM classified the killings as gross human rights violations in 2012.
PKI (Partai Komunis Indonesia)
The Communist Party of Indonesia, founded in Semarang in May 1920 from the ISDV; widely considered the first communist party established in Asia; revived under D.N. Aidit from 1951 and by 1965 the world's largest non-ruling communist party with approximately three million members and 22 million affiliated organisation members; destroyed in the 1965–66 mass killings following the 30 September Movement.
PKK
Pembinaan Kesejahteraan Keluarga (later Pemberdayaan dan Kesejahteraan Keluarga — Family Welfare Guidance / Empowerment) — the New Order's nationwide village-level women's organisation, established 1973, serving as the primary community delivery vehicle for state programmes including family planning, nutrition, childcare, and Pancasila ideological education.
POLRI
Kepolisian Negara Republik Indonesia (Indonesian National Police) — separated from the armed forces (TNI/ABRI) by MPR Decree No. VI/2000 as part of Reformasi military reform; since 2000 an independent civilian force reporting directly to the President.
poenale sanctie
(Dutch: "penal sanction") The clause in the 1880 Coolie Ordinance of the Netherlands East Indies that made breach of a plantation labour contract — by absconding, refusing work, or showing insubordination — a criminal (not merely civil) offence, enforceable with imprisonment or forced labour on public works. Formally abolished in 1931 under international labour pressure.
Police Actions (politionele acties)
Dutch euphemism for two large-scale military offensives against the Indonesian Republic: Operatie Product (21 July – 5 August 1947) and Operatie Kraai (19 December 1948 – 5 January 1949); termed "Dutch Military Aggressions" (Agresi Militer Belanda) in Indonesian historiography; both triggered UN intervention and ultimately hastened Dutch recognition of Indonesian sovereignty.
politik uang
(Indonesian: "money politics") The practice of vote-buying, patronage distribution, and cash payments to voters at every level of Indonesia's electoral system; intensified by the open-list proportional representation system adopted under Reformasi, which requires individual candidates to outbid party-list rivals for voter preferences.
Posyandu
Pos Pelayanan Terpadu (Integrated Service Post) — community-level health post, staffed by trained volunteer women (through PKK), providing family planning services, maternal and child health monitoring, immunisation, and nutrition support; the primary point of contact between the state health system and rural communities.
PPHAM Team
Tim Penyelesaian Non-Yudisial Pelanggaran Hak Asasi Manusia yang Berat Masa Lalu (Non-Judicial Resolution Team for Past Gross Human Rights Violations) — established by Presidential Regulation No. 17/2022; its report led to President Jokowi's acknowledgment on 11 January 2023 of 12 categories of past gross human rights violations, the first such presidential admission in Indonesian history.
PPKI (Panitia Persiapan Kemerdekaan Indonesia)
Preparatory Committee for Indonesian Independence, established by the Japanese military on 7 August 1945 and repurposed by the nationalists as the founding body of the Republic; adopted the 1945 Constitution and elected Sukarno as president and Hatta as vice-president on 18 August 1945.
prasasti
(Sanskrit: "praise inscription") An inscription on stone, copper plate, or bronze recording royal edicts, land grants, religious dedications, or genealogies; the primary epigraphic source base for the Hindu-Buddhist period (c. 4th–15th centuries). The oldest dated examples from the Indonesian archipelago are the Kutai yupa pillars (c. 400 CE).
pribumi
(Indonesian: "native/indigenous") Indigenous Indonesians, as distinct from groups of foreign descent (especially Chinese Indonesians and Indo-Europeans); a fraught colonial-era legal and social category that has continued to shape economic policy, affirmative action, and social tensions in independent Indonesia.
priyayi
The Javanese aristocratic-administrative class; in Clifford Geertz's typology, one of three Javanese social categories (alongside abangan and santri), associated with Hindu-Buddhist court culture. Under colonial rule the priyayi staffed the Pangreh Praja indigenous bureaucracy as intermediaries between Dutch authorities and the peasant majority; their Dutch-educated children produced the nationalist intelligentsia of the early twentieth century.
PRRI (Pemerintah Revolusioner Republik Indonesia)
("Revolutionary Government of the Republic of Indonesia") — rebel government proclaimed in Padang, West Sumatra, on 15 February 1958 by dissident military officers and Masyumi politicians; allied with the Permesta movement in Sulawesi (hence the combined shorthand "PRRI-Permesta"); demanded political-fiscal decentralisation rather than secession; received covert CIA arms support; suppressed by mid-1961.
puputan
(Balinese: "the finish/ending") A Balinese concept of ritual mass death in battle — a final charge to certain death by a ruler, court, and retainers against an overwhelming enemy, choosing honourable annihilation over surrender; notable instances at Badung (1906) and Klungkung (1908) against Dutch colonial forces, witnessed by foreign journalists and creating a moral scandal in Europe.
Putera
Pusat Tenaga Rakyat ("Center of the People's Power") — the Japanese-sponsored Indonesian nationalist mass organisation established in March 1943 and led by the Empat Serangkai; dissolved by the Japanese in January 1944 and replaced by the more tightly controlled Jawa Hokokai.
rantau
(Minangkabau/Malay: "the outside world beyond the home village") The Minangkabau tradition of young men leaving their matrilineal home village to seek education, commercial fortune, or spiritual experience before returning with gained status; in wider Indonesian usage, out-migration generally.
ratu adil
(Javanese: "Just Ruler") Messianic concept rooted in prophecies attributed to the twelfth-century Kediri king Jayabaya, predicting a divinely chosen leader who would expel foreign oppressors and restore justice and prosperity; recurred in periods of social upheaval. Prince Diponegoro was widely identified as the ratu adil by his followers during the Java War (1825–30).
REDD+
"Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation plus" — the international climate finance mechanism under the UNFCCC that compensates developing countries financially for demonstrated reductions in forest-carbon emissions; Indonesia has been a major REDD+ recipient and hosts significant pilot projects in Kalimantan and Papua.
Reformasi
(Indonesian: "Reform") The post-1998 era of democratic reform and institutional transformation following Suharto's resignation on 21 May 1998; encompasses constitutional amendments (1999–2002), direct elections, press freedom, military reform, regional autonomy, and civil liberties restoration.
Regeringsreglement
(Dutch: "Government Regulation") The fundamental constitutional document of the Netherlands East Indies; first issued in 1818 by the Commissioners-General, comprehensively revised in 1854 to codify the tripartite racial classification of the colonial population (Europeans / Vreemde Oosterlingen / Inlanders) and the respective legal regimes applying to each.
rencong
(Acehnese) The traditional Acehnese asymmetric dagger, carried by adult Acehnese men as a marker of identity and used in Atjèh-moord suicide attacks against Dutch soldiers during the Aceh War.
REPELITA
Rencana Pembangunan Lima Tahun (Five-Year Development Plan) — the series of six national development plans (REPELITA I–VI, 1969–1998) that structured New Order economic policy; designed by Berkeley Mafia technocrats through Bappenas under Widjojo Nitisastro.
residentie
(Dutch: "residency") The basic territorial unit of Dutch colonial administration, each headed by a European resident (resident) responsible to the Governor-General; established by Daendels in 1808 and maintained throughout the colonial period; approximately 20 residencies in Java and more in the Outer Islands.
rijsttafel
(Dutch: "rice table") An elaborate colonial-era feast incorporating dozens of Indonesian dishes served around a rice centrepiece, associated with the hybrid culinary culture of the nyai household and the Indo-European colonial world; a distinctively colonial performance of abundance later nostalgicised in tempo doeloe memory.
RIS (Republik Indonesia Serikat)
(Republic of the United States of Indonesia) The federal state created by the December 1949 Round Table Conference settlement, composed of sixteen constituent states including the Indonesian Republic; dissolved into the unitary Republic of Indonesia on 17 August 1950, less than eight months after its creation.
romusha
(Japanese: "labour worker") Forced or conscripted Indonesian labourers — overwhelmingly Javanese — mobilised by the Japanese occupation authorities for infrastructure construction, military fortifications, and agricultural projects across Southeast Asia, 1942–45; hundreds of thousands died from malnutrition, disease, and violence.
Round Table Conference (Konferensi Meja Bundar)
Negotiations at The Hague, 23 August – 2 November 1949, between the Indonesian Republic, the Netherlands-created federal states, and the Dutch government; produced the transfer of sovereignty on 27 December 1949, established the short-lived Netherlands-Indonesia Union, and required the Republic to assume the Dutch colonial debt of approximately 4.3 billion guilders.
rumah gadang
(Minangkabau: "big house") The traditional communal matrilineal longhouse of the Minangkabau people of West Sumatra, owned by the women of the clan and housing the extended matrilineal family; characterised by its multi-gabled buffalo-horn roof form, with the number of gables indicating the status of the clan.
santri
In Clifford Geertz's typology, devout and observant Indonesian Muslims oriented toward Islamic law, scholarship, and mosque-centred life; contrasted with abangan. Also designates a student resident at a pesantren (Islamic boarding school) in the non-typological sense. The typology has been critiqued by subsequent scholars as reifying a political distinction.
Sarekat Islam
("Islamic Union") Originally Sarekat Dagang Islam (Islamic Traders' Union, 1911), renamed Sarekat Islam in 1912; under Omar Said Tjokroaminoto's leadership it became the Netherlands East Indies' first mass political movement, claiming approximately two million members by 1919 before fracturing over communist-Islamic tensions in the early 1920s.
sarong-kebaya
A hybrid colonial dress — a fitted, open-fronted blouse (kebaya) worn with an Indonesian wrap skirt (sarong) — popularised by nyai women and worn by both European and Indonesian women in the colonial Indies through the early twentieth century; consciously abandoned by European women from the 1920s as a marker of racial distinction.
SBKRI
Surat Bukti Kewarganegaraan Republik Indonesia (Certificate of Proof of Indonesian Citizenship) — a document required specifically of Chinese Indonesians under the New Order, not required of other citizens; functioned as a discriminatory bureaucratic barrier to schooling, banking, employment, and government services; abolished by the 2006 Citizenship Law.
Sejarah Melayu
("Malay Annals") A court chronicle of the Malacca sultanate, compiled in its present form at Johor in the early seventeenth century; a masterpiece of classical Malay literary prose but primarily a legitimising, genealogical text, tracing the descent of Malay rulers from Alexander the Great, to be read critically as a historical source.
Serambi Mekah
(Malay: "Veranda of Mecca") Aceh's traditional epithet, reflecting its role as the first Indonesian port of call for pilgrims bound for Mecca and as a centre of Islamic scholarship connected to the Haramayn; used to describe Aceh from at least the seventeenth century.
Serangan Umum
(Indonesian: "General Offensive") The military operation of 1 March 1949 in which Republican forces under the field command of Lieutenant-Colonel Suharto briefly recaptured Yogyakarta from Dutch occupation for several hours; demonstrated to the United Nations Security Council that the Republic continued to exist as a functioning armed force.
Siwa-Buddha
The syncretic court theology of late Hindu-Buddhist Java, especially at Majapahit, in which Shiva and the Buddha were understood as complementary aspects of a single supreme divine reality; elaborated in the Nagarakretagama of 1365 and the Sutasoma of approximately the same period.
SOB (Staat van Oorlog en Beleg)
(Dutch: "State of War and Siege") Emergency powers declaration made by Sukarno in March 1957 following the collapse of the Ali Sastroamidjojo II cabinet and the outbreak of regional rebellions; transferred authority over key areas to the military and effectively ended parliamentary governance.
Soeloeh Indonesia Moeda
(Indonesian: "Torch of Young Indonesia") The journal of the Algemeene Studieclub in Bandung, in which Sukarno published his landmark 1926 essay "Nationalism, Islam and Marxism" (Nasionalisme, Islamisme dan Marxisme), attempting to synthesise the three main currents of the nationalist movement.
SPSI
Serikat Pekerja Seluruh Indonesia (All-Indonesia Workers' Union) — the sole legally recognised trade union federation under the New Order, integrated into the corporatist state apparatus and controlled by the Ministry of Manpower; independent unions such as SBSI (Serikat Buruh Sejahtera Indonesia) were suppressed.
State Ibuism (Ibuisme Negara)
Term coined by sociologist Julia Suryakusuma (1988 thesis; 1996 publication) for the New Order's official gender ideology, which constructed Indonesian womanhood around domesticity, reproductive compliance, and subordination — defining women primarily as mothers and wives in service to the state — institutionalised through Dharma Wanita and PKK.
Suikerwet
(Dutch: "Sugar Law," 1870) Legislation phasing the colonial government out of forced sugar cultivation between 1878 and 1890, liberalising the sugar industry for private enterprise; enacted simultaneously with the Agrarische Wet to inaugurate the Liberal Period.
suluk
(Javanese, from Arabic suluk: "mystical path") Javanese Sufi devotional poetry fusing Islamic mysticism, especially the doctrine of wahdat al-wujud (unity of being), with older Indic notions of pantheism and monism; a genre that served as a major vehicle of Islamisation at Javanese courts.
Sumpah Pemuda
(Indonesian: "Youth Pledge") The resolution adopted at the Second Youth Congress in Batavia on 28 October 1928, affirming: one homeland (Indonesia), one nation (the Indonesian nation), and one language (Bahasa Indonesia). A founding moment of the nationalist movement commemorated as Youth Pledge Day (Hari Sumpah Pemuda).
super app
A mobile application platform that consolidates multiple services — ride-hailing, food delivery, digital payments, e-commerce, financial products, logistics — within a single user interface. Gojek's GoApp was Indonesia's pioneering example; the model proved particularly suited to markets where smartphone adoption outpaced legacy banking and retail infrastructure.
swidden
Shifting or rotational cultivation (Indonesian: ladang), in which a plot is cleared — typically by cutting and burning vegetation — cultivated for one or a few seasons, then left to regenerate over a fallow period; under traditional management with adequate fallow, a sophisticated and sustainable agroforestry system practiced across the Indonesian Outer Islands.
syahbandar (shahbandar)
(from Persian shāh-bandar: "king of the port") Harbour-master of a port city in the Indian Ocean and Southeast Asian world; at Malacca, a college of four shahbandars each managed a group of foreign merchant nationalities, allotting warehouses and settling commercial disputes. The role was often held by prominent foreign merchants.
syair
Classical Malay narrative verse form in four-line rhyming quatrains (AAAA scheme); used for devotional, romance, and historical purposes from at least the early seventeenth century; the Acehnese mystic Hamzah Fansuri is among its earliest masters.
tajug
The multi-tiered pyramidal timber roof form of traditional Javanese and Indonesian mosque architecture, derived from Hindu-Buddhist meru-tower precedent; exemplified by the Great Mosque of Demak (c. 1477–79), regarded as one of the earliest surviving mosques in Java.
tanam paksa
(Indonesian: "forced planting") The Indonesian historiographical designation for the Dutch Cultuurstelsel; the term emphasises the coercive nature of the system. See Cultuurstelsel.
taqlid
(Arabic: "imitation/conformity") Unquestioning conformity to established Islamic legal rulings and the authority of the classical legal schools, as opposed to ijtihad (independent reasoning); defended by traditionalist scholars in Nahdlatul Ulama and opposed by modernists such as Ahmad Dahlan of Muhammadiyah.
tarekat
(Malay/Indonesian, from Arabic tariqa: "path") A Sufi order or brotherhood organised around a master (syaikh) who transmits a chain of spiritual authority (silsila) traced back to the Prophet; attested in indigenous Indonesian sources mainly from the seventeenth century onward; played a major role in the spread of Islam and in anti-colonial resistance.
tempo doeloe
(Malay: "the old time," in Dutch-colonial spelling) A nostalgic evocation of the colonial Indies elite lifestyle — bungalow life, clubs, rijsttafel, veranda evenings — that acquired its distinctively melancholic charge in the retrospective memories of Dutch and Indo-European colonial residents after Indonesian independence; a genre of memoir, photography, and popular culture.
TGPF
Tim Gabungan Pencari Fakta (Joint Fact-Finding Team) — established by President Habibie on 23 July 1998 to investigate the May 1998 riots; its Final Report of 23 October 1998 documented widespread violence including organised sexual violence against ethnic-Chinese women; a founding document of post-New Order human rights accountability.
thalassocracy
(Greek: thalassa, sea + kratos, power) A state whose power rests primarily on control of the sea and maritime trade rather than on territorial agriculture; Srivijaya (7th–13th centuries) is the classic Indonesian example. See also emporium.
Tim Mawar
(Indonesian: "Rose Team") A covert unit of Kopassus Group IV that abducted 23 political activists — student leaders and democracy advocates — between 1997 and 1998; 9 were later released, 1 body was found, and 13 remain missing as of this writing; a defining case of New Order enforced disappearance.
Tipikor
Pengadilan Tindak Pidana Korupsi (Corruption Crimes Court) — Indonesia's specialised anti-corruption tribunal, created alongside the KPK, which hears KPK-prosecuted cases exclusively; achieved early conviction rates approaching 100%; modelled on the Philippines' Sandiganbayan.
Tionghoa
The preferred Indonesian term for ethnic Chinese Indonesians in the post-1998 era, replacing the derogatory colonial-era term Cina in official, scholarly, and increasingly everyday usage after Reformasi; its adoption signals a shift from exclusion toward civic recognition.
TNI (Tentara Nasional Indonesia)
Indonesian National Armed Forces, formally constituted during the National Revolution from Peta, Republican military formations, and pemuda militias; renamed from ABRI after the separation of POLRI in 2000; formally stripped of its Dwifungsi socio-political role and reserved legislative seats under Reformasi constitutional amendments.
Toba catastrophe theory
The hypothesis (Ambrose 1998) that the approximately 74,000 BP super-eruption of the Toba caldera in northern Sumatra caused a volcanic winter and population bottleneck severe enough to reduce global human populations to a small founding group; contested by subsequent archaeological evidence (Petraglia et al. 2007 and later studies) showing human population continuity through the event at sites in India and Africa.
tongkonan
The traditional Toraja clan house of central Sulawesi, raised on piles with a dramatically pitched saddle roof whose upswept gables evoke a boat or buffalo horns; functions simultaneously as residence, clan hall, and ancestral shrine; the social and cosmological centre of Torajan life and the focus of spectacular mortuary ceremonies.
totok
(Malay/Javanese: "pure/full-blooded") Ethnic Chinese Indonesians of more recent migration, or those who maintained Chinese-language use (Hokkien, Teochew, Cantonese), cultural orientation toward China, and homeland-facing identity; contrasted with peranakan (locally acculturated Chinese). The distinction shaped differential roles in colonial and post-colonial commercial and political life.
TPNPB
Tentara Pembebasan Nasional Papua Barat (West Papua National Liberation Army) — the armed wing of the OPM/Free Papua Movement; responsible for sporadic attacks on security forces and foreign workers in Papua from the 2010s; designated a terrorist organisation by Indonesia in April 2021.
transmigrasi
(Indonesian: "transmigration") State-sponsored programme relocating settlers — predominantly Javanese, Balinese, and Madurese — from densely populated inner islands to less-densely populated Outer Islands (Sumatra, Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Papua); begun under the Ethical Policy (1905), vastly expanded under the New Order (c. 5.5 million migrants, 1969–1999); associated with deforestation, land conflict, and demographic transformation in receiving provinces.
Treaty of Bongaya
The 1667 agreement between VOC commander Cornelis Speelman and Sultan Hasanuddin of Gowa (Makassar) ending the VOC-Gowa war; its 30 articles established Dutch commercial monopoly, vassal status for Gowa, the expulsion of rival European and Asian traders, and the construction of Fort Rotterdam as a permanent Dutch garrison.
Trikora
Tri Komando Rakyat ("People's Triple Command") — Sukarno's December 1961 proclamation launching the military campaign to recover West Irian from the Netherlands, comprising three commands: prevent a Dutch puppet state, raise the Indonesian flag in West Irian, and mobilise the people for liberation. Led to the 1962 New York Agreement and the transfer of West Irian to Indonesian administration.
TVRI
Televisi Republik Indonesia (Republic of Indonesia Television) — the state television network established in 1962 in time for the Asian Games in Jakarta; held a monopoly on Indonesian broadcast television until 1989 when private stations were permitted; operated under Ministry of Information editorial guidelines aligned with New Order developmental ideology.
ulama
(Arabic plural of ʿālim: "scholar") Muslim religious scholars trained in Islamic law (fiqh), theology, and Quranic interpretation; networks of ulama linking the archipelago to the Haramayn (Mecca and Medina) were the primary conduit of Islamic learning and reform from the seventeenth century; played formal roles as qadi (judges) and mufti (jurisconsults) at Acehnese and other courts.
uleebalang
(Acehnese) Hereditary territorial chiefs of Aceh who held authority at the village and district level under customary (adat) law and through control of land and trade; the Dutch identified uleebalang as co-optable counterweights to the ulama; their authority was largely eliminated in the Acehnese social revolution of December 1945 – March 1946, in which many were killed.
ULMWP
United Liberation Movement for West Papua, founded 7 December 2014 in Vanuatu; an unarmed political umbrella body seeking West Papuan independence through international diplomacy and MSG engagement, led from exile by Benny Wenda; observer status in the Melanesian Spearhead Group from 2015.
UNAMET
United Nations Mission in East Timor — the UN body that organised and administered the August 1999 independence referendum; the 78.5% vote for independence triggered a wave of Indonesian military-backed militia violence, leading to INTERFET's deployment.
undha-usuk
(Javanese: "ascending-descending steps") The Javanese speech-level system encoding social hierarchy in vocabulary choice rather than grammar; the principal registers are ngoko (informal/low, used to subordinates and intimates) and krama (formal/high, used to superiors and strangers), with honorific (krama inggil) and humble (krama andhap) sub-registers.
UNTEA
United Nations Temporary Executive Authority — the UN transitional administration that governed West New Guinea from 1 October 1962 to 1 May 1963 under the terms of the New York Agreement, before transferring the territory to Indonesian administration pending the Pepera.
UUD 1945
Undang-Undang Dasar 1945 (1945 Constitution) of the Republic of Indonesia, adopted by the PPKI on 18 August 1945; a brief document of 37 articles establishing a strong presidential executive; in force 1945–49, restored by Sukarno's decree in 1959, and fundamentally amended in four rounds between 1999 and 2002 to introduce direct elections, regional autonomy, and human rights provisions.
UUDS 1950
Undang-Undang Dasar Sementara (Provisional Constitution of 1950), governing Indonesia 17 August 1950 to 5 July 1959; established parliamentary cabinet government, extensive civil liberties, and an independent judiciary, pending adoption of a permanent constitution by the Konstituante; abrogated by Sukarno's presidential decree restoring the 1945 Constitution.
Van Mook line
The military demarcation line imposed after the first Dutch Police Action (August 1947), named after Lieutenant Governor-General Hubertus van Mook; defined the territories under Dutch vs. Republican control; left the Republic controlling only a reduced portion of Java and parts of Sumatra until the second Police Action.
Vergaan Onder Corruptie
(Dutch: "Perished Under Corruption") A popular eighteenth-century inversion of the VOC's initials (standing for Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie), satirising the systematic embezzlement, nepotism, and illegal private trading that characterised the company in its final decades before dissolution in 1799.
VOC
Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (Dutch East India Company), chartered by the States General of the United Provinces on 20 March 1602; the world's first publicly traded joint-stock company; held a 21-year renewable monopoly on Dutch trade and navigation east of the Cape of Good Hope; exercised quasi-sovereign powers including war, treaty-making, and coinage. Dissolved and nationalised in 1799.
voorcompagnieën
(Dutch: "pre-companies") The competing Dutch merchant companies that sent approximately 15 fleets to Asia between 1595 and 1601; their ruinous price competition in Asian spice markets and mutual hostility prompted the States General to charter the unified VOC monopoly in 1602.
Vreemde Oosterlingen
(Dutch: "Foreign Orientals") The colonial legal category in the Netherlands East Indies encompassing ethnic Chinese, Arabs, South Asians, and other non-indigenous, non-European Asians; positioned between Europeans and Inlanders in the tripartite legal hierarchy; subject to a hybrid regime combining elements of Dutch civil law and their own community law.
wahdat al-wujud
(Arabic: "unity of being") The Sufi metaphysical doctrine — associated with the Andalusian mystic Ibn ʿArabī (1165–1240) — holding that all existence is a manifestation of a single divine reality; central to Malay and Javanese Islamic mysticism and a recurring point of dispute between heterodox and orthodox Islamic currents in the archipelago.
Wallace Line
The biogeographical boundary running through the Lombok Strait (between Bali and Lombok) and the Makassar Strait (between Borneo and Sulawesi), identified by Alfred Russel Wallace during his 1854–62 fieldwork; separates the Asian-continental fauna and flora to the west from the Australasian fauna and flora to the east.
Wallacea
The biogeographic transition zone of central Indonesia — principally Sulawesi, Maluku, and the Lesser Sunda Islands east of Bali — that was never connected to either the Asian (Sunda) or the Australasian (Sahul) continental shelves by dry land during Pleistocene sea-level lowstands; noted for extreme species endemism and relevance to debates about early human migration.
Wali Songo
(Javanese/Indonesian: "Nine Saints" or "Nine Guardians") The nine revered Sufi teachers traditionally credited with the Islamisation of Java in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, associated with the Demak Sultanate; remembered for spreading Islam through Javanese cultural forms (wayang, gamelan, architecture). Each has a recognised grave site that remains a major pilgrimage destination.
wayang
(Javanese: "shadow/puppet") Traditional puppet theatre of Java and Bali; the paramount form is wayang kulit (shadow puppet theatre), in which flat leather puppets are manipulated by the dalang behind a backlit screen. Episodes are drawn primarily from the Hindu epics Mahabharata and Ramayana and from the Javanese Panji cycle. UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage since 2008.
wijkenstelsel
(Dutch: "quarter system") The colonial Netherlands East Indies residential-segregation system requiring non-European inhabitants to reside within designated ethnic neighbourhoods — pecinan for Chinese, kampung Arab for Arabs — in colonial cities; formalised in the early nineteenth century and formally abolished in 1919, though the residential patterns it created persisted.
Yayasan
(Indonesian: "foundation") A charitable or nonprofit foundation; under the New Order, dozens of yayasan ostensibly devoted to welfare, education, or religion — including the Suharto family's Yayasan Supersemar, Dharmais, and others — functioned as compulsory collection vehicles for the family's personal enrichment, estimated at US$15–35 billion.
yupa
(Sanskrit: "sacrificial post") A Vedic ritual pillar; specifically, the seven Sanskrit-inscribed stone pillars of Kutai in East Kalimantan (c. 400 CE), recording the ritual donations of King Mulavarman; the earliest dated writing from the Indonesian archipelago and the earliest evidence of Indic influence in the region.
