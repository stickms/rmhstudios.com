Religion in the Indonesian archipelago is not one story but many, unfolding across three thousand inhabited islands over two millennia, and the act of narrating it carries its own risks of simplification. No other nation on earth accommodates such a variety of living religious traditions within a single constitutional framework: here Islam, represented by more than 235 million adherents and internally fractured across modernist, traditionalist, and Islamist lines; there Balinese Hinduism, a synthesis of Shaivite cosmology, Buddhist iconography, and animist spirit-world that has survived six centuries of Islamization on the surrounding islands; alongside them a Protestant Christianity rooted in the granite highlands of Toba Batak country, a Catholicism that arrived with Portuguese spice traders in the sixteenth century, and a Confucian tradition that was formally recognized, stripped of recognition, and recognized again over the course of a single generation. Beneath all of them — and in many places still actively shaping daily practice — lie the older spirit-world traditions that predated India's cultural exports and the Prophet's revelation alike, and that have never been fully displaced.1 Understanding this landscape requires attending to the deep time of pre-Hindu animism, to the classical era of Indic civilization, to the long Islamization of the archipelago's commercial coasts, to the European missions that took root in the east, and to the political negotiations through which the independent Republic of Indonesia attempted — with partial and contested success — to manage diversity through the doctrine of Pancasila.

Pre-Hindu and Pre-Islamic Foundations: Spirit Worlds and Ancestor Cults

Before the first Sanskrit inscription was carved on the banks of an East Kalimantan river in the early fifth century CE, the peoples of the archipelago had constructed elaborate spiritual cosmologies organized around the proposition that the world of the living was permeable to — and dependent upon — the world of the dead and the non-human. Ancestor veneration was the central ritual institution of this world: the spirits of deceased forebears were not simply memorialized but actively engaged through offerings, invocations, and ceremonies whose purpose was to maintain their benevolence and forestall their potentially malevolent attention.2 Spirit categories proliferated: mountain spirits, river spirits, the spirits of trees of particular size or location, house-spirits, field-spirits whose cooperation was indispensable to agricultural success. The ritual specialist — known across different societies by different names, but broadly identifiable as the shaman or medium — served as the technician of this communication, entering trance states in which contact with spirit interlocutors became possible.

What is remarkable about the history of religion in Indonesia is not that these traditions were replaced by the world religions that successively arrived — Hinduism, Buddhism, Islam, and Christianity — but that they were not. Anthony Reid, in his foundational study Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce (Yale University Press, 1990), observed that the "coming of Islam" to the archipelago was less a displacement of indigenous cosmology than an overlay upon it, in which Islamic ritual vocabulary and Islamic moral teaching were progressively adopted while underlying assumptions about the spirit world, ancestral efficacy, and the proper management of invisible forces continued to structure daily practice.3 The same pattern had operated, centuries earlier, with the spread of Indic religion. The result, visible in communities across Indonesia from Aceh to Papua, is a layering of religious traditions in which the most recently arrived formal religion supplies the language of public self-identification and communal obligation while older spirit-world logics persist in domestic ritual, healing practice, agricultural ceremony, and the management of life transitions.

This persistence is not simply "survivalism" or incomplete conversion. The anthropologist Anna Tsing, in her ethnographic work among the Meratus Dayak of South Kalimantan, documented how animist practice actively engages with and reinterprets modernity, demonstrating that so-called "premodern" religious forms maintain their vitality precisely because they address questions that universal religions do not — questions about the specific spirits of specific places, about the genealogical obligations linking the living to particular dead, about the negotiation of local ecological relationships that no globally formulated creed can specify.4 The practical import is that in any analysis of religion in Indonesia, the layer that anthropologists call "folk religion" or "popular religion" cannot be treated as mere residue; it constitutes a continuing stratum of religious life that shapes the practice even of devout Muslims, Christians, and Hindus.

Hinduism and Buddhism in the Classical Era: Indianization and Its Limits

The arrival of Indic religious culture in the archipelago is conventionally dated to the earliest inscriptions, and the historiographic debate over how this arrival should be interpreted has occupied scholars for over a century. The French scholar George Coedès, in his monumental synthesis The Indianized States of Southeast Asia (originally published in French in 1948, English translation published by the University of Hawaii Press in 1968), proposed the concept of "Indianization" to describe the spread of Indian political, religious, and cultural forms across mainland and maritime Southeast Asia.5 For Coedès, this process involved the implantation of Indian cosmological concepts of kingship — the king as devaraja, divine king, whose spiritual potency guaranteed cosmic order and agricultural prosperity — alongside the Sanskrit language, the Brahmanical ritual complex, and Hindu-Buddhist iconography. The Indianization model implied an essentially passive Southeast Asian substrate upon which Indian civilization stamped its imprint, whether through direct colonization, trade-based cultural diffusion, or the deliberate importation of Brahmanical specialists by local rulers seeking legitimating ideologies.

This diffusionist model was subjected to sustained critique in the later twentieth century by scholars who emphasized the active role of local rulers and societies in selecting, adapting, and transforming Indic elements for their own purposes. The debate is sometimes framed as "Indianization" versus "localization." O.W. Wolters, in his influential concept of "mandala" polities, argued that Southeast Asian rulers were not passive recipients of Indian ideology but active agents who deployed Indic religious language to express and reinforce indigenous political relationships organized around the personal charisma of rulers rather than the territorial sovereignty of states.6 On this reading, what looks like Hinduism or Buddhism in the early kingdoms of the archipelago is better understood as a local political culture that has borrowed Indic vocabulary while retaining fundamentally Southeast Asian organizational logics.

The earliest epigraphic evidence in the archipelago comes from the Kutai kingdom in East Kalimantan, where seven stone inscriptions in Sanskrit using Pallava script — associated with South India and datable to c. 375–400 CE — describe a ruler named Mulawarman conducting Brahmanical sacrificial rituals involving the gift of cattle to priests.7 These inscriptions, the oldest known in the archipelago, testify to a court culture that had adopted at least the outward forms of Brahmanical Hinduism, including the Sanskrit language and the institution of ritual gift-giving that reproduced Indic models of cosmic kingship. Somewhat later, the kingdom of Tarumanagara in West Java — attested by at least seven stone inscriptions datable to the reign of King Purnawarman (c. 450 CE) — produced the earliest known inscriptions in Java, which associate the king with Vishnu and describe public works projects framed in Sanskritic cosmological terms.7 These early polities demonstrate that Indic cultural influence had reached the archipelago by the fifth century at the latest, but their thin documentary record prevents confident conclusions about the depth of Hinduism's penetration into the wider population.

The fullest expression of classical Indic civilization on Java is found in the magnificent temple complexes of the eighth and ninth centuries, products of the two dynasties that competed for dominance in central Java: the Sailendra and the Sanjaya. The Sailendra, whose name derives from the Sanskrit for "Lords of the Mountain," were associated with Mahāyāna Buddhism and were responsible for the construction of Borobudur, the largest Buddhist monument in the world, built on the Kedu Plain in central Java between approximately 780 and 840 CE.8 Borobudur is constructed as a three-dimensional mandala, a cosmological diagram in stone, whose nine stacked platforms represent the Buddhist cosmological hierarchy from the sphere of desire at the base through the sphere of form to the formless sphere at the crown, culminating in a great central stupa containing an unfinished or deliberately empty Buddha. The monument's 2,672 bas-relief panels and approximately 504 Buddha statues make it the world's most concentrated site of Buddhist sculpture. The pilgrim's circumambulation of each level was a ritual journey toward enlightenment. James Blake Wiener, writing for World History Encyclopedia, notes that Borobudur was constructed during the Sailendra dynasty's rule and remains "the world's largest Buddhist temple," abandoned in the 1400s as Islamic conversion accelerated before its "rediscovery" by British colonial administrators in 1814.8

The Sanjaya dynasty, by contrast, was associated with Shaivite Hinduism and competed with the Sailendra for dominance in central Java during the same period. The culminating monument of Sanjaya power was Prambanan, the largest Hindu temple complex in ancient Java and the second largest in Southeast Asia, whose construction began under Rakai Pikatan around 850 CE and was completed under subsequent rulers.9 The Prambanan complex is dedicated to the Trimurti — Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva — with the towering central temple of Shiva (Loro Jonggrang) flanked by temples for his attendants. The coexistence of these two great monuments — Buddhist Borobudur and Hindu Prambanan — within a few dozen kilometers of each other reflects what De Casparis and other historians have described as a period of political coexistence and theological pluralism in which Sailendra and Sanjaya rulers shared power in central Java for a century and a half, their differing religious patronage coexisting without apparent sectarian violence.9 This period of Hindu-Buddhist pluralism established a precedent for the syncretic religious culture that would characterize Javanese civilization for centuries.

Contemporaneous with the temple-building of central Java, the kingdom of Srivijaya in southern Sumatra — centered on Palembang from the seventh century CE — developed as the dominant Buddhist polity of maritime Southeast Asia. Srivijaya's power derived from its control of the strategic Strait of Malacca, through which the trade between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea necessarily passed, and from its cultivation of Buddhism as both a religious ideal and a diplomatic instrument. The Chinese Buddhist monk Yi Jing, who visited Palembang in 671 CE while traveling to India to study Sanskrit Buddhist texts, described finding more than a thousand Buddhist scholars in residence and recommended that Chinese monks traveling to India spend at least a year there to study Sanskrit grammar and Buddhist philosophy — testimony to the intellectual weight of Srivijayan Buddhist scholarship.10 Srivijayan rulers maintained diplomatic relations with Indian Buddhist institutions, sent missions to the Tang court in China, and patronized the construction of Buddhist monasteries and educational institutions across the maritime world. The kingdom's famous Kedukan Bukit inscription of 682 CE, composed in Old Malay, is the earliest known document in the Malay language and attests to the cosmopolitan court culture that combined Indic religious ideology with Southeast Asian commercial and maritime enterprise.

The late Majapahit period (founded 1293 CE) in Java represented the fullest development of the Hindu-Buddhist synthesis. The court epic Nagarakretagama, composed by the court poet Mpu Prapanca in 1365 during the reign of King Hayam Wuruk, provides the most detailed indigenous account of Majapahit's religious culture.11 The text describes a state religion organized around the Siwa-Buddha doctrine — a uniquely Javanese theological synthesis in which Shiva and the Buddha were understood as complementary aspects of the supreme divine reality — whose institutional expression included both Hindu Brahmanical and Buddhist monastic establishments receiving royal patronage simultaneously. Prapanca's poem depicts a king who is simultaneously the avatar of Vishnu and the emanation of the Buddha, whose ritual authority encompasses both Hindu and Buddhist ceremonial systems. This syncretic theological framework, sometimes called "Siwa-Buddha," was not understood by its practitioners as syncretism in the modern sense of an eclectic mixture of incompatible elements, but rather as the expression of a unitary divine truth in its Hindu and Buddhist modes simultaneously — an approach to religious pluralism deeply embedded in the Javanese intellectual tradition.

The Survival of Hindu-Balinese Culture

As Islam spread across Java from the late fifteenth century onward, the Hindu-Javanese court tradition did not simply disappear; it migrated east to Bali, where it took root in the form that is now known as Balinese Hinduism or Agama Hindu Dharma. The historical process involved successive waves of migration of Javanese Hindu-Buddhist elites — priests, poets, court musicians, dancers, and members of the aristocracy — who fled the advancing Muslim sultanates and brought their literary traditions, ritual practices, temple architectures, and genealogical claims with them to the small island to the east of Java's volcanic chain.12 The Majapahit conquest of Bali in 1343, under the great prime minister Gajah Mada, had already established a significant Javanese Hindu presence on the island; the later migrations after the fall of Majapahit in the late fifteenth century deepened and consolidated it. Bali thus preserves, in uniquely developed form, a Javanese Hindu cultural world that has been elsewhere largely obscured by subsequent Islamization.

Balinese Hinduism is distinctive in ways that reflect both its Indic heritage and its deep integration with pre-Hindu animist traditions. The ritual system centers on the pura (temple) as the site of communal worship, with each Balinese village maintaining multiple temples organized around the veneration of deified ancestors, the gods of the Hindu pantheon, and the local spirits of the landscape. Bali's elaborate calendrical system — the intersection of a 210-day sacred calendar (wuku) and a 365-day solar calendar — generates a continuous cycle of festival observances that provide the primary framework of collective religious life. The great temple of Pura Besakih on the slopes of Mount Agung, regarded as the "mother temple" of Balinese Hinduism, serves as the supreme ritual center for the entire island, its multiple shrines dedicated to the Trimurti and the deified royal ancestors of Bali's principal dynasties.12

The Tengger community of Mount Bromo in East Java represents a different — and in some ways even more remarkable — case of Hindu survival. Approximately 100,000 Tenggerese people, living in the isolated highlands of the Bromo-Tengger-Semeru National Park, have maintained a form of Javanese Hinduism rooted in Majapahit-era traditions since the pre-Islamic period, observing a distinctive ritual calendar centered on the annual Yadnya Kasada festival, in which offerings are cast into the active crater of Mount Bromo as gifts to the mountain deity Mahadeva and the cosmic lord Hyang Widi Wasa.13 The Tengger are the only Javanese ethnic group to have remained Hindu since the Majapahit era and their survival represents an extraordinary case of religious continuity maintained through geographic isolation and strong communal identity in the face of centuries of surrounding Islamization.

Siwa-Buddha — the court theology of late Hindu-Buddhist Java, especially associated with the Majapahit empire (founded 1293 CE), in which Shiva and the Buddha were understood as complementary aspects of the supreme divine reality rather than as competing deities. Detailed in the Nagarakretagama of Mpu Prapanca (1365), this syncretic doctrine is expressed in the dual royal title Jinamurti-Shivamurti. It established a theological precedent for pluralist religious coexistence that has influenced Indonesian Islamic and nationalist thought.

The Coming of Islam: Ports, Sufis, and Saints

The Islamization of the Indonesian archipelago was a process that unfolded over more than four centuries — from the establishment of the first sultanate at Samudra Pasai in north Sumatra in the late thirteenth century through the conversion of most of Java's coastal populations by the early seventeenth century — and its character was decisively shaped by the commercial geography of the Indian Ocean world. Islam arrived in the archipelago not primarily through conquest or coordinated missionary enterprise but through the networks of Muslim merchants who had become dominant in the Indian Ocean trade routes from the eleventh century onward, and who established Muslim communities in the port cities of the archipelago as their commercial activities grew.14 The ruler of Samudra Pasai — the first polity in insular Southeast Asia to adopt Islam as a state religion, with its founding conventionally dated to c. 1267 CE through the grave inscription of Sultan Malik al-Salih — converted for reasons that were simultaneously commercial and political: to align with the dominant traders of the Indian Ocean system and to access the networks of Islamic scholarship and political legitimation centered in the holy cities of Arabia.15

Anthony Reid's analysis in Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce frames the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries as the crucial period of accelerated Islamization, coinciding with — and partly caused by — the boom in spice trade that drew Southeast Asian port cities into an intensifying commercial and cultural relationship with the wider Islamic world. The fall of Malacca to the Portuguese in 1511 disrupted but did not halt this process; Muslim merchants and scholars simply relocated to other ports — Aceh, Demak, Banten, Gresik — that continued to serve as nodes of Islamic cultural transmission. By the sixteenth century, the north coast of Java was a series of Muslim sultanates, and the interior Hindu kingdom of Majapahit — weakened by political fragmentation and the diversion of commercial revenue to the new coastal polities — was in terminal decline.

The character of Indonesian Islam as it developed through this period was profoundly shaped by Sufism — the mystical dimension of Islam that emphasizes the inner spiritual path toward union with God, the cultivation of direct spiritual experience, and the mediation of saintly figures (wali) who serve as conduits of divine grace. Sufi orders (tariqa), particularly the Qadiriyya and Naqshbandiyya, established themselves across the archipelago from at least the fifteenth century and played a central role in the actual work of conversion. The Sufi approach to conversion was strategically well adapted to the social and cultural environment of the archipelago: its emphasis on inner spiritual transformation rather than strict legal conformity, its cultivation of charismatic saints whose tombs became pilgrimage sites, and its capacity to incorporate elements of existing spiritual practice within an Islamic framework all made Sufi Islam accessible and attractive to populations whose existing spiritual life was organized around precisely these features — the veneration of spiritually potent individuals, the use of sacred sites as foci of ritual practice, and the cultivation of mystical states through meditation and ritual performance.16

In Javanese popular tradition, the primary agents of this Sufi-inflected conversion are the wali songo — the Nine Saints, a revered group of Muslim missionaries to whom the conversion of Java is attributed by popular memory and whose graves remain major pilgrimage sites.17 Historical scholarship is cautious about the wali songo as a literal historical entity — the precise membership of the group and the historical details of their activities are embedded in traditions that mix history and legend — but there is no doubt that the period of Java's conversion (roughly the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries) did produce a group of extraordinarily influential Muslim scholars and teachers, some of Javanese origin and some from the wider Islamic world, whose tombs at Gresik, Ampel, Bonang, Demak, Kudus, Giri, and other locations remain among the most visited religious sites in Java. The establishment of the Demak Sultanate in the early sixteenth century under the patronage of Sunan Kalijaga is historically well attested, and the Masjid Agung Demak, founded in the first years of the sixteenth century and regarded as Java's first great mosque, continues to function as a major center of Islamic ritual life and pilgrimage.17

wali songo — the "Nine Saints," a group of revered Sufi teachers traditionally credited with the Islamization of Java during the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. While the historical record is intertwined with hagiographic tradition, each member of the group has a recognized grave site that functions as a major pilgrimage destination. The wali songo tradition embodies the Sufi approach to conversion characteristic of Indonesian Islam: emphasis on inner spiritual transformation, accommodation of local cultural forms, and the mediation of saintly figures as conduits of divine grace.

The Santri/Abangan Debate: Geertz, Woodward, Hefner, and Ricklefs

No scholarly framework has done more to shape outside understanding of Indonesian Islam — and no framework has generated more sustained critique — than Clifford Geertz's tripartite model of Javanese religious life, set out in his 1960 monograph The Religion of Java (University of Chicago Press, 1960; reprint 1976).18 Based on fieldwork conducted in the town he called "Modjokuto" (identified as Pare in East Java) in the early 1950s, Geertz divided Javanese society into three religious variants or "streams" (aliran): the abangan, identified with the Javanese peasantry and characterized by a syncretic blend of animist, Hindu-Buddhist, and Islamic elements centered on the communal feast (slametan); the santri, identified with pious orthodox Muslims and the merchant class, organized around the mosque, the Quranic school (pesantren), and conformity to Islamic legal norms; and the priyayi, the Javanese aristocracy and administrative class, characterized by Hindu-Buddhist-derived court culture, ethical mysticism, and aesthetic refinement.

Geertz's model offered an elegant and superficially compelling framework for explaining Javanese religious diversity, and it proved enormously influential in both academic and policy circles. Yet it attracted sustained criticism almost from the moment of publication, and the critique has intensified with time. Mark Woodward's Islam in Java: Normative Piety and Mysticism in the Sultanate of Yogyakarta (University of Arizona Press, 1989) challenged Geertz's characterization of abangan practice as essentially non-Islamic, arguing that the slametan feast and other practices Geertz had attributed to a pre-Islamic Javanese substratum were in fact thoroughly Islamic practices whose apparent syncretism reflected the Sufi theological frameworks within which their practitioners understood them rather than a failure to become properly Muslim.19 Woodward's argument implied that Geertz had misread the Islamic content of Javanese practice because he had used an externally derived criterion of Islamic orthodoxy — essentially a Middle Eastern Sunni criterion — that was inappropriate to the Sufi-inflected, locally adapted Islam that the majority of Javanese actually practiced.

Robert W. Hefner's Civil Islam: Muslims and Democratization in Indonesia (Princeton University Press, 2000) approached the question from a different angle, situating the religious variation Geertz had mapped within a long-run analysis of the politics of Muslim civil society.20 Hefner argued that Indonesia's Muslim community had, over the course of the twentieth century, developed a robust tradition of democratic civic engagement — what he called "civil Islam" — that drew on Quranic values of consultation, equity, and community welfare to support democratic norms rather than to oppose them. This was not despite Islam's deep roots in Indonesian society but because of the way those roots had grown: in the decentralized, pluralist, Sufi-inflected soil of the archipelago's commercial culture, not in the hierarchical, state-centered frameworks of the Arab world or the theocratic visions of the Islamist political movements. Hefner's account directly challenged the assumption embedded in Geertz's framework that Javanese Islam was somehow deficient or insufficiently Islamic; the "deficiencies" were, on Hefner's reading, precisely what made Indonesian Islam democratically productive.

M.C. Ricklefs, in his trilogy on the Islamization of Java — of which Islamisation and Its Opponents in Java: A Political, Social, Cultural and Religious History, c. 1930 to the Present (NUS Press, 2012) is the concluding volume — traced a long historical process through which Java's population shifted from largely nominal Islamic identification toward a more pervasive, practised religiosity, while simultaneously generating vigorous opposition in the form of mystical traditions, kejawen movements, and secular nationalism.21 Ricklefs's argument complicates both the Geertz portrait of a static abangan/santri division and the triumphalist narrative of progressive Islamization: what he documents is a dialectical process in which rising Islamic piety generated both its own deepening and an intensification of the mystical and syncretic alternatives to it.

The broader scholarly consensus that has emerged from this debate is that Geertz's framework, while a productive heuristic for its time, misrepresented the nature of Indonesian Islam in at least three important ways: it treated Islamic practices through a normative lens derived from Middle Eastern orthodoxy rather than from the practices' own internal logics; it reified what were actually fluid and overlapping social categories into rigid types; and it dated the abangan-santri distinction to an ancient pre-Islamic substrate when in fact the distinction was substantially a product of the political and social conflicts of the colonial and post-colonial period. The category of abangan — as Ahmad Najib Burhani and others have argued — was never a self-ascription; it was an ascription by santri critics who used it to delegitimize the religious practices of communities they regarded as insufficiently orthodox.22

santri — in Geertz's influential but contested typology, Javanese Muslims characterized by strict observance of Islamic law (syariah), mosque attendance, Quranic education, and identification with the formal institutions of Islamic community life. The term is also used non-typologically to refer to students at Islamic boarding schools (pesantren). Subsequent scholars, particularly Mark Woodward and M.C. Ricklefs, have questioned the sharp dichotomy between santri and abangan that Geertz constructed, arguing it reifies a political distinction and misrepresents the Islamic character of supposedly "abangan" practices.

NU and Muhammadiyah: Institutional Islam and Islam Nusantara

The two organizational giants of Indonesian Islam — Nahdlatul Ulama and Muhammadiyah — represent not merely competing theological orientations but different visions of what the relationship between Islam, culture, and the modern world should be, and their rivalry and cooperation has shaped Indonesian public life for over a century.

Muhammadiyah was founded on November 18, 1912, in Yogyakarta by the religious scholar Ahmad Dahlan, who had been deeply influenced by the Islamic reformist currents circulating in Cairo under the influence of Muhammad Abduh and Rashid Rida — currents that called for the purification of Muslim practice from what they identified as syncretic accretions, superstition, and blind deference to medieval legal schools, and for a return to direct engagement with the Quranic text through independent reasoning (ijtihad). Muhammadiyah was thus from its origins a modernist organization, critical of the saint-veneration, grave-visiting, and syncretic ritual practices associated with the pesantren tradition and the Sufi orders, and committed to an agenda of social transformation through modern education, healthcare, and welfare institutions organized on Islamic principles. By the twenty-first century it managed thousands of schools, more than 170 universities, and hundreds of hospitals and clinics — one of the world's largest private educational and social welfare networks. Its membership, estimated at between 29 and 35 million in the early 2000s, was concentrated in urban and middle-class communities.23

Nahdlatul Ulama — the "Revival of Religious Scholars" — was founded fourteen years later, on January 31, 1926, in Surabaya, East Java, by a coalition of traditionalist ulama led by the revered scholar Kiai Hasyim Asy'ari. Its founding was explicitly reactive: a response to the Wahhabi reformism that had just triumphed in the Arabian Peninsula with the Saudi conquest of the Hejaz (1924–25), threatening the traditional pilgrimage practices and Islamic cultural patrimony that the pesantren tradition cherished, and a response to the modernist pressure that Muhammadiyah represented within Indonesia. NU championed the Sunni traditionalist tradition — adherence to the four classical schools of jurisprudence, veneration of saints and graves, observance of communal prayers for the dead (tahlilan), and the integration of Javanese and broader Nusantara cultural practices into Islamic life. Its primary organizational base was the vast network of Islamic boarding schools (pesantren) in rural Java, whose teachers and students constituted the core of its institutional constituency. By the early twenty-first century NU's membership and affiliated constituency was estimated at between 60 and 90 million — making it one of the largest religious organizations of any kind in the world.24

The concept of Islam Nusantara — "Archipelago Islam" — emerged in the late 2000s primarily through the intellectual work of scholars and activists close to NU as a theological and civilizational framework for understanding and promoting the distinctively Indonesian form of Islam that had developed over six centuries of synthesis between Islamic teaching and local cultural tradition. Islam Nusantara is not presented by its advocates as a new legal school or a new sect, but as a historically grounded methodology: the recognition that Islamic teaching, when it arrived in the archipelago, entered into productive dialogue with existing cultural traditions, absorbing their positive elements and adapting its expression to local contexts, rather than imposing a culturally specific Arabian form of religious life. This dialogue, NU scholars argue, has produced an Islam that is authentically faithful to the Quran and Sunna while being distinctively hospitable to pluralism, cultural diversity, and civic tolerance — an Islam "with a Nusantara face."25 NU has explicitly promoted Islam Nusantara as a counter-narrative to Salafi and Islamist interpretations, both within Indonesia and increasingly in international dialogue.

The NU-Muhammadiyah relationship has been marked by long-standing theological disagreement — over the permissibility of traditional practices, the authority of classical scholarship, and attitudes toward Sufi orders — but also by fundamental convergence on the question of the Indonesian state. Both organizations have historically rejected the goal of an Islamic state and the imposition of sharia law through constitutional means, a position that sets them apart from Islamist political movements that emerged in strength after 1998. The political scientist Jeremy Menchik, writing on what he calls "godly nationalism," argued that this convergence reflects a distinctive normative framework in which tolerance operates at the communal rather than the individual level: recognized religious communities are accorded legitimacy within the Pancasila framework, even if the liberal individualist pluralism characteristic of Western secularism is not the operative norm.26

Christianity across the Archipelago: From Portuguese Maluku to Toba Batak

Christianity's history in the archipelago begins with the Portuguese, whose capture of Malacca in 1511 gave them a strategic base from which to extend their commercial and missionary enterprise into the spice islands of the east. The first Catholic baptisms in the archipelago were performed by lay Portuguese traders in Halmahera in 1534, and from 1546 to 1547 the Jesuit missionary Francis Xavier spent fourteen months in Indonesia, visiting Ternate and North Sulawesi and founding a minor seminary in the Moluccas. From the 1550s onward, Dominican missionaries operating from Malacca established missions in Flores, Timor, Solor, and Alor — the cluster of islands in eastern Indonesia where spice trade and Christian mission overlapped — and by 1599 there were more than 22,000 Catholics in the archipelago, with the number growing to some 50,000 by the early seventeenth century spread across Maluku and the Lesser Sunda Islands.27

The Dutch displacement of the Portuguese in the seventeenth century transformed the Christian landscape of the archipelago. The VOC suppressed Catholicism in territories it controlled, replacing the Portuguese Catholic infrastructure with Dutch Protestant institutions — in Ambon, in the North Moluccas, in Banda, and in the Minahasa region of North Sulawesi, which became the most intensively Christianized region in the archipelago under Dutch colonial management. In Minahasa, as Ariel Lopez has argued, mass conversions to Christianity in the 1840s and 1850s were driven not simply by missionary activity but by the structural effects of Dutch colonial administrative reforms that centralized authority and created new incentives for local leaders to affiliate with the Dutch-Protestant institutional complex.28 The Gereja Masehi Injili di Minahasa (GMIM), formed in 1934 from Reformed missionary foundations, grew to approximately 850,000 members in North Sulawesi, and Minahasa became a region in which Christian identity served as a primary marker of ethnic and regional distinctiveness vis-à-vis the predominantly Muslim majority of the Indonesian nation.

The most dramatic and best-documented case of large-scale Christian conversion in the nineteenth-century archipelago was the Batak mission in the highlands of North Sumatra, transformed by the work of Ludwig Ingwer Nommensen (1834–1918) of the German Rhenish Missionary Society. Nommensen arrived in the Batak region in 1864, having been preceded by initial Rhenish mission contacts from 1861, and spent the remaining fifty-four years of his life in North Sumatra, becoming known to generations of Batak Christians as the "Apostle to the Batak." His approach combined Lutheran theological commitments with a pragmatic respect for Batak cultural norms: rather than demanding the abandonment of adat (customary law), Nommensen sought to "baptize" it — incorporating Batak social structures, clan organization, and customary ceremonies into the life of the new church to the extent this could be reconciled with Christian ethics. He founded a village called Huta Dame (Village of Peace), translated the Bible into the Batak language, and developed a church organizational structure that drew on the authority patterns of Batak society. By the time of his death in 1918, his Huria Kristen Batak Protestan (HKBP — Batak Christian Protestant Church) comprised 180,000 members, with 34 ministers and 788 teachers and preachers.29

The HKBP grew through the twentieth century to become one of the largest Lutheran churches in the world, with an estimated four million members by the twenty-first century. Its growth was accompanied by the emergence of a distinctly Batak Protestant identity in which Christianity, Batak ethnicity, and clan culture were deeply intertwined — a synthesis not unlike the relationship between the Minahasa church and Minahasan ethnic identity. This pattern — in which Christian conversion did not produce religious assimilation to a generic international Christianity but rather the formation of a distinctly local Christian culture fusing Christian theology with indigenous social structures — was replicated with variations in the Flores Catholic community (where the Christianization of the sixteenth century merged with the cosmological systems of the local Manggarai and Ngada peoples), in East Timor (where Catholicism became so central to Timorese identity that it served as a primary marker of opposition to Indonesian occupation after 1975), and in Papua (where Protestant and Catholic missions from the nineteenth century onward produced a Christian identity now central to Papuan cultural self-understanding).30

As of the 2020 census, Protestants constituted approximately 7.5 percent of Indonesia's national population, Catholics 3.1 percent — totaling more than 28 million Christians. Their geographic distribution is highly uneven: eastern Indonesia (Maluku, Papua, East Nusa Tenggara, North Sulawesi) is predominantly Christian, while western and central Indonesia (Sumatra, Java, Kalimantan, West Nusa Tenggara) is predominantly Muslim. This geographic pattern of religious difference has been a significant factor in regional tensions and in the inter-communal violence that erupted in parts of Maluku, Central Sulawesi, and Kalimantan during the political transition of the late 1990s and early 2000s.

The Pancasila Settlement: Seven Words, Six Religions, and the Limits of Pluralism

The constitutional framework within which Indonesia manages its religious plurality was forged in the crucible of the independence settlement, in a debate whose resolution has shaped every subsequent contest over the place of religion in Indonesian public life. In June 1945, as the preparatory committee for Indonesian independence deliberated over the philosophical foundation of the new state, Sukarno presented his concept of Pancasila — Five Principles — including as the first principle a capacious theistic commitment he described as Ketuhanan, belief in God, deliberately formulated to accommodate all the religious traditions of the archipelago rather than to privilege any one of them. This original formulation was acceptable to secular nationalists and to the representatives of Christian, Hindu, and Buddhist communities, but it was perceived by Muslim political leaders as insufficiently recognizing the centrality of Islam to Indonesian national identity and the obligation of Muslims to live under Islamic law.31

The compromise document that emerged from negotiations on June 22, 1945 — known as the Jakarta Charter (Piagam Jakarta) — contained the five Pancasila principles in modified form, but appended to the first principle, Ketuhanan, a clause of seven words in Indonesian: dengan kewajiban menjalankan syariat Islam bagi pemeluk-pemeluknya — "with the obligation for adherents of Islam to carry out Islamic law." This formulation, which would have imposed a constitutional obligation of Islamic law observance on approximately 90 percent of the population while excluding non-Muslims from the state's foundational covenant, represented a significant concession to Muslim political demands.32 But on the morning of August 18, 1945 — one day after the proclamation of independence — the seven words were removed from the preamble to the constitution. The removal was the result of overnight negotiations in which the representatives of eastern Indonesia had warned that the inclusion of the Islamic obligation clause would cause the Christian-majority territories to secede from the new republic, threatening the territorial unity of the state. Sukarno, who had always preferred a pluralist formula, supported the removal, and the committee agreed. The first principle became simply Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa — "Belief in the One Almighty God" — a formulation capacious enough to encompass Islam, Christianity, Hinduism, and Buddhism simultaneously, though sufficiently theistic to exclude explicitly atheist or non-theistic belief systems.

The formal architecture of recognized religions was established by Presidential Decree PNPS No. 1 of 1965, which enumerated six agama (religions) as the objects of state recognition and protection: Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, Buddhism, and — originally but briefly — Confucianism. Confucianism was de-recognized by Suharto in 1979 as part of his administration's anti-Chinese cultural policies (Presidential Instruction No. 14/1967 had already prohibited public expressions of Chinese religion and culture), leaving the recognized system as a set of five religions. It was President Abdurrahman Wahid who, in 2000, restored Confucianism to official recognition through Presidential Decision No. 14/2000, reversing Suharto's discriminatory policies and extending formal state recognition to the Chinese Indonesian community's primary religious tradition. A Ministry of Religion circular in 2006 confirmed Confucianism's standing as the sixth official religion, restoring the original six-religion framework.33 Helen Pausacker, writing in Inside Indonesia, documented the practical significance of this restoration for Chinese Indonesian families who had spent decades claiming Buddhist or Christian identity on official documents because Confucianism was not a recognized legal option.34

Recognition carries substantial practical consequences that define the two-tier structure of Indonesian religious life: recognized religions may register their marriages legally, receive state-funded religious instruction in public schools, build houses of worship more easily, and appear on the national identity card. Adherents of unrecognized faiths — indigenous spiritual traditions, heterodox Islamic movements, minority religious communities — are denied these rights and face discrimination in employment, education, and civil administration. The Pancasila settlement has thus produced a framework that is genuinely pluralist with respect to the six recognized religions while maintaining a structure of exclusion toward all others — a limited pluralism whose limits have been both contested and enforced throughout the history of the republic.

Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa — the first principle of Pancasila, translated as "Belief in the One Almighty God." Adopted on August 18, 1945, following the removal of the "seven words" clause that had briefly imposed an obligation of Islamic law on Muslims, this formulation was designed as a capacious theistic commitment that could accommodate Islam, Christianity, Hinduism, and Buddhism simultaneously, without privileging any one. It has been the subject of ongoing theological and political debate about whether it implies an Islamic monotheism, a generic theism, or something else, and it serves as the constitutional foundation for Indonesia's "six recognized religions" framework.

Javanese Mysticism: Kebatinan, Kejawen, and the Politics of Spiritual Recognition

Running parallel to both formal Islam and the politics of Pancasila in Java is a rich tradition of spiritual practice organized around the concept of the inner life (batin) and the cultivation of refined inner knowledge of ultimate reality — a tradition known collectively as kebatinan (from the Arabic batin, "inner" or "hidden") or kejawen (Javanese beliefs, from Jawi, Javanese). Kejawen is best understood not as a single religion but as a family of related spiritual orientations sharing a concern for the cultivation of inner refinement (rasa), the alignment of personal existence with the cosmic order, and the achievement of spiritual states — ranging from heightened meditative awareness to union with the divine — through meditation, ascetic practice, and ritual observance.35 Its cosmological framework draws on Javanese pre-Islamic animist traditions, on the Hindu-Buddhist mystical heritage of the Majapahit court, and on the Sufi mystical philosophy that entered Java with Islamization — it is itself a synthesis, a layering of multiple spiritual traditions into a distinctively Javanese spiritual idiom.

Sukarno's spiritual inclinations were broadly consonant with this tradition: his speeches and writings reveal a syncretic religious sensibility that combined Islamic piety, Javanese mystical concepts, theosophical influences, and a vision of the unity of all religious traditions in a common divine reality. Sukarno was not a practicing Muslim in any formal sense — he engaged in mystical practices associated with kejawen and was closely associated with several spiritual teachers (guru kebatinan) — and his construction of Pancasila reflected in part this syncretic spiritual orientation, its formulation of Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa being broad enough to accommodate the diverse spiritual traditions of the archipelago including kejawen itself.36

The institutional organization of kebatinan movements intensified in the early independence period. The First Kebatinan Congress was held in Semarang in 1955, with the aim of unifying the diverse syncretic spiritual organizations and petitioning for state recognition. This effort was complicated by the deeply ambivalent attitude of orthodox Muslim organizations toward kebatinan — Muhammadiyah and sections of NU regarded syncretic spiritual practices as un-Islamic at best and as apostasy at worst — and by the government's difficulty in accommodating within the constitutional framework of "recognized religions" traditions that did not fit the category of agama as defined by state bureaucracy. In the early 1960s, Muslim conservatives persuaded Sukarno to take measures against syncretic sects they characterized as threats to Islamic integrity, despite his own personal sympathies with kebatinan.36

Under Suharto's New Order (1966–98), kebatinan organizations occupied an uncertain position: the government was hostile to any organizational mobilization that challenged its political control, but Suharto himself maintained deep personal connections to Javanese mystical traditions, having spiritual advisers and practitioners of Javanese ritual among his close associates. The New Order formally categorized kebatinan and similar traditions as kepercayaan (beliefs) rather than agama (religion), placing them under the jurisdiction of the Ministry of Education and Culture rather than the Ministry of Religion, and explicitly denying them the legal standing of religion. This categorization subjected their practitioners to legal disability in matters of marriage registration, identity documentation, and access to religious education in public schools, generating ongoing grievance and advocacy among the estimated 12 million Indonesians estimated by the Ministry of Culture and Education to practice one of approximately 1,200 indigenous and syncretic spiritual traditions.37

kebatinan — a family of Javanese spiritual practices organized around the cultivation of inner refinement (rasa) and alignment with the cosmic order, drawing on pre-Islamic animist traditions, Hindu-Buddhist mystical heritage, and Sufi philosophy. The word derives from the Arabic batin (inner, hidden). Kebatinan practitioners, unlike practitioners of recognized religions, were categorized by the Indonesian state as kepercayaan (belief holders) rather than agama (religion) adherents until the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling, which has begun to alter their legal standing.

Penghayat Kepercayaan: Indigenous Belief and the 2017 Constitutional Court Ruling

The category of penghayat kepercayaan — loosely translatable as "indigenous belief practitioners" or "holders of conviction" — encompasses the diverse communities of Indonesians who practice non-recognized spiritual traditions: kebatinan organizations in Java, animist communities in Kalimantan, indigenous ritual specialists in Nusa Tenggara and Papua, practitioners of syncretic spiritual systems across the archipelago. Before the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling, these communities occupied the worst legal position in Indonesia's religious hierarchy: unlike adherents of recognized religions, they could not legally register their faith on the national identity card, leaving the religion column blank or claiming a recognized religion falsely; they could not legally register their marriages under their own religious norms; and they faced systematic disadvantage in access to public services, employment in government, and children's religious education in schools.38

On November 7, 2017, the Constitutional Court issued Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016, a unanimous ruling that declared articles of the Population Administration Law requiring adherents of indigenous faiths to leave the religion column on their national identity cards blank were discriminatory and unconstitutional. The ruling held that the state's failure to accommodate penghayat kepercayaan in its civil registration system violated the constitutional guarantees of equal treatment under the law and freedom of religion and belief. Following the decision, penghayat kepercayaan adherents could list their faith status as "penghayat kepercayaan" on their family cards and identity documents, without specifying a particular recognized religion they did not practice.38 The Library of Congress's analysis of the ruling noted that it "opens the way to recognition of native faiths" — though the recognition extended was administrative rather than the full formal recognition accorded to the six established religions, and ongoing discrimination in practice remained a concern for advocacy organizations.

The ruling was welcomed by human rights advocates and indigenous communities, but its practical implementation encountered resistance in local bureaucracies and among communities organized around the recognized religion framework. The deeper question — whether Indonesia's constitutional framework could genuinely accommodate non-theistic or polytheistic indigenous traditions within its founding commitment to Ketuhanan Yang Maha Esa — remained unresolved, as did the question of access to religious education in public schools, where the curriculum remained organized exclusively around the six recognized religions. The LSE Southeast Asia Blog's analysis of the ruling noted that while it was a significant advance in constitutional recognition, it did not resolve the fundamental tension between Indonesia's pluralist constitutional aspirations and the exclusionary architecture of its religion-recognition system.39

Reformasi and Its Tensions: Islamism, Communal Violence, and the Limits of Pluralism

The political liberalization that followed Suharto's fall in May 1998 simultaneously expanded the space for civil society expression and unleashed competitive religious mobilization that subjected Indonesia's pluralist framework to its most severe tests since independence. The combined effects of democratic decentralization, economic crisis, and the removal of the authoritarian political controls that had suppressed religious organization under the New Order created conditions in which both the pluralist traditions represented by NU and Muhammadiyah and the Islamist movements that had been constrained under Suharto expanded their organizational and political scope simultaneously.

The most devastating expression of this dynamic was the communal violence that erupted in Maluku in January 1999 and continued until the Malino II peace accord of February 2002. The conflict — which claimed an estimated 5,000 lives and displaced more than 500,000 people — was triggered by a street fight between a Christian bus driver and a Muslim passenger in Ambon on January 19, 1999, but its rapid escalation into sustained inter-communal violence was driven by deeper structural factors: decades of demographic change in which Muslim migrants from Sulawesi and Java had altered the historical balance of the Maluku population; political competition for government positions in the post-New Order decentralization environment; economic grievances; and the deliberate instrumentalization of religious identity by local political actors.40 The arrival of the Laskar Jihad, a militant Islamist organization, in July 1999 dramatically intensified the conflict, bringing trained fighters and an explicitly jihadist ideological framework to what had begun as a local political dispute. The violence in Maluku, alongside contemporaneous communal conflict in Poso (Central Sulawesi), represented the darkest expression of the risks inherent in a polity in which religious community and political identity were closely intertwined.

The question of Ahmadiyah — a Muslim movement founded in nineteenth-century India whose adherents regard Mirza Ghulam Ahmad as a prophet after Muhammad, a position mainstream Sunni Islam regards as heresy — crystallized the tensions between constitutional religious freedom and the exclusionary tendencies in the recognized religion framework.41 The Indonesian Ahmadiyah community, numbering in the hundreds of thousands, had been present since the colonial period. In 2005, the Majelis Ulama Indonesia issued a fatwa declaring Ahmadiyah to be outside the fold of Islam; in 2008, a joint ministerial decree ordered the Ahmadiyah to "stop spreading interpretations and activities that deviate from the principal teachings of Islam." The decree did not formally ban Ahmadiyah but created legal and social space for systematic persecution: the forced closure of more than 30 mosques, mob violence against Ahmadiyah communities with police complicity, and the displacement of hundreds of families. In 2010, the Constitutional Court upheld the 1965 blasphemy law in an 8-1 decision, explicitly preserving the state's authority to define and prohibit "deviant" religious interpretation within recognized faiths.41

The province of Aceh, at the northwestern tip of Sumatra, represents the extreme case of Indonesia's religious-political tensions — and the unique outcome of a negotiated settlement that made Aceh the only Indonesian province with formal legal authority to implement Islamic law across its full domain. The "Veranda of Mecca" (serambi Mekkah), as Aceh has been known since the colonial era, was the first Indonesian territory to develop a strong Islamic identity and the site of a sustained separatist insurgency by the Free Aceh Movement (GAM) from 1976 until the 2005 Helsinki Peace Agreement. In exchange for ending the insurgency, the central government granted Aceh the authority to implement sharia through the Law on the Governing of Aceh (Law 11/2006), leading to the enactment of qanun (Islamic bylaws) covering Islamic dress, prohibition of alcohol, gambling, and khalwat (unmarried cohabitation), enforced by the Wilayatul Hisbah Islamic morality police.42 Aceh thus stands as a permanent exception to the secularist-pluralist framework of the Indonesian national state — an exception that Islamist political movements elsewhere in Indonesia have periodically invoked as a model for extension.

Against these centrifugal pressures, NU's Islam Nusantara framework has functioned as the most significant institutional counterweight. NU intellectuals have argued vigorously that the tolerant, pluralist, culturally adaptive Indonesian Islam that developed over six centuries is not a compromise or an incomplete Islam but a positive theological achievement — a model for what Islam looks like when it is not distorted by the political pressures and cultural specificities of the Arabian Peninsula. This argument has gained international attention: at the 33rd NU Congress in Jombang in 2015, Islam Nusantara was formally endorsed as the organization's flagship theological concept, and subsequent NU leaders have carried the message to international forums as a counterweight to Salafi and jihadist interpretations of Islam. The Hudson Institute's analysis of NU's "Humanitarian Islam" movement described it as "the most influential civil society challenge to extremism in the Muslim world."43

Islam Nusantara — "Archipelago Islam": a theological and civilizational concept articulated primarily by Nahdlatul Ulama scholars, arguing that the historically developed synthesis of Islamic teaching with local cultural traditions in the Indonesian archipelago represents a positive and authentically Islamic achievement, not a syncretic deviation. The concept emphasizes moderation, pluralism, tolerance of diversity, and contextual legal reasoning, and has been promoted internationally as a model for democratic, civil-society-oriented Muslim practice. Formally endorsed at the 33rd NU Congress in 2015.
penghayat kepercayaan — "indigenous belief practitioners": adherents of non-recognized spiritual traditions in Indonesia, including kebatinan organizations, animist communities, and indigenous ritual practitioners. Estimated at approximately 12 million people practicing some 1,200 distinct traditions. Before the Constitutional Court's ruling of November 7, 2017 (Decision No. 97/PUU-XIV/2016), penghayat kepercayaan were denied the right to list their faith on national identity documents, generating systematic civil disadvantage. The 2017 ruling ordered that they be permitted to list "penghayat kepercayaan" on official documents, a partial recognition that fell short of the full standing accorded to the six recognized religions.

Summary

Religion in Indonesia has never been a single story but an archipelago of overlapping traditions whose interactions over two millennia have produced one of the most complex and plural religious landscapes on earth. The pre-Hindu spirit-world traditions of the archipelago's peoples were not displaced but layered over by successive arrivals: first the Brahmanical and Buddhist traditions of India's Gupta-era cultural expansion, which produced the magnificent temples of Borobudur and Prambanan and the cosmopolitan Buddhist center of Srivijaya, and whose most enduring legacy is the uniquely developed Balinese Hinduism that has survived six centuries of surrounding Islamization. Then Islam, arriving through the commercial networks of the Indian Ocean from the thirteenth century, and spreading across the coasts through the work of Sufi teachers whose approach to conversion privileged inner transformation over legal conformity and accommodation of indigenous cultural forms over their replacement — producing an Indonesian Islam whose character has been debated by generations of scholars, from Clifford Geertz's controversial santri/abangan framework to the more empathetic analyses of Mark Woodward, Robert Hefner, and M.C. Ricklefs. Then Christianity, arriving in two distinct waves — the Portuguese Catholic missions of the sixteenth century in the spice islands of the east, and the Protestant missions of the nineteenth century in Toba Batak country and Minahasa — producing Christian communities whose faith became inseparably fused with local ethnic and cultural identity. Alongside these, the Chinese Indonesian community's Confucian tradition, recognized and then stripped of recognition by Suharto, and restored by Abdurrahman Wahid in 2000; the Javanese syncretic traditions of kebatinan and kejawen; and the vast and varied world of penghayat kepercayaan whose partial recognition by the 2017 Constitutional Court ruling remains incomplete.

The Pancasila settlement of 1945 — its first principle the result of a negotiation that removed seven words committing Muslims to Islamic law in exchange for a capacious theism that could accommodate all the archipelago's traditions — provided the constitutional framework within which this diversity has been managed, with partial success and genuine tension. The six recognized religions framework has protected religious pluralism while creating a two-tier system that excludes millions of citizens from full religious recognition. The Reformasi era since 1998 has tested this framework severely: inter-communal violence in Maluku and Poso, Islamist pressure against minorities, the weaponization of the blasphemy law, and the implementation of sharia in Aceh represent centrifugal forces. Against them stand the organizational weight of Nahdlatul Ulama's Islam Nusantara, the civic traditions of Muhammadiyah, and the resilience of Indonesia's constitutionalist pluralist framework — an imperfect but enduring achievement of one of the world's most diverse societies.
