Learning Objectives

  • Explain Portugal's strategic logic in the Indian Ocean and why Malacca was the pivotal target of the 1511 campaign.
  • Describe the Estado da India model — the cartaz system, the feitoria network, and the role of the casados — and assess how far it achieved its commercial aims.
  • Trace the Portuguese presence in the Maluku Islands from António de Abreu's 1511–1512 expedition through Sultan Babullah's expulsion of the garrison in 1575, and explain why local resistance proved decisive.
  • Analyse why the Portuguese ultimately failed to sustain dominance over the spice trade, and evaluate the cultural and linguistic legacy they left in the archipelago.

In August 1511 a Portuguese fleet of fewer than twenty ships captured the wealthiest port in maritime Asia. The conquest of Malacca by Afonso de Albuquerque opened the Indonesian archipelago to direct European commercial and military penetration for the first time. Yet the Portuguese empire in Southeast Asia was always more fragile than its fortresses suggested: too few soldiers, too long a coastline, and too resilient a network of Islamic sultanates meant that the Estado da India's grip on the spice trade was contested from the moment it was established. What survived the Portuguese century in the archipelago was not territorial control but something more durable — words woven into everyday Indonesian speech, Catholic parishes on islands the Dutch never fully converted, and Eurasian communities whose surnames still echo a vanished empire.

The Road to the East: Portugal's Maritime Strategy

Portugal was a small Atlantic kingdom of fewer than two million people at the opening of the sixteenth century, yet within a single generation its mariners had restructured the commercial geography of the Indian Ocean world. The impulse was partly crusading — Portugal had completed its domestic Reconquista in 1249 and its nobility retained an appetite for holy war against Islam beyond the Atlantic — and partly mercantile: the spice trade that flowed from the Indonesian archipelago through Arab, Indian, and Venetian intermediaries to European markets enriched every middleman and excluded northern Atlantic competitors entirely. To break this circuit, the Portuguese crown directed systematic exploration down the African coast through the second half of the fifteenth century. The crucial breakthrough came in 1498, when Vasco da Gama rounded the Cape of Good Hope and reached Calicut on the Malabar Coast of India, establishing for the first time a direct sea route from Lisbon to the source of Asian spices.1

King Manuel I (r. 1495–1521) moved rapidly to institutionalize this advantage. The Estado da India — literally the "State of India," the administrative apparatus governing all Portuguese possessions and activities east of the Cape of Good Hope — was formally established in 1505 under the first Viceroy, Francisco de Almeida.2 Its capital eventually settled at Goa on the Malabar Coast (from 1530), but its governing logic was maritime, not territorial: the Estado was not a land empire comparable to the Ottoman or Mughal states but a chain of fortified ports and licensed sea-lanes stitched across the Indian Ocean basin. Every viceroy departed Lisbon carrying a written regimento — detailed governing instructions — and held supreme civil, military, and financial authority over an empire that stretched, at its greatest extent, from Mozambique to Japan.

The man who translated Manuel's vision into territorial fact was Afonso de Albuquerque (c. 1453–1515). Born into the lesser nobility near Lisbon and educated in mathematics and Latin at the court of King Afonso V, Albuquerque brought to the Indian Ocean a strategic clarity that set him apart from the raiding admirals who preceded him.3 His governing idea was the control of chokepoints rather than hinterlands: seize the three or four great harbor-mouths through which the oceanic spice trade must pass, and the profits of Asia would flow through Portuguese hands. He identified four such pivots — Hormuz at the mouth of the Persian Gulf, Goa on the Malabar Coast, Aden at the mouth of the Red Sea, and Malacca at the entrance to the South China Sea — and spent his years as Governor-General (1509–1515) systematically targeting them.4 Goa fell in 1510 and became the Estado's administrative capital. Hormuz was subdued in 1515. Aden, heavily fortified, resisted his 1513 attack and would never fall to the Portuguese — a failure that left the Red Sea route partially open and gave Ottoman and Egyptian merchants a continuing avenue to European markets. But Malacca, the most valuable of the four, fell in August 1511 with the speed and completeness Albuquerque had calculated.

Afonso de Albuquerque and the Conquest of Malacca, 1511

At the moment of Portugal's arrival, Malacca was the foremost entrepôt of maritime Asia. Founded around 1400 on the southwestern tip of the Malay Peninsula by a refugee Sumatran prince, the city had in a century grown to a population exceeding 100,000. The Portuguese apothecary and chronicler Tomé Pires, who visited Malacca in the months after its conquest and composed the first comprehensive European account of the Malay Archipelago — the Suma Oriental, written 1512–1515 and published for the first time by the Hakluyt Society in 1944 — estimated that the city hosted speakers of more than eighty languages, that perhaps a thousand junks called each year, and that the trade it cleared surpassed that of Alexandria, then the greatest Mediterranean emporium.5 In a formulation that has never been improved, Pires summarized Malacca's geopolitical significance: "Whoever is lord of Malacca has his hand on the throat of Venice."6 The sultans who had ruled since the early fifteenth century had converted to Islam and made the city the pivot of a Muslim commercial world stretching from Gujarat in the west to Java and the Spice Islands in the east. Chapter 12 of this book addresses Malacca's rise in fuller detail; for the purposes of this chapter, what matters is that Malacca controlled the strait through which every cargo bound from the South China Sea to the Indian Ocean had to pass, and that Albuquerque understood this perfectly.

Albuquerque arrived before Malacca in July 1511 with a force of approximately 1,200 to 1,500 soldiers and seventeen or eighteen ships — a tiny flotilla against a city defended by Sultan Mahmud Shah with an estimated 20,000 men, several thousand firearms, and twenty war elephants.7 What the Portuguese lacked in numbers they compensated through firepower, naval discipline, and the political disunity of Malacca's polyglot merchant population. The city's Hindu and Chinese traders, long resentful of the Malay-Muslim commercial oligarchy, provided tactical intelligence and logistical support to the invaders. After a first assault on 25 July was repulsed, a second on 8 August captured the central bridge over the Malacca River — the strategic key to the harbor — and the city fell on 24 August 1511, reportedly at a cost of only twenty-eight Portuguese lives, a figure that almost certainly undercounts casualties among allied auxiliaries.7 Sultan Mahmud Shah escaped by boat with the royal treasury, retreating to Bintan Island off the southern tip of the Malay Peninsula, from which he organized counter-raids and enlisted the Javanese sultanate of Demak for naval assistance in 1511 and again in 1521. His descendants would found the Johor Sultanate and spend the following century attempting to reclaim the city.

Albuquerque's conduct in the aftermath revealed the dual logic of Portuguese conquest: selective violence alongside commercial pragmatism. He ordered the destruction of Malacca's mosques and the killing of the city's Muslim commercial elite, while extending full protection and trading rights to Chinese, Hindu, and Javanese merchants whose business Malacca needed to function.8 He replicated the administrative model he had developed at Goa: appointing Malay-speaking officials from non-Muslim communities to traditional court titles — bendahara, temenggung, shahbandar — and issuing safe-conduct passes to trade partners across the region. Portuguese garrison soldiers were encouraged to marry local women; young women from Portugal were dispatched as Órfãs do Rei ("royal orphans") to wed settlers and reinforce cultural continuity.

The fortification of Malacca was immediate and formidable. Albuquerque commissioned A Famosa — "The Famous" — on the hill commanding the harbor, built from the quarried stone of the city's own mosques and notable tombs, a calculated symbolism of Portuguese religious supremacy over the Muslim trading world it had displaced.9 The keep rose to over eighteen meters, mounting dozens of cannon, and the fortress would resist direct attack for 130 years. The sole surviving remnant, the Porta de Santiago gate, was nearly demolished by British engineers in the early nineteenth century and preserved only because Stamford Raffles personally intervened to halt its destruction.

The Estado da India: Trade by Force

The Estado da India that Albuquerque built across the Indian Ocean was a novel kind of empire, quite unlike the great land-based states of Asia. The historian Sanjay Subrahmanyam has described it as fundamentally a maritime commercial enterprise — a "necklace of fortified ports" (feitorias) aimed at controlling sea-lanes rather than governing hinterlands.10 The Estado's goal was not to administer Asian societies but to tax their trade, and to do so it relied on two complementary instruments: the feitoria and the cartaz.

Estado da India — the "State of India," the administrative apparatus governing all Portuguese territories, posts, and naval forces east of the Cape of Good Hope. Formally established in 1505 under Viceroy Francisco de Almeida, it was governed from Goa and lasted in various forms until Indian military annexation in 1961.
feitoria (pl. feitorias) — a Portuguese fortified trading post or factory in an Asian port. Staffed by a Crown-appointed feitor (factor) who collected customs duties and issued trade licences, the feitoria was the Estado da India's commercial node at each chokepoint.

The feitoria was a fortified warehouse-cum-customs house in a key port: sometimes a few buildings behind a wall, sometimes a substantial keep with artillery, but always sited to command the harbor entrance. Malacca, Goa, Hormuz, and smaller posts from East Africa to Macao and Japan formed the nodes of this network. The feitor collected duties on goods entering and leaving under Portuguese protection and kept accounts financing the garrison, the fleet, and the bureaucracy.

cartaz — a sea-pass issued by Portuguese port authorities to Asian merchant vessels, authorizing transit without interference. Ships found at sea without a valid cartaz were subject to seizure: cargo confiscated, crew enslaved or executed. The cartaz system was the Estado da India's primary mechanism for taxing Indian Ocean trade.

The cartaz — from Portuguese carta, "letter" or "document" — was the system's enforcement mechanism. Authorities at major Portuguese ports issued cartazes specifying a vessel's master, crew, cargo, and permitted destination. Any ship found without a valid cartaz was subject to seizure. Certain "reserved" commodities — pepper, cinnamon, and initially cloves — could be carried only on Portuguese or licensed vessels. The cartaz thus converted the Indian Ocean from an open highway into a toll road, with Portugal levying fees at every strategic junction. By the 1580s cartaz income and customs duties at Goa and Malacca constituted the great majority of the Estado's operating revenue.11

Historiographical debate: how effective was the cartaz system? Scholars disagree about the extent to which the cartaz genuinely monopolized Indian Ocean trade. Subrahmanyam and other revisionist historians have argued that the system was chronically under-enforced: the Portuguese never had the patrol capacity to cover the entire ocean, bribery of local customs officials was endemic, and major re-routing nodes such as Aceh and Johor flourished precisely because they lay outside the cartaz perimeter. On this reading, the Estado's commercial control was real but partial — significant enough to enrich Portugal and disrupt rivals, but insufficient to prevent Islamic commercial networks from surviving and routing around Portuguese checkpoints. The debate bears on broader questions of Portuguese "decline": was the system fundamentally unsound from the start, or viable until Dutch capital and organization arrived?

casados — "married men," the class of Portuguese settlers in Asia who had taken local wives with official encouragement and financial incentives. The Eurasian households they founded conducted inter-Asian trade networks that extended Portuguese commercial reach far beyond what Crown ships could achieve.

A third pillar of the Estado was the casados. Albuquerque recognized that Portugal's population was too small to staff an oceanic empire with rotationally deployed soldiers alone. He therefore encouraged his men to marry local women by offering land grants, trading privileges, and social status. The Eurasian households that resulted occupied a peculiar in-between position in colonial society — neither fully Portuguese nor fully indigenous — speaking Malay or local languages at home while maintaining a nominal Portuguese identity and Catholic faith. By 1600 there were perhaps 5,500 casados across the Estado, roughly 2,000 families in Goa alone, with significant communities in Malacca and Cochin.12 They played a disproportionate role in the empire's commercial reach, trading on their own account in textiles, spices, and luxury goods through kinship and clientage networks that the Crown's own ships could not replicate — sustaining Portuguese commercial presence in ports far from any feitoria. The Goa Inquisition, established in 1560 as Counter-Reformation pressure intensified, created serious tensions within these communities: its investigations targeted casados whose household practices blended Catholic observance with Hindu or Muslim elements, a friction inherent in the Estado's cultural contradictions.

Into the Spice Islands: Abreu, Serrão, and the Malukus

Even as Malacca fell, Albuquerque's eyes were fixed on the eastern horizon. He knew that the city was a staging post, not the final objective: the real commercial prize was the tiny cluster of volcanic islands in the northeastern Indonesian archipelago — the Maluku (Molucca) Islands — where cloves, nutmeg, and mace grew, and nowhere else on earth. The demand for these spices in European, Chinese, and Middle Eastern markets was enormous; the supply was geographically unique. Chapter 14 of this book discusses the global spice trade in full; for the purposes of this chapter, Malacca's strategic value lay precisely in its position as the commercial gateway through which every Maluku cargo bound for the Indian Ocean had to pass.

In November 1511, barely three months after Malacca's conquest, Albuquerque dispatched an expedition of four ships and roughly 120 soldiers and sailors under the command of António de Abreu (c. 1480–c. 1514), a captain who had served under him during the river assault, sustaining severe facial wounds.13 Francisco Rodrigues sailed as cartographer, producing the first European maps of the Maluku archipelago — charts later used by both sides in the negotiations that produced the Treaty of Zaragoza. The third commander was Francisco Serrão. Guided by Malay pilots who knew the eastern archipelago intimately, the fleet coasted south through the Java Sea, along the Lesser Sunda Islands, and reached the Banda Islands — the tiny nutmeg archipelago southeast of the island of Seram — in early 1512. They were the first Europeans to do so, and they filled their ships with cloves and nutmeg before Abreu turned for home, arriving back at Malacca by December 1512 with proof of concept for the entire Portuguese spice project.14

What the expedition had not planned was its most consequential result. During the return voyage, Francisco Serrão's vessel was wrecked on a reef. He and his surviving crew were rescued by agents of Sultan Abu Lais of Ternate — one of the two dominant sultanates of the northern Maluku Islands — and brought to the island. Rather than returning to Malacca, Serrão became the sultan's personal military advisor, fighting on Ternate's behalf against its perennial rival Tidore and winning sufficient trust that he remained as a permanent resident until his death, probably in 1521.14 Serrão wrote letters to his friend Ferdinand Magellan describing the islands and their wealth in such extravagant terms that Magellan later cited them as motivation for his westward voyage — the very voyage whose success would trigger the Tordesillas-Zaragoza crisis.

Ternate and Tidore were the two great clove-producing sultanates of the northern Maluku Islands, locked in a centuries-old competition for commercial and political dominance. Cloves — the dried flower buds of Syzygium aromaticum, a tree that grew naturally on perhaps five small islands in the world — were among the most valuable commodities of the pre-modern era by weight. A pound of cloves purchased in the Malukus for copper coins could sell in Lisbon for many times that price. The Portuguese coveted a monopoly on this trade; the Ternate sultanate sought a military ally who could tip the balance against Tidore. The alliance seemed mutually advantageous.

The Portuguese built their first permanent fort on Ternate in 1522, establishing what they intended as a lasting commercial and military presence at the spice trade's epicenter.15 The arrangement quickly soured. The office of fort governor rotated on short terms, and successive governors — serving far from Goa's scrutiny and accountable to no one close at hand — proved serially exploitative: extracting maximum cloves at minimum prices, interfering in succession disputes, and attempting the forced conversion of the island's Muslim population. King Tabariji of Ternate, who had initially cooperated with the Portuguese, was deposed by the fort governor in 1535, shipped to Goa, and converted to Christianity under the name Dom Manuel; he died on the return voyage to Ternate in 1545, an episode that poisoned relations with the successor sultans.15

The crisis came to a head in 1570. Sultan Hairun of Ternate, who had long attempted to negotiate a more equitable commercial arrangement, was summoned to a meeting with the Portuguese governor and murdered — stabbed on the governor's orders. The assassination galvanized the islands. Hairun's son Babullah organized a coalition of Maluku sultanates and laid siege to the Portuguese fort on Ternate. The siege lasted four years. In 1575 the Portuguese garrison, reduced by disease, starvation, and combat attrition to a shadow of its original strength, capitulated and was evacuated.16 Babullah expelled the Portuguese from Ternate, converted the fort into his palace, and expanded Ternate's influence across the northern Maluku Islands, transforming the sultanate — in the assessment of one historian — into "an expanding, fiercely Islamic and anti-Portuguese state."15 The Portuguese retained minor footholds on Tidore and at Ambon, but their claim to control the Maluku clove trade was broken permanently, fifteen years before the Dutch even arrived in Asian waters.

Dividing the World: The Treaties of Tordesillas and Zaragoza

The Portuguese presence in the Maluku Islands raised a question latent since 1494: who owned the world? In that year Spain and Portugal had signed the Treaty of Tordesillas, dividing the known and unknown globe between them along a meridian 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands. All territory to the east was Portugal's; all to the west, Spain's.17 For the Atlantic and for Brazil — whose coast lay unexpectedly east of the line — the division was workable. For the Pacific and the Indian Ocean it was a cartographic fiction waiting to become a diplomatic crisis, because the treaty specified the Atlantic meridian precisely but said nothing definitive about its counterpart on the other side of the globe — the antimeridian that should, in theory, divide the hemispheres across the Pacific. The length of the league was not defined, the specific Cape Verde island was unspecified, and the navigational technology of 1494 made the antimeridian essentially unverifiable.17

The crisis materialized when Ferdinand Magellan, a Portuguese captain who had entered Spanish service after quarreling with the Lisbon court, proposed a westward voyage to reach the Spice Islands by crossing the Pacific. Magellan's expedition left Spain in 1519, crossed the Pacific, and reached the Maluku Islands in 1521 — after Magellan's own death at Mactan in the Philippines. Spanish survivors established a trading post at Tidore, directly competing with Portugal's Ternate alliance, and declared the Maluku Islands to lie within Spain's hemisphere under Tordesillas.18 The Iberian courts were now in direct conflict over the most valuable real estate on earth.

Bilateral negotiations continued for eight years. A joint commission at Badajoz-Elvas in 1524 — involving cartographers, astronomers, and geographers from both sides — collapsed in futility: each delegation produced maps that placed the antimeridian exactly where their nation's interests required.19 The scientific question was genuinely unanswerable with available technology, and both sides knew it. Resolution came through money rather than measurement.

On 22 April 1529, in the city of Zaragoza, King John III of Portugal and Emperor Charles V of Spain signed the Treaty of Zaragoza. The terms were blunt: Portugal paid Spain 350,000 gold ducats in exchange for Spain's formal renunciation of all claims to the Maluku Islands. The agreed demarcation line ran 297½ leagues east of the islands, placing them unambiguously in the Portuguese hemisphere.18 The price represented a major fiscal concession on Portugal's part — Spain's initial demand had been 1,000,000 ducats — but Charles V desperately needed cash for his European wars and accepted the reduced sum.19 Spain also inserted a retro-vendendo clause allowing it to revoke the agreement if it repaid Portugal's money — a theoretical escape hatch that was never exercised.20

The practical limitations of Zaragoza were apparent almost immediately. Spain colonized the Philippines from 1565 onward and eventually the Marianas — islands that lay in the nominally Portuguese zone — without effective Portuguese objection, because Portugal lacked the naval capacity to enforce treaty boundaries across an ocean it could barely patrol. Neither France, England, nor the Ottoman Empire recognized Tordesillas or Zaragoza; the Iberian "division of the world" was an agreement between two parties who could not enforce it against those who declined to be bound by it. Nonetheless, Zaragoza's practical effect was real for as long as Portugal could maintain its Maluku presence — which, as the events of 1570–1575 demonstrated, proved far shorter than anyone in Lisbon had anticipated.

God and Commerce: Catholic Evangelization

The Portuguese in Asia were not only merchants and soldiers; they were, by the terms of the Padroado — the royal patronage over the Catholic Church in Portuguese territories, formally recognized by a series of papal bulls from the 1450s onward — the official agents of Catholic evangelization across the known world. Every Portuguese fleet carried chaplains; every feitoria was expected to have its church. The Padroado gave the Portuguese crown the right to nominate bishops and clergy for all territories within its maritime sphere, making the church an arm of colonial administration. The resulting union of cross and commerce was never comfortable, but it was structurally inescapable.

Padroado — the system of royal patronage by which the Portuguese Crown held the right to nominate Catholic clergy and establish churches in its overseas territories, granted by papal bulls beginning in the 1450s. The Padroado made the Portuguese state the official instrument of Catholic mission across the Indian Ocean and beyond.

Systematic transformation of Southeast Asian populations came less from soldiers and factors than from the men of the Society of Jesus, the Jesuit order founded in 1540. Francis Xavier (1506–1552), a Navarrese Jesuit who had been among Ignatius of Loyola's original companions in Paris, arrived in Goa in 1542 as the papal legate for the Portuguese East. He spent four years on the Malabar Coast before the condition of small Christian communities in the Maluku Islands — planted by Portuguese soldiers and sailors with fragmentary knowledge of any consistent doctrine — drew him eastward. Xavier reached the Maluku Islands around January 1546, departing from Malacca and visiting Ambon, then Ternate, Baranura, and Morotai over the following eighteen months.21

Xavier's methods were carefully adapted to his circumstances. He worked through interpreters, trained local catechists to teach basic prayers and doctrine in the vernacular, and deliberately avoided the forced baptism that had characterized earlier Iberian evangelization in the Atlantic world. He baptized hundreds in each location — conservative estimates credit him with thousands of baptisms across the Maluku region — but his most durable contribution was institutional: the networks of catechists, the small chapels, and the regular correspondence with Goa that kept the missions supplied after his departure in mid-1547.21 Xavier himself complained bitterly to King John III about the conduct of Portuguese fort officials in the Malukus, whose exploitation of local people he considered an obstacle to genuine conversion — a tension between the commercial and evangelical impulses of the Estado da India that was never resolved, only managed with varying success.

The growth of Maluku Christianity after Xavier's visit was exponential. By the 1560s, roughly a decade after his eighteen-month stay, perhaps 10,000 Roman Catholics lived in the Maluku region; by the 1590s the figure had grown to 50,000–60,000, primarily concentrated on Ambon and the coastal settlements accessible by sea.22 The growth reflected the institutional framework Xavier had established more than his personal presence: trained catechists sustained the faith in Portuguese-administered communities even across the long gaps between visits from ordained clergy.

The Jesuits were not alone. Dominican friars established missions on the Lesser Sunda Islands — on Solor and Flores — that operated somewhat independently of the Estado's commercial logic. The Dominicans on Solor built a fortified settlement that doubled as a trading post for sandalwood from Timor; the mission served evangelical and commercial purposes simultaneously. By the 1590s the combined Catholic and mixed-heritage population on Solor was estimated at 25,000.23 The Dominican mission in the Lesser Sundas proved extraordinarily durable — far more so than the Jesuit missions in the northern Malukus. Unlike Ternate and Ambon, where Dutch Protestant conquest after 1605 created institutional pressure toward Calvinism, the Lesser Sundas remained largely outside the Dutch commercial orbit long enough for Catholicism to become a deep cultural identity. A Portuguese-Dutch treaty of 1859 formally guaranteed freedom of Catholic practice at Larantuka on eastern Flores, enshrining in international law what centuries of mission had achieved on the ground.23

The Limits of Maritime Power: Why the Portuguese Failed to Dominate

The Estado da India in Southeast Asia was simultaneously impressive and fragile. At its peak it had reorganized the commercial architecture of the Indian Ocean, inserted European military power into the spice trade's heartland, and established Christian communities across the archipelago. Yet within less than a century of Malacca's conquest, Portuguese dominance was being systematically dismantled by structural limitations, local resistance, and the arrival of competitors organized in a fundamentally superior form.

Manpower and overextension. The Estado spanned, at its greatest extent, a chain of posts from East Africa to Japan — an arc of some 25,000 kilometers — sustained by a metropolitan population of fewer than two million people. At no point could the Estado field more than 2,000 to 3,000 effective European and Eurasian troops across the entire empire, while larger Asian states could mobilize tens of thousands.24 The Portuguese compensated through superior firepower, fortification, and alliance politics, but these advantages had limits. Fort governors operated with wide autonomy far from Goa's scrutiny; personnel rotated on three-year terms, ensuring that each new governor arrived as a stranger, extracted maximum personal profit, and departed before consequences arrived. Administrative dysfunction and endemic corruption were structural features of the system, not aberrations.

Local resistance: Johor, Aceh, and the Maluku sultanates. The conquest of Malacca triggered an immediate and sustained reaction from the Islamic commercial world that Malacca had anchored. The displaced sultanate's successors founded Johor at the tip of the Malay Peninsula, from which they organized repeated naval attacks on Malacca and offered competing trade terms to merchants wishing to avoid cartaz obligations. The Sultanate of Aceh on the northern tip of Sumatra became a powerful rival hub and mounted major military attacks on Malacca in 1537, 1547, 1568, and 1574. In the Maluku Islands, Sultan Babullah's four-year siege and the expulsion of the Portuguese from Ternate in 1575 demonstrated that motivated local sultanates could overcome even well-armed Portuguese fortifications. The lesson was clear: the Estado could hold a fortified coastal position as long as the local population tolerated it; once it became intolerable, no fort was permanent.

Structural economics. The cartaz system raised transaction costs without adding economic value. It did not produce goods, improve logistics, or create new markets; it inserted a Portuguese toll-collector into existing trade circuits that predated European arrival. Merchants who could route cargoes through Aceh, Johor, or Javanese ports had every incentive to do so and pay no cartaz fee at all. Multiple attempts to reconstitute the Estado on a joint-stock corporate basis — proposals for a Portuguese East India Company were advanced in 1628, 1669, and 1685 — all collapsed from disagreements between the Crown and merchants, and from investor skepticism that the Estado's existing structure could generate reliable dividends.24 This institutional failure proved fatal when set against the Dutch VOC, which possessed precisely the joint-stock structure and investor confidence that Portugal's Crown monopoly never achieved.

Dutch competition and the Iberian Union. The arrival of the first Dutch fleet in Indonesian waters in 1596 opened a new phase that Portuguese structural weaknesses made impossible to survive. Within four decades the Dutch had taken Ambon (1605), founded their headquarters at Batavia in western Java (1619), and — in alliance with Johor — captured Malacca on 14 January 1641, ending 130 years of Portuguese rule; they then took Colombo in 1656 and Cochin in 1663, stripping the Estado of its major eastern nodes.25 Complicating all of this was the Iberian Union: from 1580 to 1640, Portugal and its empire were absorbed into the Spanish Crown under Philip II and his successors. The union brought administrative disruption, directed Portuguese resources toward Spain's European wars, and made Portuguese Asia a legitimate target for Spain's enemies — the Dutch, in open revolt against Habsburg rule from 1568, attacked Portuguese possessions not merely as commercial competitors but as enemy territory. The Iberian Union accelerated the Dutch commercial offensive in Asia and deprived Portugal of the independent capacity to respond.

The Enduring Imprint: Language, Faith, and Eurasian Identities

The Portuguese commercial empire in Southeast Asia lasted, in its dominant form, barely seventy years — from the conquest of Malacca in 1511 to the systematic Dutch offensive of the 1590s and 1600s. Yet its traces persist in ways that would have surprised the soldiers and merchants who built it, embedded in the everyday language of the world's most populous Muslim-majority nation and in the religious demographics of its easternmost provinces.

Language. Portuguese contact with Malay — the lingua franca of maritime Southeast Asia — was linguistically the most consequential European encounter in the archipelago before Dutch colonialism, and its footprint in Indonesian remains visible today. A 1913 study by the linguist Sebastião Rodolfo Dalgado identified 431 Portuguese loanwords in Malay, a figure that dwarfs the early contribution of any other European language.26 Indonesian — the standardized form of Malay that became the Republic of Indonesia's national language — inherited this lexicon intact. The borrowed words cluster in telling semantic domains. Christian religious vocabulary preserves the Portuguese introduction of Catholic practice: gereja (church, from igreja), misa (Mass), natal (Christmas), sabtu (Saturday, from sábado), and minggu (Sunday, and by extension "week," from domingo) record the arrival of the Christian liturgical calendar in a society that had not previously organized time around a seven-day week.27 Household furnishings and foods document the material culture of the casados: meja (table, from mesa), almari (wardrobe, from armário), kemeja (shirt, from camisa), mentega (butter, from manteiga), keju (cheese), garpu (fork, from garfo), and nanas (pineapple, from ananás) — the last a New World fruit that reached Southeast Asia aboard Portuguese ships, carrying its Portuguese name.27

Beyond loanwords absorbed into Indonesian, a Portuguese creole — Papia Kristang, literally "speak Christian" — survived into the twenty-first century as a living language in Melaka, spoken by a small Eurasian community descended from the casados and their Malay-speaking wives.26 Other Portuguese-derived creoles that once existed at Batavia (Jakarta), Tugu on the north coast of Java, and in the Maluku Islands are now extinct or nearly so — the consequence of Dutch colonial language policy and population dispersal. Papia Kristang's survival in Melaka is attributed partly to geographical concentration and partly to the community's strong Catholic identity, which gave the language a liturgical function that resisted displacement.

Papia Kristang — a Portuguese-based creole language ("speak Christian") developed by the Eurasian casado communities of Malacca and surviving into the present in the Melaka "Portuguese Settlement." It is the only extant Portuguese-derived creole in Southeast Asia.

Faith. The Catholic communities planted by Xavier and the Dominicans in the Maluku Islands and the Lesser Sundas proved remarkably durable. In the Lesser Sundas the island of Flores — whose very name is Portuguese for "flowers" — remained Catholic-majority throughout the VOC period and beyond. Larantuka on Flores' eastern tip became the center of a uniquely syncretic Catholic folk tradition, its annual Holy Week processions drawing on Portuguese liturgical forms overlaid with Austronesian ceremonial elements — a fusion that preserved Catholic identity across four centuries. The 1859 Portuguese-Dutch treaty formally guaranteeing Catholic practice at Larantuka institutionalized in law what had long been cultural fact.23

Today, East Nusa Tenggara — the Indonesian province encompassing Flores and the Lesser Sundas — has a Catholic population of approximately 55 percent, making it the most heavily Catholic territory in the world's most populous Muslim-majority nation.28 This demographic reality is a direct legacy of sixteenth-century Jesuit and Dominican missions. The contrast with the rest of Indonesia — where Islam predominates even in islands close to the old Portuguese forts — illustrates how the specific geography of colonial presence shaped the religious map that Indonesia inherited.

The most dramatic case is Timor. East Timor's relationship with Portuguese colonialism extended far longer than any other Southeast Asian Portuguese possession — until 1975 — and the combination of early Dominican evangelization, prolonged colonial presence, and the Indonesian occupation's association of Catholicism with national resistance made the faith central to Timorese identity. When Timor-Leste declared independence in 2002, it made Portuguese one of its two official languages alongside Tetum — a decision grounded less in practical daily usage than in the strategic need to distinguish Timorese identity from Indonesian, and to honor the Portuguese-Catholic cultural inheritance that had defined the resistance movement.

Eurasian communities. The casados and their descendants formed a class that the Dutch and later the Republic of Indonesia inherited. In Melaka the "Portuguese Settlement" (Padang Portugal) remained inhabited into the twenty-first century by a Kristang-speaking Eurasian community. In Flores and Timor, mixed-heritage families bearing Portuguese surnames — Soares, Mendonça, da Costa, Fernandes — have been woven into local social fabrics for five centuries. These communities are evidence that the Portuguese empire was not purely extractive: it also created people, cultures, and languages that outlasted the forts and the flags by far.

Summary

The Portuguese conquest of Malacca in 1511 inaugurated the first sustained European military and commercial presence in the Indonesian world. Afonso de Albuquerque's strategy — controlling Indian Ocean chokepoints through the Estado da India's network of fortified feitorias, the cartaz pass system, and the casados settler communities — achieved striking results in the short term and generated enormous revenue from the spice trade. António de Abreu's 1511–1512 expedition reached the Banda Islands as the first Europeans to do so; Francisco Serrão's accidental settlement on Ternate gave Portugal a foothold in the northern Malukus. The Treaties of Tordesillas (1494) and Zaragoza (1529) formalized Iberian claims to the archipelago, though enforcing them against local resistance proved impossible. Francis Xavier's 1546–1547 Jesuit mission and Dominican evangelization in Flores and Solor planted Catholic communities that outlasted the commercial empire. Portuguese dominance collapsed before a combination of structural overextension, local resistance — above all Sultan Babullah's expulsion of the Ternate garrison in 1575 — and the arrival of the Dutch VOC after 1596. What survived was more durable than the forts: over 430 Portuguese loanwords in Malay, the living creole Papia Kristang, the Catholic demography of East Nusa Tenggara, and Timor-Leste's official Portuguese language.

Review & Discussion Questions

15.1 Albuquerque's strategic vision centred on controlling chokepoints rather than governing territory. How did this logic shape the specific targets he chose to attack? What did the strategy achieve, and where did it fail?
15.2 The cartaz system has been described both as an effective commercial monopoly and as a chronically under-enforced toll that traders simply routed around. Which assessment do you find more compelling, and what evidence supports your view?
15.3 Francisco Serrão's accidental stranding on Ternate gave Portugal its first foothold in the Maluku Islands without any strategic planning. How significant was contingency — as opposed to deliberate policy — in shaping the Portuguese presence in the archipelago?
15.4 The Treaty of Zaragoza resolved the Tordesillas antimeridian dispute through payment rather than cartography. What does this tell us about the relationship between scientific knowledge and political power in the early sixteenth century?
15.5 How did Francis Xavier's missionary methods differ from the coercive conversion associated with Iberian evangelization in the Atlantic world, and why might those differences have produced more durable results in the Maluku Islands?
15.6 Sultan Babullah's expulsion of the Portuguese from Ternate in 1575 can be read as a local episode or as evidence of structural limits on Portuguese power. Which framing do you find more historically illuminating, and why?
15.7 East Nusa Tenggara (Flores, the Lesser Sundas) remains Catholic-majority today within a predominantly Muslim Indonesia. What combination of factors — mission activity, Dutch commercial geography, treaty protection, geographical isolation — best explains this religious distinctiveness?
15.8 The 431 Portuguese loanwords in Malay include terms for church, table, shirt, fork, and Sunday. What do these semantic domains tell us about the kind of contact Portuguese and Malay-speaking people actually had — and what kinds of contact they apparently did not have?
