Learning Objectives

  • Assess Anthony Reid's argument that women in the precolonial "Age of Commerce" archipelago held relatively high economic and social status compared with contemporaries elsewhere in Asia, and evaluate the scholarly debate around that claim.
  • Explain how the Minangkabau matrilineal system works and how it has coexisted with devout Islamic practice, noting the tensions that urbanization and migration have created.
  • Describe the experiences of royal women and queens — Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi of Majapahit and the four Acehnese sultanas — and what they reveal about gender and political authority in premodern Indonesia.
  • Trace the transformation of Indonesian women's lives through colonialism: the nyai institution, Kartini's feminist consciousness, and the 1928 Women's Congress.
  • Analyse the destruction of Gerwani after 1965 through the lens of military sexual propaganda, and explain how the New Order's State Ibuism replaced mass feminist politics with state-managed domesticity.
  • Evaluate the post-Reformasi paradox of women's advancement — the presidency of Megawati, the 30 percent parliamentary quota — alongside the rise of Islamist gender conservatism, the anti-pornography law, mandatory headscarf politics, and the anti-LGBT backlash.

The history of women and gender in the Indonesian archipelago defies any simple narrative of either liberation or oppression. A region where women once traded independently and occasionally ruled empires was also one in which colonial concubinage systemized their sexual exploitation. A land that produced the world's largest matrilineal society also produced — in the same Islamic cultural environment — the New Order's vision of the perfectly obedient civil servant's wife. The 1965 destruction of the most powerful women's mass movement in Southeast Asia through fabricated stories of sexual depravity was followed, three decades later, by the election of Southeast Asia's first female head of a Muslim-majority state. To write gender into Indonesian history is to hold these contradictions in view simultaneously — to see how structures of patriarchy, colonial hierarchy, and religious authority have shaped women's lives while acknowledging the persistent forms of agency, organization, and resistance that women have exercised across the centuries.

The Southeast Asian Baseline: Women in the Precolonial Archipelago

Any account of women in Indonesian history must reckon with a foundational historical argument: that precolonial Southeast Asian societies afforded women a degree of economic autonomy and social recognition substantially greater than in contemporary South Asia, China, or the Middle East. The most influential statement of this position belongs to Anthony Reid, whose two-volume Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce, 1450–1680 (1988–93) devoted sustained attention to what he called the "relatively high status" of women in the region's commercial societies.1

Reid's evidence is wide-ranging. Women in the archipelago — as in mainland Southeast Asia — characteristically controlled household finances, managed retail trade in markets, and could inherit property. European visitors repeatedly remarked on the difference from norms they knew: women in Javanese and Malay markets conducted business independently, negotiated prices, and kept their own earnings. Marriage arrangements were more flexible and divorce easier to obtain than in European law. Widows and unmarried women could operate with a social visibility that was unusual elsewhere. The pattern derived partly from the bilateral kinship systems prevalent in much of the region (descent reckoned through both parents, not patrilineally alone) and partly from the practical demands of a trade-oriented economy in which women's commercial skill was economically valued.

Barbara Watson Andaya's The Flaming Womb: Repositioning Women in Early Modern Southeast Asia (2006) built on and complicated Reid's picture. Andaya argued that the "age of commerce" framework, while illuminating, tended to romanticize precolonial gender arrangements: alongside women's economic roles ran pervasive slavery (in which enslaved women were especially vulnerable to sexual exploitation by owners), aristocratic households with strong patriarchal hierarchies, and a set of religious and cultural frameworks in which female power was simultaneously celebrated as potent and feared as dangerous.2 The title's reference to the "flaming womb" — drawn from a Javanese legend about a princess whose reproductive power is both terrifying and politically transformative — captures this ambivalence. Precolonial gender was neither uniformly egalitarian nor uniformly subordinating; it was variable, contested, and embedded in specific social structures of class, ethnicity, and religious affiliation.

These debates matter because the precolonial baseline determines how one reads the subsequent story. If women's status was genuinely higher before the arrival of Islam (in Reid's later formulation) or before intensified colonial contact, then religious and colonial change becomes a narrative of decline. If the precolonial picture was itself more complicated (Andaya's position), then the subsequent centuries look less like a fall from grace and more like the reshaping of existing inequalities into new forms. Most contemporary gender historians of Southeast Asia tend toward Andaya's more nuanced position, while acknowledging that Reid identified a real and important regional pattern.

Bilateral kinship — a system in which descent, inheritance, and family obligation are reckoned through both the mother's and father's lines equally, as distinct from patrilineal or matrilineal systems. Much of insular Southeast Asia practised bilateral kinship, which contributed to relatively flexible gender roles in property and commerce.

The Minangkabau World: Matrilineal Adat and Islamic Piety

The Minangkabau of West Sumatra represent an extreme case within the regional pattern: the largest matrilineal society in the world. With a population of roughly four million in West Sumatra alone (and millions more in the rantau, the diaspora spread across Indonesia and beyond), they organize descent, clan membership, and property inheritance entirely through the female line. Land — in a rice-farming economy, the foundational resource — passes from mother to daughter, not from father to son. After marriage, a husband moves into his wife's household; he remains, in the idiom of Minangkabau adat, a "guest" in the house of his wife's clan. Children belong to their mother's lineage, not their father's.3

The most important female figure in the system is the Bundo Kanduang — literally "real mother" — a concept that invests mature women with ceremonial authority, custodianship of lineage property, and a recognized voice in communal decisions. Women lead wedding ceremonies, manage the distribution of inherited land, and are the living archives of genealogical knowledge on which the entire system depends. As one Minangkabau woman explained to researcher Sadiq Bhanbhro: "I can buy whatever I like from the market for eating, drinking and wearing" — a statement of economic independence grounded in the material fact that she controlled the household's land and income.4

The apparent paradox — that this matrilineal system flourishes in one of Indonesia's most devoutly Islamic regions — has attracted considerable scholarly attention. The Minangkabau have resolved it pragmatically: adat governs the inheritance of ancestral property (which passes through women); Islamic law governs acquired property (which a man may distribute according to shariah inheritance norms). Public religious leadership — delivering the Friday sermon, leading mosque affairs — is male. But the matrilineal household remains the foundation of social life. As Bhanbhro's informants put it: "Adat does not contradict but complements values of Islam."

Contemporary pressures are real. Rantau migration — the traditional Minangkabau practice of young men leaving home to seek education or fortune — has accelerated under modernization. When men migrate to cities and succeed in the national economy, they accumulate property under national (civil law) rather than adat norms, weakening matrilineal inheritance for that wealth. Urbanized Minangkabau women lose some of the structural protections that land ownership in West Sumatra provides. National family law, which does not recognize matrilineal inheritance, creates further tension. The Minangkabau matrilineal system survives — and remains socially powerful in its heartland — but it does so through continuous negotiation, not by standing still.

Queens and Rulers: Women at the Summit of Power

The women's history of Indonesia is not confined to households and markets. Several major polities were at critical moments governed by women, raising important questions about how gender and political authority intersected in Indonesian statecraft.

Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi — also known as Dyah Gitarja — reigned as the third monarch of Majapahit from 1328 to 1350. She was the daughter of the founder Raden Wijaya by his principal consort Dyah Gayatri, who held the title of Rajapatni (queen mother of the realm) and wielded considerable authority in her own right. Tribhuwana's reign was of exceptional historical consequence: it was she who appointed Gajah Mada as prime minister, and it was during her rule that Gajah Mada pronounced the famous Palapa Oath, swearing not to taste the spiced foods of the palace until he had brought the entire archipelago under Majapahit's sovereignty. In 1350 Tribhuwana abdicated in favour of her son Hayam Wuruk, whose reign would become Majapahit's classical apogee — a golden age made possible by the political and administrative foundations her own twenty-two years of rule had laid.5

Even more striking, because it occurred in an explicitly Islamic context, was the half-century of female rule in the Aceh Sultanate. From 1641 to 1699, Aceh — the most Islamically learned and commercially powerful state in the western archipelago — was governed by a succession of four sultanas. The first and most celebrated was Safiatuddin Tajul Alam (born Putri Sri Alam, 1612–1675), daughter of the great Sultan Iskandar Muda and widow of his successor Iskandar Thani. When her husband died unexpectedly in 1641, the Acehnese court nobility moved within three days to enthrone her as sultana. She ruled for thirty-four years — longer than any king in Acehnese history — maintaining the sultanate as a major regional power even as Dutch East India Company pressure intensified on the Malay coasts. She was followed by Naqiatuddin (r. 1675–78), Zaqiatuddin (r. 1678–88), and Kamalat Syah (r. 1688–99).6

Historians disagree about what this sequence of female rulers signifies. One interpretation stresses weakness: the nobility installed women precisely because female rulers were easier to manage, making the sultanas screen figures behind whom oligarchic factions operated. A rival reading, supported by Andaya's broader argument about female authority in the Age of Commerce, sees the sultanas' accessions as reflecting an older Southeast Asian tradition in which female lineage could legitimately transmit and exercise sovereign power.7 In favour of the second reading stands the length of Safiatuddin's reign, during which she issued diplomatic correspondence, directed foreign policy, and supported a flourishing of Islamic scholarship and literature — not the record of a puppet.

Whatever one makes of the debate, the Acehnese experiment ended for explicitly Islamic reasons. In 1699, Islamic scholars in Mecca issued a fatwa ruling that female rule was contrary to shariah. The Acehnese ulama used this ruling to end the sultanas' dynasty. Kamalat Syah was the last; thereafter, only male sultans ruled Aceh.8 The episode prefigures a tension that would run through the subsequent centuries: the relationship between local Southeast Asian traditions of female authority and the norms of an increasingly globalised Islamic jurisprudence.

The Colonial Crucible: Nyai, Kartini, and the Gendering of Race

Dutch colonialism did not simply impose a single gender order on the archipelago; it created a layered and contradictory set of gender arrangements that varied by race, class, and era. Three figures help illuminate the colonial crucible: the nyai, the European colonial woman, and Kartini.

The nyai was the Indonesian concubine of a European man — the Dutch term njai originating in Malay/Javanese words for a housekeeper or older woman. From the earliest VOC period, the Company brought large numbers of male employees to the Indies but very few European women. The solution — openly acknowledged, institutionally tolerated — was that Dutch men took Indonesian women as domestic partners: housekeepers, companions, mothers of their children, and de facto wives, though without legal protection or recognized status. The nyai managed the household, translated between cultures, and bore the Indo-European children who would form a distinctive intermediate social stratum. She was simultaneously essential to the colonial order and denied a place within it. She could be dismissed without recourse; her children might be acknowledged or abandoned according to the father's will; if he took a European wife, she was typically discarded.9

As Elsbeth Locher-Scholten's landmark study Women and the Colonial State (2000) shows, the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries brought increasing numbers of European women to the Indies, which in turn intensified colonial anxieties about racial purity and the proper ordering of domestic life. European women remade themselves as bearers of colonial modernity — adopting Western fashions and household standards that marked racial distinction from Indonesian domestics and concubines. Frances Gouda's parallel analysis of Dutch colonial culture traced how gendered imagery — the "good mother" of colonial rhetoric, the sexually threatening Indonesian woman of colonial anxiety — served to rationalize and reproduce the racial hierarchy that the colonial state depended upon.10 The nyai persisted, but concubinage was increasingly stigmatized, and formal marriage between European men and Indonesian women was regulated and discouraged.11

Into this world was born Raden Adjeng Kartini (21 April 1879 – 17 September 1904), and her brief life became the most contested single biography in Indonesian gender history. The daughter of the Regent of Jepara, Kartini received the unusual benefit of a Dutch-language primary education, which gave her access to European feminist ideas and the ability to conduct an extensive correspondence with Dutch women intellectuals and friends. Her letters, published posthumously by J.H. Abendanon in 1911 under the title Door duisternis tot licht ("Through Darkness into Light") — translated into English as Letters of a Javanese Princess — expressed an anguished, sophisticated feminist consciousness. She argued for girls' education as a precondition of national progress. She mourned the polygamy her own father practised. She dreamed of a world in which Indonesian women's minds, like hers, could develop through learning rather than being sealed away in the pingitan (seclusion customary for aristocratic Javanese girls of marriageable age). She died at twenty-five from complications following childbirth.12

The history of Kartini's appropriation is almost as instructive as her actual life. Dutch colonial authorities celebrated her as the exemplary "modern native" who found liberation through Dutch education — an ideologically convenient reading that wrote out her anticolonialism and her critique of the civilizing mission's hypocrisies. Indonesian nationalists made her into the heroic foremother of the national awakening. The New Order regime institutionalized her birthday as a national holiday — Hari Kartini — but imposed a domesticated image: women in traditional dress, not feminist thinkers. Blackburn (2004) notes that the actual Kartini — a complex, angry, politically sophisticated writer — was far more radical than any of these appropriations acknowledged.13

Women's Organizations and the National Awakening

The first decades of the twentieth century saw Indonesian women move from the margins of political life toward organized collective action, riding the same currents of education, print culture, and nationalist sentiment that shaped the broader national awakening.

The earliest formal women's organizations predated the 1928 watershed. Putri Mardika ("Free Woman"), founded in 1912 and associated with Budi Utomo, focused on girls' education and the promotion of women's participation in public life — a secular-nationalist orientation. Aisyiyah, established in 1917 as the women's wing of the Islamic modernist organization Muhammadiyah, organized Muslim women around education, health clinics, and Islamic practice, framing its feminism in religious rather than secular terms. These two strands — secular-nationalist and Islamic-modernist — would run in parallel through Indonesian women's politics for the rest of the century, sometimes cooperating, sometimes competing.14

The landmark event came on 22–25 December 1928, barely two months after the Sumpah Pemuda (Youth Pledge) of October 28. The Kongres Perempuan Indonesia Pertama (First Indonesian Women's Congress) convened in Yogyakarta, drawing delegates from more than thirty women's organizations across Java and Sumatra, with over a thousand participants. The congress established the Perserikatan Perkumpulan Perempuan Indonesia (PPPI), an umbrella body that united women's organizations across ideological lines — nationalist, Islamic, and socialist wings — under the shared project of Indonesian women's advancement. The congress's founding declaration affirmed that women's emancipation was inseparable from national liberation: the two goals were not in tension but constitutive of each other.

The central substantive demand of the interwar women's movement was reform of marriage law — specifically, restrictions on polygamy, a minimum age of marriage to end child brides, and the requirement that women give meaningful consent to their unions. These demands addressed forms of subordination that cut across class and religious lines and that Kartini had already identified a generation earlier. Blackburn (2004) notes the bitter irony that this core demand was not substantially met until the 1974 Marriage Law — more than four decades after the first congress, and under a political regime (Suharto's New Order) that was simultaneously constructing the very ideology of State Ibuism that limited women's public agency.15 December 22 was eventually designated Hari Ibu (National Mother's Day), a commemoration whose name encapsulated the same paradox: a milestone of feminist mass organizing was recalled as a celebration of motherhood.

Kongres Perempuan Indonesia — the Indonesian Women's Congress, first convened in Yogyakarta on 22–25 December 1928, which established the national women's movement as a political force and set a reform agenda that included marriage law, education, and women's public participation. The date is now marked as National Mother's Day (Hari Ibu).

Gerwani and the 1965 Catastrophe: Gender as a Weapon of Mass Violence

By the early 1960s, Indonesia was home to the largest women's political organization outside the communist world. Gerwani (Gerakan Wanita Indonesia, Indonesian Women's Movement), founded in Semarang in June 1950 as the smaller Gerwis before adopting its final name in 1954, had grown to claim 1.5 million members by 1963 and perhaps three million by 1965.16 Gerwani was formally linked to the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI), which in the Sukarno era was the third-largest communist party in the world. But, as Saskia Wieringa's foundational study Sexual Politics in Indonesia (2002) argues, Gerwani was not simply a PKI transmission belt: it contained a genuine feminist wing that pursued its own agenda — marriage law reform, literacy campaigns for rural women, labor rights for women factory workers — sometimes in tension with the Party's class-first priorities.17

The night of 30 September–1 October 1965 ended all of this. A group of military officers, calling themselves the "30 September Movement," seized and killed six senior army generals, depositing their bodies in a well at a site known as Lubang Buaya (Crocodile Hole) near Jakarta. Within hours General Suharto had moved to crush the Movement, blaming it on the PKI and on Gerwani. What followed was one of the most devastating episodes of mass killing in twentieth-century history: between 500,000 and one million people were killed in the anti-communist purge of 1965–66, with hundreds of thousands more imprisoned without trial for years or decades. Geoffrey Robinson's The Killing Season (2018), drawing on declassified records, demonstrates that the Indonesian army planned and coordinated the violence as a systematic "annihilation operation" directed at the PKI and its affiliated organizations.18

For women — and for Indonesian gender politics — the most consequential element of the army's response was the propaganda campaign directed against Gerwani. Military and aligned media outlets disseminated an account of the generals' deaths that placed Gerwani women at its lurid centre. According to this story, female Gerwani members had danced naked around the captured generals at Lubang Buaya, sexually mutilated them with razor blades and ice picks, and gouged out their eyes before the killings. The accounts were detailed, visceral, and widely distributed; they were reproduced in newspapers, broadcast on radio, and eventually enshrined in a military-produced docudrama film, Pengkhianatan G30S/PKI (Treachery of the G30S/PKI, 1984), which was compulsory viewing in Indonesian schools until 1998.19

The allegations were fabricated. Autopsies conducted on 4 and 5 October 1965 — within days of the generals' deaths — showed clearly that the men had died of gunshot wounds, that there was no sexual mutilation, and that their eyes had not been removed. Wieringa's careful analysis of the autopsy evidence and the mechanics of the propaganda campaign demonstrates the systematic, deliberate nature of the fabrication.20 The army created the story it needed.

Why sexual slander specifically? The fabricated narrative served several simultaneous purposes. It mobilized popular rage against the PKI by invoking deep anxieties about female sexual immorality — communist women were framed as the exact inversion of the "proper" Indonesian woman: lustful, violent, and monstrous rather than chaste, nurturing, and submissive. It provided a gendered moral justification for the violence that followed: women who had transgressed all norms of femininity deserved what they received. And it destroyed the organizational infrastructure of Indonesian feminism, since Gerwani's members were among those arrested, tortured, raped, and in many cases killed. Survivors carried the stigma of "communist whore" for decades, unable to speak publicly, find employment, or seek justice.

McGregor and Hearman (2007) have documented how, even after Suharto's fall in 1998 opened the possibility of Reformasi-era truth-telling, former Gerwani members and their descendants found rehabilitation elusive. The stigma proved durable: the New Order had used the fabricated story not just to destroy an organization but to define what a "bad" Indonesian woman was, against which the regime's image of the "good" Indonesian woman could then be constructed.21

Gerwani — the Indonesian Women's Movement (Gerakan Wanita Indonesia), founded 1950; the largest women's organization in the non-communist world by 1965, affiliated with the PKI. Banned and destroyed after the 1965 coup, it was subjected to a military propaganda campaign of fabricated sexual atrocity stories whose falsity autopsies confirmed. Its destruction reshaped Indonesian gender politics for a generation.

State Ibuism: The New Order's Ideal Woman

Having destroyed the organized feminist left, the New Order (1966–1998) proceeded to construct an official ideology of femininity that left no space for organized women's politics outside state control. This ideology has been analysed with the term State Ibuism, coined by scholar and journalist Julia Suryakusuma in her influential 1988 thesis (published in expanded bilingual form in 2011).

Ibu is the Indonesian word for "mother." State Ibuism, as Suryakusuma defines it, was "the social construction of womanhood in New Order Indonesia," in which women were systematically identified with their roles as wives and mothers, and those roles were reframed as duties to the national-developmental state rather than personal choices.22 The ideology was institutionalized through two complementary organizations. Dharma Wanita (Women's Duty, established 1974) enrolled the wives of civil servants, organizing them by their husbands' administrative ranks: the wife of a senior official automatically outranked the wife of a junior one. Women could not advance in this organization through their own achievements, only through their husbands'. PKK (Pemberdayaan dan Kesejahteraan Keluarga, Family Welfare Guidance) extended a version of this ideology to the entire female population through a nationwide hierarchy of village-level programs focusing on family nutrition, child-rearing, and domestic skills.

The New Order officially articulated women's responsibilities through "five duties": as the wife's companion and emotional supporter of her husband; as household manager; as generator of the next generation; as socializer of children in national values; and as citizen — but the last duty was explicitly bounded by the first four. Women were citizens of the nation insofar as they were good wives and mothers; political activity outside this domestic frame was implicitly reserved for men, and explicitly for the state.23

The headscarf offers a revealing footnote to New Order gender ideology. Under Suharto's regime, Muslim schoolgirls were prohibited from wearing the jilbab (Islamic headscarf) in state schools — it was associated with political Islam, which the regime suppressed. Students who wore it were expelled. As Ahmad Najib Burhani notes, the Suharto regime linked the headscarf to Islamic extremism and treated it as a symbol of political opposition.24 The prohibition was lifted only in the early 1990s, when Suharto began to court Muslim political constituencies. The trajectory — ban, then permit, then in the Reformasi era mandate — illustrates how the female body became a site of contested state and religious authority.

Meanwhile, the gap between the ideological ideal and the economic reality was vast. Indonesian women were the majority of workers in export-oriented textile and garment factories; they constituted a large proportion of the agricultural labour force; they dominated informal trade in markets across the archipelago. State Ibuism described the ideal woman as a domestic figure oriented entirely toward husband, children, and household; the actual Indonesian woman was overwhelmingly also a worker whose labour underpinned the New Order's celebrated economic growth. The ideology did not describe reality — it disciplined it, defining any female ambition or political consciousness outside the domestic frame as deviant.

Reformasi and Its Contradictions: Women in Democratic Indonesia

The fall of Suharto in May 1998 opened new possibilities for Indonesian women — and new dangers. The Reformasi era produced both the most visible advance in formal women's political power in Indonesian history and a significant turn toward Islamist gender conservatism that created new constraints on women's bodies, dress, and sexuality.

The most symbolically charged achievement came in 2001, when Megawati Sukarnoputri — daughter of Indonesia's founding president Sukarno, and leader of the Indonesian Democratic Party of Struggle (PDI-P) — assumed the presidency after the parliamentary impeachment of Abdurrahman Wahid. Megawati served as Indonesia's fifth president until 2004, becoming simultaneously the first woman to lead Indonesia and the first female head of government of any majority-Muslim nation in the modern world.25 Her path to the presidency had been profoundly gendered: she faced explicit opposition from Islamic conservatives who argued that a woman could not legitimately lead a Muslim state, and she was frequently characterized in the press through domestic and family metaphors rather than political ones. Megawati's own political style — reserved, focused on stability and democratic consolidation — did not draw explicitly on feminist politics, and critics noted that her government did relatively little on women's rights per se. Nevertheless, her presidency mattered as a signal of the possible.

More directly consequential for the mass of Indonesian women was the 2003 election law, passed under Megawati's government, which introduced a gender quota requiring political parties to include at least 30 percent women among their legislative candidates. The provision was initially voluntary and lacked enforcement teeth, producing only a modest increase in women's parliamentary representation (from approximately 9 percent to around 11 percent in the 2004 elections). Subsequent revisions strengthened enforcement, and by the 2014 elections women held approximately 18 percent of DPR (parliament) seats — a significant improvement, though still well below the quota's own aspirational target.26

Yet Reformasi's democratic opening empowered conservative Islamic movements as much as feminist ones, and the resulting competition over the legal definition of Indonesian womanhood was sharp. The Undang-Undang Anti-Pornografi (Anti-Pornography Law) of 2008, championed primarily by the Islamist Prosperous Justice Party (PKS), was passed after years of parliamentary struggle.27 Its critics were diverse and vocal: feminist organizations, civil libertarians, artists, Balinese Hindu groups, and ethnic minority representatives argued that the law defined pornography so broadly as to criminalize traditional art forms (Balinese dance, Papuan attire), restrict women's freedom of movement and dress, and empower local majorities to impose Islamic modesty standards on religious minorities. Supporters framed it as necessary protection for families and children. The law passed, and its implementation — particularly through local regulations (perda) at the regency and district level — disproportionately affected women.

Suryakusuma, revisiting her State Ibuism argument in 2012, argued that after Reformasi the architecture of female subordination had been rebuilt under new management: where the New Order state had constructed and enforced the "good Indonesian mother" through Dharma Wanita and PKK, conservative Islamic forces were now constructing an "Islamist State Ibuism," with wifely Islamic duty replacing the civil servant's domestic obligation as the frame through which women's social roles were policed.28 Decentralization, which gave local governments far greater autonomy after 1998, accelerated this process: by the 2010s, hundreds of regencies and municipalities had issued local regulations requiring women to wear Islamic dress in government offices, restricting their movement after dark, and framing women's bodies as sources of male moral temptation requiring management. Aceh, granted special autonomy from 2001, implemented a provincial Islamic criminal code under which public caning was applied for infractions including same-sex relations and women found without appropriate dress.

The headscarf completed its remarkable political journey. Banned under Suharto as a symbol of subversive political Islam, permitted from the early 1990s, normalized through the 1990s and 2000s, the jilbab was by the 2010s socially compulsory in many professional and educational contexts across much of Indonesia — not by national law but by a combination of local regulation, employer expectation, and social pressure. The 2021 controversy at a state high school in Padang, West Sumatra, where non-Muslim girls were required to wear the hijab by teachers and administrators, provoked national outrage and a ministerial decree banning such coercion — followed, as with many such decrees, by continued local resistance. The politics of Islamic dress remained deeply unresolved, reflecting a wider contest between pluralist and Islamist visions of Indonesian public space.

For LGBT Indonesians, the Reformasi era began with relative tolerance — homosexuality was neither criminalized at the national level nor openly persecuted — and ended with serious legal and social danger. A dramatic shift occurred in early 2016, when a wave of official anti-LGBT rhetoric from cabinet ministers, the National Child Protection Commission, and military figures triggered what Human Rights Watch documented as an "unprecedented moral panic." Police raids on private gatherings, vigilante attacks, and parliamentary proposals to criminalize same-sex relations all intensified. In May 2017, two young men in Aceh were publicly caned 83 strokes each under provincial Islamic law for consensual sexual relations. Nationwide, police arrested hundreds of people for presumed sexual orientation or gender identity in 2017 alone — the highest number ever recorded in Indonesia.29 The 2022 revised Criminal Code included provisions criminalizing sex outside marriage — a provision without specific mention of homosexuality but applicable to same-sex couples who cannot marry legally — extending the legal precarity further.

The post-Reformasi women's movement, though operating in this contested terrain, was not silenced. Komnas Perempuan (the National Commission on Violence against Women), established by presidential decree in 1998, documented and advocated against domestic violence, sexual harassment, and discriminatory local regulations. Civil society organizations continued to contest the anti-pornography law in court and in public debate. Indonesian feminists, including Wieringa (2015), tracked what she termed the increasing "gender conservatism" of Indonesian public life as a structural feature of democratic competition in a majority-Muslim society, not a temporary deviation from a liberal norm.30 The task of the Indonesian women's movement in the democratic era was, in this reading, not simply to push back against an authoritarian state — as it had done in the New Order period — but to contest within democratic politics the definition of Islamic womanhood itself.

Summary

Indonesian gender history traces a path of profound variation and recurring paradox. Precolonial women in the archipelago participated actively in trade and commerce — a pattern Anthony Reid associated with the "Age of Commerce" — while matrilineal societies like the Minangkabau institutionalized female inheritance and authority in forms that persist today. Royal women governed: Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi ruled Majapahit for twenty-two years and appointed the architect of its expansion; four Acehnese sultanas reigned in succession from 1641 to 1699, until an Islamic fatwa ended female succession. Dutch colonialism created the nyai institution, placing Indonesian concubines at the intersection of sexual exploitation and racial hierarchy, while simultaneously producing Kartini, whose feminist letters became the founding text of Indonesian women's political thought. The 1928 Women's Congress organized the national women's movement around education and marriage law reform. The 1965 destruction of Gerwani — the world's largest non-communist women's organization — through a military propaganda campaign of fabricated sexual atrocity stories was one of history's most consequential uses of gender as a weapon of mass political violence. The New Order replaced feminist mass politics with State Ibuism: the domestication of women as wives-in-service-to-the-state, institutionalized through Dharma Wanita and PKK. After 1998, Reformasi brought Megawati's presidency and a 30 percent parliamentary quota, but also intensifying Islamist pressure through the anti-pornography law, mandatory headscarf regulations, and an anti-LGBT moral panic. The contest over what Indonesian womanhood should mean has been central to Indonesian politics throughout — not a footnote to the main story, but one of its animating engines.
