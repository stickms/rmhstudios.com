Learning Objectives

  • Describe Joko Widodo's background and explain why his election in 2014 was a historic departure in Indonesian politics.
  • Assess the infrastructure and development record of the Jokowi presidency, including the Belt and Road cooperation and the Nusantara capital relocation project.
  • Analyse the democratic regression critiques of Jokowi's later years, including the weakening of the KPK (Corruption Eradication Commission) and concerns about dynastic politics.
  • Trace Prabowo Subianto's political trajectory from Suharto-era general to elected president, and evaluate the significance of his contested human-rights record.
  • Explain the Constitutional Court controversy that allowed Gibran Rakabuming Raka to stand as vice-presidential candidate, and assess its implications for Indonesian democracy.
  • Describe the results and reception of the February 2024 presidential election and the early priorities of Prabowo's government.

On 20 October 2014, Joko Widodo was inaugurated as Indonesia's seventh president. A furniture trader from Surakarta who had never attended a military academy or served in a party aristocracy, Jokowi — as he was universally known — seemed to embody everything the Reformasi era had promised: a leader drawn from the people rather than from the interlocking worlds of the barracks and the boardroom that had governed Indonesia since independence. Ten years later, on the same date in 2024, a very different figure took the same oath. Prabowo Subianto — Suharto's former son-in-law, special forces commander, and a man who had twice lost the presidency to Jokowi — was sworn in as the republic's eighth president. Standing at his side as vice president was Gibran Rakabuming Raka: Jokowi's eldest son, barely thirty-six years old, whose candidacy had been enabled by a court ruling handed down by his own uncle. The arc from Jokowi's arrival to Prabowo's inauguration encapsulates a decade of genuine development achievements, accelerating democratic backsliding, and the unfinished business of Indonesia's post-authoritarian transition.

From Furniture Trader to President: Jokowi's Rise

Joko Widodo was born on 21 June 1961 in Surakarta (Solo), Central Java, into modest circumstances. His family home was demolished when he was a child to make way for a development project — an early experience of displacement he later cited when defending relocations in his own presidency. He studied forestry at Gadjah Mada University, then built a teak furniture export business that by the early 1990s was selling to European markets.1 His entry into politics came through the Solo mayoral election of 2005, which he won with just over a third of the vote; he was re-elected in 2010 with a remarkable ninety percent.2

As mayor, Jokowi became known for two things. First, a practice he called blusukan — impromptu, unannounced visits to markets, slums, and riverside communities — which projected an image of accessibility and directness strikingly different from the formal distance typical of Indonesian officials. Second, tangible urban improvements: the revitalization of traditional markets, the construction of a city walk, and the introduction of a batik-themed bus rapid transit system that became a model elsewhere. When he stood for the Jakarta governorship in 2012, defeating the well-funded incumbent Fauzi Bowo, the blusukan style translated readily to the megacity's voters. In Jakarta he launched universal health and education card programmes and initiated the city's long-delayed mass rapid transit, while tax revenues nearly doubled between 2012 and 2014.3

The 2014 presidential election pitched Jokowi, backed by the Indonesian Democratic Party of Struggle (PDI-P) of Megawati Sukarnoputri, against Prabowo Subianto, a former special forces general running for the Gerindra party. The contrast was theatrical: the career soldier who had married into the Suharto family against the civilian who had never worn a uniform. Jokowi won 53.15 percent (nearly 71 million votes) to Prabowo's 46.85 percent.4 He was the first Indonesian president to emerge from neither the military nor the established political dynasties — a break so significant that the historian and journalist Endy Bayuni would later call it a structural transformation of Indonesian politics.5 Prabowo refused to accept the result, briefly withdrawing from the official count process before the Constitutional Court upheld Jokowi's victory.

blusukan — an Indonesian term for an impromptu, unannounced inspection visit by a public official to an ordinary neighbourhood or workplace; popularised as a governing style by Joko Widodo during his tenure as Solo mayor and Jakarta governor.

A Development Presidency: Infrastructure as Political Strategy

Jokowi's governing identity centred on infrastructure. Previous Indonesian administrations had acknowledged the country's crumbling roads, inadequate ports, and absent railways; Jokowi spent on them with exceptional persistence. His 2016 infrastructure budget reached Rp 290 trillion (approximately US$22 billion), more than double what his predecessor Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono had allocated in comparable years.6 The projects that followed were continental in ambition: a Trans-Java toll road connecting the island's northern coast, a Trans-Sumatra highway, a sea toll road linking 24 strategic ports across the archipelago, and rail lines in Sulawesi and Papua that had never existed before.7

The most symbolically charged project was the Jakarta–Bandung High-Speed Railway, 142 kilometres of track connecting the capital to West Java's provincial city, completed under a Chinese-led consortium and inaugurated in October 2023. The project was formally part of China's Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), the centrepiece of Jokowi's infrastructure diplomacy with Beijing. By 2022, China had become Indonesia's largest trading partner for the tenth consecutive year, and Chinese foreign direct investment in Indonesia had grown from some US$300 million in 2013 to US$3.6 billion in the first half of 2022.8 The Morowali Integrated Industrial Park in Central Sulawesi, a 3,200-hectare Chinese-Indonesian joint venture for nickel processing and stainless steel production, became one of BRI's headline successes: it employed roughly 38,000 Indonesian and 5,000 Chinese workers, and by the early 2020s it had transformed Indonesia into the world's second-largest stainless steel producer.9

Critics raised several concerns. Indonesia's national debt rose forty-eight percent between 2014 and early 2018, reaching some US$181 billion.10 An economist at the University of Melbourne, Howard Dick, judged that infrastructure spending proceeded "often opportunistically, without much prior assessment" and with limited cost control; the chronic failure to raise Indonesia's tax-to-GDP ratio constrained the government's fiscal room for manoeuvre throughout the decade.11 In the nickel sector, where Chinese-financed smelters brought jobs but also environmental degradation and occasional violent labour disputes — including a January 2023 riot at Morowali that killed two workers — the distributional benefits of the BRI partnership remained contested.12

Nusantara: The New Capital and Its Discontents

No project illustrated both Jokowi's ambition and its complications more starkly than Nusantara — the planned new national capital in East Kalimantan on the island of Borneo. Jokowi announced the relocation in August 2019, citing Jakarta's chronic flooding, severe air pollution, and sinking land; estimates suggested that by the mid-twenty-first century large parts of north Jakarta would be below sea level. Parliament passed the capital relocation law on 22 February 2022, and the name "Nusantara" — simultaneously the classical archipelago concept and the new city's identity — was announced the following month.13

Nusantara — used in this chapter in its second sense: the planned new national capital of Indonesia in the Penajam North Paser and Kutai Kartanegara regencies of East Kalimantan, formally named in 2022. For the classical archipelago concept, see Chapter 9.

The new capital was designed across 2,562 square kilometres, with an initial governmental core zone of under 70 square kilometres. The estimated total cost stood at Rp 523 trillion (approximately US$35 billion), with the government covering roughly 19 percent and the remainder expected from public-private partnerships.14 Construction began in August 2022; by August 2024 Jokowi held Indonesia's annual Independence Day ceremony at the site, in a presidential palace surrounded by still-unfinished ministry buildings.15

Opposition to Nusantara ran across several registers. Environmentalists warned that development would destroy thousands of hectares of mangrove ecosystem in Balikpapan Bay, threatening fourteen watersheds and three regions of endemic flora and fauna. More pressingly, the Alliance of Indigenous Peoples of the Archipelago (AMAN) documented that the development zone was not, as officials claimed, empty land: at least twenty-one indigenous communities with an estimated 20,000 people faced displacement, on land where thirty agrarian conflicts over 64,707 hectares had already been recorded in the preceding five years alone.16 The project's critics argued that the rationale of relieving Jakarta's burden concealed a deeper political logic: creating a legacy monument and a new bureaucratic geography that would favour Jokowi allies.17 Whether Nusantara will achieve its ambitious targets — a city of one million by 2028, full governmental functions by that date — remains uncertain, with private investment and international interest developing more slowly than projected.18

Economic Legacies: Growth, Poverty, and the Middle-Income Trap

Jokowi entered office promising economic growth of seven percent annually, a target his New Order predecessors had sometimes achieved on a smaller economic base. He never came close: GDP growth averaged approximately five percent throughout his term, disrupted further by the COVID-19 pandemic, which in 2020 pushed Indonesia into its first recession since the 1997–98 Asian financial crisis.19 By conventional measures, however, the structural record was respectable. The World Bank assessed that extreme poverty had been "basically eliminated" by 2023, a significant social achievement in a country of 280 million people.20 A universal health insurance scheme (JKN, Jaminan Kesehatan Nasional) became, by enrollment, the largest single-payer health system in the world. Jakarta received its first serious urban mass transit investment since the colonial era, a project begun under Jokowi that finally opened the MRT in 2019.

Yet the deeper structural weaknesses — insufficient revenue collection, commodity dependence, inadequate investment in education and skills — proved persistent. Indonesia's tax-to-GDP ratio remained among the lowest of any G20 economy, limiting the government's room for social investment in ways that infrastructure spending alone could not remedy.21 Howard Dick noted that Jokowi's fiscal position depended heavily on commodity booms, particularly in palm oil and nickel, and that the 2016 tax amnesty — which disclosed Rp 4,865 trillion (roughly US$366 billion) in previously undeclared assets — was an index of elite tax evasion rather than a durable revenue solution.22 Critics also observed that while headline poverty numbers fell, roughly sixteen percent of the population remained below the national poverty line, with many more on the edge of vulnerability, and that rural and eastern-island Indonesia lagged far behind Java's urban centres.23

Jokowi maintained approval ratings above seventy percent for most of his presidency — the highest sustained ratings of any Indonesian leader in the democratic era — driven by visible infrastructure, by social protection programmes, and by a communication style that remained relatively accessible compared to most of his predecessors.24 Whether this popularity accurately reflected substantive welfare gains or reflected a simpler identification of the leader with activity and visibility is a debate that economists and political scientists continue to contest.

Democratic Regression: KPK, the Press, and the Return of Old Habits

The clearest counter-narrative to Jokowi's development story concerned democracy itself. The man who had risen as a symbol of Reformasi presided over, and in key instances accelerated, a decade-long deterioration in Indonesia's democratic institutions. Freedom House, which tracks political rights and civil liberties globally, recorded Indonesia's overall freedom score declining from 62 in 2019 to 57 in 2024, placing the country in the "Partly Free" category; comparable movement appeared in the Economist Intelligence Unit's Democracy Index, which re-classified Indonesia as a "flawed democracy."25

The most damaging institutional blow was to the Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi — the KPK, or Corruption Eradication Commission — the independent anti-corruption body established after the fall of Suharto as a cornerstone of post-authoritarian accountability. In 2019, parliament passed revisions to the KPK law within a single week, over the objections of civil society and academics: the amendments transformed the commission from an independent body into one subordinate to the executive branch, created a supervisory board with veto powers over investigations, and transferred its employees to civil servant status, making them vulnerable to political pressure.26 Allen Hicken, a political scientist at the University of Michigan who studied Indonesian democracy, called it "an unprecedented attack on two of Indonesia's most important institutions" and observed that "Indonesia's democracy doesn't expand. It doesn't deepen. It stagnates."27

Beyond the KPK, Jokowi's government deployed the Electronic Information and Transactions Law (UU ITE) to prosecute government critics for defamation, banned the Islamic Defenders Front (FPI) and Hizbut Tahrir Indonesia, and jailed the FPI's cleric Rizieq Shihab for four years.28 Jokowi executed approximately seventy drug traffickers in three rounds between 2015 and their cessation — a record that drew international condemnation. Press freedom organisations documented growing self-censorship in the media. Political rivals were, by multiple accounts, "routinely investigated for corruption" in ways that appeared calibrated to electoral timing.29 A June 2020 poll by Indikator Politik Indonesia found that public satisfaction with democracy had dropped to 45.2 percent, from 75.6 percent just months earlier — the lowest rating in sixteen years.30

By the end of his presidency, Jokowi's political coalition had expanded to encompass all but one of the nine parties represented in the House of Representatives, controlling roughly ninety percent of DPR seats and leaving almost no meaningful parliamentary opposition.31 The ISEAS–Yusof Ishak Institute volume edited by Sana Jaffrey and Eve Warburton characterised his tenure as a period of "authoritarian revival," in which "the return of statist developmentalism in the economic sphere" was accompanied by "the resurrection of New Order–era tactics of co-optation and repression in the political sphere."32

Dynastic Ambition: Jokowi's Family and the Succession Question

The final year of Jokowi's presidency was dominated by a controversy that fused every strand of concern about democratic backsliding into a single, scandalously clear episode: the engineering of his eldest son's entry into the vice-presidential race. But the dynastic impulse had been accumulating for years. Jokowi's son-in-law Bobby Nasution was elected mayor of Medan in 2020; his younger son Kaesang Pangarep, age twenty-eight, became chair of the Indonesia Solidarity Party (PSI) in 2023 and immediately explored a gubernatorial run in Jakarta; Jokowi loyalist Bahlil Lahadalia was installed as chair of Golkar, one of Indonesia's two largest parties.33 The commentary by Bloomberg's Karishma Vaswani in October 2023 captured a growing public sense that "a new political dynasty is being built in Indonesia" — invoking, deliberately, the Suharto era that the Reformasi generation had toppled.34

The most structurally significant member of the Jokowi family network was, however, none of the above: it was Anwar Usman, Jokowi's brother-in-law, who since 2022 had served as Chief Justice of the Constitutional Court. Anwar's position would prove decisive in enabling Gibran's candidacy in a manner that legal scholars described as a serious institutional rupture.

Prabowo Subianto: The General Who Would Not Quit

The man who would ultimately benefit from Jokowi's dynastic manoeuvring was Prabowo Subianto, born on 17 October 1951 in Jakarta into a privileged family: his father, Sumitro Djojohadikusumo, was one of Indonesia's most eminent economists. Prabowo was educated partly abroad — at the Victoria Institution in Malaysia and at an American school in London — before entering the Indonesian Military Academy, from which he graduated in 1974 alongside the future president Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono.35 He rose rapidly through the army's elite Kopassus special forces, serving in East Timor from his twenties and establishing himself as one of the most capable (and feared) officers of his generation. He married Siti Hediati Hariyadi — a daughter of Suharto — in 1983, binding himself to the apex of New Order power.

Prabowo's career in East Timor has been the subject of sustained scrutiny by human rights organisations and the Timorese truth commission (CAVR). The CAVR documented that in September 1983, in the aftermath of a Fretilin-led uprising, Indonesian forces carried out the Kraras massacre (approximately 200 killed), the Mount Bibileo massacres (196 documented deaths on 16–17 September 1983), and established the Lalerek Mutin concentration camp, in which over 1,000 people died from hunger and violence.36 Prabowo arrived in East Timor on 28 August 1983 with Chandraca 8 unit and established a base near the massacre sites; eyewitness accounts placed him in the area during the killings.37 The 1991 Santa Cruz cemetery massacre, in which approximately 250 peaceful demonstrators were killed, is a further episode to which Prabowo has been linked, as are post-1999 pro-Indonesian militia networks.38 Across the full period of the Indonesian occupation (1975–1999), estimates of excess deaths range from 90,000 to 200,000, with roughly seventy percent attributed to Indonesian forces and their allies.39

His best-documented violation in Indonesia itself was the abduction of at least twenty-three pro-democracy activists in 1997 and 1998, at the height of the protests against Suharto. A 2005 Komnas HAM (National Human Rights Commission) investigation concluded that the Army Special Forces acted on Prabowo's orders: nine of the abducted activists were eventually returned, one was found dead, and thirteen have never been accounted for.40 In August 1998, Prabowo was discharged from the military for his role; the United States subsequently barred him from entering the country on human rights grounds, a ban that remained in force until 2019 when Jokowi appointed him Minister of Defence.41 Prabowo himself has denied wrongdoing beyond "misinterpreting orders" and has never faced formal criminal charges, a situation that Indonesian human rights advocates have characterised as emblematic of the country's "entrenched impunity problem."42

Despite — or, in the analysis of some observers, partly because of — this record, Prabowo remained a significant political figure throughout the Reformasi era. He ran as vice-presidential candidate alongside Megawati in 2009, then launched his own presidential bids in 2014 and 2019, losing both to Jokowi. After his 2019 defeat he surprised his supporters by accepting Jokowi's offer of the Defence Ministry rather than remaining in opposition — a decision that gave him access to the levers of state and, his critics argued, allowed the Jokowi government to neutralise the most credible opposition leader.43

The Constitutional Court Coup: Gibran's Path to the Ballot

The formal obstacle to Prabowo's use of Jokowi's popularity as a political asset was electoral law. Under Article 169(q) of the 2017 Electoral Law, candidates for president and vice president were required to be at least forty years old. Gibran Rakabuming Raka, Jokowi's eldest son and mayor of Solo since 2020, was thirty-six. In mid-2023, several petitioners asked the Constitutional Court to lower the age threshold; the court rejected these petitions, treating age requirements as "open legal policy" — a legislative matter beyond the court's purview to alter unilaterally. Conventional jurisprudence was clear and consistent.44

On 16 October 2023, three days before the deadline for candidate registration, the court issued a ruling — decided 5-4 — that reversed course without adequately explaining the jurisprudential basis for the change: Article 169(q) would now be read to allow candidates under forty if they had experience as elected regional officials. The decisive fifth vote was cast by Chief Justice Anwar Usman. Justice Saldi Isra dissented on procedural grounds, noting that the majority had changed the law without a coherent constitutional rationale and in a manner that violated the principle of consistency with previous rulings.45

The ethics council of the Constitutional Court subsequently investigated Anwar and found him guilty of five violations of judicial conduct: compromising impartiality, integrity, competence, independence, and propriety. The council concluded that Anwar had "deliberately welcomed external intervention" — widely understood to mean his brother-in-law's — in the court's deliberations. He was removed from the chief justiceship and barred from presiding over election-related cases, though he retained his seat as associate justice, a sanction that legal scholars including Yance Arizona of Gadjah Mada University condemned as insufficient.46 Freedom House's 2024 annual assessment noted the drop in Indonesia's political rights score specifically because the Constitutional Court "effectively created an exception to an elections law" in a manner that reflected "relatives of top politicians entering key roles" in ways that distorted political competition.47

The ruling immediately cleared the path for Gibran to register as Prabowo's running mate, which he did before the candidate registration window closed. The Jokowi family had, through the judiciary, reshaped the electoral playing field. For Alexander Arifianto of Singapore's Institute for Defence and Strategic Studies, the message was plain: "A vote for Gibran as vice president will ensure that Jokowi will be able to continue having behind-the-scenes influence during the Prabowo administration."48

KPK (Komisi Pemberantasan Korupsi) — the Corruption Eradication Commission, an independent anti-corruption agency established in Indonesia in 2002 as part of the post-Suharto institutional reform agenda; controversially restructured and brought under executive supervision by legislation passed in 2019.

The 2024 Election: Campaign, Victory, and Controversy

The February 2024 presidential election presented Indonesian voters with three tickets. Prabowo Subianto ran with Gibran Rakabuming Raka; Anies Baswedan, a former Jakarta governor and favourite of Islamic civil society, was paired with PKB chair Muhaimin Iskandar; and PDI-P's chosen candidate Ganjar Pranowo, the outgoing Central Java governor, ran with former Constitutional Court chief justice Mahfud MD. The division of the anti-Prabowo vote between Anies and Ganjar was itself a sign of the fractured nature of the opposition, which struggled to unite behind a single candidate against the Prabowo–Jokowi alignment.49

The campaign was notable for two contrasting dynamics. In formal debate and policy terms, Prabowo was often accused of evasion on human rights questions; activists outside venues displayed photographs of the disappeared 1998 activists.50 In informal and digital terms, however, Prabowo's team executed one of the most effective social-media rebranding operations in Indonesian electoral history: he was presented on TikTok and Instagram as a grandfatherly figure who loved cats and performed "awkward dad-dance moves" at rallies, projecting warmth to the large cohort of young voters who had been children during the events of 1997–98.51 Critics noted that this campaign deliberately sought to displace memory of his military record among voters who had no personal experience of it.

Reports emerged of systematic mobilisation of state resources in favour of the Prabowo–Gibran ticket: cabinet ministers campaigning while in office, police pressuring community leaders to turn out voters for the government's preferred candidate, and an additional IDR 11 trillion channelled into direct social assistance programmes in the run-up to voting day — timed, critics argued, to build goodwill.52 Jokowi, who had initially spoken of presidential neutrality, eventually asserted his personal right to campaign, an assertion that blurred the line between the incumbent's institutional authority and his partisan preferences.53

When voting took place on 14 February 2024 — simultaneously a presidential and parliamentary election — the results were decisive. Prabowo–Gibran won 96,214,691 votes, or 58.59 percent of valid ballots: the highest vote total ever received by any candidate in a democratic Indonesian election, surpassing even Jokowi's own 85 million votes in 2019.54 Anies–Muhaimin received 24.95 percent and Ganjar–Mahfud 16.47 percent. Turnout was 82.39 percent of the 204 million registered voters.55 No runoff was required: Prabowo had exceeded fifty percent nationally with sufficient provincial spread to satisfy the constitutional threshold. The General Elections Commission (KPU) formally confirmed the result on 20 March 2024.56

Both losing tickets filed complaints with the Constitutional Court alleging "widespread distribution of social aid" and other irregularities that had distorted the contest. The court rejected all presidential election challenges on 22 April 2024, though it issued a statement urging the government to exercise more restraint in using social programmes near election periods — a veiled rebuke that fell short of overturning the result.57 The election was endorsed by international observers as procedurally valid in most technical respects; the deeper concerns about competitive fairness centred on structural advantages rather than ballot-box fraud.

Prabowo's Presidency Begins: The Red and White Cabinet

Prabowo Subianto was inaugurated as Indonesia's eighth president on 20 October 2024, at the Parliamentary Complex in Jakarta, in a ceremony attended by representatives of thirty-three countries. Leaders present included Malaysian Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim, Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr., Chinese Vice President Han Zheng, and, with particular symbolic resonance, Timor-Leste Prime Minister Xanana Gusmão — the former guerrilla commander who had opposed Indonesian occupation and whose own past intersected with Prabowo's at several points of violence.58 The inauguration had originally been planned for the Nusantara site, but was moved back to Jakarta in June 2024, a decision that signalled the new capital's persistent unreadiness.59

Prabowo's first major decision was the composition of his cabinet, which he named the "Red and White Cabinet" after the colours of the Indonesian flag. With 103 members — forty-eight ministers and fifty-five deputy ministers — it was the largest Indonesian cabinet since 1966, expanding significantly on his predecessor's thirty-four-member government.60 The scale reflected both the demands of coalition management, given the broad multi-party alliance Prabowo had assembled, and a governing philosophy that emphasised visible state presence across policy domains. A military-style retreat at the Indonesian Military Academy in Magelang four days after inauguration set a decidedly martial tone for the early cabinet.

The centrepiece of Prabowo's first-term social policy was the Free Nutritious Meal programme (Makan Bergizi Gratis), launched on 6 January 2025 and targeted at schoolchildren. Initial rollout covered 650,000 children across thirty-one provinces, with a stated goal of reaching all school-age children by the end of 2025.61 The programme was among Prabowo's most prominent campaign pledges and signalled a shift from purely infrastructure-led development toward direct welfare provision. The government also announced a 6.5 percent minimum wage increase for 2025, an MSME (micro, small, and medium enterprise) debt write-off programme affecting one million businesses, and a Rp 38.6 trillion economic stimulus package that notably declined to extend a planned VAT increase to basic necessities, applying it only to luxury goods.62

In February 2025, Prabowo established Danantara (Daya Anagata Nusantara), a sovereign wealth fund with an estimated US$900 billion in state assets under management, intended to consolidate and direct the investments of Indonesia's state-owned enterprises. Critics noted the fund's governance structures were unclear and its concentration of assets in a single vehicle created accountability risks; supporters argued it was a necessary instrument for channelling investment into industrial policy goals, particularly the downstream processing of Indonesia's critical mineral wealth in nickel, cobalt, and bauxite.63

Internationally, Prabowo moved quickly to demonstrate diplomatic independence alongside continuity. A six-country tour in November 2024 — visiting China, the United States, Peru, Brazil, the United Kingdom, and the United Arab Emirates — secured US$18.5 billion in investment commitments and signalled that Indonesia intended to maintain the multi-vector foreign policy it had practiced since Suharto's fall: balancing relationships with major powers rather than aligning with any bloc.64 Prabowo's inaugural address had included support for Palestinian independence, reflecting both domestic political sentiment and his long-standing rhetorical positioning on the international stage.

One early act attracted sharp criticism from human rights organisations. On Heroes Day in November 2025, Prabowo declared ten people "national heroes," among them the former dictator Suharto — the man under whose presidency hundreds of thousands had been killed in 1965–66, East Timor had been occupied, and Prabowo himself had built his military career. Human Rights Watch noted the irony that a president facing documented allegations of human rights violations was rehabilitating the regime under which those violations occurred; more than eighty public figures, including historians and a former attorney general, signed a letter opposing the honour, citing "three decades of military dictatorship and systematic human rights violations."65

A Kompas Litbang survey taken at the end of Prabowo's first hundred days found 80.9 percent of respondents satisfied with his leadership — notably higher than Jokowi's 65.1 percent approval at the start of his first term in 2015.66 Whether this reflected genuine satisfaction, the particular optimism of early presidencies, or the continued structural advantage of controlling state institutions and media access is a question that Indonesian democracy scholars will be examining for years to come.

Note on contested and recent events: This chapter covers events through 2025, including the early Prabowo presidency. Claims about the direction of the Prabowo government should be treated as provisional; many policy outcomes described here were in early implementation stages as of the chapter's research date. Human-rights allegations against Prabowo have been documented by multiple credible bodies (Komnas HAM, CAVR, Human Rights Watch, Inside Indonesia) but have not resulted in formal legal proceedings; they are presented here as documented allegations rather than legal findings.

Summary

Joko Widodo's decade in power (2014–2024) was defined by a genuine infrastructure revolution, measurable poverty reduction, and a communication style that made him the most personally popular Indonesian leader of the democratic era — while simultaneously presiding over a significant erosion of the institutional safeguards of democracy. The weakening of the KPK, the suppression of critics and civil society, the concentration of coalition power, and the engineering of his son's vice-presidential candidacy through a compromised Constitutional Court ruling placed Jokowi's legacy in fundamental tension with the ideals that had brought him to power. The 2024 election, which Prabowo Subianto won with an unprecedented margin after three candidacies, brought to the presidency a man whose record in East Timor and in the 1997–98 activist abductions represented the unresolved human-rights debts of the New Order era. Prabowo's early government signalled ambitions in social welfare and industrial policy alongside continuities with the developmental state model; the deeper question — whether Indonesian democracy would stabilise, regress further, or find a new equilibrium — remained open at the close of 2025.
