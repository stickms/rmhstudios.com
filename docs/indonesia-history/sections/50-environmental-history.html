Learning Objectives

  • Evaluate the evidence for and against the Toba catastrophe hypothesis, and understand why the debate about a prehistoric human population bottleneck remains scientifically contested.
  • Explain what the Wallace Line is, why Alfred Russel Wallace identified it, and why Indonesia's position between two continental biogeographic zones produces such extraordinary biodiversity.
  • Trace the transformation of Indonesian forests from pre-colonial management systems through the Dutch colonial timber and teak regimes to the mass deforestation of the twentieth and twenty-first centuries.
  • Analyse the social and environmental consequences of the three great mineral extraction frontiers — colonial tin mining in Bangka-Belitung, copper-gold extraction at Grasberg in Papua, and the contemporary nickel rush — and connect them to Indonesia's position in global commodity chains.
  • Understand how the 1997–98, 2015, and 2019 peatland fire crises were produced by a convergence of ecological, economic, and political factors, and how the resulting haze became a subject of transboundary diplomacy.
  • Assess Indonesia's paradoxical position in the climate crisis: simultaneously one of the world's largest greenhouse-gas emitters (through land-use change) and one of the most vulnerable nations to sea-level rise and climate disruption.

No nation of comparable size is more decisively shaped by its physical environment than Indonesia, and no environment of comparable richness has been more dramatically remade by human hands over the past four centuries. The archipelago sits at the intersection of two tectonic plates, two continental shelves, and two of the earth's great biogeographic realms — a position that produced, over tens of millions of years, some of the most fertile soils, most diverse forests, and most dangerous geology on the planet. Human history in the archipelago is inseparable from that physical inheritance. The supervolcano beneath what is now Lake Toba in Sumatra may have pushed our ancestors to the edge of extinction seventy-four thousand years ago. The unique biological world that Alfred Russel Wallace catalogued across the same islands in the 1850s and 1860s illuminated the mechanism of evolution. The colonial teak and timber regimes that cleared Java's hillsides were, in their ecological consequences, as decisive as any military campaign. And the fires that periodically swallow the peatlands of Kalimantan and Sumatra — burning carbon laid down over millennia in a matter of weeks — remind the world that land use in the Indonesian archipelago is a matter of global atmospheric chemistry. This chapter reads Indonesian history through the lens of environment: as the setting that made certain histories possible, as the object of extraction and transformation, and as the increasingly imperilled commons on which more than 280 million people depend.

Fire in the Deep Past: Toba and the Human Bottleneck Debate

Beneath the waters of a long, narrow lake in northern Sumatra lies the caldera of the most violent volcanic event in at least two million years of geological history. Lake Toba, 100 kilometres long and 30 kilometres wide, fills the crater left by a super-eruption that scientists have dated to approximately 74,000 years before the present — designated the Youngest Toba Tuff event (YTT) to distinguish it from two earlier eruptions at the same site. The YTT ejected an estimated 2,800 cubic kilometres of material, ranking it Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI) 8, a category so rare that only a handful of such events are known in the entire Quaternary period. For comparison, the 1815 Tambora eruption — which caused the "Year Without a Summer" and is described in Chapter 1 as the most violent eruption in at least 1,300 years of recorded history — was a VEI 7 event, an order of magnitude smaller. Toba deposited ash across South and Southeast Asia in a layer 15 centimetres thick at a distance of 2,000 kilometres from the eruption site, and a 6-metre-thick layer near the source. The aerosol veil it injected into the stratosphere could, in principle, have produced a prolonged volcanic winter, with global temperature drops of up to 3–5 degrees Celsius for years afterward.

The question of what this eruption meant for our own species has generated one of the most vigorous debates in paleoanthropology. In 1998, Stanley H. Ambrose of the University of Illinois proposed what he termed the "volcanic winter hypothesis" or Toba catastrophe theory: that the eruption's severe cooling decimated the human population outside a few tropical refugia, reducing the entire ancestral stock of Homo sapiens to a tiny remnant from which all living humans subsequently descended.1 Ambrose's argument rested on convergent lines of evidence: the presence of Toba ash layers precisely at the level in ice-core records that shows abrupt cooling; genetic studies suggesting that all living humans share an ancestor no more distant than 70,000–80,000 years ago; and mitochondrial DNA diversity patterns consistent with a severe demographic bottleneck. The hypothesis offered an elegant unification of geology, climatology, and human genetics: a Sumatran volcano as the pivotal event in human prehistory.

Subsequent research has substantially complicated, and for many scholars effectively overturned, the catastrophe interpretation. The most direct challenge came from archaeology in India. In 2007, Michael Petraglia and fourteen co-authors published a landmark study in Science reporting Middle Palaeolithic stone-tool assemblages from the Jurreru Valley site of Jwalapuram, in southern India, stratified both below and above a clear layer of YTT ash. The tools from the two layers showed no technological break — the same reduction strategies, the same core forms, the same flake types — indicating that a human population survived the eruption in India with no apparent demographic catastrophe, let alone extinction.2 Later work at the Dhaba site in the Son River valley of north-central India confirmed continuity of occupation spanning the YTT event, with ages bracketing the ash layer demonstrating uninterrupted habitation through and after the eruption. Genetic studies have also proved less supportive of the bottleneck: subsequent analysis of ancient DNA and more complete modern-human genome data showed patterns inconsistent with the sharp reduction and recovery that Ambrose's model required. The present scholarly consensus — while not unanimous — holds that the YTT eruption was a severe but survivable event for at least some human populations, particularly in refugia close to the tropics, and that it did not produce the nearly total extinction once hypothesised.

The debate is worth dwelling on not merely as paleoanthropological trivia but as a window into the role of deep geology in shaping human possibility. Whether or not Toba caused a bottleneck, the conditions in which early modern humans dispersed from Africa and across South and Southeast Asia were conditions shaped by the volcanic and climatic instability of this part of the world. The same tectonic forces that built the mountains of Sumatra and Java, that generated the fertile volcanic soils that later supported the densest agrarian populations in Southeast Asia, also produced events capable — as the debate shows — of reshaping the global human population. The environment of the Indonesian archipelago was never a passive backdrop; it was an actor with its own agenda.

Toba catastrophe theory — the hypothesis, first proposed by S. H. Ambrose (1998), that the super-eruption of Mount Toba in Sumatra approximately 74,000 BP caused a volcanic winter severe enough to reduce global human populations to a small founder group, creating the genetic bottleneck detectable in modern human DNA. The theory remains contested; archaeological evidence from India suggests human populations survived in refugia without catastrophic collapse.

The Great Archive: Wallacea and Indonesia's Extraordinary Biodiversity

When the English naturalist Alfred Russel Wallace set out in 1854 on the eight-year expedition that would take him across the Malay Archipelago — from Singapore through Borneo, Sulawesi, the Moluccas, and New Guinea — he was driven by a desire to understand the geographical distribution of species. What he found in the eastern Indonesian islands overturned his assumptions about biogeography and led him independently to formulate, at the same moment as Charles Darwin, the principle of evolution by natural selection. The intellectual breakthrough came from the islands themselves: from the arresting fact that Bali and Lombok, separated by a strait barely 35 kilometres wide and visible to one another on clear days, harboured fundamentally different faunas. On Bali, Wallace found Asian birds, Asian mammals, Asian reptiles. On Lombok, barely across the water, the fauna was Australasian — cockatoos, honeyeaters, and other creatures associated with the continent to the south-east. "I believe," he wrote in The Malay Archipelago (1869), "that the western part of the Archipelago is essentially Asiatic, the eastern essentially Australian in its natural productions." The boundary between them — now known as the Wallace Line — runs through the Lombok Strait, between Borneo and Sulawesi, and through the Makassar Strait.3

The explanation for this remarkable faunal boundary is now understood in terms of tectonic history (as described in Chapter 1): the deep ocean trenches between Bali and Lombok, and between Borneo and Sulawesi, were never bridged by dry land even during glacial maxima when sea levels dropped by 120 metres, so the fauna of Asia and the fauna of Australasia never mingled freely. Wallacea — the transitional zone of islands between the two continental shelves — is accordingly not simply poor in species but uniquely rich in endemic ones, creatures found nowhere else on earth, that evolved in isolation over millions of years. The consequence is that the Indonesian archipelago is, by most measures, the most biologically diverse country on earth. It contains approximately 10 percent of all the world's flowering plant species, 12 percent of all mammal species, 16 percent of all reptile and amphibian species, and 17 percent of all bird species, despite occupying less than 2 percent of the world's land surface. The forests of Kalimantan (Indonesian Borneo), Sumatra, and Papua are among the few remaining expanses of lowland tropical rainforest of comparable ecological complexity to the Amazon basin.

Among the most iconic inhabitants of these forests are the orangutans — the only great apes native to Asia, and the largest arboreal animals on earth. Two species were recognised for most of the twentieth century: the Bornean orangutan (Pongo pygmaeus) and the Sumatran orangutan (Pongo abelii). In 2017 a third species was formally described — the Tapanuli orangutan (Pongo tapanuliensis), confined to a small area of the Batang Toru forests of northern Sumatra and probably the rarest of all the great apes, with a population estimated at approximately 800 individuals.4 All three species are listed as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List, a reflection of the scale of habitat destruction in both islands over the past half-century. Wallace himself was struck by orangutans when he observed them in Sarawak (the portion of Borneo then under Rajah Brooke's jurisdiction): he shot numerous specimens for the British Museum and, characteristically, was also moved by their resemblance to humans and their evident intelligence. His collected observations on orangutan behaviour, made in the 1850s, remain among the earliest detailed accounts of great ape ecology in the wild.

Beyond the orangutans, the Indonesian forests harbour charismatic species whose conservation status tells the story of twentieth-century environmental change in stark terms. The Sumatran tiger (Panthera tigris sumatrae), the only surviving tiger subspecies in the Sunda region, numbers fewer than 400 mature individuals according to the most widely cited IUCN assessments.5 The Sumatran rhinoceros (Dicerorhinus sumatrensis), the smallest and most primitive of living rhinoceroses, has been reduced to fewer than 80 mature individuals in fragmented populations across Bukit Barisan Selatan, Gunung Leuser, Way Kambas, and a handful of other protected areas, with some estimates placing the wild population as low as 34–47 animals.6 The Javan rhinoceros (Rhinoceros sondaicus), once widespread across Southeast Asia, now survives only in Ujung Kulon National Park at the western tip of Java, in a population of fewer than 70 individuals. Papua's forests, separated from the Asian biogeographical zone by the deep Banda Sea, harbour extraordinary endemic fauna including 42 species of birds of paradise and some of the world's largest insects. This biodiversity did not persist without a history; it was created by geological time and sustained — or diminished — by human choices.

Wallace Line — the biogeographical boundary running through the Lombok Strait (between Bali and Lombok) and the Makassar Strait (between Borneo and Sulawesi), identified by Alfred Russel Wallace in the 1850s, separating the fauna of the Asian continental shelf (to the west) from the fauna of the Australasian zone (to the east). The boundary reflects the deep ocean trenches that prevented land connections between the two continental systems even during glacial sea-level minima.

Forests as Resource: Pre-Colonial Management and Colonial Timber Regimes

For most of Indonesian history, the relationship between people and forest was a relationship managed by local communities through customary adat systems. Swidden (ladang) agriculture — the rotation of cultivated plots through secondary forest — was, and in many outer-island communities remains, a sophisticated land-use strategy rather than the destructive "slash and burn" of colonial characterisation. Swidden under traditional rotation cycles allowed forest to regrow and soils to recover; it was a form of agroforestry adapted to the thin soils and high rainfall of the outer islands. The forests themselves were not simply left unmanaged: communities maintained complex tenure systems over trees, gathering areas, and hunting grounds, encoded in adat law. The Dayak peoples of Kalimantan, for example, practised tembawang — the cultivation and maintenance of fruit-tree gardens in the forest that blur the boundary between wild and managed landscape. These systems sustained dense human populations at a level compatible with the forest's ecological integrity for centuries.

The first sustained pressure on Indonesian forests from an external commercial system came with the VOC and the demand for ship timber. Teak (Tectona grandis), native to the seasonal deciduous forests of central and eastern Java, was prized by European and Asian shipbuilders alike for its durability and resistance to decay. The VOC's ambitions to maintain a large fleet in Asian waters translated directly into demand for Javanese teak, and as early as the seventeenth century the Company was extracting timber from Java's forests on a scale that began to alarm Dutch administrators concerned about long-term supply.7 Under the Cultivation System of the 1830s–1870s, Javanese peasants were required to devote labour to colonial crops including in some districts the clearance of hill forests for coffee and indigo cultivation, a process that historians of Indonesian environmental history have connected to the widespread deforestation of Java's mountain slopes that became visible by the middle of the nineteenth century.

Peter Boomgaard, the foremost historian of the Indonesian natural environment, has documented this process meticulously in a series of studies that collectively constitute the field of Indonesian environmental history. In Paper Landscapes: Explorations in the Environmental History of Indonesia (1997), Boomgaard and his co-editors assembled evidence showing that Java's teak forest coverage fell from an estimated 9.6 percent of the island's land area in the early nineteenth century to substantially less by the century's close, as commercial extraction outpaced regeneration.8 Concern about this depletion, Boomgaard argued, was one of the principal drivers behind the establishment of a colonial Forest Service in the 1860s–1890s: teak was a strategic industrial resource, and the Dutch administration was alarmed by the prospect of running out of it. The Forest Service imposed a "scientific forestry" model borrowed from German Forstwissenschaft — systematic rotation, selective harvesting, replanting, and exclusion of traditional users — that effectively converted much of Java's remaining teak zone into a state-managed industrial plantation system. Local communities who had managed the forest under adat tenure found themselves reclassified as encroachers.

Boomgaard's 1999 study of colonial nature conservation traced a parallel trajectory: by the late nineteenth century, Dutch administrators and naturalists had begun to recognise that the campaign to extract and clear was destroying species and landscapes of scientific and aesthetic value. The result was a nascent conservation movement within colonial administration — the establishment of nature reserves, restrictions on hunting, and eventually the creation of protected areas such as Ujung Kulon in Java (gazetted as a nature reserve in 1921, partly to protect the Javan rhinoceros) and Komodo (designated a reserve in 1938).9 These conservation efforts were, however, geographically limited and administratively fragile; they made no serious dent in the broader trajectory of forest conversion that the colonial economy demanded. The outer islands of Kalimantan, Sumatra (outside the plantation zone), and the eastern archipelago remained largely forested through the colonial period, simply because they lacked the roads, labour, and market connections that made large-scale conversion economically viable. Their exploitation was deferred, not avoided.

The Mining Frontier: Tin, Copper, Gold, and Nickel

If forests constituted one axis of colonial and post-colonial resource extraction, mineral wealth defined another. The Indonesian archipelago is geologically extravagant in mineral deposits as well as in volcanic activity: tin, bauxite, coal, petroleum, copper, gold, and nickel are distributed across the island chain in quantities that have attracted exploitative attention for several centuries and continue to do so. The environmental consequences of mining at each stage of this history reveal a consistent pattern: the externalisation of environmental costs onto local landscapes and communities while the economic benefits flow outward to corporate shareholders and national treasuries.

Tin mining on the islands of Bangka and Belitung, off the eastern coast of Sumatra, is among the oldest continuous industrial extraction operations in Southeast Asia. The Dutch VOC began commercial tin production on Bangka in the early eighteenth century, replacing an earlier informal trade managed by the Sultan of Palembang. The Dutch colonial state assumed direct control in 1819, issuing the Tin Reglement that made Bangka tin a state monopoly and prohibited private extraction. The transition from surface alluvial mining to hydraulic sluicing — water-cannon technology adopted from American practices in the mid-nineteenth century — dramatically accelerated production and dramatically accelerated environmental damage: hillsides were dissolved by high-pressure jets, riverbeds were raised by metres of accumulated sediment tailings, flooding of adjacent agricultural land became endemic, and the distinctive scrubby wasteland of exhausted mine sites — known locally as kolong, shallow pools in abandoned workings — began to cover a growing fraction of Bangka's landscape. Post-independence, the state enterprise PT Timah inherited and expanded this infrastructure; the contemporary landscapes of Bangka-Belitung, pocked with thousands of kolong ponds and largely denuded of their original forest cover, are in significant measure a product of three centuries of tin extraction.10

The Grasberg mine in the central highlands of Papua represents a qualitatively different order of environmental intervention. Located in the Sudirman Range at altitudes above 4,000 metres — one of the world's most remote high-altitude mining locations — the Grasberg deposits were among the richest copper-gold ore bodies ever discovered. American company Freeport-McMoRan obtained its first Contract of Work with the Indonesian government in 1967, just one year after Suharto's assumption of power and in the same year that the Act of Free Choice formally incorporated the former Dutch New Guinea into Indonesia. The financial and geopolitical stakes were enormous: at peak production, Grasberg was the largest gold mine and one of the largest copper mines in the world, contributing billions of dollars annually to the Indonesian state budget and to Freeport's revenues.11

The environmental consequences were correspondingly severe. PT Freeport Indonesia's chosen disposal method for mine tailings — the 167 million or more metric tonnes of pulverised rock produced each day at peak production — was riverine discharge: the slurry was released directly into the Ajkwa River system, which flows from the highland site to the Arafura Sea. By 2016, a peer-reviewed remote sensing study published in Scientific Reports by Alonzo, Van Den Hoek, and Ahmed had documented 138 square kilometres of combined rainforest, mangrove, and agricultural-land loss in the Ajkwa deposition area between 1987 and 2014 — an affected area more than 42 times larger than the open-pit mine itself.12 The river has been effectively converted into a tailings conduit; even small boats cannot navigate coastal areas once accessible to fishing communities. Indigenous Amungme and Kamoro peoples, whose ancestral territories encompassed the Ajkwa lowlands, lost both agricultural land and fishing grounds to the expanding deposition area. The Indonesian government's 2018 acquisition of a 51 percent controlling stake in PT Freeport Indonesia (for US $3.85 billion) transferred operational responsibility but not the accumulated environmental liability, estimated at more than US $13 billion to remediate.

A third mining frontier has emerged with particular intensity in the twenty-first century: the nickel rush driven by global demand for batteries in electric vehicles. Indonesia holds an estimated 48 percent of the world's known nickel reserves, concentrated in Sulawesi, North Maluku, and Papua, and the Indonesian state has positioned the country as the indispensable supplier of nickel for the global energy transition.13 The rapid expansion of nickel smelting operations — Indonesia imposed a ban on raw nickel ore exports in 2020 to force value-adding processing within the country — has drawn enormous Chinese investment into the Morowali Industrial Park in Central Sulawesi and similar complexes elsewhere, creating a new industrial zone within tropical forest that is ecologically one of the most sensitive regions on earth. Sulawesi, Halmahera, and their surrounding islands lie within the Wallace transitional zone, hosting endemic species found nowhere else. Nickel mining in these landscapes destroys forest, acidifies rivers with runoff from lateritic ore bodies, and generates fine airborne particles from smelting operations that cause respiratory disease in surrounding communities. The paradox is acute: the green energy transition demanded by the climate crisis is being powered by an extraction industry that is itself destroying forest carbon stocks and biodiversity in one of the world's most irreplaceable ecosystems.

Coal in Kalimantan: Indonesia is one of the world's largest coal exporters, with Kalimantan accounting for the vast majority of production. The coal mining boom of the 1990s–2010s added a fourth extraction frontier to the archipelago's environmental history, converting large areas of secondary forest and peatland into open pits. The environmental politics of coal — a commodity essential to Indonesia's domestic electricity system and to export revenues — sit in tense relationship with the country's climate commitments.

Fire and the Chainsaw: Deforestation, Palm Oil, and the Peatland Crisis

The scale of forest loss in Indonesia over the second half of the twentieth century is one of the defining environmental facts of modern history. Between 1950 and 2013, Indonesia lost an estimated half of its total forest cover, falling from approximately 162 million hectares to around 82 million hectares of forest of all types. The causes were multiple and they compounded each other: government-sponsored transmigration programs from the 1970s to the 1990s moved millions of people from densely populated Java and Bali to Kalimantan, Sulawesi, and Sumatra, where new settlements carved agricultural land from forest; commercial logging concessions granted under the New Order to military and political cronies stripped the accessible portions of the outer-island forests through the 1970s and 1980s; and the pulpwood and paper industry, centred on Riau province in Sumatra, created an insatiable demand for wood fibre that drove conversion of natural forest to Acacia plantations.

From the 1990s onward, the palm-oil frontier became the dominant driver of deforestation. Oil palm (Elaeis guineensis), a West African crop introduced to Southeast Asia by the Dutch in the nineteenth century and commercialised from the 1960s, proved extraordinarily productive in the tropical lowlands of Sumatra and Kalimantan. By the 2020s, Indonesia had become the world's largest producer of palm oil — a commodity used in roughly half of all packaged supermarket products as well as in biofuels — with more than 16 million hectares under cultivation. According to Global Forest Watch monitoring data, from 2001 to 2023 Indonesia lost a total of 30.8 million hectares of tree cover, representing a 19 percent decrease from the country's forest extent at the millennium; over the period 2001–2022, palm-oil expansion accounted for approximately 70 percent of deforestation nationwide.14

A dimension of this deforestation with global significance that exceeds the already alarming headline numbers is the drainage and burning of peatlands. The lowland peat swamp forests of Kalimantan and Sumatra accumulate organic matter over millennia: the peat deposits of Central Kalimantan in places reach depths of 10–12 metres, representing thousands of years of carbon sequestration. Under natural conditions, these peat domes are perpetually waterlogged, preventing the decomposition of their organic content. Draining them for palm-oil plantation and pulpwood development — through networks of drainage canals that lower the water table — exposes the peat to oxidation and dramatically increases its susceptibility to fire. In dry years, burning peat becomes self-sustaining, smouldering underground for months and releasing carbon that has been stored since before the founding of the Majapahit empire.

The most studied of these fire crises is the El Niño year of 1997–98. The exceptionally strong El Niño event of that year reduced rainfall across Kalimantan and Sumatra to a fraction of normal, and the combination of drought and the millions of hectares of drained peatland produced catastrophic fires. In a landmark study published in Nature in 2002, S. E. Page and colleagues used satellite analysis of a 2.5-million-hectare study area in Central Kalimantan to calculate that 32 percent of the area had burned, of which peat accounted for 91.5 percent; they estimated that between 0.81 and 2.57 gigatonnes of carbon were released from these fires, equivalent to 13–40 percent of mean annual global carbon emissions from fossil fuels at the time.15 In one season, the burning peatlands of Borneo altered the global carbon budget at a scale comparable to years of industrial production in major emitting nations.

The political consequences of the 1997–98 fires extended beyond carbon accounting. A toxic haze of smoke and particulate matter settled over a region 3,000 kilometres by 5,000 kilometres, affecting Malaysia, Singapore, Brunei, Thailand, and the Philippines for months, causing respiratory illness across the region and closing airports, schools, and businesses. Singapore's air quality index reached hazardous levels repeatedly. The haze exposed the inadequacy of existing international environmental law to address transboundary pollution originating in the domestic land-management decisions of a sovereign state, and eventually prompted the negotiation of the ASEAN Agreement on Transboundary Haze Pollution, which came into force in 2003 (though it lacked effective enforcement mechanisms for many years).

History repeated itself on a comparable scale in 2015, again driven by an El Niño drought. A peer-reviewed study by Huijnen and colleagues, published in Scientific Reports in 2016, found that fire carbon emissions across maritime Southeast Asia in 2015 were the highest since 1997, with daily carbon emissions from Indonesian fires at peak periods exceeding the total daily fossil-fuel emissions of the entire European Union.16 The 2019 fire season — a year without a severe El Niño — demonstrated that fire had become endemic rather than episodic, driven by year-round drainage and land-clearing rather than solely by drought. In response to domestic and international pressure, the Indonesian government imposed a moratorium on new primary-forest and peatland clearing licenses (first in 2011, made permanent in 2019) and a moratorium on new palm-oil plantation licenses in 2018. By the early 2020s, GFW data showed a significant decline in primary forest loss from its peak levels, though observers warned that the moratoriums faced serious implementation challenges on the ground.

peatland — a type of wetland ecosystem in which organic matter accumulates as peat: partially decomposed plant material deposited over millennia under waterlogged, oxygen-poor conditions. Indonesian peatlands, concentrated in the lowlands of Kalimantan and Sumatra, store enormous quantities of carbon; their drainage for agriculture exposes this carbon to oxidation and burning, releasing it as CO₂ and contributing disproportionately to Indonesia's greenhouse-gas emissions.

Earth and Sea Unbound: The 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami

At 7:58 on the morning of 26 December 2004, a rupture of approximately 1,300 kilometres propagated northward along the Sunda subduction zone off the west coast of northern Sumatra. The Indo-Australian Plate, driving northeast at roughly 60 millimetres per year, had been locked against the overlying Burma Plate; in a matter of minutes, the accumulated stress of centuries released in what geologists subsequently measured as a magnitude-9.1 earthquake — the third-largest ever recorded instrumentally — at a depth of approximately 30 kilometres below the ocean floor. The rupture of the seafloor displaced a column of water extending from the ocean bottom to the surface across an area comparable in extent to the state of California, generating a series of tsunami waves that propagated at the speed of a commercial aircraft across the Indian Ocean in every direction.17

In Aceh province at the north-western tip of Sumatra — the closest inhabited land to the rupture — the first waves arrived in minutes, before any warning could be issued. Wave heights along the Acehnese coast reached 24–30 metres in most areas and were recorded at up to 51 metres in some inlets and river valleys. The provincial capital Banda Aceh was devastated: entire neighbourhoods within a kilometre of the coast were swept clean of all structures. The official Indonesian death toll for Aceh alone exceeded 130,000, with an additional 37,000 listed as missing and presumed dead; estimates of total Indonesian deaths range from 167,000 to more than 200,000. Across all fourteen affected countries, the death toll reached approximately 227,899, making it by far the deadliest tsunami in recorded history.18

The ecological dimensions of the tsunami were severe and, in some respects, longer lasting than the immediate human catastrophe. Mangrove forests along the Acehnese coast, which in many areas had already been partially cleared for aquaculture and shrimp farming in preceding decades, were stripped or killed by saltwater inundation; subsequent research suggested that intact mangrove zones had provided meaningful wave attenuation and that their partial clearance may have amplified mortality in some areas. Coral reef systems in the Andaman Sea and around the Nicobar Islands suffered significant physical damage from the wave energy and subsequent sedimentation, with recovery in some areas delayed by pre-existing stresses from bleaching and destructive fishing. Coastal fisheries — the primary livelihood of hundreds of thousands of Acehnese — were disrupted not only by the immediate destruction of boats and equipment but by the saltwater contamination of coastal freshwater systems and agricultural land.

The tsunami had an unexpected political consequence that bears on Indonesian environmental history: it contributed to the ending of a three-decade separatist conflict. The Free Aceh Movement (GAM) had been fighting for independence from Indonesia since 1976, and the province had been under partial martial law since 2003. The scale of the humanitarian catastrophe — and the impossibility of effectively managing reconstruction amid armed conflict — opened a space for negotiations that resulted in the Helsinki Memorandum of Understanding of August 2005, which ended the armed insurgency and created special autonomy arrangements for Aceh. The reconstruction process that followed, channelled through the multi-donor Aceh-Nias Reconstruction and Rehabilitation Agency (BRR), invested approximately US $7.2 billion over four years, fundamentally transforming the province's infrastructure. Environmental considerations were incorporated into the reconstruction framework, including the creation of the Ulu Masen ecosystem conservation area and attention to coastal mangrove restoration, though the overall balance between development imperatives and ecological recovery remained contested throughout.

The tsunami also prompted a major upgrade of regional early-warning infrastructure. Before 2004, the Indian Ocean had no coordinated tsunami warning system comparable to those in place for the Pacific. The Indian Ocean Tsunami Warning and Mitigation System (IOTWS), established under UNESCO auspices in the years following the disaster and formally operational by 2011, created a network of seismometers, deep-ocean pressure sensors, and tide gauges connected to national warning centres. Indonesia's own Meteorology, Climatology and Geophysics Agency (BMKG) established a warning centre in Jakarta capable of issuing alerts within minutes of a large subduction-zone earthquake. The system was tested sorely in the decades that followed: the 2018 Palu earthquake and tsunami, the 2018 Anak Krakatau-triggered tsunami (which struck without seismic warning, exposing a gap in systems geared to earthquake-generated events), and the 2022 Cianjur earthquake all demonstrated that Indonesia's exposure to geological hazard is not historical but ongoing.

Counting What Remains: Biodiversity Loss and Conservation

The cumulative effect of two centuries of forest clearance, hunting, and land conversion is a biodiversity crisis that conservation scientists describe in terms of Indonesia's global significance. The country is consistently ranked among the world's highest in biodiversity at risk: it has more threatened mammal species, more threatened bird species, and more threatened plant species than almost any other country. The direct cause of most of this threat is habitat loss — the same deforestation frontier that releases carbon also destroys the habitat matrix on which hundreds of endemic species depend. The Sumatran rhinoceros, the Javan rhinoceros, and the Sumatran tiger all require large continuous patches of lowland forest that are precisely the habitat type most valued by palm-oil and pulpwood companies. The fragmentation of forest into isolated patches by roads, canals, and plantation monocultures is in many ways more destructive to wildlife than outright clearance, because it creates "island" populations too small to maintain genetic viability over time.

The institutional response to this biodiversity crisis has a history reaching back to the late colonial period. The Dutch Forest Service, as Boomgaard traced, began establishing nature reserves in the early twentieth century as part of its broader programme of scientific land classification. After independence, the Indonesian state inherited a network of colonial reserves and expanded it significantly: by the 1980s and 1990s, under the New Order's international engagement with conservation organisations, Indonesia had designated a system of national parks and nature reserves covering millions of hectares. Gunung Leuser National Park in Aceh and North Sumatra (4.1 million ha), Kerinci Seblat in Sumatra (1.4 million ha), and Bukit Barisan Selatan (3.6 million ha) together form the Tropical Rainforest Heritage of Sumatra, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004. Betung Kerihun, Kayan Mentarang, and Bukit Baka-Bukit Raya protect stretches of Kalimantan's interior rainforest. Lorentz National Park in Papua (2.5 million ha), one of the largest protected areas in Asia-Pacific, encompasses a continuous gradient from glaciated peaks (themselves now retreating under climate change) to mangrove coastline.

The effectiveness of these protected areas has been contested. Academic studies have shown that park designation alone does not stop encroachment where law enforcement is weak, community land rights are unresolved, and the economic incentives for conversion are powerful. The concept of REDD+ (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation, plus conservation and sustainable management), which entered international climate policy in the 2000s and attracted significant donor funding to Indonesia, was premised on the idea that forest-owning developing countries could be compensated financially for maintaining their forests as carbon sinks. Indonesia became a major recipient of REDD+ funding, with bilateral agreements with Norway (the Norway-Indonesia Partnership on Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation, valued at US $1 billion) and the development of social forestry programmes that for the first time began to formally recognise customary adat forest rights as a basis for community conservation. By 2022 the government had allocated approximately 12.7 million hectares of forest to communities under social forestry schemes — a significant shift in forest tenure toward local control, though far short of the scale of community forest rights claims.

REDD+ — "Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation plus" — an international climate mechanism that compensates developing countries financially for maintaining or increasing their forest carbon stocks. Indonesia has been one of the largest REDD+ recipients, receiving substantial funds from Norway as part of a bilateral initiative to reduce deforestation and develop alternative livelihoods for forest-dependent communities.

Indonesia's Climate Double Bind

Indonesia occupies an uncomfortable position in the global politics of climate change: it is simultaneously a major contributor to the problem and among the most acutely vulnerable to its consequences. Understanding both sides of this paradox requires understanding the environmental history traced in this chapter.

On the emissions side, Indonesia's greenhouse-gas profile is dominated by land use. According to Climate Action Tracker, "Indonesia's emissions from land use, land use change and forestry (LULUCF) sector, on average, have accounted for almost half of the country's total emissions over the last 20 years."19 This is an extraordinary statement: a single sector — essentially the draining, burning, and clearing of tropical forests and peatlands — accounts for roughly as much of Indonesia's contribution to climate change as all its energy production, transport, manufacturing, and other economic activities combined. Including these LULUCF emissions, Indonesia ranks among the world's six largest greenhouse-gas emitters by national total. It is a country at a middle level of per-capita income making a disproportionate contribution to atmospheric CO₂ concentrations, not through industrial overconsumption but through the conversion of its natural landscape to commodity production.

Recognising this, Indonesia committed under its Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) to the Paris Agreement to a Forestry and Other Land Use (FOLU) Net Sink target: by 2030, the country aims to achieve net negative emissions from the FOLU sector of 140 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent, meaning that Indonesian forests and land use would absorb more carbon than they release.20 Achieving this target would require dramatically reducing deforestation, restoring 2 million hectares of degraded peatland, and rehabilitating 12 million hectares of other degraded land — an immense programme whose credibility Climate Action Tracker has described as uncertain. The instruments exist: the moratoriums on peat and forest clearance, the social forestry programme, the peatland restoration agency (BRR Gambut, established 2016). Whether the political will to enforce them against the combined pressure of the palm-oil, pulpwood, coal, and nickel industries remains the central question of Indonesian environmental governance.

On the vulnerability side, no country of comparable population faces a more diverse array of climate risks. Jakarta, with a population of more than 10 million in the city proper and 35 million in the greater metropolitan area, is sinking at rates of 1–25 centimetres per year in different districts, driven by groundwater extraction, as global sea levels simultaneously rise. Parts of North Jakarta are already below mean sea level at high tide, protected only by a seawall. It was, in part, this crisis of urban sinking — compounded by flooding, extreme heat, and the pressure of centralisation — that drove the decision in 2022 to legislate the relocation of the national capital from Jakarta to Nusantara, in the forests of East Kalimantan: a decision that itself prompted concern about the environmental impact of building a new city in one of the outer islands' remaining forest zones. The provinces of coastal Java, the rice-producing lowlands of Sumatra, the fishing communities of the Maluku and Nusa Tenggara islands — all face intensifying exposure to floods, droughts, storm surges, and the thermal bleaching of the coral reefs on which marine livelihoods depend. The nickel boom in Sulawesi offers the further irony of Indonesia destroying its own ecological reserves to supply the batteries of electric vehicles in Europe, China, and North America: a global green transition built, in part, on Indonesian ecological sacrifice.

These contradictions are not unique to Indonesia; they are the contradictions of the global political economy writ large across one of the most geologically, biologically, and humanly complex places on earth. But they are peculiarly sharp here, precisely because the Indonesian environment is peculiarly consequential. The peatlands that burn when they dry out represent a globally significant carbon store; the forests that are cleared for palm oil are among the last remaining habitats of species found nowhere else; the volcanic soils that support a quarter-billion people are maintained by the same tectonic forces that periodically destroy tens of thousands of lives in hours. Environmental history in Indonesia is not a speciality for those interested in nature for its own sake: it is a lens through which the entire history of the archipelago — from deep geological time to the climate negotiations of the present — comes into focus.

Summary

Indonesian environmental history spans geological deep time and the climate negotiations of the present, connecting them through a continuous thread of human interaction with one of the world's most dynamic and biologically rich landscapes. The Toba super-eruption of approximately 74,000 BP — the largest volcanic event in two million years — may or may not have caused a human population bottleneck, as Ambrose proposed in 1998; subsequent archaeological evidence from India, including Petraglia and colleagues' landmark 2007 study, has cast serious doubt on the bottleneck interpretation, but the debate illuminates the role of catastrophic geology in shaping the conditions of human survival. Alfred Russel Wallace's eight-year expedition across the archipelago, culminating in The Malay Archipelago (1869), identified the biogeographical boundary that bears his name and demonstrated that the islands' unique position between two continental shelves had produced extraordinary biodiversity — biodiversity now threatened by the same forces of extraction and conversion that have characterised the last four centuries of Indonesian history.

Colonial timber and teak regimes cleared much of Java's forests, creating the scientific forestry model that simultaneously industrialised extraction and attempted to manage it. Post-colonial deforestation, driven by transmigration, commercial logging, and above all palm-oil expansion, removed roughly half of Indonesia's forest cover in the second half of the twentieth century. The peatland fires of 1997–98 and 2015, releasing gigatonnes of ancient carbon in a matter of weeks, made visible the global consequences of this land-use change. Mineral extraction — from colonial tin in Bangka-Belitung to the Freeport-Grasberg mine in Papua to the contemporary nickel rush in Sulawesi — has added further layers of environmental impact across the archipelago. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, killing more than 227,000 people in fourteen countries, was a reminder that geological hazard in Indonesia operates at civilisational scale. And Indonesia's climate double bind — as a major LULUCF emitter and one of the most vulnerable nations to sea-level rise — frames the environmental challenges of the twenty-first century as both a national and a global responsibility.

49.1 Why does the Toba catastrophe theory remain scientifically contested despite initial support from both geology and genetics? What kinds of evidence would be needed to resolve the debate decisively?
49.2 Alfred Russel Wallace described the line between Bali and Lombok as one of the most striking faunal boundaries in the world. What geological processes produced it, and why does it matter for understanding Indonesia's biodiversity today?
49.3 Compare and contrast the Dutch colonial teak regime in Java with the post-independence palm-oil frontier in Kalimantan and Sumatra. What are the similarities in the political-economy logic of each, and what are the key differences in scale and ecological impact?
49.4 The 1997–98 peatland fires released carbon equivalent to 13–40 percent of annual global fossil-fuel emissions. How does this figure reframe our understanding of "Indonesian deforestation" as a local issue versus a global one? What diplomatic and legal challenges does it raise?
49.5 The 2004 tsunami contributed to the end of the Aceh insurgency through the opening it created for negotiations. What does this suggest about the relationship between environmental catastrophe and political change? Is this connection unique to this case?
49.6 Indonesia's nickel boom is often framed as part of the global solution to climate change (through supplying EV batteries) but also as a major environmental problem. How would you evaluate this paradox from the perspective of Indonesia's own climate commitments?
49.7 Compare the environmental governance record of the Dutch colonial state (forest services, nature reserves) with that of the post-independence Indonesian state. In what ways was there continuity, and in what ways did independence change the relationship between state power and the natural environment?
49.8 Jakarta is sinking while global sea levels rise, and the capital is being moved to a forested zone of Kalimantan. What does this decision reveal about the trade-offs between urban environmental crisis, regional development aspiration, and the conservation of remaining natural landscapes?
