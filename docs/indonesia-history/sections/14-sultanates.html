Learning Objectives

  • Apply Anthony Reid's "Age of Commerce" framework to analyze how the Indonesian sultanates of the fifteenth through seventeenth centuries participated in an integrated global commercial and religious system.
  • Trace the founding, expansion, and fragmentation of Demak, the first major Muslim polity in Java, and explain the role of the Wali Songo in shaping its religious and political character.
  • Evaluate the Islamic Mataram Sultanate under Panembahan Senapati and Sultan Agung, including the causes and consequences of the failed sieges of Batavia in 1628 and 1629.
  • Analyse the political economies of Banten and Aceh as pepper-exporting maritime sultanates, and assess the reign of Sultan Iskandar Muda as emblematic of the Age of Commerce at its apex.
  • Describe the structure and fall of the Gowa-Tallo confederation in Sulawesi, and trace how the Makassar War (1666–1669) remade the balance of power in eastern Indonesia.
  • Explain the geopolitical significance of the Maluku sultanates of Ternate and Tidore, including their entanglement with successive European powers — Portuguese, Spanish, and Dutch.

Between the fifteenth and late seventeenth centuries, the Indonesian archipelago was home to some of the wealthiest and most ambitious polities in the world. From the pepper ports of Sumatra's west coast to the clove gardens of Maluku, a patchwork of Muslim sultanates dominated the region that the historian Anthony Reid called the "Age of Commerce" — an era in which maritime trade, court Islam, and gunpowder politics reshaped the entire eastern half of Southeast Asia. These sultanates were not exotic survivals or pre-modern backwaters; they were active agents of a cosmopolitan global order, trading with India, China, Arabia, and eventually Europe, financing armies and navies, producing legal codes and religious scholarship, and constructing urban centres that in some cases surpassed contemporary European cities in population. The great sultanates — Demak, Mataram, Banten, Aceh, Gowa-Tallo, Ternate, and Tidore — rose, flourished, and ultimately struggled against both internal fragmentation and the relentless commercial and military pressure of the VOC, which arrived in the archipelago in 1596 and transformed the region's political economy over the following century. Understanding these states on their own terms, not merely as victims of European imperialism, is the central task of this chapter.

The Age of Commerce: Anthony Reid's Framework

The indispensable conceptual framework for understanding the fifteenth-to-seventeenth-century Indonesian sultanates is Anthony Reid's two-volume work Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce, 1450–1680 (Yale University Press, 1988–1993). Reid argued that the period between roughly 1450 and 1680 constituted a distinctive historical era defined by a surge in international trade, urban growth, military modernization through the adoption of gunpowder technology, and religious transformation through the spread of Islam (and, to a lesser extent, Christianity). The first volume, The Lands below the Winds, reconstructed the material culture, social organization, and everyday life of Southeast Asia using European travel accounts, Chinese diplomatic records, and indigenous texts. The second, Expansion and Crisis, charted the rise of trade-based city-states, the consolidation of scriptural religions emphasizing personal morality, the increasing absolutism of maritime rulers, and ultimately the seventeenth-century crisis that terminated the Age of Commerce and opened the way for sustained European hegemony.12

Reid's central argument was that Southeast Asia between 1450 and 1680 was fully integrated into the global economy long before Europeans arrived as colonizers. The monsoon wind system connected the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea into a single maritime domain, and the islands of the Indonesian archipelago — sitting astride the main sea-lanes between India, China, and the Spice Islands of Maluku — were ideally positioned to prosper from transit trade and the export of prized commodities: pepper, cloves, nutmeg, mace, camphor, and gold. In Reid's reconceptualization, the sultanates were not passive recipients of external influences but dynamic agents of a cosmopolitan and interconnected world. He rejected both the nationalist historiographies that had tended to treat individual Indonesian polities in isolation and the Eurocentric frameworks that portrayed Southeast Asia as a region waiting to be colonized, demonstrating instead that long before European imperialism the region was part of a global historical continuum on its own terms.3

The "crisis of the seventeenth century" that Reid identified as the Age of Commerce's terminal phase was produced by several reinforcing factors: the VOC's strategy of monopolizing the spice trade through violence and exclusion; an economic contraction linked to demographic pressures and the global climate disruption of the Little Ice Age; and the internal contradictions of the agrarian and maritime sultanates — their vulnerability to succession disputes, their difficulty integrating coastal Muslim merchant communities with inland aristocratic expectations, and the high cost of sustained warfare against an organization backed by Dutch financial capital. Understanding this crisis helps explain why the vibrant sultanates described in this chapter did not simply capitulate to Dutch power but resisted fiercely before eventually succumbing — and why their legacies, in Islamic practice, legal tradition, and court culture, outlasted the VOC itself.2

Age of Commerce — the term coined by historian Anthony Reid for the period c. 1450–1680 in Southeast Asia, characterized by booming international trade, urban growth, the spread of scriptural religions (principally Islam), the adoption of gunpowder technology, and the political dominance of maritime trading states. The concept reframes Southeast Asia as a dynamic participant in early globalization rather than a passive object of colonial history.

Demak and the Wali Songo: Java's Muslim Transformation

The Sultanate of Demak occupies a unique position in Indonesian history as the first major Muslim polity on the island of Java — an achievement all the more significant because Java had been for centuries the heartland of the Hindu-Buddhist civilization represented most gloriously by Majapahit. Demak's rise was intimately connected to Majapahit's decline, though historians debate both the timing and the nature of that connection. The sultanate traces its founding to Raden Patah, a figure whose origins are contested in the sources but who is consistently described as having claimed descent from Majapahit royalty — specifically from King Kertabhumi — which allowed him simultaneously to present himself as an Islamic reformer and a legitimate heir to Java's greatest political tradition. The chronogram marking Demak's formal establishment as an Islamic polity corresponds to 1481 CE, and Raden Patah is conventionally given reign dates of approximately 1475 to 1518, though the sultanate's assertion of independence from whatever remained of Majapahit's authority was a gradual process rather than a single event.5

Demak's economic foundations were those of a successful north-coast trading port. Positioned on a navigable waterway connecting the interior of Java to the Java Sea, the sultanate imported spices from Maluku and the eastern islands and exported rice to Malacca and the spice islands — a complementary trade that made Demak, in some accounts, the wealthiest and most populous harbour city on Java. The sultanate incorporated the ancient solar emblem of Majapahit — the Surya Majapahit — into its visual vocabulary while removing its explicitly Hindu associations, an act of symbolic continuity and transformation typical of how Javanese Muslim rulers managed the transition between religious orders.5

This commercial strength underwrote military ambition. Raden Patah's successor, Pati Unus (r. 1518–1521), led a substantial naval expedition against Portuguese-held Malacca in an attempt to dislodge the European newcomers who had captured that great commercial hub in 1511 (an event treated in Chapter 12). The expedition failed, and Pati Unus died shortly after its return, but it demonstrated that Demak could project power well beyond Java and that its rulers understood the strategic threat posed by Portuguese monopolization of Malacca's entrepôt trade.5 The sultanate reached its territorial apogee under Sultan Trenggana (r. 1521–1546), who expanded authority across Central Java, suppressed remaining centres of Hindu-Buddhist power in the interior, and conquered territories in West Java, establishing the Banten Sultanate as a dependency. Scholars — notably M. C. Ricklefs — have debated whether Trenggana's campaigns constituted ideological jihad against unbelievers or pragmatic military expansion exploiting Majapahit's internal weaknesses; the consensus now leans toward seeing the two motivations as inseparable in practice, since Islamic legitimation and territorial ambition mutually reinforced each other at every Javanese Muslim court.4

Trenggana's death during an East Java campaign in 1546 triggered a catastrophic succession crisis. The conflict between rival claimants escalated through the violent career of Arya Penangsang, who murdered multiple competitors, until a coalition of regional lords under Hadiwijaya defeated and killed Penangsang in 1568. Hadiwijaya established the Kingdom of Pajang in the interior of Java, absorbing what remained of Demak's regalia and political inheritance. Thus within barely two generations of its founding, Java's first great Muslim sultanate had fragmented — bequeathing to its successors the project of Javanese Muslim statehood, the Masjid Agung Demak (see Figure 13.1), and the living tradition of the Wali Songo, but not the political structure to sustain it.5

The religious transformation that Demak catalyzed was inseparable from the activities of the Wali Songo — the "Nine Guardians," the council of Islamic scholars who are traditionally credited with the Islamization of Java. Raden Patah appointed leading Wali Songo figures to positions of religious authority within his court: Sunan Kudus served as qadi (judge of religious law), Sunan Giri as mufti (issuer of legal opinions), and Sunan Kalijaga — the most culturally creative of the nine — as court imam. Sunan Ampel, meanwhile, served as Raden Patah's teacher and father-in-law, illustrating how closely intertwined the new Muslim political elite and the Islamic scholarly establishment were at Demak's founding.5

Sunan Kalijaga's role deserves particular attention because it illuminates the cultural method by which Islam spread across Java. Rather than demanding the immediate abandonment of Hindu-Buddhist artistic and ritual traditions, Kalijaga is credited in later tradition with adapting the wayang kulit (shadow puppet theatre), the gamelan music tradition, and the Javanese ritual calendar to carry Islamic themes and stories — a strategy of inculturation that allowed Islam to take root in a society saturated with older cultural forms. Demak's great mosque, the Masjid Agung Demak, embodies this synthesis architecturally: its triple-tiered tajug roof is an adaptation of Hindu-Javanese tiered-roof temple forms rather than the dome-and-minaret idiom of Middle Eastern mosques (Figure 13.1). The mosque's enduring status as the most sacred Muslim site in Java testifies to the success of the Demak synthesis.5

Wali Songo — literally "Nine Guardians" or "Nine Saints" (wali from Arabic for a friend of God; songo from Javanese for nine); the council of Islamic scholars traditionally credited with converting Java to Islam in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries. Each wali was associated with a specific region and is venerated at a pilgrimage tomb. The tradition blends historical figures with legendary ones, and the exact composition of the nine varies across different textual traditions.

The Islamic Mataram Sultanate: Senapati, Sultan Agung, and the Limits of Javanese Power

When Demak collapsed and Pajang proved equally short-lived, the centre of Javanese political gravity shifted away from the coast and into the rice-farming interior of the Kedu Plain — the agricultural heartland of Central Java associated in cultural memory with the Hindu-Buddhist Mataram of the Sailendra dynasty (as discussed in Chapter 8). It was here that a new Javanese Muslim state took shape in the 1580s: the Islamic Mataram Sultanate, sometimes called simply the Sultanate of Mataram, its name deliberately evoking the ancient Hindu-Buddhist polity while claiming a new Islamic legitimacy. The founder, Panembahan Senapati (born Sutawijaya; r. c. 1584–1601), was a Javanese nobleman who had served the Kingdom of Pajang as a district chief, exploited Pajang's internal conflicts to assert autonomy, and consolidated an independent principality around the village of Mataram in approximately 1584. He then prosecuted campaigns against rivals — Madiun (c. 1590–91) and Kediri (1591) — while positioning himself as the legitimate heir to Java's political tradition and Islamic virtue simultaneously.6

The sultanate reached its apex under Senapati's grandson, Sultan Agung Anyokrokusumo (r. 1613–1645), whose reign transformed Mataram into the dominant power in Java and one of the most formidable states in Southeast Asia. Sultan Agung ascended the throne at approximately age twenty and immediately prosecuted an ambitious programme of territorial expansion. He systematically subjugated the major port cities of Java's north coast — Lasem (1616), Pasuruan (1617), Tuban (1619), and Sukadana in Borneo (1622) — each of which had maintained semi-independent status under its own Muslim ruler. He crossed to Madura (1624) and finally, after a brutal five-year siege of starvation and blockade, forced the proud and wealthy port city of Surabaya to submit in 1625. At its greatest extent, Agung's Mataram encompassed virtually all of Central and East Java plus Madura — the largest unified Javanese polity since Majapahit's zenith under Hayam Wuruk.7

The one prize that eluded him was Batavia, the fortified Dutch settlement on the northwest coast of Java established by Jan Pieterszoon Coen in 1619 (treated more fully in Chapter 15). Sultan Agung viewed Batavia as both a commercial rival — it was diverting trade from his coastal vassal cities — and an affront to his claim to supremacy over all of Java. He launched two major military campaigns against it. The first siege of Batavia, launched in August 1628, was commanded by Tumenggung Bahureksa and Prince Mandurareja. An estimated ten thousand troops advanced along Java's north coast, blockading the city by October. But the Mataram forces had not prepared for a prolonged siege in an area devoid of local logistical support; by December they were running short of food, and Dutch naval superiority allowed resupply of the garrison while cutting off the besiegers. When news of the failure reached Agung, he executed both commanders; seven hundred and forty-four headless corpses were reportedly found near the city.8

For the second siege of 1629, Agung took elaborate logistical precautions. He ordered the establishment of rice-farming settlements along the north coast of West Java — from Cirebon to Karawang — to create supply depots for an army of approximately twenty thousand troops that would advance in two columns. The plan failed for multiple reasons. One commander, Dipati Ukur of the Sundanese Priangan, rebelled mid-campaign following abuses committed by Mataram troops against civilians in his territory. Disease — malaria and cholera — devastated both the attacking Javanese forces and the Dutch garrison; VOC Governor-General Jan Pieterszoon Coen himself died of cholera on 21 September 1629 at the height of the crisis. Dutch naval interception destroyed Agung's supply fleets before they could reach the army. The second siege ended as the first had: in failure and retreat.8 Scholars have argued that Batavia's survival in 1628–29 marks the moment at which the VOC secured its long-term position in Java, since a successful Mataram assault on Batavia would have eliminated the Dutch presence in the archipelago before it could consolidate into the colonial system of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries.4

Sultan Agung's legacy was not merely military. In 1633 he introduced a reformed calendar blending the Islamic lunar year (Hijri) with the pre-Islamic Javanese Saka cycle — an act simultaneously of cultural synthesis and of royal authority, since controlling the calendar was a prerogative of divine kingship in Javanese tradition. In 1641 he dispatched envoys to Mecca who returned with formal permission to bear the title Sultan, and he thereafter styled himself Sultan Abdullah Muhammad Maulana Mataram. He also commissioned the royal cemetery of Imogiri (1632) in the hills above Yogyakarta, which became the burial place of subsequent Mataram rulers and remains a sacred pilgrimage site.7 The arts of gamelan, wayang, batik, and formal court dance were all consolidated under his patronage, creating the version of Javanese high culture that later courts of Yogyakarta and Surakarta would inherit.

The form of Islam practiced at the Mataram court, however, was deeply shaped by pre-Islamic Javanese tradition, generating a syncretic religious culture known as Kejawen ("Javanese-ness"). In this synthesis, the conception of the ruler as a semi-divine cosmic intermediary — rooted in Hindu-Buddhist kingship theory — coexisted with the Islamic conception of the sultan as God's deputy (khalifatullah) on earth. Courtly ritual, the arts of wayang and gamelan, and the traditions of the royal kraton all bore the marks of this layered inheritance. As one analysis of Mataram's governance puts it, "the concept of the king as a semi-divine, priestly being was gradually replaced with the concept of the ruler as a messenger of God" — but the replacement was never complete, and the pre-Islamic mystical traditions remained embedded in Javanese Islam at every social level.9

Agung's death in spring 1645 inaugurated a prolonged period of internal instability. His successor, Amangkurat I, was a tyrannical ruler who massacred thousands of Islamic scholars and provoked a series of rebellions. The most dangerous was the revolt led by Trunajaya (1675–1679), which forced Amangkurat II to flee and seek Dutch military assistance. The VOC's intervention — supplying the force that crushed Trunajaya — came at a steep price: the cession of large coastal territories and exclusive commercial rights. By the early eighteenth century Mataram was a VOC client state, and in 1755 the Treaty of Giyanti formally divided what remained into the Sultanate of Yogyakarta and the Sunanate of Surakarta, ending the Islamic Mataram Sultanate as a unified polity but bequeathing its cultural inheritance to these successor courts.6

Kejawen — the syncretic spiritual and cultural tradition of Central Javanese court society, blending pre-Islamic Javanese mysticism (with its Hindu-Buddhist concepts of cosmic order and divine kingship) with elements of Islamic piety and practice. Kejawen is best understood not as a corrupted or incomplete Islam but as a distinct cultural synthesis with its own internal logic — one that proved remarkably durable, persisting in the kraton traditions of Yogyakarta and Surakarta to the present day.

Banten: Islam, Pepper, and the Cosmopolitan Port

If Mataram represented the agrarian, inland face of Javanese Muslim power, the Banten Sultanate represented its maritime, mercantile face — and the contrast illuminates the range of political and economic forms that Islam could sustain in the archipelago. The Banten Sultanate was established in 1552 by Maulana Hasanuddin, son of Sunan Gunungjati (one of the Wali Songo), who had conquered the area from the Hindu Sunda Kingdom and established an Islamic presence there in 1526 as a Demak dependency. When Hasanuddin declared independence in 1552 following Demak's fragmentation, he transformed a vassal port into an autonomous sultanate anchored on the northwest coast of Java near the Sunda Strait — the gateway between the Indian Ocean and the Java Sea.10 Under subsequent rulers, Banten expanded its territorial reach, eventually incorporating the former Sunda Kingdom lands west of the Citarum River and establishing control over pepper-producing territories in southern Sumatra (Lampung), giving it command over one of the most productive agricultural zones in the western archipelago.

Banten's prosperity rested almost entirely on pepper. The sultanate's hinterland produced the black pepper that was the most sought-after commodity in world trade — a spice valued across Asia and Europe as a flavouring, preservative, and medicine. Pepper "could finance armies, attract ships, and shape the fortunes of kingdoms," and the rulers of Banten understood this perfectly: they positioned their court as the essential intermediary between inland pepper growers, coastal merchant communities, and the foreign trading fleets that arrived each year from Gujarat, Malabar, China, and eventually northern Europe.11 The political economy of pepper thus bound together a diverse coalition of interests — agricultural producers in the hinterland, Chinese and Indian merchant communities at the port, Banten's own orang kaya (mercantile nobility), and the royal court — in a web of complementary dependencies that sustained the sultanate's power as long as external actors could not sever the chain.

By the late sixteenth century, when the first Dutch fleet under Cornelis de Houtman arrived in 1596, Banten already hosted merchant communities from China, India, Turkey, Portugal, England, and Denmark, living in separate residential quarters around the harbour — a cosmopolitan mosaic that made the port one of the most diverse commercial cities in Asia. The English East India Company established a permanent factory in 1602; the VOC followed in 1603. The arrival of multiple competing European companies initially benefited Banten's rulers, who played the rivals against each other to maintain commercial leverage.10

The sultanate's golden age came under Sultan Ageng Tirtayasa (r. 1651–1683), who pursued an ambitious programme of agricultural development, diplomatic expansion, and commercial modernization. He constructed extensive canal and irrigation networks converting tens of thousands of hectares into productive rice paddies, resettling some thirty thousand people from coastal areas inland. He established a trading fleet modelled on European designs and maintained diplomatic correspondence with rulers across Asia, the Middle East, and Europe — including the English king, the French court, and the Grand Shareef of Mecca, from whom he received formal recognition as a legitimate Muslim sultan (1638, the first such recognition for a Javanese ruler). Under Ageng, Banten junks traded with Tonkin, Siam, Cambodia, Bengal, Surat, China, and Japan, and the sultan established commercial offices as far away as London and Surat — a genuine global commercial reach that testified to the vitality of Age of Commerce networks even as the VOC tightened its grip elsewhere.10

The fatal contradiction in Banten's position was its proximity to Batavia, which from 1619 onward served as the VOC's Southeast Asian headquarters and a structural commercial rival. A succession of conflicts, blockades, and uneasy truces followed the Dutch arrival, punctuated by agreements that Banten sought to circumvent by encouraging rival European powers — especially the English — to trade directly. The crisis came in 1682–1683 when a dispute between the aging Sultan Ageng and his son Sultan Haji escalated into civil war. Sultan Haji, inclined toward accommodation with the Dutch, invited VOC military intervention. Dutch forces rescued Sultan Haji and defeated his father, who died a prisoner in Batavia in 1692. The price was total: Sultan Haji signed treaties granting the Dutch a monopoly on Banten's pepper trade, the expulsion of all non-Dutch European merchants, and the right to garrison a fort at the harbour entrance. Banten was from 1683 effectively a Dutch protectorate, its days as a cosmopolitan free port definitively over.10

orang kaya — literally "rich person" in Malay; used to designate the merchant and noble elite of port city sultanates such as Banten, Makassar, and the Maluku spice islands. The orang kaya formed a crucial intermediary class between the sultan and the trading population, often wielding considerable political influence through their control of commercial networks and their capacity to finance — or withhold — the sultan's military campaigns.

Aceh Under Iskandar Muda: The Sumatran Golden Age

In the hierarchy of Age of Commerce sultanates, Aceh stands out for the combination of commercial wealth, military ambition, and Islamic scholarship it achieved at its peak — a peak most fully embodied in the long reign of Sultan Iskandar Muda (r. 1607–1636). Situated at the northern tip of Sumatra, the Sultanate of Aceh commanded the approaches to the Strait of Malacca and was ideally positioned to extract revenue from the spice trade passing between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. The sultanate had been formally established by the early sixteenth century; an energizing early figure was Ali Mughayat Syah (r. c. 1514–1530), who unified much of northern Sumatra under Acehnese authority and expelled the Portuguese from key ports on the Sumatran coast, establishing Aceh as the primary challenger to Portuguese Malacca.12

By the time Iskandar Muda seized power in 1607, Aceh already controlled key pepper- and gold-producing regions in Sumatra. His reign — spanning nearly three decades — transformed the sultanate into the dominant maritime power of the western archipelago. Born around 1583 and bearing the full title Perkasa Alam Darmawangsa Tun Pangkat Johan Berdaulat Zilullahi Fil Alam Paduka Seri Sultan Iskandar Muda, he extended Acehnese control systematically across Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula: Deli (1612), Johor (1613), Pahang (1617), Kedah (1619), and Perak (1620) all came under Acehnese suzerainty or suffered Acehnese attack.19 At the height of his power, Banda Aceh was one of the wealthiest and most populous cities in Southeast Asia, hosting Arab, Indian, Chinese, and European merchants in a cosmopolitan commercial district while the sultan's court embodied a self-consciously sophisticated Islamic culture.

Iskandar Muda monopolized the pepper trade with particular effectiveness, compelling foreign merchants to transact business exclusively through the capital and controlling the entire supply chain from Sumatran hinterland producers to the dockside warehouses of Banda Aceh. He financed the import of cannon and firearms from the Ottomans, English, and Dutch, building up one of the most powerful arsenals in the region and making Aceh's navy a serious threat to Portuguese shipping in the Strait of Malacca.12 He also undertook significant administrative reforms: he codified the Adat Aceh, a legal framework integrating customary law with Islamic jurisprudence, and replaced hereditary princes with appointed district officers (panglima) required to submit annual reports to the court — a centralizing move that reduced the autonomy of regional nobles at the cost of generating resentment that would haunt his successors.19

Aceh under Iskandar Muda was also a major centre of Islamic learning — so important that it earned the honorific "Porch of Mecca" (Serambi Mekkah), designating it as a gateway through which Malay Muslim pilgrims passed on their way to the Hijaz and a place where the Qur'an was translated into Malay and Islamic jurisprudence taught. The court hosted some of the most significant Islamic writers in the Malay world: the Sufi mystic poet Hamzah Fansuri, whose ecstatic verse drew on the waḥdat al-wujūd (unity of being) tradition; his student Shamsuddin of Pasai; and — after Shamsuddin's death — the more orthodox and combative scholar Nuruddin al-Raniri, who arrived at Aceh's court in 1637 and condemned Fansuri and Shamsuddin's mystical tradition as heretical, burning their books and spearheading a doctrinal purge that illustrated the fierce internal debates within the Islamic world of this period.12

The most catastrophic moment of Iskandar Muda's reign was a massive naval assault on Portuguese Malacca in 1629. The sultan assembled an enormous fleet and army, but combined Portuguese and Johor forces destroyed the Acehnese armada, killing approximately nineteen thousand soldiers and wrecking nearly all his ships. Aceh never fully recovered its offensive military capacity after this disaster. Iskandar Muda died in December 1636 without a surviving legitimate heir — a succession crisis that he had partly caused by reportedly executing his own son on suspicion of disloyalty — and his death opened the sultanate to prolonged political instability.19

The post-Iskandar Muda era saw Aceh's relative decline. After the death of his successor Iskandar Thani in 1641, the sultanate was governed by four female sultanas (Sultanah) in succession for nearly sixty years — an unusual pattern that Islamic scholars from Mecca eventually attempted to prohibit on grounds of Quranic injunctions against female rule. Regional governors (panglima sagi) grew increasingly autonomous, and by the 1680s, according to one European account, "every corner shelters a separate king or governor."12 Yet Aceh retained enough political coherence to resist Dutch encroachment for far longer than most other Sumatran polities. Its ultimate conquest came only in the Aceh War of 1873–1914 — one of the most prolonged and costly colonial military campaigns in Indonesian history.

Adat Aceh — the customary legal code of the Sultanate of Aceh, systematized under Sultan Iskandar Muda in the early seventeenth century, blending local customary norms (adat) with Islamic jurisprudence into a comprehensive administrative framework governing trade, court protocol, and civil disputes. Its codification illustrates the characteristic Age of Commerce project of sultanates that sought to reconcile Islamic universalism with local juridical traditions.

Gowa-Tallo: Makassar's Maritime Empire and Its Fall

Far to the east, on the southwestern peninsula of Sulawesi, the twin kingdoms of Gowa and Tallo formed a confederation that became, in the early seventeenth century, the pre-eminent maritime trading state of eastern Indonesia. The Makassar region — Makassar referring both to the city-port and the Makassarese people who dominated these kingdoms — occupied a strategic geographical position as a transit point between the clove-producing islands of Maluku to the northeast, the rice- and textile-producing lands of Java to the west, and the sandalwood islands of eastern Indonesia to the south. The region's rulers exploited this position to build one of the most dynamic commercial cities in Southeast Asia.13

Gowa had been established as a kingdom from approximately 1320 CE, though for much of its early history it was a relatively localized political entity. The decisive transformation came in 1605, when the ruler of Tallo, Karaeng Matowaya Tumamenanga ri Agamanna, converted to Islam under the influence of three Minangkabau scholars and received the Islamic name Sultan Alauddin. He then persuaded the Gowa ruler to follow suit, in a conversion that became the state's official religious turning point. The newly Islamized Gowa-Tallo confederation subsequently launched campaigns to Islamize neighboring kingdoms across South Sulawesi, including the Buginese states of Bone, Soppeng, and Wajo — a project that was as much political as religious, since Islamization extended Makassar's cultural and political influence over the entire southwestern peninsula.13

The mid-seventeenth-century port city of Makassar was one of the most sophisticated urban environments in the region. Its harbour functioned as a free port, open to all comers — a deliberate policy that reflected the city's position as a transit hub dependent on maximizing the volume of traffic rather than extracting monopoly rents. The articulation of this free-trade philosophy as a positive political principle directly challenged the VOC's monopolistic commercial model. The Makassar court's stance — that the sea belonged to all and that no power could legitimately forbid free navigation — was a philosophical as well as a commercial position, and it resonated deeply with the network of Asian merchants whose livelihoods the Dutch monopoly threatened.14

The VOC's determination to destroy Makassar as a free port was driven by the need to enforce the spice monopoly it had constructed since the Banda massacre of 1621. As long as Makassar remained open, cloves and nutmeg from Maluku leaked out through Makassarese merchants who purchased them from non-VOC producers and sold them to competing buyers — undermining the entire Dutch commercial strategy. VOC authorities tried repeatedly to negotiate exclusive agreements with Gowa's rulers but met consistent refusal. The decision to use overwhelming force was taken after Sultan Hasanuddin (r. 1653–1669, Figure 13.3) proved equally unwilling to concede.18

The Makassar War (November 1666 – June 1669) was one of the most consequential military conflicts in pre-colonial Indonesian history. The VOC commander, Admiral Cornelis Speelman, assembled a combined force of approximately 1,860 personnel — Dutch sailors, soldiers, and native troops from Ambon, plus Bugis fighters — supplemented by war canoes from the Sultanate of Ternate.14 Speelman's most effective ally, however, was not European but local: Arung Palakka (1634/35–1696), a Bugis prince from the Sultanate of Bone, who had led a failed revolt against Makassarese hegemony in 1660, been defeated and exiled, and now returned at the head of Bugis forces burning for revenge against the kingdom that had long dominated South Sulawesi. The alliance between the VOC's naval firepower and Arung Palakka's intimate knowledge of the political vulnerabilities of Gowa's subordinate kingdoms proved decisive — and illustrated the characteristic Dutch strategy of exploiting pre-existing inter-state rivalries rather than fighting alone.15

The war's first major turning point was the Treaty of Bungaya (18 November 1667), which Sultan Hasanuddin signed under military pressure. The terms required Gowa to accept VOC trade monopolies, prohibit other European ships from entering the port, cede strategic fortifications, pay reparations, and recognize VOC supremacy. But the terms were so humiliating that Hasanuddin, feeling the honour of the state at stake, tore up the treaty and resumed fighting — demonstrating that political legitimacy and the will to resist could outweigh military disadvantage, at least for a time. A renewed VOC offensive, reinforced from Batavia, culminated in the assault on Gowa's greatest fortress, Somba Opu, on 12 June 1669. After engineers created a breach with explosive charges, Dutch and Bugis forces overwhelmed the garrison. Sultan Hasanuddin submitted; he died one year later, on 12 June 1670.14

The aftermath of the Makassar War reshaped eastern Indonesia. Speelman demolished the great fortress of Somba Opu and constructed Fort Rotterdam as the new VOC headquarters in Sulawesi (Figure 13.4). The Sultanate of Bone, under Arung Palakka, replaced Gowa as the dominant power in South Sulawesi — but as a VOC client rather than an independent actor; Arung Palakka dominated regional politics until his death in 1696. The defeated Makassarese did not simply acquiesce: thousands scattered across the archipelago, settling in Java, Banten, Bali, Borneo, the Philippines, and Siam, carrying their martial culture and Islamic identity with them. Some joined anti-Dutch resistance movements — a group under the exile Karaeng Galesong participated in Trunajaya's Javanese revolt — while others established diaspora communities that bore witness to the vitality of the culture that Speelman had attempted to destroy.14

karaeng — the Makassarese title for a nobleman or chief, roughly analogous to the Javanese adipati (duke). The rulers of Gowa held the supreme title Sombaya ri Gowa (Lord of Gowa), but most male nobles of rank were addressed as karaeng. After the Makassar War, the title became a marker of identity for Makassarese diaspora communities across the archipelago and beyond.

Ternate, Tidore, and the Spice Sultanates of Maluku

If any single commodity drove the Age of Commerce in the eastern Indonesian archipelago, it was cloves. These aromatic dried flower buds, harvested before opening and valued across Asia and Europe as a spice, medicine, and breath-freshener, grew in their wild state only on five small volcanic islands in northern Maluku. The two sultanates that controlled the main clove-producing areas — Ternate and Tidore — became among the wealthiest polities in Southeast Asia as a result. Cloves were, in the medieval and early modern period, worth enormously more in European markets than their weight in silver, and the desire of European states to access them directly — eliminating the long chain of Muslim and Indian Ocean intermediaries who taxed each exchange — was one of the primary drivers of European oceanic exploration in the fifteenth century.17

The Sultanate of Ternate had existed as a political entity since approximately 1257 CE, when regional leaders on the island unified under the ruler known as Cico (Kaicil Mashur Malamo). It formally became an Islamic sultanate during the reign of Sultan Zainal Abidin (r. 1468–1500), who studied in Java and returned to introduce Islamic governance including a council of religious advisors (the Jolabe) and Islamic legal institutions. Ternate and its neighbour and rival Tidore were in constant competition — for clove supply, for influence over the smaller islands of northern Maluku and surrounding areas, and for the loyalty of the populations of Halmahera and the coast of New Guinea. This rivalry made both sultanates targets for manipulation by successive European powers, each of which aligned itself with one side against the other.16

The Portuguese were the first Europeans to reach Ternate. Francisco Serrão arrived in 1512, a direct consequence of Albuquerque's conquest of Malacca the previous year, which gave Portugal access to Malay sailors who knew the route to the Spice Islands. Serrão was welcomed by Sultan Abu Lais after a shipwreck — the sultan saw in the Portuguese adventurer and his firearms a useful military asset — and became a personal advisor to the Ternate court. The Portuguese were subsequently permitted to build a fort on Ternate (1522), but relations deteriorated sharply as they attempted to impose an exclusive trade monopoly and sponsor Jesuit missionary activity among the population. The tensions reached their crisis under Sultan Hairun (r. c. 1535–1570), who initially collaborated with the Portuguese before attempting to organize resistance. In 1570 the Portuguese governor António de Mesquita invited Hairun to a meeting and had him stabbed to death — a treacherous murder that united Ternate and the wider Muslim population of Maluku against Portuguese rule.17

Sultan Hairun's son, Sultan Baabullah (r. 1570–1583), took personal command of the resistance, drawing on Muslim solidarity and Ternate's authority over surrounding islands. He besieged the Portuguese fort on Ternate for four years until the garrison, reduced to starvation, capitulated and evacuated the island in 1575. Baabullah then pursued the Portuguese to their other Maluku strongholds, consolidating Ternate's authority over an enormous maritime zone stretching from eastern Sulawesi through Ambon, Ceram, and the Banda Islands to western New Guinea (Figure 13.5). Under Baabullah, Ternate reached the greatest territorial extent it would ever achieve — a remarkable demonstration of what indigenous Muslim power could accomplish against a European colonial presence when the political will and popular support were present.16

The late sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries brought new European rivalries to Maluku. Spanish forces from the Philippines captured the Portuguese fort on Ternate in 1606 and deported the sultan to Manila; they allied with Tidore against Ternate and the Dutch, who had been building their own Maluku networks since their first successful spice voyage in 1598–99. The VOC's ultimate objective was not partnership with Ternate but total monopoly control over the clove supply. By the 1650s, successive sultans of Ternate — Hamzah (r. 1627–1648) and Mandar Syah (r. 1648–1675) — had been compelled to cede territories and sign agreements granting the VOC effective control over clove production and export. The most devastating instrument of Dutch monopoly enforcement was the extirpatie (eradication) programme, under which VOC agents systematically destroyed clove trees on islands outside a few designated production zones, killing the livelihood of village communities who had cultivated the trees for centuries and who had little recourse against VOC military power.17 The Treaty of Zaragoza (1529) had theoretically awarded all rights to the Maluku spice trade to Portugal in exchange for a Spanish payment, but by the mid-seventeenth century it was the Dutch, not any Iberian power, who held the archipelago in their grip.16

The Ternate-Tidore rivalry itself illustrates a broader pattern observable across Maluku and indeed across the Indonesian archipelago during the Age of Commerce: the presence of powerful external actors with large navies and willingness to use systematic violence dramatically altered the bargaining positions of local rulers who had previously managed inter-state conflicts within a framework of regional competition without existential risk of conquest. The two sultans, like the rulers of Banten and Makassar, could use European rivals against one another for a time, but as the European actors consolidated their positions, the room for local manoeuvre narrowed inexorably.

Fragmentation, European Pressure, and the End of the Age of Commerce

Reading the histories of these six sultanates together, patterns of similarity and difference become visible that illuminate a major historical question: why did the Indonesian sultanates ultimately succumb to Dutch power in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, rather than successfully adapting to European commercial and military competition? The question resists simple answers. Several of the sultanates — Aceh, Makassar, Banten, Ternate — fought the VOC fiercely and sometimes successfully for decades. The answer must take account of structural factors rather than attributing colonial success merely to European technological superiority.

Anthony Reid's "crisis of the seventeenth century" provides one structural explanation. The global climate disruption of the Little Ice Age (particularly severe between roughly 1620 and 1650), combined with demographic pressure, agricultural shortfalls, and the violence of successive wars, produced economic contraction across much of Southeast Asia in the mid-seventeenth century. This contraction reduced the trade revenues of the sultanates at precisely the moment when they needed to finance sustained military resistance against the VOC, which could draw on the capital resources of the Amsterdam bourse — then the most sophisticated financial market in the world. The result was an asymmetry of staying power: the sultanates could win battles but could rarely sustain prolonged campaigns against an organization backed by Dutch merchant capital and global supply chains.2

Internal politics compounded the structural problem. Every sultanate covered in this chapter experienced destabilizing succession crises at critical moments: Demak fragmented after Trenggana's death (1546); Mataram was repeatedly destabilized by rebellions and was partitioned in 1755; Banten collapsed into civil war in 1682; Aceh suffered a prolonged leadership crisis after Iskandar Muda died without a clear male heir in 1636; Ternate was weakened by the Spanish kidnapping of its sultan in 1606 and internal factional conflict thereafter. The VOC exploited these crises systematically, offering military assistance to one faction in exchange for commercial concessions — the strategy it used with Mataram's Amangkurat II, with Banten's Sultan Haji, and with Bone against Gowa. Each intervention deepened the sultanate's dependency on Dutch support and narrowed its future room for manoeuvre.4

Yet the legacy of these sultanates was not simply one of defeat. The forms of Islam they shaped — Aceh's engagement with Sufi scholarship, Mataram's Kejawen synthesis, Ternate's Islamic maritime culture, Banten's cosmopolitan religious toleration, and Makassar's assertion of free navigation as a principle of Islamic governance — persisted long after VOC flags flew over their harbours. The legal codes, administrative traditions, and court cultures of the Age of Commerce continued to structure the lives of ordinary people and provided resources of identity, memory, and resistance that would resurface in later centuries. Demak's mosque still stands and still receives pilgrims; the kratons of Yogyakarta and Surakarta still perform the cultural traditions of Mataram; the tomb of Sultan Hasanuddin in Sungguminasa still draws visitors who recognize in his resistance an early expression of what later generations would call Indonesian nationalism. The Age of Commerce left a deposit in Indonesian civilization that no colonial administration could fully erase.

Summary

Between the fifteenth and seventeenth centuries, the Indonesian archipelago was home to a remarkable array of Muslim sultanates that participated fully in Anthony Reid's "Age of Commerce" — an era defined by expanding maritime trade, Islamic conversion, urban growth, and political centralization. Demak, Java's first significant Muslim sultanate, arose from the wreckage of Majapahit and achieved its greatest territorial extent under Sultan Trenggana (r. 1521–1546) before fragmenting into successor states after Trenggana's death; the Wali Songo played an essential role in shaping its synthesis of Islamic and Javanese traditions. The Islamic Mataram Sultanate, founded inland by Panembahan Senapati around 1584, reached its apogee under Sultan Agung (r. 1613–1645), who conquered virtually all of Java except the VOC fortress at Batavia — a target the great sultan attacked twice, in 1628 and 1629, and twice failed to take. Mataram's syncretic Kejawen culture and the Treaty of Giyanti (1755) that divided it into Yogyakarta and Surakarta shaped Javanese civilizational identity for centuries. Banten, established in 1552 on the northwest tip of Java, prospered as a pepper-exporting cosmopolitan port whose golden age under Sultan Ageng Tirtayasa (r. 1651–1683) ended in civil war and Dutch monopoly imposition. Aceh, at the northern end of Sumatra, achieved its greatest power under Sultan Iskandar Muda (r. 1607–1636), combining dominance of the pepper and gold trades with patronage of Islamic scholarship, before internal divisions and the catastrophic 1629 naval defeat at Malacca began its long decline. In Sulawesi, the Gowa-Tallo confederation built Makassar into the pre-eminent free port of eastern Indonesia, only to see it destroyed in the Makassar War (1666–1669) by a VOC–Bugis alliance; the Makassarese diaspora that followed attested to the vitality of the culture that could not be suppressed. The spice sultanates of Ternate and Tidore in Maluku, whose clove gardens had attracted Portuguese, Spanish, and ultimately Dutch power, were progressively subjected to the VOC's extirpation programme by the mid-seventeenth century. Across all of these cases, internal succession crises and the structural imbalance between sultanate revenues and VOC financial staying power were as important as any military factor in explaining the decline of Indonesia's Age of Commerce.
