Learning Objectives

  • Explain why Srivijaya was lost to memory for centuries and how George Coedès's 1918 article reconstructed it from inscriptions and Chinese sources.
  • Analyze the three foundational Old Malay inscriptions — Kedukan Bukit, Talang Tuo, and Telaga Batu — and what each reveals about Srivijayan society, religion, and governance.
  • Describe the thalassocratic model of Srivijaya's power: how control of the Malacca and Sunda straits structured the China–India trade and the empire's revenues.
  • Evaluate Srivijaya's role as a major center of Mahayana and Vajrayana Buddhism, from Yijing's seventh-century account to the visit of Atisha in the eleventh century.
  • Assess the scholarly debates over Srivijaya's nature and capital location, and trace the empire's decline from the Chola raids of 1025 CE to the founding of Malacca.

For more than seven hundred years, a maritime empire commanded the most valuable crossroads on earth — the narrow straits linking the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea — yet it vanished so completely from living memory that even the people of its own home region could not name it. When the French scholar George Coedès published a breakthrough article in 1918, he assembled from scattered stone inscriptions and Tang dynasty travel diaries the contours of a polity that had once dispatched fleets across two oceans, hosted a thousand Buddhist monks, and sent more tributary missions to China than almost any other Southeast Asian power. That polity was Srivijaya, centered on the swampy estuaries of southern Sumatra, and its rediscovery transformed the study of early Southeast Asian history.

The Lost Empire: Rediscovery and the Problem of Evidence

Few episodes in historical scholarship are as dramatic as the near-simultaneous recovery of an entire empire from obscurity. Until 1918, Srivijaya was essentially unknown — not merely to European historians but to the Sumatran and Malay peoples who had lived on the sites of its former ports for centuries. The French Indologist and diplomat George Coedès (1886–1969), working as director of the National Library of Thailand, published in that year an article entitled "Le royaume de Çrīvijaya" in the Bulletin de l'École française d'Extrême-Orient, the flagship journal of French colonial scholarship in Asia.1 The article's argument was elegant and far-reaching: several clusters of puzzling evidence — stone inscriptions in an archaic form of Malay, Chinese records referring to a maritime power called Sanfoqi (三佛齊), and reports from Buddhist pilgrims — all referred to the same polity: a Sumatran empire that had dominated Southeast Asian seas from the seventh to the thirteenth century.2

The identification required Coedès to disentangle two layers of confusion. Earlier scholars who encountered the Chinese term Sanfoqi in Tang and Song dynasty records had misread it as "Sribhoja" or "Sribogha," failing to recognize it as a phonetic rendering of "Srivijaya." Coedès cross-referenced these Chinese references with the epigraphic corpus of Old Malay inscriptions and demonstrated they were documenting the same power.3 His methodology — treating epigraphy, Chinese annals, and Buddhist pilgrimage literature as mutually illuminating sources — established the foundational approach to Srivijayan studies and, more broadly, inaugurated scholarly study of Old Malay as a historical language in its own right.4

The reason Srivijaya needed rediscovering at all is itself historically significant. Unlike the Khmer Empire, which left colossal stone monuments at Angkor, or the Central Javanese Mataram kingdom, which carved Borobudur into the volcanic landscape, Srivijaya built largely in wood, bamboo, and thatch on a flood-prone estuary. The thirteenth-century Chinese observer Zhao Rukuo, writing in his Zhu Fan Zhi (Treatise on the Various Barbarians), described Srivijayan subjects as living "scattered outside the city on the water, within rafts lined with reeds," suggesting that floating houses rather than stone palaces defined the capital's built environment.5 Wood rots; thatch burns; estuary soil is chemically hostile to preservation. The Musi River delta at Palembang yields Chinese ceramics and Indian statuary in abundance, but almost no monumental architecture, and this physical invisibility conspired with political discontinuity to erase Srivijaya from living memory. As Coedès himself observed, the Sumatrans living near Palembang in the early twentieth century had literally never heard of the empire that had once ruled from their own doorstep.6

Sanfoqi — the Chinese dynastic transcription (三佛齊) for Srivijaya, used in Tang and Song dynasty records. George Coedès's 1918 identification of Sanfoqi with the Old Malay inscriptional name "Srivijaya" was the key insight that assembled scattered evidence into a coherent historical empire.

Origins and the Rise from Palembang

The problem of evidence never goes away in Srivijayan studies, because almost everything we know about the empire's origins rests on a handful of inscriptions, a few Chinese accounts, and a large quantity of archaeological inference. The inscriptional evidence points firmly to the second half of the seventh century as the critical moment of formation, and to the estuary of the Musi River in what is now South Sumatra as the primary locus of power.

The context was propitious. The seventh century was a period of intense maritime activity across the Indo-Pacific world. The early Tang dynasty (618–907 CE) was asserting hegemony over the Chinese coastline and creating booming demand for tropical forest products — camphor, benzoin resin, aloes wood, tin — as well as luxury goods from India and the Middle East. Arab and Persian merchants were pushing east from the Persian Gulf. The Malabar and Coromandel coasts of South India teemed with long-distance traders. All of this commercial energy converged on a geographic bottleneck: the Strait of Malacca, the narrow passage between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra that connects the Indian Ocean to the South China Sea. Whoever could tax and facilitate the passage of ships through those straits commanded immense revenues without cultivating a single field or mining a single ounce of metal.7

It was in this context that a Sumatran ruler named Dapunta Hyang Sri Jayanasa organized what the inscriptions call a siddhayatra — a sacred journey undertaken to acquire spiritual power — that was simultaneously a military expedition of formidable scale. The Kedukan Bukit inscription, dated to 1 May 683 CE, records that he came from a place called Minanga Tamwan with 20,000 troops, 312 persons in boats, and 1,312 foot soldiers, concluding in the triumphalism of foundation: "Great Sriwijaya! Prosperity and riches."8 At roughly the same time, the kingdom that Chinese sources called Melayu — centered on the Batang Hari River near modern Jambi and valued for its gold — was absorbed into Srivijaya's orbit, probably in the 680s CE.9

The speed of this consolidation is striking. Within a decade of the Kedukan Bukit inscription, Srivijaya had assembled a network of ports stretching along the east coast of Sumatra, across the Sunda Strait into western Java, and up the Malay Peninsula. The Kota Kapur inscription, found on Bangka Island and dated to 686 CE, records the conquest of the Bangka region and territories as far as Lampung, while threatening supernatural curses upon those who fail in loyalty — suggesting that allegiance was as much commanded as freely given.10 Within a generation of the initial expansion, a Chinese Buddhist monk described the result: a wealthy, well-organized state with more than a thousand monks and scholars operating at the highest level of Buddhist learning known outside India.

The Voice of Stone: Old Malay Inscriptions

Srivijaya left behind no royal chronicles, no court poetry, and no administrative archives. Its literature, if it possessed a broad literary culture, was written on perishable materials that the Sumatran climate consumed long ago. What survives — seven key inscriptions in Old Malay, written in the Pallava script adapted from South India — constitutes the entire contemporary documentary record of the empire. These texts are not narratives; they are performative acts: proclamations, dedications, and above all oaths, whose authority depended on the supernatural power of the written word itself. Yet from these austere documents historians have reconstructed, however imperfectly, the political vocabulary, religious orientation, and administrative reach of Srivijaya.

Three inscriptions found near Palembang stand at the heart of the evidence. The Kedukan Bukit inscription, discovered on 29 November 1920 by the Dutch official C. J. Batenburg on the banks of the Tatang stream near Palembang, is dated 1 May 683 CE and is now recognized as the oldest surviving specimen of written Malay in any form.11 Its text is brief — roughly twenty lines in Pallava characters — but it establishes the essential vocabulary of Srivijayan power. The ruler undertakes a siddhayatra, a term scholars have debated: some have translated it as a "magic potion" ritual; others as a "prosperous journey"; but the consensus now favors the latter meaning, designating a ritual expedition whose success would demonstrate and reinforce the ruler's sacred authority.12 The armies mentioned — 20,000 troops, the boats, the foot soldiers — suggest not a defensive garrison but a state already capable of projecting force across considerable maritime distances.

The Talang Tuo inscription, also discovered in November 1920 — this time by Louis Constant Westenenk at the foot of Seguntang Hill west of Palembang — is dated 23 March 684 CE, barely a year after Kedukan Bukit.13 Its tone is entirely different: where Kedukan Bukit rings with martial triumph, Talang Tuo breathes the spirit of Buddhist benevolence. King Sri Jayanasa announces the establishment of a park called Śrīksetra "for the welfare of all creatures," listing the plants to be cultivated — coconut, areca palm, bamboo, sago — and wishing that all beings might find food, shelter, and eventual enlightenment.14 The inscription expresses, in a formula characteristic of Buddhist kingship, the ruler's aspiration to accumulate merit by providing for his subjects. Taken together, the two texts of 683 and 684 present a ruler who combined military dominance with Buddhist piety in the manner of the great Aśokan ideal: conquest followed by dharma.

The Telaga Batu inscription, found at Sabokingking in eastern Palembang and also dated to approximately 683 CE, is the most architecturally remarkable of the three.15 The stone is carved with seven cobra heads at the top — the seven nāga — and features a spout at the bottom designed to channel liquid. This design was functional in a ritual sense: water poured over the inscription's text would flow over the sacred words of the oath and into a vessel, which those swearing fealty would then drink, ingesting the curse into their own bodies. The text catalogues approximately twenty ranks of officials — from crown princes and generals down to merchants, craftsmen, and slaves — who must swear loyalty to the Kadatuan, the royal citadel of Srivijaya.16 The catalog of titles reveals a highly differentiated social order: court functionaries, naval commanders, scribes, artisans. Anyone committing treason against the Kadatuan would be killed by supernatural powers.

What these three stones reveal collectively is a polity that was simultaneously expansive and insecure: expanding rapidly through military force, anchoring loyalty through ritual oaths rather than through the bureaucratic administration typical of agrarian states, and framing both conquest and welfare through a Buddhist idiom. The reliance on oath-taking rather than written law codes or administrative infrastructure suggests, as historians have argued, that the core challenge of Srivijayan statecraft was the constant problem of maintaining the allegiance of semi-autonomous port chiefs spread across hundreds of kilometres of coastline.17

Kadatuan — the royal citadel or palace-complex of Srivijaya, which the Telaga Batu oath inscription specifically invoked as the object of loyalty. The term appears repeatedly in Srivijayan inscriptions as the locus of royal authority and the symbolic center of the polity.
Old Malay — the Austronesian language of the Srivijayan inscriptions, written in Pallava script adapted from South India; the direct ancestor of modern Malay and of Indonesian (Bahasa Indonesia). The Kedukan Bukit inscription is the oldest surviving specimen of the Malay language in written form.

Lord of the Straits: Commerce and the Thalassocratic Model

The single most important geographic fact about Srivijaya is its location at the confluence of two critical maritime passages: the Strait of Malacca, the roughly 550-kilometre-long channel between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra, and the Sunda Strait between Sumatra and western Java. Between them, these two corridors carry almost all ship traffic between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea. Any state that could monitor, tax, and protect passage through these waters could extract revenue from the entire eastern segment of the maritime Silk Road without producing anything itself beyond naval force and administrative capacity. Srivijaya built its wealth and power on exactly this logic.

The empire's economic model rested on what historians call the entrepôt function — a port of exchange where goods from different production zones were gathered, repackaged, and redistributed. The Musi River basin gave Srivijaya access to Sumatra's interior, rich in camphor, benzoin resin, and other forest products highly prized in Chinese pharmacology and ritual. The Malay Peninsula produced tin. Spices from the eastern islands moved westward through Srivijayan ports. In exchange, textiles from India and ceramics from China flowed in the opposite direction.18 Arab geographers of the ninth and tenth centuries described the Maharaja of Srivijaya as one of the wealthiest monarchs in Asia, his islands producing "camphor, aloes, cloves, sandal-wood, nutmegs, cardamom and cubeb, ivory, gold and tin" in quantities that made him "as rich as any king in the Indies."19

The mechanism of control was partly naval and partly fiscal. Srivijaya maintained war fleets capable of interdicting the straits and enforcing compliance from smaller ports. Ships that bypassed the entrepôt without paying duties risked seizure; those that called at Palembang received protection, access to warehousing, credit networks maintained by Tamil and Chinese merchant communities, and the political legitimacy that came from trading under a recognized sovereign. Zhao Rukuo's Zhu Fan Zhi records that Srivijaya administered fifteen subordinate regions, from Peninsular Malaya to western Java — a sprawling network of tributary dependencies held together less by garrison forces than by economic interest and royal prestige.20

The China relationship was paramount. Srivijaya was one of the most assiduous senders of tributary missions to the Tang and Song courts in all of Southeast Asia, dispatching envoys to China on more than thirty documented occasions. The New World Encyclopedia records specific missions in 853, 871, and 902 CE, and then again repeatedly between 1079 and 1088 CE during the Song dynasty, when Srivijaya also maintained a temple in Guangzhou endowed by royal funds.21 The tributary system worked to Srivijaya's advantage in ways that went beyond the immediate exchange of goods: by framing its trade as political submission, the kingdom secured access to Chinese markets, received imperial patents that legitimized its regional authority, and obtained the prestige goods — Chinese silks, porcelain, copper cash — that functioned as markers of status within Southeast Asian exchange networks.22 The Chinese framing was a fiction both parties understood: what looked like tribute was in fact regulated commerce, and what looked like imperial benevolence was in fact China's need for tropical commodities.

Kenneth R. Hall, in his study of maritime trade and state development in early Southeast Asia, argued that Srivijaya was not merely a passive toll-booth on the straits but an active architect of regional political economy, developing "elaborate systems of statecraft" that integrated coastal depots with their hinterlands and managed the complex separation of international traders from local producers of commodities.23 The empire was, in this reading, as much a commercial and diplomatic achievement as a military one — a polity whose primary product was the infrastructure of trust that made long-distance maritime commerce possible.

The Dharma Kingdom: Srivijaya as Buddhist Center

The best-documented aspect of Srivijaya's cultural life is also among the most surprising: a maritime port-city in Sumatran mangrove estuaries, remote from the great monastic universities of India, became one of the most important centers of Buddhist learning in the world between the seventh and eleventh centuries. The evidence for this comes from multiple and mutually reinforcing directions — the testimony of the Chinese monk Yijing, the extended residence of the Tibetan Buddhist patriarch Atisha, the Nalanda copper plate grant, and the archaeological record of the Muaro Jambi temple complex — and together they present a consistent picture of Srivijaya as what one might fairly call a dharma capital: a place where maritime wealth subsidized Buddhist scholarship of the highest order.

The primary witness is Yijing (635–713 CE), the Chinese monk who traveled to India to collect Sanskrit texts and study at the great monastery of Nalanda in Bihar. On his initial voyage home, Yijing stopped at Srivijaya in 671 CE for six months; he was so impressed by the quality of Buddhist scholarship he found there that he returned to Srivijaya in 689 to retrieve supplies and a scribe, and finally completed his translation work in Srivijaya in 695 before sailing back to China.24 His two major works — the Nanhai Jigui Neifa Zhuan (Record of Buddhist Practices Sent Home from the Southern Sea) and his record of eminent monks who sought the Dharma in the western regions — were written or completed in Srivijaya, making them, in a sense, products of Srivijayan intellectual culture.

Yijing's account of the Buddhist community at Srivijaya is precise and enthusiastic. He noted that the fortified city of Bhoga (his name for the Srivijayan capital, identifiable with Palembang) harbored "more than 1,000 Buddhist priests whose minds are bent on learning and good practice," and that they "investigate and study all the subjects that exist just as in India; the rules and ceremonies are not at all different."25 He recommended explicitly that Chinese monks planning to travel to Nalanda spend one or two years in Srivijaya first to master Sanskrit grammar and Buddhist logic before proceeding to India. This was not a recommendation born of courtesy; it reflects a considered judgment that Srivijaya's monasteries were genuine rivals to Nalanda in academic quality.

Three centuries later, around 1011–1023 CE, the Indian monk Atisha (982–1054 CE) — who would later become a pivotal figure in the reformation of Tibetan Buddhism — made the arduous thirteen-month sea voyage from Bengal to Suvarnadvipa, the "Golden Island" (Sumatra), to study under the renowned teacher Suvarnadvipi Dharmakirti (known in Tibetan sources as Serlingpa, meaning "the man from the Golden Island").26 Atisha spent approximately twelve years in Sumatra, studying the Abhisamayalamkara and, above all, receiving Serlingpa's distinctive teaching on bodhichitta — the aspiration to enlightenment for the benefit of all beings — which Atisha would later transmit to Tibet as the foundation of the lam-rim (Stages of the Path) genre of Buddhist literature.27 The Muaro Jambi archaeological site — covering more than 2,000 hectares and containing 84 red-brick temple complexes connected by canals — has been identified by scholars as the likely location of Serlingpa's monastery; its layout closely resembles Nalanda's architectural design, and excavations have uncovered Chinese ceramics dating from the ninth to twelfth centuries alongside Buddhist statuary.28

The third strand of evidence comes from India itself. The Nalanda copper plate grant, discovered in 1921 in Monastery 1 at Nalanda and dated to approximately 860 CE, records that King Devapaladeva of the Pala Empire granted five villages in the Rajagriha district for the upkeep of a Buddhist monastery at Nalanda that had been requested and built by Balaputra, the Srivijayan king of the Sailendra line.29 The grant provided for offerings, alms, medicines, garments, and the copying of manuscripts — the full apparatus of a functioning monastic library in the ancient Indian tradition. That a Southeast Asian maritime king was building and endowing monasteries at Nalanda, the greatest Buddhist university in the world, demonstrates the degree to which Srivijayan rulers had embedded themselves in the pan-Asian Buddhist network as prestige players, not provincial imitators.

Srivijayan Buddhism was Mahayana in its broader orientation but had strong Vajrayana (tantric) elements by the tenth century, reflected in the Muaro Jambi site's connection to Dharmakirti's Vajrayana lineage and in the royal iconography of the Sailendra-Srivijayan court, which depicted the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara prominently.30 The coexistence of Hindu and Buddhist elements in Srivijayan religious practice is confirmed by the recovery of both Śaiva and bodhisattva statuary from the Musi River basin at Palembang, suggesting that the rulers patronized both traditions — a common pattern in classical Southeast Asian courts, where eclectic royal patronage served political as much as spiritual purposes.

siddhayatra — in Srivijayan Old Malay inscriptions, a sacred journey undertaken by a ruler to acquire spiritual power (siddhi) that would legitimate and empower political authority. The Kedukan Bukit inscription describes Dapunta Hyang's siddhayatra as simultaneously a military expedition and a ritual act of royal self-empowerment.

The Sailendra Connection: Java and Sumatra Intertwined

Among the most intriguing aspects of Srivijayan history is its relationship with the Sailendra dynasty, the Central Javanese rulers who erected the colossal Buddhist stupa of Borobudur. The two courts were initially separate powers — Srivijaya based in Sumatra, the Sailendras in Central Java's Kedu Plain — yet they were bound by marriage alliances, shared Buddhist patronage, and eventually by dynastic merger, so that from the late ninth century the rulers of Srivijaya were themselves Sailendras. Understanding this relationship matters because it connects Srivijaya to one of the world's great artistic achievements and helps explain how a Sumatran maritime kingdom came to exercise influence simultaneously over the Buddhist intellectual traditions of India, the commercial arteries of Southeast Asia, and the court culture of Java.

The Sailendras rose to prominence in Central Java around the late eighth century as active Mahayana Buddhist patrons, covering the Kedu Plain with Buddhist monuments. Their most famous achievement was the colossal stupa of Borobudur, whose construction was overseen by the Sailendra ruler Samaratungga and completed around 825 CE.31 The two dynasties were linked through marriage: Samaragrawira, a Sailendra king, married Dewi Tara, the daughter of a Srivijayan ruler named Dharmasetu, creating a dynastic bond that complicated the political relationship between the two powers and gave each a stake in the other's stability.32

The connection became dramatically closer around 852 CE, when the Sailendra ruler Balaputra was defeated in Central Java by his Sanjaya rival Pikatan and forced to flee across the Java Sea to Sumatra, where he became the paramount ruler of Srivijaya.33 The Nalanda copper plate inscription of 860 CE, already discussed in connection with Buddhist scholarship, identifies Balaputra as "king of Suvarna-dvipa" (the golden island, i.e., Sumatra) and describes him explicitly as "a descendant from the Śailendra dynasty."34 From this point, the rulers of Srivijaya were simultaneously Sailendras: a Javanese Buddhist dynasty governing a Sumatran maritime empire. This merger explains a historiographical puzzle — why do the Buddhist traditions associated with Borobudur appear to echo those documented in Srivijayan-era Sumatran and Tamil South Indian archaeology? — and suggests that what appears in the sources as two separate powers was, from the late ninth century, substantially one interlocking court.

The Sailendra presence in Srivijaya also intensified the empire's connection to both Nalanda and, eventually, Tibet. The copper plate grant of 860 CE is itself a Sailendra-Srivijayan document; the visit of Atisha to study Dharmakirti's Vajrayana teachings in the early eleventh century falls within the period of Sailendra-Srivijayan rule. The Sailendras brought to Srivijaya's maritime pragmatism a tradition of monumental Buddhist patronage and Sanskrit learning that elevated the Sumatran court to prominence in the pan-Asian world of Buddhist scholarship — a prominence it would maintain until the Chola raid of 1025 CE disrupted its authority.

What Kind of State? The Mandala Debate

Srivijaya has been described by different scholars at different times as an empire, a confederation, a thalassocracy, a mandala, a "port polity," and a "galactic polity." The disagreement is not merely terminological: it reflects genuine uncertainty about whether any of the political concepts developed to describe European or Chinese states can be applied to a Southeast Asian maritime power that left no administrative archives, enforced no territorial frontier, and governed through a combination of ritual oaths, economic incentives, and the personal authority of charismatic rulers.

O. W. Wolters, whose 1967 study Early Indonesian Commerce remains foundational to the field, proposed that Srivijaya's political structure was what he later called a "mandala" — borrowing the Sanskrit cosmological term to describe a polity defined not by lines on a map but by radiating degrees of loyalty from a charismatic center.35 In Wolters's model, authority in Southeast Asian polities derived from "men of prowess" — rulers whose personal charisma, ritual power, and demonstrated ability to produce prosperity attracted followers, who in turn formed networks of allegiance that could dissolve and re-form around new centers of power. Srivijaya's Maharaja held his network together through trade revenue (which made loyalty commercially profitable), Buddhist prestige (which made loyalty spiritually meritorious), and the ever-present threat of the curse-bearing inscriptions (which made disloyalty supernaturally dangerous). This was a remarkably effective system, but also an inherently fragile one: the whole structure depended on the center's continued ability to project wealth and prestige outward to the periphery.

Kenneth R. Hall's reading emphasizes the systemic commercial logic of the empire. In his analysis, Srivijaya was "essentially a Sumatran state" that developed "elaborate systems of statecraft" integrating coastal depots with their hinterlands; the port chiefs who submitted were incorporated not through bureaucratic conquest but through commercial partnership, receiving access to international trade in exchange for loyalty and commodities.36 This model explains why Srivijaya never built a large standing army or dense administrative hierarchy: economic dependency was a more reliable tool of control than military garrison when the network spanned hundreds of kilometres of coastline inhabited by seafaring peoples who could, if maltreated, simply sail away.

The debate about Srivijaya's capital location serves as a proxy for these deeper uncertainties. Coedès himself placed the capital at Palembang, and Pierre-Yves Manguin's archaeological surveys of the early 1990s and again in 2010–2011 confirmed this conclusion: his excavations along the Musi River between Bukit Seguntang and Sabokingking uncovered Chinese ceramics in quantities "unmatched at any other site in southeast Sumatra," alongside Buddhist statuary, beads, and inscription fragments, while aerial photography from 1984 had already revealed traces of ancient man-made canals, moats, and artificial islands consistent with a substantial urban center.37 A competing hypothesis, advanced by Indonesian archaeologists in 2013, suggests the initial core was at Muaro Jambi on the Batang Hari River, which has richer extant brick monuments. The weight of epigraphic evidence — the major inscriptions cluster around Palembang — and Manguin's archaeological surveys support Palembang as the capital of classical Srivijaya.38 An older suggestion that the capital was at Chaiya in southern Thailand, based on the concentration of Buddhist remains in the Thai-Malay Peninsula, is now generally rejected.39

What makes the debate difficult to resolve finally is that the very concept of a fixed "capital" may be anachronistic for Srivijaya. The Telaga Batu inscription's catalog of officials from many regions, and the sparse monumental archaeology at Palembang itself, both suggest a polity whose political center was a mobile royal court rather than a bureaucratic metropolis — a palace-barge civilization as much as a city-state. In that respect, Srivijaya resembles less a territorial empire than an extraordinarily successful protection racket whose product was safe passage through the world's most valuable waterways, and whose political architecture was calibrated to deliver that product with minimal fixed infrastructure.

Scholarly Debate: Thalassocracy vs. Mandala. The two dominant frameworks for understanding Srivijaya lead to different historical conclusions. The thalassocracy model (emphasizing naval dominance and straits control) treats Srivijaya as a coherent empire with a clear center and clear periphery. The mandala model (Wolters) treats it as a fluid network of overlapping allegiances with no fixed boundaries, where the "empire" was less a territory than a personal following. Both models capture something real: the thalassocratic model explains the mechanism of revenue extraction; the mandala model explains why the empire was so vulnerable to commercial disruption and why the Chola raids had such devastating political effects.

The Chola Catastrophe of 1025

The defining blow in Srivijaya's long decline was delivered not from within the region but from across the Indian Ocean. In 1025 CE, the Chola king Rajendra I launched a large naval expedition from South India that attacked Srivijayan ports along the Malay Peninsula and in Sumatra itself — an operation historians have described as without precedent in the generally peaceful history of relations between the Indian subcontinent and Southeast Asia.40

The Chola Empire, based in the Tamil Nadu region of South India, was at the apex of its power in the early eleventh century. Rajendra I had already completed a famous "Gangetic campaign" deep into northern India and was now extending Chola military reach across the Bay of Bengal. Tamil inscriptions at the Brahadesvara temple at Thanjavur record in triumphant verse the names of the ports attacked: Kadaram (Kedah on the Malay Peninsula), the Srivijayan capital Palembang, and ports along Sumatra's west coast including Barus.41 The Chola fleet employed an unexpected approach: rather than entering via the Strait of Malacca, where Srivijayan naval defenses were strongest, it sailed across the open Bay of Bengal to Barus on Sumatra's vulnerable west coast, catching the Srivijayan navy unprepared.

The sack of Palembang was systematic. The Cholas plundered the Kadatuan — the royal palace complex — and the Buddhist monasteries, carrying off the Vidhyadara Torana, a jeweled ceremonial gate of great symbolic value. More politically devastating, they captured King Sangrama Vijayatunggavarman, the Srivijayan ruler, making him a prisoner of war.42 The political consequences cascaded outward: the network of port chiefs who had submitted to Srivijaya because it appeared invincible and commercially indispensable now had clear reason to doubt both qualities. Subsidiary ports began initiating direct contact with Chinese and Indian merchants, bypassing the entrepôt whose protection they had been paying for. The commercial monopoly that had underpinned Srivijayan revenues for three and a half centuries was fractured.

Why did the Cholas attack? Scholars have proposed several overlapping motivations. The most direct was commercial rivalry: some historians argue that Srivijaya was obstructing Tamil merchant access to Chinese markets, and that Chola traders — organized through the famous ainnūṟṟuvar (Five Hundred) guild networks — pressured Rajendra into military action to break the Srivijayan monopoly on the straits trade.43 A second motivation was the prestige logic of royal conquest: Rajendra's explicit aspiration to digvijaya (world conquest) extended its ideological ambitions across the ocean. A third possibility involves Mainland Southeast Asian geopolitics: competing powers may have encouraged the Cholas to strike at Srivijaya to weaken a common rival. Whatever the combination of causes, the 1025 attack did not immediately destroy Srivijaya — Chinese sources continued to record missions from the Srivijayan region for another two centuries. But the raids broke the structural conditions that had made Srivijaya dominant: they demonstrated that the straits could be violated by an outside power, that the Srivijayan king could be captured and humiliated, and that the protection Srivijaya sold was not reliable. From this moment, the thalassocracy entered a slow structural decline from which it never recovered.

The Long Decline: From Srivijaya to Malacca

Empires rarely die in a single moment, and Srivijaya's dissolution was a drawn-out process spanning nearly three centuries, shaped by internal fragmentation, Javanese aggression, Thai expansion, and the gradual shift of the monsoon-trade economy toward Islamic commercial networks. The story of Srivijaya's long decline is also, paradoxically, the story of how "Srivijaya" as a political idea outlasted the actual political structure that had given it meaning.

After 1025, Chinese sources continued to use the term Sanfoqi for the dominant maritime power in the Strait of Malacca region, but scholars now generally agree that this designation had shifted: where it had once clearly referred to Palembang-based Srivijaya, it now increasingly designated the Melayu/Dharmasraya polity centered on the Batang Hari River and Jambi — the kingdom that Srivijaya had itself absorbed in the 680s and that now reasserted its independence as the Srivijayan core weakened.44 The Grahi inscription of 1183 CE, found in southern Thailand, refers to a ruler with the title "Maharaja Srimat Trailokyaraja," suggesting a continuing if diminished suzerainty over the Thai-Malay Peninsula.45 By 1225, Chinese records identify Sanfoqi-Jambi as one of the two most powerful kingdoms in Southeast Asia alongside Java — a remarkable testament to the staying power of the Srivijayan political tradition even as the old center at Palembang faded into secondary status.

The decisive blow from the east came from Singhasari, the rising East Javanese kingdom that immediately preceded Majapahit. Around 1275 CE, King Kertanegara of Singhasari dispatched a military expedition known in Javanese sources as the Pamalayu — the "conquest of Melayu" — to assert Javanese hegemony over the Sumatran successor states.46 Javanese forces under General Mahesa Anabrang occupied Palembang and Jambi by approximately 1288, installing Javanese-backed administration over the remnant Srivijayan network. Kertanegara himself was overthrown and killed by a Javanese rival in 1292, but his successors founded Majapahit in 1293 and eventually absorbed the Sumatran domains into their archipelagic empire.

Simultaneously, from the north, the Thai kingdom of Sukhothai expanded southward down the Malay Peninsula in the late thirteenth century, gradually detaching Srivijaya's northern peninsular dependencies. The combination of Javanese pressure from the south and east, Thai pressure from the north, and the continuing erosion of commercial confidence after the Chola raids left the Srivijayan network without the revenue, military deterrent, or political prestige needed to maintain its confederation of port chiefs.

The religious landscape was shifting in ways that proved fatal to the old commercial order. From the late thirteenth century, Muslim traders and missionaries established footholds at the northern tip of Sumatra — at Samudra Pasai, where the first Sumatran Islamic sultanate emerged — and along the Malay Peninsula. The Tamil Buddhist merchant networks that had underpinned Srivijayan commercial life were being supplemented and eventually displaced by Gujarati, Arab, and Persian Muslim merchants who operated within a different commercial and religious framework that required no allegiance to a Buddhist Maharaja.47

In 1347 CE, the Majapahit queen regent Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi appointed Adityawarman — a prince of mixed Srivijayan-Javanese descent — as governor of the Sumatran territories, ending even nominal independent authority at Palembang.48 The symbolic end came in a poignant epilogue: the last prince of the old Srivijayan line at Palembang, Parameswara, was driven out by a Majapahit-aligned rival around 1390, fled north to the tip of the Malay Peninsula, and there, around 1400 CE, founded the city that would become the next great maritime entrepôt of the straits — Malacca, soon to convert to Islam and rise as the dominant trading power of the fifteenth century.49 The ghost of Srivijaya lived on in its institutional successor: the same geographic logic, the same entrepôt function, the same political model of maritime commerce as the foundation of state power, now operating under a different dynasty and a different faith.

The modern legacy of Srivijaya is complex and contested. Indonesian nationalists of the early twentieth century celebrated Srivijaya alongside Majapahit as evidence that a great empire had existed in the archipelago before Dutch colonialism — that a united, sovereign Indonesian space had historical precedent. Sukarno's government named a university in Palembang Universitas Sriwijaya, and the empire figures prominently in school curricula as an emblem of pre-colonial greatness. Yet the scholarly reconstruction of Srivijaya is fundamentally a European achievement — Coedès's article, the French epigraphic tradition, the Dutch colonial-era archaeologists who physically discovered the inscriptions — and subsequent Indonesian scholarship has both built upon and critiqued that framework. The very completeness of Srivijaya's historical amnesia, as Coedès remarked with a scholar's wonder, speaks to how thoroughly political rupture can erase even a dominant civilization from the living memory of its own descendants.

Summary

Srivijaya was a maritime empire centered at Palembang on the Musi River estuary in southern Sumatra, which dominated the Strait of Malacca and the broader Southeast Asian maritime trade network from approximately the seventh to the thirteenth century CE. It was essentially unknown to modern scholarship until the French historian George Coedès published his landmark 1918 article identifying the empire from Old Malay inscriptions and Chinese records. Three foundational stone inscriptions — Kedukan Bukit (683 CE), Talang Tuo (684 CE), and Telaga Batu (c. 683 CE) — provide the primary contemporary evidence for its political vocabulary, religious orientation, and administrative structure. The empire operated as a thalassocracy, extracting revenue through control of the straits and the entrepôt trade between China, India, and Arabia; it sent more than thirty tributary missions to China and became simultaneously a major center of Mahayana-Vajrayana Buddhist learning, as attested by the Chinese monk Yijing in the seventh century, the Indian monk Atisha in the eleventh, and the Nalanda copper plate grant of 860 CE. Scholars debate whether it is best understood as an empire, a mandala, or a port polity; O. W. Wolters's model of the "mandala" — a radiating network of personal allegiances built on commercial and spiritual prestige rather than territorial conquest — has been most influential. The Chola raids of 1025 CE shattered the empire's monopoly on straits commerce; a gradual decline followed, shaped by Javanese expansion (the Pamalayu expedition of 1275), Thai encroachment, and the Islamization of maritime trade. The last Srivijayan prince founded Malacca around 1400 CE, transmitting the entrepôt model into the Islamic era.

Review & Discussion Questions

6.1 Why was Srivijaya "lost" to historical memory even among the Sumatran people who lived near its former capital? What does this amnesia tell us about the kind of state Srivijaya was — specifically, about the durability of states that build in perishable materials and govern through personal allegiance rather than permanent institutions?
6.2 Compare the three key inscriptions: Kedukan Bukit (683 CE), Talang Tuo (684 CE), and Telaga Batu (c. 683 CE). Each uses a different idiom — military, Buddhist, and juridical. What do these three registers together reveal about how Srivijayan rulers legitimated their authority?
6.3 The thalassocracy model describes Srivijaya as a power based on control of the sea rather than of territory. What are the advantages and vulnerabilities of this kind of state? How does the Chola raid of 1025 illustrate the vulnerabilities?
6.4 Yijing's seventh-century account describes Srivijaya as a center of Buddhist learning comparable to Nalanda. Two centuries later, Atisha sailed from India to spend twelve years studying there. What does this tell us about the relationship between maritime commercial wealth and the patronage of religion and scholarship in the classical world?
6.5 O. W. Wolters proposed interpreting Srivijaya through the concept of the "mandala" — a polity defined by radiating personal allegiances rather than fixed territorial boundaries. What evidence from the Srivijayan inscriptions supports this model? What evidence might challenge it?
6.6 The debate over Srivijaya's capital location (Palembang vs. Muaro Jambi vs. Chaiya) remains unresolved. What kinds of evidence have scholars used to argue each position, and why is the question difficult to settle definitively?
6.7 The Sailendra dynasty simultaneously built Borobudur in Central Java and eventually ruled Srivijaya in Sumatra. What does this dual role tell us about the nature of political and religious networks in the classical Southeast Asian world?
6.8 Srivijaya's last prince founded Malacca, which became the dominant fifteenth-century entrepôt and eventually converted to Islam. To what extent was Malacca a continuation of the Srivijayan model, and to what extent did it represent something genuinely new?
