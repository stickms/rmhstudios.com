Learning Objectives

  • Explain how Japan conquered the Netherlands East Indies in barely two months in 1942 and why Dutch rule collapsed so completely.
  • Distinguish the three occupation administrations — the Army in Java and in Sumatra and the Navy in the eastern archipelago — and the different policies each pursued.
  • Assess how the occupation mobilized the Indonesian population through forced labour (romusha), the PETA militia, and mass organizations, and weigh the human cost of that mobilization.
  • Evaluate the collaboration question: why Sukarno and Hatta chose to work openly with the Japanese while Sjahrir built an underground, and how historians have judged that choice.
  • Understand how the occupation's instrumental promotion of nationalism, the Indonesian language, and a promise of independence transformed the conditions for the revolution that followed.
  • Confront honestly the atrocities of the period — the famine of 1944–45, the death of forced labourers, and the sexual enslavement of women.

For three and a half years, from the spring of 1942 to the late summer of 1945, the islands that would become Indonesia were ruled not from The Hague but from Tokyo. The Japanese occupation was brief, but it was the hinge on which modern Indonesian history turns. It shattered the myth of Dutch permanence overnight; it drained the countryside of rice and conscripted millions of labourers, many of whom never returned; it armed and trained a generation of young Indonesians; and, as defeat closed in, it handed the nationalist movement the institutions, the language, and finally the promise of statehood. The occupation was at once catastrophe and catalyst — a period of famine, forced labour, and sexual slavery that was also, paradoxically, the incubator of national independence. No chapter in this book demands more careful moral bookkeeping.

The eighty-day conquest

The campaign that ended three centuries of Dutch presence in the archipelago lasted less than two months. Japan's strategic objective was unambiguous: oil. In 1940 the Netherlands East Indies was among the largest oil exporters in the world, and the American embargo of mid-1941 made the Indies' wells the indispensable prize of Japan's southern advance.1 When the offensive opened on 11 January 1942 with landings at Tarakan off Borneo, the defenders were a patchwork coalition. The hastily assembled American-British-Dutch-Australian (ABDA) command fielded on paper some 148,000 personnel — the great majority of them locally raised colonial troops — against a Japanese invasion force of roughly 108,000 well-coordinated men backed by overwhelming air and naval power.2

The decisive blow fell at sea. On 27 February 1942, a scratch ABDA squadron under the Dutch rear admiral Karel Doorman intercepted the Java invasion convoy in the Battle of the Java Sea, the largest surface engagement since Jutland. Over a confused seven-hour running fight the Allies were methodically destroyed: Doorman's flagship De Ruyter and the cruiser Java were sunk by Japanese torpedoes — one of which, fired by the heavy cruiser Haguro, struck the destroyer Kortenaer at a range of some 22,000 yards in what has been called the longest-range torpedo hit in naval history — and Doorman himself went down with his ship. The Allies lost roughly 2,300 sailors and the better part of their fleet; the Japanese, thirty-six men and not a single ship sunk.3 With the seas cleared, Japanese troops poured ashore on Java. On 8 March 1942, at Kalijati airfield, the governor-general, Jonkheer A. W. L. Tjarda van Starkenborgh Stachouwer, and the army commander, Lieutenant-General Hein ter Poorten, surrendered unconditionally — ter Poorten ordering all colonial forces to lay down their arms without even consulting his British and American allies.4

The speed and totality of the collapse mattered as much as the fact of it. For decades the Dutch had projected an aura of unshakeable authority; in eighty days that authority evaporated, and ordinary Indonesians watched their European masters herded into internment camps by an Asian army. Some 100,000 European and Eurasian civilians were eventually interned, along with around 80,000 Allied prisoners of war.5 The psychological revolution was immediate and, as the historian Anthony Reid observed, the occupation became "a watershed" precisely because the spell of white invincibility could never be recast.6 Many Indonesians initially greeted the Japanese as liberators, encouraged by Japanese propaganda invoking the slogan of "Asia for the Asians" and the prophecy of a coming age of justice. That welcome would curdle quickly, but the rupture with the colonial past was permanent.

Three empires in one

Japan did not govern the conquered Indies as a single territory. Reflecting the bitter inter-service rivalry of the Japanese military, the archipelago was carved into three separate administrations that never fully cohered. Java and Madura — the demographic and political heart of the colony, home to some two-thirds of its people — fell to the Sixteenth Army, headquartered in Jakarta (the Japanese promptly renamed Batavia, adopting the nationalists' own preferred name for the city). Sumatra, with its oil and plantations, went to the Twenty-Fifth Army, first under General Tomoyuki Yamashita and initially administered from Singapore before its headquarters moved to Bukittinggi in 1943. The vast eastern third of the archipelago — Kalimantan, Sulawesi, Maluku, and the Lesser Sundas — was placed under the Imperial Navy's Second Southern Fleet, which ran its naval civil administration (the Minseifu) from Makassar.7

Gunseikan — the chief of the Japanese military administration in an occupied territory; on Java the gunseikan headed a civil bureaucracy organized into seventeen shū (residencies), into which Indonesians were promoted to fill the posts vacated by interned Dutch officials.

These were not administrative niceties. The three zones followed divergent policies that would leave lasting marks. The Army in Java, facing the largest and most politically mobilized population, ultimately decided that nationalism was a force to be harnessed; it courted Indonesian leaders, tolerated nationalist symbols, and eventually sponsored steps toward independence. The Navy in the east, governing a more fragmented and strategically peripheral region and wary of any mass politics, was markedly more repressive and far slower to make political concessions — a divergence that helps explain why the revolution would erupt first and most forcefully on Java.8 Across all three zones, the wholesale internment of roughly 170,000 Dutch officials and the expulsion of the Dutch language created a sudden administrative vacuum, and that vacuum was filled, on the lower and middle rungs, by Indonesians who for the first time gained real experience of running a modern state.9

Mobilizing a population

Japan came to the Indies not as a developer but as a belligerent fighting a widening war, and from 1943 the occupation's overriding purpose was to extract labour, food, and manpower for that war. The instruments of mobilization were threefold: forced labour, a network of mass organizations, and an auxiliary army of Indonesians.

The most notorious was romusha — forced labour. The Japanese drafted Indonesians, overwhelmingly poor Javanese villagers, to build airfields, railways, fortifications, and roads. The scale is genuinely uncertain and remains debated: estimates of those mobilized within Java alone run from four to ten million, the looseness of the figure itself a measure of how little the occupation recorded.10 Of these, somewhere between 200,000 and 300,000 were shipped off Java to labour on projects across the Japanese empire — the Burma–Siam "Death Railway," the Pekanbaru railway in Sumatra, and installations scattered from Borneo to the Moluccas.11 Recruitment began with deception: as the Japanese official Shinozaki Mamoru later admitted, labourers were lured with promises of "good food, wages and accommodation in return for easy work." When survivors trickled home with the truth, volunteering ceased almost entirely, and the Japanese turned to outright coercion — press-ganging men from streets, markets, and cinemas, and imposing labour quotas on village headmen.12

The death rates were appalling. Of the more than 120,000 romusha sent to the Pekanbaru railway in Sumatra, perhaps 16,000 survived — a death rate above eighty per cent, against roughly fifteen per cent for the Allied prisoners of war working the same line.13 Of the hundreds of thousands sent off Java, by one account no more than 70,000 were still alive at the war's end, and only some 52,000 of around 270,000 sent to the outer islands were ever repatriated.14 Takuma Melber's comparative study of wartime labour recruitment across occupied Southeast Asia underscores that Java was the empire's principal labour reservoir, and that the romusha system, far less documented than the famous POW railways, may have consumed more lives than the Allied captives whose suffering is far better remembered.15

Alongside labour, the Japanese mobilized political energy through a succession of mass organizations. An early effort, the clumsy "Triple-A" movement (proclaiming Japan the "Leader, Protector, and Light of Asia"), fell flat. In March 1943 it was replaced by the Center of the People's Power, Putera (Pusat Tenaga Rakyat), led by four prominent nationalists. The Japanese intended Putera to channel popular sympathy toward their war effort; the nationalist leadership used its platform — its access to radio, press, and public assembly — to spread their own message of unity and eventual independence. The occupation authorities soon concluded that Putera had, in their own assessment, "exacerbated the desire for Indonesian independence" rather than served Japanese ends, and in January 1944 they dissolved it in favour of the more tightly controlled Java Service Association, the Jawa Hokokai, which reached down through neighbourhood associations into every village and bound the population to slogans of "self-sacrifice, unity, and obedience."16

The third instrument was military. In October 1943 the Sixteenth Army authorized the creation of PETA — Pembela Tanah Air, "Defenders of the Homeland" — a volunteer auxiliary force of Indonesians, officered by Indonesians, raised ostensibly to help repel an anticipated Allied invasion. By the war's end PETA numbered some sixty-nine battalions and roughly 37,000 men on Java, Madura, and Bali, with a parallel force of about 20,000 (the Giyugun) in Sumatra.17 For the nationalist cause this was a development "that could never have taken place under the Dutch": the Japanese had given young Indonesians military training, weapons, and the experience of command. Among PETA's junior officers were men — Sudirman and Suharto foremost among them — who would form the backbone of the republican army during the revolution and dominate the nation's politics for decades.18

PETA (Pembela Tanah Air) — "Defenders of the Homeland," the Indonesian-officered volunteer army raised by the Japanese on Java from October 1943. Though created to serve Japan's war, it trained the cadre of the future Indonesian National Armed Forces.

The collaboration question

The occupation forced on the nationalist movement a wrenching strategic choice: collaborate with a brutal occupier in hope of advancing the national cause, or resist and forfeit all influence over events. The movement's leaders answered in two directions at once — a division that was, according to a durable tradition, deliberate. Sukarno and Mohammad Hatta, released from Dutch internment by the Japanese, chose open cooperation, calculating that they could extract concessions, keep the nationalist idea before the public, and shield the population where they could. Sutan Sjahrir, by contrast, refused any public role and built a clandestine network, preserving a nationalist conscience untainted by collaboration and a line to the Allies. By a widely repeated understanding, the three men coordinated this split before the Japanese arrived, so that whichever way the war turned, the movement would have credible leaders positioned to claim its fruits.19

Sukarno and Hatta were the most visible of the four nationalists the Japanese elevated to lead Putera — the "Empat Serangkai," literally the "four-in-a-cluster" or, as it is sometimes rendered, the nationalist "four-leaf clover": Sukarno, Hatta, the educator Ki Hajar Dewantara, and the Muslim leader Kyai Haji Mas Mansur.20 Working within the Japanese apparatus, Sukarno toured Java addressing vast crowds, his oratory ostensibly mobilizing them for the war but in practice forging an unprecedented direct bond between the nationalist leadership and the masses. The cost of this collaboration was heavy and real. Sukarno publicly exhorted Indonesians to enlist as romusha, and in later years he acknowledged the burden of having helped send so many countrymen to suffering and death — a stain that critics, then and since, have not let fade.21

Historians have debated the balance sheet of collaboration ever since. Theodore Friend, in his comparative study of occupied Java and the Philippines, framed the occupation through the racial ideology of a regime that exhorted its troops to annihilate "the blue-eyed enemy and their black slaves," and showed how Indonesian leaders manoeuvred within a system that despised them even as it used them.22 Ethan Mark, in the first full transnational history of the occupation of Java, has argued against reading the period as a simple morality tale of collaboration and resistance, insisting instead that it be understood as the fraught meeting of two Asian societies with their own ambitions, in which Indonesian actors were never merely puppets.23 The judgement that has largely prevailed is pragmatic: the open collaboration of Sukarno and Hatta and the hidden resistance of Sjahrir together gave the movement both mass mobilization and moral standing, and it was the combination — not either alone — that positioned the nationalists to seize the moment in August 1945.

Language, propaganda, and the nation

Perhaps the occupation's most enduring and least intended gift to Indonesian nationalism was linguistic. The Japanese banned Dutch — the language of the deposed colonizer — from administration, schooling, business, and public life. They hoped to install Japanese in its place, but Japanese could not be taught to tens of millions overnight, and so the practical language of government, media, and mobilization became Malay, which the nationalists had already proclaimed in 1928 as bahasa Indonesia.24 To run their propaganda machine the Japanese needed the language standardized and expanded, and they sponsored committees of Indonesian writers to coin terminology and regularize usage. For the first time a single indigenous language carried the official business of the entire archipelago, broadcast over radio and printed in newspapers and magazines such as Djawa Baroe that circulated nationally.25

The cultural apparatus the Japanese built to serve their war thus became, in effect, a nation-building machine. Indonesian writers, broadcasters, and artists — the educated youth, the pemuda, courted by the Japanese to staff news agencies and propaganda bureaus — acquired skills, audiences, and a vocabulary of national struggle, even as they grew to despise their Japanese patrons.26 And in adopting the nationalists' own name, "Indonesia," for the territory they had taken from the Dutch, the Japanese lent official weight to the very idea of a single Indonesian nation. As one Indonesian later put it, the Japanese "could not use the machine that they created"; when independence was proclaimed, public support came easily "because they were already mobilized by Japan."27

Atrocity and famine

None of these unintended benefits can be allowed to soften the record of suffering. The occupation was, for most Indonesians, a time of deepening misery, and for many it was lethal. The most catastrophic single episode was the famine that struck Java in 1944–45. Its causes were substantially man-made: the Japanese imposed a rigid system of compulsory rice deliveries, with quotas levied on districts and villages and purchase prices set far below market value and then gutted by runaway inflation, so that farmers had every incentive to hide grain rather than surrender it. The free movement of rice was restricted, the rail network that redistributed food decayed, and an extended dry season in 1944 delayed the harvest.28 The result was mass death by starvation and the diseases that follow it.

The scale was long obscured and remains contested. In careful demographic reconstructions, the economic historian Pierre van der Eng estimates that the crisis produced on the order of 1.8 to 1.9 million excess deaths — roughly 0.7 million in 1944 and over a million in 1945 — together with some 1.4 to 1.5 million "missing" births, for a net population loss of around 2.4 to 3.4 million on Java between 1943 and 1945.29 A figure of "approximately four million" deaths for the occupation as a whole, advanced by Indonesian officials after the war and equivalent to some eight per cent of Java's population, has circulated widely; van der Eng notes that, in proportional terms, this would exceed even the contemporaneous Bengal famine.30 The demographic data are imperfect, and scholars such as Shigeru Sato have warned that early estimates may have been inflated for the purposes of post-war reparations claims; van der Eng's reconstruction represents the most rigorous attempt to settle the question, while acknowledging its uncertainties.31 Why the famine was for decades almost absent from Indonesia's national memory is itself revealing: the post-war republic, courting Japanese aid and reparations and increasingly under Sukarno's authority, had little political incentive to dwell on a disaster in which its own founding generation had been complicit.32

A contested toll. Estimates of total Indonesian deaths under the occupation — from famine, forced labour, and violence — generally range between three and four million. These figures rest on incomplete wartime records and are debated by demographers; this chapter follows the scholarship in treating them as informed estimates, not exact counts, while insisting that the human catastrophe they describe is beyond dispute.

Beyond the famine lay a wider landscape of atrocity. The Kenpeitai, Japan's military police, ruled through terror: arbitrary arrest, torture, and execution — sometimes for offences as trivial as listening to Allied radio — were routine, and episodes such as the Pontianak massacres in Borneo saw hundreds, and by some accounts well over a thousand, of the local elite arrested and beheaded on suspicion of conspiracy.33 And across the occupied territories the Japanese operated a system of military sexual slavery whose victims were euphemistically termed "comfort women" (jugun ianfu). In the Indies the system ensnared both interned European women and, in far larger numbers, Indonesian women and girls — many of them adolescents deceived with promises of factory work or schooling, others simply seized.34 A post-war Dutch inquiry concluded that of some 200 to 300 European women in Japanese military brothels, at least sixty-five had certainly been forced; one survivor, Jan Ruff O'Herne, testified decades later that after her abduction from a Java internment camp she was "systematically beaten and raped day and night."35 The number of Indonesian victims is unknown and unknowable — the Japanese destroyed the records, and most survivors, integrated into the communities to which they had been trafficked, kept their silence — but it ran into the many thousands.36 A Batavia war-crimes court in 1948 convicted eleven Japanese officers in connection with the brothels, one of whom was sentenced to death.37

The promise of independence

As the war turned decisively against Japan through 1944, Tokyo's calculus shifted. With the Co-Prosperity Sphere crumbling and an Allied invasion of the Indies expected, the Japanese sought to bind Indonesians to a last-ditch defence by dangling the prospect of independence. In September 1944 Prime Minister Koiso publicly promised future independence for the "East Indies"; the concrete step came on Java the following spring. On 1 March 1945 the Sixteenth Army announced the creation of the Investigating Committee for the Preparation of Independence — the BPUPK (Badan Penyelidik Usaha Persiapan Kemerdekaan) — and it convened in Jakarta in late May.38

BPUPK — the Badan Penyelidik Usaha Persiapan Kemerdekaan, or Investigating Committee for the Preparation of Independence, a body of some sixty members (the great majority Indonesian) convened by the Japanese on Java from late May 1945 to debate the foundations of an independent state.

Benedict Anderson argued that the Japanese established the BPUPK partly "as a concession to Indonesian nationalists," hoping it would safely redirect nationalist energy into factional debate rather than action.39 If so, the gambit produced something far larger than intended. On 1 June 1945, on the final day of the committee's first session, Sukarno delivered the speech that would give independent Indonesia its philosophical charter. Searching for a common ideological ground broad enough to hold together Muslims and secularists, Javanese and outer islanders, he proposed five principles — Pancasila: nationalism, internationalism (or humanitarianism), representative government, social welfare, and belief in one God. The speech resolved, at least provisionally, the deepest fault line in the movement, between those who wanted an Islamic state and those who wanted a secular one, and 1 June is commemorated in Indonesia to this day as the birthday of Pancasila.40

The committee met again in July, voting decisively for a republic over a monarchy and approving a draft constitution. As Japan's collapse accelerated after the atomic bombings, the BPUPK was dissolved on 7 August 1945 and replaced by a smaller body charged with the act of founding itself: the Preparatory Committee for Indonesian Independence, the PPKI (Panitia Persiapan Kemerdekaan Indonesia).41 Events then outran the Japanese timetable entirely. Japan surrendered on 15 August; under pressure from impatient pemuda who feared an independence "granted" by a defeated occupier would be tainted, Sukarno and Hatta proclaimed Indonesian independence on 17 August 1945, and the next day the PPKI adopted the constitution and named them president and vice-president. The institutions the Japanese had built to manage a controlled transition had become the scaffolding of a unilateral revolution.42

An occupation that made a revolution

How could so brief and so brutal an interlude transform Indonesia's prospects so completely? The answer lies in the accumulation of changes the occupation set in motion, almost none of them intended for Indonesian benefit. It destroyed the prestige and the administrative machinery of Dutch colonialism beyond repair. It thrust Indonesians into the running of their own government and gave tens of thousands of them military training and arms through PETA. It elevated Sukarno and Hatta to a direct, mass relationship with the people that no nationalist had ever enjoyed under the Dutch, while leaving Sjahrir's underground in reserve. It made bahasa Indonesia the working language of the nation and saturated the archipelago with the vocabulary of national struggle. And in its final months it convened the very bodies — the BPUPK and the PPKI — that drafted the constitution, proclaimed the five principles of Pancasila, and supplied the legal form of the new republic.43

It did all this, however, atop a foundation of catastrophe — millions dead of hunger and forced labour, women enslaved, a society militarized and brutalized. Anthony Reid judged that the Japanese conquest "undoubtedly hastened Indonesian independence," and that the occupation's unifying effect — making the masses aware, for the first time, of who their leaders were — was among its most consequential legacies.44 Benedict Anderson located the revolution's decisive energy not in the established intellectuals or in an oppressed proletariat but in the radicalized youth the occupation had mobilized and armed.45 When the Dutch returned in late 1945 expecting to reclaim a docile colony, they found instead a proclaimed republic defended by a population that had been mobilized, organized, and weaponized — by the Japanese. The occupation had not granted Indonesia its freedom; it had, against its own intentions, made that freedom possible to seize. That is the paradox at the heart of these three and a half years, and the bridge to the revolution that is the subject of the next chapter.

Summary

Japan conquered the Netherlands East Indies in under three months in early 1942, destroying Dutch authority and dividing the archipelago among three rival administrations — the Army on Java and on Sumatra, the Navy in the east. To wage its war the occupation mobilized Indonesians on an enormous scale: through romusha forced labour that killed hundreds of thousands, through mass organizations such as Putera and the Jawa Hokokai, and through the PETA militia that trained the future republican army. Sukarno and Hatta chose open collaboration while Sjahrir built an underground, a division historians still debate. The occupation banned Dutch and entrenched bahasa Indonesia, inadvertently strengthening national consciousness — even as it inflicted a man-made famine in 1944–45 that killed on the order of two million people on Java, ran a system of military sexual slavery, and ruled through terror. As defeat neared, Japan sponsored the BPUPK and the PPKI, the bodies in which Sukarno proclaimed Pancasila on 1 June 1945 and which framed the new state. Two days after Japan's surrender, Indonesia declared its independence. The occupation, brutal and unintended in its effects, had transformed the conditions for revolution.
