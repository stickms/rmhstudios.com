Learning Objectives

  • Trace Sukarno's early life, education, and intellectual formation, from his boarding in the home of nationalist leader Cokroaminoto to his Bandung engineering degree.
  • Explain the ideological argument of Sukarno's 1926 essay "Nationalism, Islam and Marxism" and the concept of Marhaenisme as a synthesis suited to Indonesian social conditions.
  • Describe the founding and programme of the Partai Nasional Indonesia (PNI, 1927) and assess Sukarno's strategy of non-cooperation and mass mobilisation.
  • Contrast Sukarno's approach to independence with that of Mohammad Hatta and Sutan Sjahrir and the Perhimpoenan Indonesia in the Netherlands.
  • Analyse the Sumpah Pemuda (Youth Pledge) of 28 October 1928 — its content, its organisational origins, the choice of Bahasa Indonesia, and the premiere of "Indonesia Raya."
  • Assess the significance and limits of Sukarno's 1930 trial speech "Indonesia Menggugat" as political manifesto and propaganda.
  • Describe the Dutch crackdown of the 1930s, the exiles of the major leaders, and the condition of Indonesian nationalism on the eve of Japanese occupation.

On the evening of 28 October 1928, in a modest building on Jalan Kramat Raya in Batavia, a young man named Wage Rudolf Supratman raised a violin to his chin and played an anthem that did not yet belong to any nation. The several hundred delegates at the Second Indonesian Youth Congress had just endorsed a three-clause pledge affirming one homeland, one nation, and one language — all called Indonesia. As the music carried across the room, the Dutch colonial state that had ruled the archipelago for three centuries was being named, for the first time, as the thing that must end. The moment crystallised a decade of political ferment: the rise of two very different styles of nationalist leadership, the crystallisation of a common language and a common identity, and the beginning of a confrontation with colonial power that would dominate the next two decades. This chapter traces that decade — from the formation of the nationalist vanguard in the mid-1920s through the Sumpah Pemuda and its anthem, through Sukarno's landmark trial, and into the exile years of the 1930s, when the Dutch believed they had broken the movement but had only deferred it.

The nationalist landscape before the leaders

As Chapters 24 and 25 of this book have shown, Indonesian nationalism did not emerge from a void. The Ethical Policy proclaimed in 1901 had, with deep and unintended irony, trained the very generation that would dismantle colonial rule: Western-educated Indonesians who could read Marx and Mill, argue in Dutch and Malay, and compare their own condition with emancipatory movements in India, Egypt, and China.1 Budi Utomo (1908) had shown that educated Javanese could organise; Sarekat Islam had demonstrated that mass politics was possible across class and regional lines; the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI) had attempted insurrection and been crushed in the uprisings of 1926–27. By the mid-1920s each of these streams had hit a ceiling: Budi Utomo's Javanese elitism limited its national reach; Sarekat Islam was riven between secular and pan-Islamic factions; and the PKI's two successive revolts — in West Java (November 1926) and West Sumatra (January 1927) — had been suppressed swiftly, their leaders killed, imprisoned, or shipped to the dreaded internment camp at Boven Digul in western New Guinea.2

What the movement needed was a new synthesis: a leader who could weld the three great streams — nationalism, Islam, and Marxism — into a single anti-colonial force without making enemies of any. In 1926 and 1927, two such leaders emerged, working from opposite ends of the Dutch colonial world: one in Bandung, the other in the Netherlands. Their differing temperaments, strategies, and social bases would shape Indonesian nationalism — and, after 1945, Indonesian politics — for a generation.

Sukarno: from Surabaya to Bandung

Sukarno was born Kusno Sosrodihardjo on 6 June 1901 in Surabaya, East Java, the son of a Javanese Muslim schoolteacher of lower-priyayi (aristocratic) descent and a Balinese Brahmin mother from Buleleng. The mixed parentage was symbolically apt: the man who would synthesise a nation straddled two of its great cultural worlds from birth. The name Sukarno — drawn from the Mahabharata hero Karna — was given to him after the sickly infant survived a childhood illness, in keeping with Javanese custom of renaming a child who recovers.3

In 1916, at fifteen, Sukarno was sent to the Hogere Burgerschool (HBS), the Dutch-language secondary school in Surabaya — the highest rung of colonial education available to indigenous students. More formative than the school itself was the household where he boarded: that of Haji Omar Said Tjokroaminoto, founder and chairman of Sarekat Islam and arguably the most influential Indonesian political figure of the era. Contemporaries called Tjokroaminoto the "King of Java," and his Surabaya home was a salon where politicians, clerics, journalists, and labour organisers debated the future of the Indies.4 Sukarno absorbed it all. He listened to Tjokroaminoto's Islamic reformist oratory; he met the socialist labour leader Semaoen and the communist Darsono; he read the Dutch translations of Marx, Hegel, and the German social democrats that circulated in the household. He also read the nationalist literature of India — Gandhi, Aurobindo, Tilak — and compared it with the Egyptian nationalist and pan-Islamic movements he encountered in the popular press.

In 1921 Sukarno moved to Bandung to study civil engineering at the Technische Hoogeschool te Bandoeng (Bandung Institute of Technology), where he earned his Ingenieur degree in 1926. Bandung in the 1920s was the administrative and intellectual capital of the colony's European élite — and, for that same reason, a city in which a politically awakened Indonesian engineer could feel colonialism's weight in every professional and social interaction. Sukarno was "intensely modern," in the words of his principal biographer, J. D. Legge: he despised traditional Javanese feudalism as an obstacle to nationalist unity and Western imperialism as what he called, quoting Karl Marx, "the exploitation of man by man."5 In Bandung he read voraciously in multiple languages — Dutch, German, English, French, Arabic — and began formulating the synthesis that would define his political career.

Nationalism, Islam, and Marxism: the synthesis of 1926

In 1926, while still completing his degree, Sukarno published a three-part essay in Soeloeh Indonesia Moeda (Torch of Young Indonesia), the journal of the Algemeene Studieclub (General Study Club), a discussion group he had helped found among Bandung's Indonesian intellectuals. The essay's title stated its ambition plainly: "Nationalism, Islam and Marxism."6 Its argument was the most important statement of Indonesian nationalist ideology to appear before independence.

Sukarno's starting point was a diagnosis of the movement's weakness. The three great streams of Indonesian political thought were at war with one another: secular nationalists distrusted Islamic clerics; Muslim activists feared Marxist atheism; communists dismissed religious mobilisation as false consciousness. Meanwhile, the colonial state ruled with relative ease. For Sukarno, the antagonisms were a product of misunderstanding, not of genuine incompatibility. Nationalism provided the emotional and organisational framework of the independence struggle — the idea that all Indonesians, regardless of faith or class, shared a common homeland and a common oppressor. Islam provided its moral vocabulary, its mass reach, and its tradition of resistance to foreign domination. Marxism supplied the economic analysis: imperialism was a system of exploitation that kept Indonesia poor not by accident but by design, enriching European metropoles while immiserating the archipelago. The three streams could and must become tributaries of a single river flowing toward independence.7

Marhaenisme (Marhaenism) — the political ideology developed by Sukarno, named after a Sundanese peasant farmer he reportedly met south of Bandung. Marhaen owned his own tiny plot and his own tools yet lived in poverty — the Indonesian equivalent, Sukarno argued, of the Marxist proletarian: not exploited by a domestic capitalist class but impoverished by colonial structures. Marhaenisme extended the concept to encompass all Indonesian masses — peasants, fishermen, petty traders, and urban workers — as the social base of nationalist revolution.

The essay also introduced the concept of the marhaen: the name Sukarno gave to the characteristic Indonesian social figure. Marxism's "proletariat" was a product of industrial capitalism; Indonesia had no large-scale domestic capitalist class to exploit its workers in the classical sense. Instead, the typical Indonesian — the marhaen — was a small peasant farmer who owned a tiny plot of land and his own tools but was nonetheless destitute, his surplus extracted not by a domestic bourgeoisie but by colonial taxes, forced cultivation, and the machinery of an imperial economy that oriented the archipelago toward European profit.8 The concept was politically inclusive: it encompassed not just factory hands but the overwhelming majority of Indonesians, transcending class divisions to define a national constituency for independence. Legge has argued that Marhaenism, for all its eclecticism, was a genuinely original adaptation of Marxist categories to Indonesian social realities — not a mere rhetorical gesture but a sustained attempt to think through what anti-colonial politics meant outside the European industrial context.9

Historians have debated how intellectually coherent the synthesis actually was. John Ingleson, whose study of the nationalist movement in the years 1927–34 draws on extensive Dutch intelligence reports as well as Indonesian sources, notes that the coalition Sukarno envisioned never fully materialised: Islamic and communist wings of the movement continued to distrust each other, and the attempt to hold them together in the same party ultimately strained PNI organisation and contributed to its vulnerability to Dutch crackdown.10 Nevertheless, the essay's strategic importance was immediate: it articulated, for the first time, why Indonesian nationalism should not be reduced to any single one of these streams.

The PNI and the politics of mass mobilisation

On 4 July 1927, Sukarno and a group of colleagues from the Algemeene Studieclub formally constituted the Partai Nasional Indonesia (Indonesian National Party, PNI) in Bandung. The date was not accidental; the American Independence Day carried a symbolic charge that Sukarno, who spoke English and read American political history, would not have missed.11 The PNI was the first major Indonesian political organisation to use the word "Indonesia" in its name, to demand full independence (kemerdekaan) rather than autonomy within the Dutch empire, and to ground itself explicitly in secular nationalism rather than Islam or Marxism — though Sukarno sought to attract all three constituencies.12

The PNI's programme rested on two pillars: noncooperatie (non-cooperation with the colonial government) and mass mobilisation. Sukarno rejected the strategy of working within colonial institutions — petitioning the Volksraad (People's Council), accepting nominated seats, negotiating incremental reforms — as collaboration that legitimised Dutch rule without advancing independence. Instead, the PNI would organise its own institutions: schools, cooperatives, discussion clubs, and above all public meetings at which Sukarno would speak.

Noncooperatie — the strategy, adopted by the PNI and similar organisations, of refusing to participate in colonial institutions (the Volksraad, advisory councils, government employment) on the ground that doing so legitimised Dutch authority. Borrowed in part from Gandhi's non-cooperation movement in India, it represented a sharper break with the reformist nationalism of earlier organisations such as Budi Utomo.

Sukarno's oratory was the PNI's most powerful asset. Fluent in Javanese, Sundanese, Malay, Dutch, and several other languages, possessed of a photographic memory and an actor's command of voice and gesture, he could hold vast outdoor crowds for hours. R. E. Elson has written that in Sukarno's speeches, the idea of Indonesia was transformed from an abstract political programme into what he calls "a mythic history of togetherness" — a vision of a primordial unity that colonialism had disrupted and that independence would restore.13 This was not, strictly speaking, history: pre-colonial Indonesia had no single political identity. But as a rhetorical construction it was extraordinarily powerful, because it gave listeners a story in which they were the heroes and reclamation was their destiny.

By late 1929 the PNI had approximately 10,000 members — a remarkable growth for an explicitly independence-seeking party under colonial rule — and branches across Java and in the outer islands.14 In 1927 Sukarno had also attempted to federate the various nationalist, Islamic, and workers' organisations into a united front, the PPPKI (Permufakatan Perhimpunan-Perhimpunan Politik Kebangsaan Indonesia, or Federation of Indonesian Political Organisations), though this body remained largely a coordinating umbrella rather than a unified party, and the ideological tensions Sukarno's 1926 essay had diagnosed did not disappear.15

The other wing: Hatta, Sjahrir, and the Perhimpoenan Indonesia

While Sukarno was building a mass movement in Java, a parallel nationalist formation was developing among Indonesian students in the Netherlands — and it was generating its own influential leaders. Mohammad Hatta was born on 12 August 1902 in Bukittinggi in the Minangkabau highlands of West Sumatra, into a family of Islamic scholars. His cultural background was markedly different from Sukarno's: Minangkabau society was matrilineal, commercially oriented, deeply Islamic but also receptive to modern education, and had produced a significant proportion of Indonesia's reformist Islamic thinkers.16 Hatta arrived in the Netherlands in 1921 to study at the Rotterdam School of Commerce (later the Netherlands School of Economics), and he did not return to Indonesia until 1932.

The organisation that shaped Hatta's political development was the Indische Vereeniging (Indies Association), a student welfare body founded in Leiden in 1908. Under the pressure of growing nationalist consciousness, it renamed itself the Indonesische Vereeniging (Indonesian Association) in 1922 — a name that deliberately used the word "Indonesia" rather than "Indies" — and then, in 1924, the Perhimpoenan Indonesia (Indonesian Union, PI). The trajectory of renaming was itself a political trajectory: from a cultural welfare club to a political organisation demanding independence.17 The PI's journal underwent the same transformation: from the anodyne Hindia Poetra (Child of the Indies) to the bluntly programmatic Indonesia Merdeka (Free Indonesia).

Hatta served as the PI's treasurer from 1922 to 1925 and as its chairman from 1926 to 1930. Under his leadership, the organisation shed its student-welfare character and became an explicitly political body that demanded Indonesian independence, practised noncooperatie, and sought to internationalise the Indonesian cause. The PI sent representatives to European anti-colonial networks and cultivated contacts with the Indian National Congress, Egyptian nationalists, and the emerging international left. In February 1927, Hatta attended the founding congress of the League Against Imperialism in Brussels — a gathering organised with Comintern support but attended by a wide spectrum of anti-colonial leaders, including Jawaharlal Nehru and Nguyen Ai Quoc (later Ho Chi Minh). Klaas Stutje's study of the PI stresses that these international connections were not merely ceremonial: they provided Indonesian nationalists with frameworks, contacts, and a sense of global solidarity that shaped their political imagination.18

The Dutch authorities were alarmed. On 23 September 1927, Hatta and three PI colleagues — Ali Sastroamidjojo, Abdulmadjid Djojoadhiningrat, and Nazir Pamontjak — were arrested and charged with seditious content in Indonesia Merdeka. Their trial in The Hague in March 1928 ended in acquittal; Hatta used it, as Sukarno would use his own trial two years later, as a propaganda platform, publishing his defence speech as "Indonesia Free." The brochure circulated widely among Indonesian nationalists both in the Netherlands and in the archipelago.19

The third figure of this generation was Sutan Sjahrir (1909–1966), a Minangkabau intellectual who studied law in Amsterdam and joined the PI in the early 1930s. Where Sukarno built mass movements and Hatta managed international strategy, Sjahrir's concern was education — the systematic preparation of a democratic citizenry that could govern itself after independence. Mrázek's biography of Sjahrir shows that he was deeply suspicious of what he called the "feudal" element in mass nationalism: the charismatic leader who demanded adulation rather than informed participation. The underground Pendidikan Nasional Indonesia (Indonesian National Education, PNI-baru) that Sjahrir and Hatta would build after 1931 was accordingly called a "pendidikan" (education) rather than a "partai" (party), signalling its cadre-training rather than mass-agitation vocation.20

The Sukarno-Hatta-Sjahrir triad was not a partnership of equals — the three men sometimes distrusted one another's methods deeply — but it did represent the full range of Indonesian nationalist strategy: the mass mobiliser, the international statesman, and the underground educator. That all three would survive the colonial period and share the stage in 1945 was due partly to luck and partly to the structural differences in their methods, which made them harder to silence simultaneously.

The Youth Pledge: Sumpah Pemuda, 28 October 1928

While the PNI and the PI were building their separate organisations, a generational movement was forming among Indonesian youth organisations throughout the archipelago. These groups — mostly organised along ethnic and regional lines, such as Jong Java (Young Java), Jong Soematranen Bond (Young Sumatrans' Union), Jong Batak Bond, Jong Islamieten Bond, Jong Celebes, Jong Ambon, and Pemoeda Kaoem Betawi — had begun meeting in the First Youth Congress of 1926 to discuss common concerns. The Second Youth Congress, held in Batavia on 27–28 October 1928, was convened with a more ambitious goal: to assert a unity that transcended all ethnic and regional identities.21

The congress was organised primarily by the Perhimpunan Pelajar-Pelajar Indonesia (PPPI, Indonesian Students' Association), whose chairman Sugondo Djojopuspito presided. Mohammad Yamin of Jong Soematranen Bond served as secretary. Delegates from nine organisations participated, representing almost every major ethnic community in the colony. The congress met across three sessions at three Batavia venues over two days; the final session, at which the pledge was adopted, convened at the building now known as the Sumpah Pemuda Museum at Jalan Kramat Raya 106.22

The pledge itself — formally the "Decision of the Congress of Indonesian Youth" — consisted of three clauses, here reproduced in their original old orthography and in English translation:

Sumpah Pemuda (Youth Pledge), 28 October 1928

Pertama: Kami poetra dan poetri Indonesia, mengakoe bertoempah darah jang satoe, tanah air Indonesia.
(First: We the sons and daughters of Indonesia acknowledge one motherland, Indonesia.)

Kedua: Kami poetra dan poetri Indonesia, mengakoe berbangsa jang satoe, bangsa Indonesia.
(Second: We the sons and daughters of Indonesia acknowledge one nation, the Indonesian nation.)

Ketiga: Kami poetra dan poetri Indonesia, mendjoendjoeng bahasa persatoean, bahasa Indonesia.
(Third: We the sons and daughters of Indonesia uphold the language of unity, the Indonesian language.)

Each clause was a political act of considerable boldness. The first asserted that the entire colonial territory — the Netherlands East Indies — constituted a single homeland, overriding the Dutch administrative fragmentation of the archipelago and the many ethnic homelands within it. The second asserted that there was a single Indonesian bangsa (nation or people), collapsing the hundreds of ethnic communities into a single political identity. The third was in some ways the most technically demanding: it designated a single language of unity, calling it simply "Indonesian."23

The language decision was not straightforward, and the congress debated it. Mohammad Yamin's original draft apparently used "Malay" (Melayu) for the third clause; Mohammad Tabrani objected that the language should be called "Indonesian" to give the concept of national unity linguistic form. The revision was adopted — and the decision had profound implications. The language that would become Bahasa Indonesia was essentially an elevated and eventually standardised form of trade Malay, already a lingua franca across much of the archipelago. The choice of Malay over Javanese was politically essential: Javanese was spoken by the largest single ethnic group, but choosing it as the national language would have made the Indonesian nation appear to be a Javanese empire, alienating the Sundanese, Minangkabau, Makasar, Acehnese, and dozens of other peoples. Malay, by contrast, belonged to no dominant ethnic group — it was the language of the market, the port, and the mosque, already supraethnic before nationalists made it national.24

Benedict Anderson's Imagined Communities (1983) offers the most influential theoretical frame for understanding this choice. Anderson argues that print capitalism — the production and circulation of newspapers, novels, and political pamphlets in a shared vernacular — creates the shared temporal and cultural space within which nationalism becomes imaginable. In the case of Indonesia, a substantial print culture in trade Malay had been growing for decades before the Youth Pledge, fed by Chinese-Indonesian newspapers, nationalist journals, and Sukarno's own movement press. The pledge of 1928 ratified a reality that was already being created in print, and in doing so gave that reality irreversible political form.25

Historians have noted limits to the Youth Pledge's inclusiveness. The congress was composed of educated urban youth, not peasants or workers; it reflected the aspirations of the nationalist intelligentsia rather than of the Indonesian masses in any statistical sense. The women present were few. Several regional and Islamic organisations held aloof or participated only marginally. And the very concept of a single Indonesian nation was, as George McT. Kahin observed in his foundational study, a political construction that required enormous ongoing work to sustain — it did not describe an existing social reality so much as project a desired future one.26 These qualifications do not diminish the pledge's historical importance, but they contextualise it: the Sumpah Pemuda was a beginning, not an achievement.

Indonesia Raya and the anthem of a nation-in-waiting

The closing of the congress brought a moment that has passed into Indonesian national mythology. Wage Rudolf Supratman (1903–1938), a violinist and journalist from Purworejo, Central Java, who had composed an instrumental piece he called "Indonesia" — later "Indonesia Raya" (Great Indonesia) — asked and received permission to perform it. He played it on violin rather than having it sung; the Dutch colonial authorities, who had observers present, might have tolerated an instrumental piece more readily than a crowd singing openly nationalist lyrics.27

Supratman had reportedly composed the piece around 1924 in Bandung after reading an essay challenge in the nationalist magazine Timbul calling for an Indonesian national anthem. The first published version of the lyrics appeared in the Surabaya newspaper Soeloeh Ra'jat Indonesia on 7 November 1928, ten days after the congress, and spread rapidly through the nationalist press. Supratman renamed the piece "Indonesia Raya" and added the subtitle "national anthem of Indonesia" in 1929 — a title the Dutch colonial authorities found seditious and promptly banned from public performance.28

The ban made the anthem. By forbidding its performance, the Dutch colonial state transformed "Indonesia Raya" from a song into a symbol of resistance. Every clandestine singing was an act of defiance; every arrest of someone caught performing it became evidence of colonial repression. The anthem would wait until 17 August 1945 to be publicly proclaimed as the national song of the Republic of Indonesia, but in the years between 1928 and 1945, it served as an audible marker of membership in the imagined nation.

"Indonesia Menggugat": the 1930 trial

The PNI's rapid growth — from a study club in 1926 to a party of 10,000 members by 1929 — alarmed the colonial government. Governor-General Andries Cornelis Dirk de Graeff was already under pressure from conservative opinion in the Netherlands, and his successor Bonifacius Cornelis de Jonge, who took office in 1931, would prove even more repressive. On the night of 29 December 1929, colonial police arrested Sukarno and eight other PNI leaders at their homes across Java. The charge: violating articles 153 and 169 of the Netherlands Indies Penal Code, which criminalised "openly expressing hostile sentiments against the government" and "participation in a secret organisation."29

Sukarno spent months in Bantjeuj prison in Bandung while awaiting trial. The trial itself was held before the Landraad (colonial court) in Bandung from August to December 1930. The colonial authorities intended it as a demonstration of the law's reach; Sukarno converted it into a demonstration of the independence movement's reach. His defence oration — delivered over several days in August 1930 and published as Indonesia Menggugat (Indonesia Accuses) — became one of the most widely read nationalist documents of the colonial era.30

The speech was a sweeping intellectual indictment of colonialism in five parts. It opened with a general theory of imperialism drawn from J. A. Hobson, Lenin, and Rosa Luxemburg, tracing the systemic logic by which metropolitan capitalism required colonial territories to absorb surplus capital and extract raw materials. It then applied this framework to the specific history of the Netherlands East Indies, documenting the extraction of Indonesian wealth since the VOC era. A third section outlined the history of the Indonesian independence movement; a fourth defended the PNI's organisation and ideology; a fifth addressed the specific charges under articles 153 and 169, arguing that the articles themselves violated elementary principles of justice.31

Kahin called Indonesia Menggugat one of the "most brilliant nationalist manifestos to emerge from Asia" in the interwar period — a judgment that reflects both its intellectual sophistication and its rhetorical power.32 It circulated in mimeographed copies throughout the archipelago, reaching audiences far beyond the courtroom. Sukarno was convicted and sentenced to four years in prison; international pressure — including from liberal opinion in the Netherlands itself — led to the reduction of the sentence to two years, and he was released on 31 December 1931.33

The trial had effects its architects had not anticipated. It had given Sukarno a national and international platform; it had circulated a comprehensive anti-colonial argument in accessible Indonesian prose; and it had made him, unmistakably, a hero. But it had also revealed the vulnerability of a mass political party operating openly under colonial law. The PNI's response was self-dissolution.

The Dutch crackdown and the years of exile

At an extraordinary congress in April 1931, the PNI voted to dissolve itself rather than allow the Dutch to dissolve it on their own terms. The dissolution produced an immediate and consequential split. One faction, calling themselves Partindo (Partai Indonesia, Indonesian Party), argued for continuing mass agitation along the lines Sukarno had pioneered; another faction, Pendidikan Nasional Indonesia (National Indonesian Education, known as PNI-baru or New PNI), argued that the time for mass mobilisation was past and that the movement needed to build cadres — trained, politically educated members capable of sustaining the independence struggle through decades of repression if necessary. Partindo was led by Sartono; PNI-baru by Hatta and Sjahrir, now returned from the Netherlands.34

Sukarno, released from prison in December 1931, initially attempted to reconcile the two factions. When that failed, he joined Partindo in 1932 and resumed mass speaking tours. His return galvanised large crowds but also galvanised the colonial police. Governor-General de Jonge — who reportedly said he intended to rule Indonesia for "another 300 years" — had no patience for the ambiguities of his predecessor. On 1 August 1933, Sukarno was arrested again. This time there was no trial. Under emergency powers, De Jonge exiled him without legal process: in January 1934, Sukarno was shipped with his family (his wife Inggit Garnasih and her foster daughter) to Ende, on the island of Flores in the eastern archipelago.35

Ende in 1934 was as remote as the colonial imagination could design: a small town on a malarial island, a month's boat journey from Batavia, with a population of around 5,000 and no Indonesian nationalist community whatever. Sukarno was permitted limited freedom of movement within the town. He taught children's drama, wrote plays with allegorical political content, and — by his own later account — meditated in the gardens of Ende on what would eventually become the five principles of Pancasila, the philosophical foundation of the independent Indonesian state.36 In 1938, alarmed by a malaria outbreak, the Dutch transferred him to Bengkulu on the western coast of Sumatra, where he taught at a Muhammadiyah school and began a relationship with a student, Fatmawati, who would become his third wife in 1943.

The treatment of Hatta and Sjahrir was simultaneously unfolding along a parallel and equally punishing trajectory. In February 1934, Sjahrir was arrested in Java; Hatta followed in March. Both were convicted in November 1934 of nationalist activities through PNI-baru. Rather than prison, the colonial authorities sent them to Boven Digul: the internment camp carved out of the malarial jungle of western New Guinea (now Papua), where survivors of the 1926–27 PKI revolts had been confined since 1927. Conditions at Boven Digul were deliberately inhospitable — compulsory self-supporting labour, constant threat of malaria, deliberate isolation. The camp represented the colonial state's most punitive instrument short of execution.37

On 11 February 1936, Hatta and Sjahrir were transferred to Banda Neira — the main island of the Banda Islands in Maluku, historically the centre of the nutmeg trade and of the VOC's most violent monopoly enforcement in the seventeenth century. The conditions were incomparably better than Boven Digul: a colonial backwater, certainly, but with adequate food, clean air, and a small community of political internees and local residents. The two men opened an afternoon school for the island's children — Hatta teaching older pupils, Sjahrir the younger — and continued their intellectual work. Sjahrir's letters from exile, later published as Out of Exile, are among the most remarkable documents of Indonesian nationalism: philosophical, introspective, and shot through with a cosmopolitan humanist sensibility very different from Sukarno's volcanic oratory.38 Mrázek's biography of Sjahrir recovers the exile years as a period of intensive intellectual production, not mere waiting; in Banda, Sjahrir engaged seriously with European philosophy, with the question of what democratic governance meant, and with the moral dangers of nationalist authoritarianism.39

By the mid-1930s, the Dutch crackdown had effectively decapitated the Indonesian nationalist movement at the institutional level. All three of its major leaders were in enforced exile; Partindo was dissolved; PNI-baru had been suppressed; the Sumpah Pemuda coalition of youth organisations was operating under severe restriction. The colonial government appears, from its own records, to have believed that the independence movement had been contained. This assessment was shared by many foreign observers. It was, as events would show, profoundly wrong.

The movement on the eve of WWII

Beneath the surface of Dutch-imposed order, the nationalist movement survived in a series of smaller, more cautious organisations. Gerindo (Gerakan Rakyat Indonesia, Indonesian People's Movement), formed in 1937, brought together nationalist politicians willing to work through legal channels while quietly maintaining the demand for independence. In 1939, Gapi (Gabungan Politik Indonesia, Indonesian Political Federation) united most of the surviving nationalist parties — secular, Islamic, and moderate-left — in a common demand that the Indies be transformed into an equal partner with the Netherlands within the Dutch empire. The demand was framed in the idiom of imperial partnership, but its logic pointed toward independence: if Indonesians were equal partners, the word "colonial" would have lost its meaning.40

The outbreak of war in Europe in September 1939 sharpened these calculations. The Indonesian nationalist leaderships — including the exiled Hatta, communicating through trusted intermediaries — were acutely aware that a Dutch colonial state weakened by German occupation could not maintain the Indies by force alone. Some, including Hatta, offered a conditional wartime cooperation: Indonesian support for the Dutch war effort in exchange for a concrete commitment to a parliament with real power and a timetable toward self-government. The Dutch authorities in Batavia, under Governor-General Alidius Warmoldus Lambertus Tjarda van Starkenborgh Stachouwer, rebuffed these overtures, refusing to concede any meaningful constitutional change as the price of loyalty.41

The movement's state on the eve of WWII was thus paradoxical: weakened in organisation but strong in ideas, its leaders in exile but not broken, its language and its anthem in circulation, its youth pledge a decade old and increasingly embedded in national consciousness. The Japanese propaganda slogan "Asia for Asians" resonated precisely because Indonesian nationalists had been arguing since the 1920s that the Indies belonged to Indonesians, not to any European colonial power. And the memory of 1905 — of Japan defeating a European empire in the Russo-Japanese War — had been a reference point in nationalist literature for three decades.

When Japanese forces swept through Southeast Asia in the months following Pearl Harbor, the Netherlands East Indies fell in nine days, from 8 to 17 March 1942. The Dutch colonial state, which had exiled its opponents without trial and invested its institutional power in suppressing the independence movement, collapsed far more quickly than the nationalists it had suppressed. On 31 January 1942, a US Navy Catalina seaplane evacuated Hatta and Sjahrir from Banda Neira before dawn; Sukarno would be released in Sumatra with the Dutch capitulation. Within months, the three leaders who had spent most of the 1930s in enforced isolation would be the central figures of Indonesia's wartime and revolutionary politics — a story told in the chapters that follow.

Summary

The 1920s and 1930s were the formative period of Indonesian nationalist leadership. Sukarno, educated in Surabaya and Bandung, developed a synthetic ideology — Marhaenisme — that sought to unify nationalists, Muslims, and Marxists against colonial rule, building a mass movement through charismatic oratory and the Partai Nasional Indonesia (1927). Mohammad Hatta and Sutan Sjahrir, working through the Perhimpoenan Indonesia in the Netherlands, developed a parallel cadre-education approach emphasising international networks and ideological preparation. The Sumpah Pemuda of 28 October 1928 crystallised both streams in a single act: the Youth Pledge affirmed one homeland, one nation, and one language (Indonesian), while Wage Rudolf Supratman's violin premiere of "Indonesia Raya" gave the movement an anthem the Dutch promptly banned and thereby made indestructible. The Dutch crackdown of 1929–34 — Sukarno's trial and exile to Flores and Bengkulu, Hatta and Sjahrir's internment at Boven Digul and Banda Neira — sought to decapitate the movement. It deferred but did not destroy it. By the eve of WWII the ideas, language, anthem, and institutional memory of Indonesian nationalism were deeply rooted, awaiting only the cataclysm of Japanese occupation to transform a movement into a revolution.

Review & Discussion Questions

26.1 Sukarno's 1926 essay argued that nationalism, Islam, and Marxism could work together against colonialism. What was the strongest part of his argument? Where were its weaknesses most apparent in practice?
26.2 How did the concept of marhaen differ from the Marxist concept of the proletariat, and why did the distinction matter for Indonesian nationalist strategy?
26.3 Compare and contrast the strategies of Sukarno (PNI, mass mobilisation) and of Hatta and Sjahrir (Perhimpoenan Indonesia, cadre education). Which approach do you think was better suited to the conditions of the 1920s–30s? Would your answer differ for the 1940s?
26.4 Why was the choice of Malay rather than Javanese as the language of unity so important for the Sumpah Pemuda? What does Benedict Anderson's theory of "imagined communities" contribute to understanding this decision?
26.5 In what sense was the Sumpah Pemuda a "construction" rather than a description of an existing reality? Does this observation weaken or strengthen its historical significance?
26.6 Sukarno converted his 1930 trial into a nationalist platform with Indonesia Menggugat. How does this compare to similar "trial speeches" by other anti-colonial leaders (Gandhi, Mandela, Nehru)? What did Sukarno draw on intellectually?
26.7 The Dutch exiled Sukarno, Hatta, and Sjahrir rather than imprisoning or executing them. What does this choice tell us about the nature of Dutch colonial rule? Did it serve its intended purpose?
26.8 By 1939–41, the Dutch authorities apparently believed they had successfully contained Indonesian nationalism. What evidence was available to them? What evidence were they ignoring or unable to see?
