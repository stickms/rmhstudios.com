Learning Objectives

  • Trace the long arc of the Indonesian economy from the classical spice trade to the twenty-first-century commodity boom, identifying structural continuities and ruptures.
  • Explain the VOC's monopoly system, the Cultivation System, and the Liberal-era plantation economy, and assess their respective impacts on Javanese and Outer Islands society.
  • Evaluate the major interpretive debates in Indonesian economic historiography: Boeke's "dual economy" thesis, Geertz's "agricultural involution," and van Zanden and Marks's long-run GDP series.
  • Analyse the New Order's developmental model — its successes, its structural flaws, and its collapse in the 1997–98 crisis — and the resource-curse and middle-income-trap debates that followed.
  • Assess the twenty-first-century commodity economy (palm oil, coal, nickel) and the hilirisasi downstreaming strategy as responses to Indonesia's persistent structural challenge: converting natural abundance into broad-based prosperity.

Few countries present an economic historian with a paradox as sharp as Indonesia's. The archipelago sits atop one of the world's most extraordinary concentrations of natural wealth — the original source of cloves and nutmeg that sent European fleets across unknown seas, later the provider of coffee, sugar, rubber, and petroleum, today the world's largest exporter of thermal coal and palm oil and the dominant supplier of nickel for electric-vehicle batteries. Yet across the five centuries separating the first VOC charter (1602) from the present, this abundance has coexisted with mass poverty, periodic catastrophic crisis, and a structural divide between an internationally connected commodity sector and an inward-looking peasant and urban informal economy that scholars have named, variously, "involution," "dualism," and "the middle-income trap." This chapter traces the full arc: how the archipelago's economy was shaped by successive regimes of extraction and accumulation, how scholars have interpreted its trajectory, and what the twenty-first century's commodity surges suggest about the prospects of finally converting natural wealth into durable development.

The Emporium Economy: Maritime Trade and the Spice World (to c. 1600)

Long before European ships appeared on the horizon, the Indonesian archipelago was the pivot of a vast commercial world. The islands of the Banda Sea produced nutmeg; the North Moluccas (Maluku) produced cloves; Borneo contributed camphor and forest resins; Sumatra, pepper and gold. What made these products consequential was that they were in global demand — in the kitchens and pharmacies of China, India, and the Mediterranean — yet grew nowhere else in sufficient quantities. The earliest economic logic of the archipelago was therefore one of natural monopoly: whoever controlled the flow of spices between their island sources and the world's consuming markets could capture enormous rents.1

The political form this logic generated was the emporium or port polity: a coastal state that derived power not from controlling agricultural hinterlands but from taxing, brokering, and facilitating trade. Srivijaya, the Sumatran maritime polity that dominated the Strait of Malacca from the seventh to the eleventh centuries CE, exemplifies the type. Its rulers extracted income from the toll of trade passing through the strait, rather than from land rents or tribute in kind. They offered merchants protection, warehousing, currency exchange, and dispute resolution; in return they levied port duties. The Chinese monk Yijing, passing through Palembang in 671 CE, described a prosperous emporium where Buddhist monks numbered in the hundreds and where goods from throughout Asia were exchanged.2 The historian Kenneth R. Hall has described Srivijaya's hinterland integration as a system in which "coastal depots had elaborate systems of statecraft that integrated depots with their hinterland and separated international traders from hinterland people who produced the commodities."3

The emporium model, as Anthony Reid mapped it in his foundational two-volume study Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce, 1450–1680, reached its apogee in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. Reid argues for a long "age of commerce" in which expanding New World and Japanese silver monetised Asian trade, urban populations in coastal polities grew dramatically, and Islamic commercial ethics spread alongside the religion itself. The port cities of Malacca, Banten, Gresik, Makassar, and Aceh were not provincial backwaters but genuinely cosmopolitan entrepôts, sustaining populations in the tens of thousands and conducting regular trans-oceanic exchange.4 Javanese polities — Majapahit and then its successor sultanates of Demak, Banten, and Mataram — occupied the agricultural and demographic core of the archipelago but depended on the Moluccan spice islands for the luxury export revenue that funded military power and court culture.

The commodity at the centre of this world was, by value, spice. A pound of cloves or nutmeg that left the Maluku islands for the value of a few copper coins would sell in Lisbon or Amsterdam for the equivalent of several months' wages. This extraordinary price gradient — rooted in the spices' unique provenance, their scarcity in long-distance trade, and the costs and risks of the voyage — explains why European powers were willing to invest in the military conquest of islands at the far end of the world.5 The Portuguese took Malacca in 1511 and reached the Moluccas within a year. But it was the Dutch, organised through the Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie (VOC), who would actually succeed in establishing a durable monopoly — and in doing so, would transform the political economy of the archipelago irreversibly.

emporium (port polity) — a coastal state whose political power and revenue rested on brokering and taxing seaborne trade rather than on agricultural production. The classic examples in the Indonesian archipelago are Srivijaya (seventh–eleventh centuries) and, later, Malacca, Banten, and Makassar.

The VOC and the Monopoly Economy (1602–1799)

The Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie — the Dutch East India Company, chartered in 1602 — was the most financially powerful joint-stock corporation the world had yet seen, and its project in the Indonesian archipelago was fundamentally economic before it was territorial.6 The VOC's business model depended on acquiring commodities in the East at controlled (ideally monopoly) prices and selling them in Europe at the enormous margins that the spice-price gradient made possible. To achieve this, the VOC needed not merely to trade but to coerce: to prevent rivals (English, Portuguese, local) from undercutting its prices and to oblige indigenous producers to deliver at rates the Company dictated.

The most brutal expression of this logic came in the Banda Islands in 1621. The Banda archipelago — a cluster of tiny volcanic islands in the Banda Sea — was the world's only source of nutmeg and mace. When the Bandanese refused to honour exclusive-delivery agreements and continued to sell to English merchants, Governor-General Jan Pieterszoon Coen launched a military expedition that effectively exterminated the indigenous population. Scholars estimate that of the approximately fifteen thousand Bandanese inhabitants, only around one thousand survived the massacres, forced deportations, starvation, and disease that followed. The islands were then resettled as Dutch-owned plantations (perken), worked by enslaved labourers imported from across the archipelago, and the nutmeg monopoly was secured for more than a century.7 The Banda case illustrates, with terrible clarity, the political economy of the VOC: the Company was prepared to exterminate a people to maintain a price differential in a European luxury market.

Beyond the Moluccan spices, the VOC gradually extended its commercial reach to other commodities and other islands. In Java, the Company imposed forced deliveries of pepper, rice, and timber on coastal sultanates, and entered into alliances — often involving territorial concessions — with the competing courts of the Mataram Sultanate. By the early eighteenth century, coffee became the dominant VOC commodity from Java: indigenous cultivators were compelled to deliver coffee at a fixed, below-market price under the contingenten and verplichte leveranties (compulsory deliveries) systems. Java's agricultural economy was, for the first time, oriented toward a single export crop delivered at administered rather than market prices.8

The VOC's commercial success was real but ultimately self-undermining. Profligate expenditure on its Asian administrative and military apparatus, endemic corruption among its officials, and the rising costs of maintaining territorial control in Java and the Moluccas progressively eroded its financial position. By the 1780s, the Company was paying dividends from debt rather than profits; it was formally wound up in 1799, its debts and territories passing to the Batavian Republic (the Netherlands under French influence). The VOC bequeathed to its successors not a commercially integrated colony but a heterogeneous patchwork of forced-delivery arrangements, client sultanates, and monopoly concessions.

VOC — Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie, the Dutch East India Company (1602–1799). Chartered by the Dutch States-General with sovereign powers including the right to make war, conclude treaties, and govern territories. In its peak decades the VOC was the largest commercial enterprise in the world, extracting spices, coffee, and other commodities from the Indonesian archipelago through a combination of monopoly contracts, forced deliveries, and outright military coercion.

Extractive Colonialism: The Cultivation System and the Liberal Era (1830–1940)

The transfer of VOC debts and territories to the Dutch crown in 1799 inaugurated a period of experimentation with colonial governance: Daendels's proto-Napoleonic administration (1808–11), the brief British interlude under Raffles (1811–16), and a return to Dutch rule thereafter. It was the fiscal crisis of the early nineteenth century — the Netherlands had been nearly bankrupted by the Napoleonic wars and had just lost Belgium (1830), its wealthiest southern province — that produced the most consequential colonial economic innovation: the Cultuurstelsel, or Cultivation System.

Introduced by Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch in 1830, the Cultivation System was, in essence, a state-run agricultural extraction machine. Javanese peasant households were required to dedicate approximately one-fifth of their cultivated land, or sixty-six days of annual labour, to growing export crops — sugar, coffee, indigo, tea, and others — delivered to the colonial government at administered prices. The government then sold these crops on European markets at world prices, pocketing the difference. The results for the Dutch treasury were spectacular: net profits from Java reached approximately 289 million guilders in the decade 1851–60, and at the system's peak the Indies budget surplus remitted to the Netherlands (the so-called batig slot) financed roughly half of Dutch state expenditure and funded the construction of the Dutch national railway network.9

The human costs in Java were substantial. Labour diverted to colonial export crops left insufficient time for rice cultivation; famines struck Central Java in the 1840s. The obligation fell unevenly: local headmen (lurah) who were responsible for organising and supervising deliveries had strong incentives to extract above the statutory minimum, and corruption was structural. Anne Booth, in her careful survey The Indonesian Economy in the Nineteenth and Twentieth Centuries: A History of Missed Opportunities (1998), concludes that the system retarded the development of market institutions and private capital in Java and created a pattern of state-mediated extraction that would recur in later periods.10

Criticism mounted in the Netherlands itself. Eduard Douwes Dekker's novel Max Havelaar (1860), written under the pseudonym Multatuli, exposed the exploitation and cruelty of the system to European public opinion, becoming one of the most politically effective novels of the nineteenth century. Partly in response, the Cultuurstelsel was progressively phased out in the 1860s and finally abolished by the Agrarian Law of 1870, which opened Java to private capital while formally prohibiting the alienation of adat (customary) land from indigenous communities.

The Liberal era that followed (c. 1870–1940) did not end extraction; it privatised it. The Agrarian Law of 1870 allowed European entrepreneurs to lease land from the colonial government on long-term contracts, underpinning the rise of large private plantations — first in Java (sugar, tea) and then explosively in Sumatra and Borneo. Deli, in East Sumatra, became the world's premier tobacco-growing region in the 1880s, worked by indentured labourers from Java and China under the infamous koeli ordonnantie (coolie ordinance), which criminalised breach of contract. By the early twentieth century, Sumatra was also the centre of the rubber industry, which expanded dramatically after the automobile-driven surge in global rubber demand after 1900. Royal Dutch Shell's discovery of petroleum in East Kalimantan in 1885 and the subsequent explosive growth of oil production made petroleum the archipelago's most valuable export by the 1930s; it would remain so for most of the twentieth century.11

Van Zanden and Marks's reconstruction of colonial-era GDP per capita, anchored in Maddison-type purchasing-power estimates, provides the most rigorous long-run measurement of this period. In 1900, Indonesia's GDP per capita stood at approximately $745 in 1990 international dollars — comparable to other Asian colonial economies but less than two-thirds of Japan's $1,180. The figure edged only modestly upward to around $840 by 1950, after a century of intensive export growth. The contrast with Japan, which by 1950 had reached approximately $1,926, illustrates what Booth calls the "missed opportunity": despite being the world's dominant producer of several global commodities, colonial Indonesia's population captured little of the gains.12

Cultuurstelsel — the "Cultivation System" imposed on Java from 1830 to approximately 1870 by Governor-General Johannes van den Bosch. Javanese peasant households were required to cultivate export crops (sugar, coffee, indigo) on one-fifth of their land or to perform sixty-six days of corvée labour annually. The system generated enormous revenues for the Dutch treasury but caused famines and structural distortions in Javanese agriculture. Phased out after the Agrarian Law of 1870, though its legacy of state-mediated extraction persisted in colonial and post-colonial policy.

The Great Debates: Dual Economy, Agricultural Involution, and Their Critics

No aspect of Indonesian economic history has generated more scholarly controversy than the question of what colonialism did to the indigenous economy — and in particular to the dense, wet-rice society of Java. Two intellectual frameworks, both enormously influential and both extensively criticised, have dominated the debate: J.H. Boeke's theory of the "dual economy" and Clifford Geertz's theory of "agricultural involution."

Julius Herman Boeke (1884–1956) was a Dutch colonial economist whose 1910 Leiden doctoral dissertation, later elaborated in a series of publications culminating in the 1953 volume Economics and Economic Policy of Dual Societies as Exemplified by Indonesia, proposed that colonial Indonesia contained not one but two fundamentally incompatible economic systems.13 The "western" sector — plantations, trading houses, mining — operated according to classical economic laws: price signals elicited supply responses, profit motives drove capital allocation, and development policies borrowed from European experience could be applied. The "eastern" or "pre-capitalistic" sector — the Javanese peasant village — operated under different logic altogether: the profit motive was weak or absent; desire for income beyond subsistence needs was limited; and attempts to introduce western institutional forms (savings banks, cooperatives, technical advice) produced negligible results because the cultural and psychological substrate was different. Boeke called this latter sector "social dualism" rooted in "oriental" attitudes toward work and wealth.

The critics were numerous and pointed. The American economist Benjamin Higgins, writing in the journal Economic Development and Cultural Change in January 1956, argued that Boeke's supposedly different "eastern economics" was not an expression of cultural difference but a rational response to technological and factor-price conditions.14 Where Boeke saw cultural immobility, Higgins and later economists saw the perfectly rational behaviour of people operating under specific constraints — lack of capital, insecure property rights, absence of reliable credit, asymmetric market power. The peasant who appeared not to respond to price incentives was frequently doing something more complex: managing risk, investing in social insurance through community reciprocity, or simply lacking access to inputs that would make intensification profitable. Boeke's framing, critics argued, served colonial apologetics: it naturalized the gap between European and Indonesian sectors as a product of "culture" rather than of deliberate extraction.

Clifford Geertz's Agricultural Involution: The Processes of Ecological Change in Indonesia (1963) operated on a different register but arrived at related conclusions.15 Geertz argued that the Javanese wet-rice agricultural system had, under the pressure of colonial policies and population growth, undergone a distinctive process he termed "involution" — not development or stagnation but a kind of internal elaboration without transformation. The Cultuurstelsel, in Geertz's reading, had forced Java to absorb its growing population into ever more labour-intensive rice cultivation while the Outer Islands were developed for export, creating a structural divide that he linked to the modern dual economy. Javanese village society responded to this pressure not by technological change or capital accumulation but by increasingly complex social arrangements — labour sharing, community feast obligations, subdivided land rights — that spread poverty more equally rather than eliminating it. Geertz called this "shared poverty."

The Geertz thesis was enormously influential — it became, as Touwen notes in his authoritative survey, almost obligatory background for any discussion of Javanese rural poverty — but it has been largely discredited empirically.16 Researchers in the 1970s and 1980s demonstrated that non-farm employment and household purchasing power had in fact increased in nineteenth-century Java, that the link between Cultivation System obligations and famine was more complex than Geertz allowed, and that the thesis rested on imprecise historical evidence and a romanticised view of pre-colonial village society. Geertz himself, in a later reflection, acknowledged that his model was too static. Yet the core question he raised — why did Java's population growth not generate a Malthusian-style response of technical innovation? — remains live and contested.

The most systematic quantitative intervention in these debates is Jan Luiten van Zanden and Daan Marks's An Economic History of Indonesia, 1800–2010 (2012), which constructs GDP estimates, wage series, welfare indicators, and market integration measures for the entire two-century span. Their data series, drawing on Maddison Project methodology, reveals a picture more nuanced than either the "exploitation only" or the "beneficial colonialism" narratives. Colonial rule generated growth in aggregate output but distributed its gains overwhelmingly to the metropolitan treasury and European and Chinese commercial intermediaries; indigenous Indonesian income per capita barely moved between 1830 and 1900. The 1900s "Ethical Policy" era produced somewhat wider welfare gains — greater access to education, modest rises in real wages — but the structural divide between the export enclave and the peasant economy persisted.17

agricultural involution — Clifford Geertz's concept (1963) for the process by which Javanese wet-rice agriculture absorbed a growing population through increasing social complexity and labour intensity rather than through technological change or capital accumulation, producing what Geertz called "shared poverty." Widely cited as a framework for understanding Javanese rural stagnation under colonial rule, though the thesis has been substantially criticised and largely superseded empirically.

Chinese Commercial Networks in the Long Run

Across every era of Indonesian economic history — from the Srivijayan port world to the New Order oligarchy — Chinese commercial networks have occupied a structural position that no account of the economy can omit. The full social and cultural history of Chinese Indonesians is treated separately in Chapter 47; here the focus is on their economic function.

Chinese maritime traders reached the archipelago's ports no later than the Song dynasty (960–1279), and Chinese settlement around the north Javanese pasisir ports was well established by the fifteenth century. Under the VOC and colonial state, Chinese entrepreneurs were deployed as tax farmers, opium concessionaires, toll collectors, and market operators — a system that deliberately inserted an ethnic intermediary layer between the colonial state and the indigenous population. The tax farm arrangement was especially consequential: Chinese entrepreneurs bid for the right to collect land rents and market fees, paid the colonial government in advance, and recovered their investment through sometimes aggressive collection from Javanese villagers. This arrangement generated revenue for the colonial state while insulating Dutch officials from the daily business of extraction — but it also concentrated commercial capital in Chinese hands and structured market relationships along ethnic lines that would prove remarkably durable.

The colonial sociologist Boeke himself recognised that the "dual economy" was not simply a European-indigenous binary but a tripartite structure, with Chinese entrepreneurs occupying the critical intermediary zone. Anne Booth's work emphasises that ethnic stratification in access to capital, credit, and market opportunities was a defining feature of the early twentieth-century colonial economy, persisting through and beyond independence.18 Richard Robison's analysis of the New Order, Indonesia: The Rise of Capital (1986), shows how the cukong arrangement — in which ethnic-Chinese businessmen provided capital and management to politically connected military and bureaucratic patrons in exchange for licences, concessions, and protection — became the dominant form of capital accumulation in the 1970s and 1980s.19 The very success of the New Order's economic programme was inseparable from this Chinese-military business nexus, which also generated the endemic corruption and economic concentration that contributed to the 1997–98 collapse.

cukong — (from Hokkien for "boss") a term for the ethnically Chinese Indonesian businessman who provided capital and managerial expertise to a politically connected military or government patron, receiving in return licences, concessions, and protection. The cukong system was central to the New Order political economy of the 1970s–90s.

Economic Nationalism and the Guided Economy (1945–1966)

Independence, proclaimed on 17 August 1945 and secured through four years of revolution, presented Indonesia's leadership with an economy shaped by colonial extraction and devastated by war and occupation. The Round Table Conference agreement of November 1949, which formalised Dutch recognition of Indonesian sovereignty, came with a significant caveat: Indonesia agreed to assume the debts of the Netherlands East Indies — approximately 4.5 billion rupiah — and to leave Dutch enterprises operating in the country undisturbed, at least initially.20 The new republic therefore inherited colonial economic institutions without inheriting the capital to transform them.

The 1950s parliamentary period saw genuine attempts at economic restructuring under successive cabinets, including the Benteng programme (1950) that reserved certain import licences for indigenous (pribumi) entrepreneurs in an effort to build a national business class. The results were disappointing: many licences were sold to Chinese traders rather than used for production, "Ali-Baba" partnerships (indigenous fronts for Chinese capital) proliferated, and the state lacked the institutional capacity to enforce development priorities. GDP per capita grew at only approximately 1.0 percent annually between 1950 and 1965, barely keeping ahead of population growth.21

The political crisis over West New Guinea (West Papua) in 1957 triggered a decisive economic turning point. Sukarno nationalised Dutch enterprises — which constituted the bulk of the modern colonial economy: plantations, banks, shipping, public utilities — in December 1957, transferring them to state or military management without adequate compensation or management capacity. The economic consequences were severe: skilled Dutch technicians and managers departed; capital fled; export revenues from the affected plantations collapsed. The "Guided Economy" that Sukarno proclaimed as the economic counterpart of Guided Democracy emphasised self-sufficiency, state direction, and the subordination of economic policy to political goals — including Konfrontasi, the armed confrontation with Malaysia that drained resources from 1963 to 1966.

The macroeconomic results were catastrophic. Deficit financing of the government budget produced hyperinflation: Jakarta's cost-of-living index rose approximately 650 percent in the final years of the Sukarno era. Foreign exchange reserves, which stood at $326 million in 1960, had fallen to $8.6 million by 1965 — insufficient to cover even a week of essential imports.22 Export earnings from rubber and petroleum were eroding as infrastructure deteriorated and investment dried up. Factories operated at minimal capacity for lack of spare parts. The comparison with Sukarno's own rhetoric — the Revolusi was to deliver social justice and national dignity — could not have been starker. By 1965, Indonesia was widely assessed in international financial circles as the most intractable economic problem in Asia, a country whose population was being progressively impoverished by the collision of anti-colonial ideology with economic reality.

The mass killings of 1965–66 and Suharto's seizure of power were not merely a political event; they were the precondition for a radical economic reorientation. The human cost of that transition — estimated at between five hundred thousand and one million dead — must be held in the reader's mind as the context in which the subsequent economic "miracle" was made possible.

The New Order "Miracle": Oil, Rice, and Growth (1966–1997)

The economic stabilisation that Suharto's New Order (Orde Baru) achieved in the late 1960s was rapid and, by any historical standard, dramatic. The team of U.S.-trained economists — led by Widjojo Nitisastro, Ali Wardhana, and Emil Salim, known informally as the "Berkeley Mafia" — applied orthodox macroeconomic stabilisation: tight fiscal policy, foreign exchange reform, and debt rescheduling through the IGGI (Inter-Governmental Group on Indonesia) creditor consortium. By 1969, inflation had been brought under control and GDP was growing at 7–8 percent per year.23 The Foreign Capital Investment Law of January 1967 opened the economy to foreign direct investment for the first time since the nationalisation wave of 1957–58.

The OPEC oil embargo of 1973–74 transformed the New Order's fiscal position overnight. Oil revenues — largely derived from production sharing contracts with foreign companies, channelled through the state oil company Pertamina — surged from a modest contributor to the dominant source of government income. At peak, in 1981, oil and gas revenues accounted for over 62 percent of total government income.24 This windfall financed the New Order's ambitious development agenda: the five-year REPELITA plans (inaugurated in 1969) built roads, irrigation systems, rural health centres, and schools. The Green Revolution — implemented through the BIMAS (Bimbingan Massal) programme, which delivered high-yielding rice varieties, subsidised fertiliser, and supervised credit to millions of smallholders — achieved what had seemed impossibly ambitious when the programme was designed: Indonesia declared self-sufficiency in rice in 1984 and received the FAO's Gold Medal the following year.

The poverty headcount tells the aggregate story. By most estimates, approximately 60 percent of Indonesians lived below the poverty line in 1970; by 1996, the official figure had fallen below 11 percent. GDP per capita, which stood at barely $748 in 1966 (in 1990 purchasing-power terms), had reached $2,516 by 1990 and continued rising toward the crisis.25 This growth rate — approximately 4.4 percent per capita per year across three decades — sustained a genuine transformation of the material conditions of Indonesian life: rising literacy, falling infant mortality, expanding urban middle class, and a manufacturing sector that diversified well beyond the resource base.

Thee Kian Wie's careful assessment of the New Order's economic performance identifies the key turning point in the mid-1980s, when the fall of oil prices (1982–83) forced a structural adjustment. Unlike the petro-states of the Middle East or the resource-curse narratives from Africa, Indonesia's government accepted sharp currency devaluations in 1978, 1983, and 1986, and liberalised its trade and investment regimes in the so-called deregulation packages of 1983–88. These measures made Indonesian manufacturing internationally competitive; by 1991, the manufacturing sector's contribution to GDP exceeded that of agriculture for the first time, and labour-intensive exports — garments, footwear, electronics — became major earners.26

How did Indonesia avoid the "resource curse" that has afflicted so many commodity exporters? Hal Hill and Reza Pasaribu, surveying the evidence, conclude that several factors combined: fiscal conservatism that prevented Dutch-disease-level currency appreciation; deliberate investment of oil revenues in traded-goods sectors (agriculture, manufacturing) rather than consumption or white-elephant prestige projects; and successive devaluations that maintained export competitiveness.27 The Berkeley Mafia's technocratic insulation from political pressures — at least until the late 1980s, when the Suharto family's business interests became more assertive — provided a measure of policy coherence unusual in developing-country governance.

Yet the New Order's economic model carried deep structural pathologies. Robison's foundational study documents how capital accumulation under Suharto was inseparable from political connection: timber concessions (HPH), import monopolies, bank licences, and infrastructure contracts flowed to a small circle of conglomerates linked to the Suharto family and to military-business partnerships. The Salim Group, the Suharto family holdings, the Bob Hasan timber empire — these were not simply market successes but artefacts of a rent-distribution system that imposed large efficiency costs on the economy as a whole.28 The World Bank's East Asian Miracle report (1993), which praised Indonesia as one of the region's "high performing" economies, acknowledged the distortions but concluded that the state had broadly co-ordinated markets effectively.29 This assessment would look considerably more optimistic than circumstances warranted within five years.

The 1997–98 Crisis and Its Aftermath

The Asian financial crisis arrived in Indonesia as contagion. On 2 July 1997, Thailand's baht collapsed under the weight of a current-account deficit and a speculative attack on its quasi-fixed exchange rate. Within six weeks, currency pressure spread to the region; on 14 August 1997, Indonesia floated the rupiah. What followed was the worst economic contraction Indonesia had experienced since the Japanese occupation. The rupiah fell from approximately 2,700 to the US dollar at the time of the float to nearly 16,000 per dollar by January 1998 — a depreciation of roughly 85 percent. GDP contracted by more than 13 percent in 1998, a larger fall than in any other affected Asian economy. The banking sector, which had expanded rapidly and recklessly after the 1988 deregulation, collapsed: the cost of recapitalising it exceeded 50 percent of annual GDP, the most expensive banking rescue in history to that point.30

The structural vulnerabilities that amplified the shock were precisely those Robison and other political economists had identified: a quasi-fixed exchange rate that had allowed short-term foreign debt to accumulate without a hedging market; a financial sector whose rapid post-1988 growth had outrun regulatory capacity; and a political system so saturated with cronyism that credible policy responses became impossible — every institutional reform that the IMF demanded impinged on the interests of some Suharto-connected conglomerate or family holding. The IMF's $43-billion stabilisation package, conditioned on bank closures, subsidy removal, and structural reforms, was signed in November 1997 but immediately undermined when Suharto signed a budget in January 1998 that flouted its terms. By May 1998, mass protests, student occupations, and the Jakarta riots had made his position untenable, and on 21 May 1998 he resigned after thirty-two years in power — ending the New Order and beginning the era of Reformasi.31

Manuel Montes, analysing the crisis in retrospect, argues that it was not simply a product of external contagion but of deep domestic structural failures: the "miracle" had been built on volatile commodity revenues, under-regulated capital flows, and a political economy in which growth and corruption were structurally intertwined.32 The distributional consequences were severe: the poverty headcount, which had fallen to around 11 percent in 1996, approximately doubled in 1998. Urban workers lost jobs; rural farmers, whose incomes were partly insulated by depreciation-induced increases in cash-crop prices, were relatively better placed — illustrating the persistent dual-economy structure in which the rural sector functions as a buffer during urban-sector crises.

Recovery, when it came, was slower than in Thailand and Korea. Indonesia's weak institutions, unresolved banking sector problems, and the political turbulence of the democratic transition (three presidents between 1999 and 2004) all constrained the rebound. It was the 2003–2004 commodity super-cycle — driven by Chinese demand for raw materials — that restored growth momentum, not so much through productivity growth as through the now-familiar engine of rising commodity prices.

The Twenty-First-Century Commodity Economy: Palm Oil, Coal, Nickel, and Beyond

In the 2010s and 2020s, Indonesia's export profile presents a striking structural echo of the colonial era: the economy's hard-currency earnings remain dominated by raw and semi-processed natural commodities, extracted from the Outer Islands and shipped primarily to East Asian markets. The commodities have changed — palm oil, coal, and nickel have replaced nutmeg, coffee, and petroleum — but the underlying political economy of extraction, state mediation, and rent distribution shows recognisable continuities.

Palm oil is the single most economically significant. Indonesia overtook Malaysia as the world's largest producer in the early 2000s, and by 2023 was producing approximately 47 million tonnes of crude palm oil annually — more than twice Malaysia's output — accounting for 54 percent of global exports.33 The industry directly and indirectly employs over 16 million people and contributes approximately 4.5 percent of GDP. Export revenues from palm oil and derivatives reached around $33 billion in 2023. But the costs are severe: the conversion of Kalimantan and Sumatra forests to oil-palm monoculture has been one of the fastest rates of tropical deforestation recorded anywhere, with massive implications for biodiversity, carbon emissions, and the livelihoods of forest-dependent communities. The European Union's Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), which restricts imports linked to deforestation, has become a major trade-policy flashpoint.

Coal has risen even more dramatically as a revenue source. Indonesia became the world's largest exporter of thermal coal in the early 2010s, and in 2023 exported a record 508 million tonnes — approximately 34 percent of global seaborne thermal coal trade — generating approximately $34.6 billion in export revenue.34 The primary markets are China and India, both of which have driven demand well beyond what the global energy transition has reduced so far. Indonesia's own electricity system still relies heavily on domestic coal, creating a tension between climate commitments and energy-security interests that successive administrations have managed by deferring decarbonisation.

The most analytically novel dimension of Indonesia's twenty-first-century commodity strategy is the nickel hilirisasi (downstreaming) policy. Indonesia holds approximately 24 percent of the world's known nickel reserves, concentrated in North Sulawesi, Central Sulawesi (Morowali), and the North Maluku islands. For most of the twentieth century, nickel ore was exported raw — to China principally, where it was processed into ferronickel, nickel pig iron, and ultimately stainless steel. Recognising the enormous value being created outside Indonesian territory, President Jokowi's government enacted a ban on raw nickel ore exports that took effect in January 2020. The subsequent surge in downstream investment — primarily from Chinese companies, who moved smelting and processing operations to Indonesian industrial parks to retain access to the ore — transformed the sector: the value of nickel-related exports rose, by Jokowi's own reckoning, from 17 trillion rupiah before the ban to over 510 trillion rupiah by 2022.35 By 2022, Indonesia accounted for approximately 58 percent of global nickel mine production, and the industrial parks of Morowali and the Weda Bay cluster had become key nodes in the global supply chain for electric-vehicle batteries.

The hilirisasi strategy has attracted both admirers and critics. Admirers note that it represents a genuine departure from raw-commodity export passivity: unlike the oil and palm oil sectors, which for decades generated large revenues without deep domestic value chains, nickel hilirisasi is building a processing industry and attracting manufacturing investment. Critics note that the beneficiaries of Chinese investment in the nickel parks have often been Chinese state-owned or state-linked enterprises rather than Indonesian firms; that labour in the parks is often imported from China; that environmental and labour standards are contested; and that the EU challenged Indonesia's export ban before the World Trade Organization (WTO), winning a provisional ruling in 2022 that Indonesia subsequently appealed.36 The hilirisasi policy, in other words, is itself a contested political economy: it is an attempt to use state power to capture more of the rent from a natural endowment, echoing — at a higher level of industrial sophistication — the logic of the VOC's spice monopoly and the colonial government's Cultuurstelsel.

The broader structural questions about Indonesia's long-run development trajectory converge on two debates that directly echo the earlier historiographic controversies. The first is the resource curse. Indonesia's partial success in avoiding Dutch disease in the 1970s–80s has not insulated it from commodity-sector volatility: the 2014–16 decline in coal and palm oil prices produced a growth deceleration, and the nickel boom risks replicating the oil-sector pattern of large revenues with insufficient spillovers to the rest of the economy. Hill and Pasaribu's assessment (2022) is cautiously optimistic — Indonesia's fiscal institutions are more robust than in the 1970s, and the manufacturing base is more diversified — but they note that governance risks remain significant: the nickel industrial parks operate in a regulatory environment where environmental enforcement is weak and labour rights are contested.

The second debate is the middle-income trap. Indonesia graduated to lower-middle-income status in 1993 and to upper-middle-income status by 2023 (World Bank GNI per capita $4,870). The high-income threshold sits at around $13,845 (2023 World Bank classification). The Asian Development Bank's 2024 report, Development Miracle or Middle-Income Trap?, identifies Indonesia as at risk of stagnating in the middle-income band unless it achieves significant productivity growth through education, research and development, and institutional reform — precisely the reforms that commodity booms tend to defer by keeping growth buoyant without demanding structural transformation.37

The digital economy represents the most plausible engine of structural transformation outside the commodity sector. Gojek (founded 2010) and Tokopedia (founded 2009, merged with Gojek into GoTo Group in 2021 and subsequently aligned with TikTok) are genuinely globally competitive digital platforms, built on the foundation of Indonesia's large, young, increasingly connected population. Measured by GMV, Indonesia's digital economy is among the largest in Southeast Asia. But services — even highly productive digital services — cannot by themselves produce the manufacturing-led industrialisation that lifted Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China through the middle-income range. Whether Indonesia can combine commodity rent capture (through hilirisasi) with digital-economy dynamism while managing environmental costs and distributional pressures remains the central economic question of the twenty-first century — and one that will look uncomfortably familiar to anyone who has followed the entire arc traced in this chapter.

hilirisasi — the Indonesian government policy of "downstreaming": prohibiting or taxing exports of raw mineral commodities to force value-added processing to occur domestically, thereby capturing industrial rents rather than exporting unprocessed ore. Most prominently applied to nickel (export ban from January 2020) and bauxite (from June 2023). The policy is both an economic development strategy and a geopolitical assertion of resource sovereignty.

Summary

The Indonesian economy has been shaped, across five centuries, by a structural constant: an abundance of globally demanded natural commodities — spices, coffee, sugar, rubber, petroleum, palm oil, coal, nickel — that have funded state revenues, attracted foreign extraction, and resisted translation into broad-based prosperity. The Portuguese and Dutch companies that arrived seeking nutmeg and cloves established the logic of monopoly extraction, institutionalised in the VOC and the Cultuurstelsel. Colonial-era scholars debated how this extraction was internalised by indigenous society: J.H. Boeke's "dual economy" thesis attributed the divide between modern and traditional sectors to cultural incommensurability; Clifford Geertz's "agricultural involution" located it in the ecological divergence between a rice-intensifying Java and an export-crop-developing Outer Islands — both theses generating important critiques. Van Zanden and Marks's long-run GDP series reveals the colonial era's central fact: despite generating enormous revenues, colonialism left Indonesian per capita incomes barely above subsistence by 1950. The Sukarno era squandered even the limited institutional inheritance through hyperinflation and nationalisation; the New Order's Berkeley Mafia stabilised the economy and presided over three decades of rapid growth, funded by oil, transformed by the Green Revolution, and diversified through manufacturing — yet embedded in a cronyist political economy that collapsed catastrophically in 1997–98. The twenty-first century has brought a new commodity super-cycle: palm oil, coal, and nickel have restored export revenues, and the hilirisasi strategy attempts to capture more of the manufacturing rent chain. Whether Indonesia can escape the middle-income trap by converting commodity wealth into industrial capability and human-capital investment is the long-run question that economic history, from the Banda nutmeg gardens to the Morowali nickel smelters, has never ceased to pose.

Review & Discussion Questions

46.1 How does the "emporium" or "port polity" model of political economy differ from the agrarian-state model? What were the structural advantages and vulnerabilities of each in the context of the Indonesian archipelago?
46.2 Compare the VOC's monopoly extraction model with the Dutch colonial state's Cultuurstelsel. In what ways did each system extract value, and how did each distribute costs across the indigenous population?
46.3 Evaluate J.H. Boeke's "dual economy" thesis and its critics. Was the divide between the modern and traditional sectors in colonial Indonesia primarily a product of cultural difference, structural economic conditions, or deliberate colonial policy?
46.4 Clifford Geertz argued that the Cultuurstelsel caused Java to "involute" while the Outer Islands developed. How does van Zanden and Marks's long-run GDP data either support or complicate this argument?
46.5 How did Suharto's New Order avoid the "resource curse" during the 1970s oil boom, and to what extent did its later political economy — cronyism, conglomerate formation, financial deregulation — ultimately produce the conditions for the 1997–98 collapse?
46.6 The 1997–98 Asian financial crisis devastated Indonesia more severely than Thailand or South Korea. What structural features of the Indonesian economy and political economy explain this outcome?
46.7 Jokowi's hilirisasi nickel policy has been described as both a development success and a continuation of the colonial "extractive state" in new form. Evaluate both characterisations.
46.8 Anne Booth calls Indonesia's economic history "a history of missed opportunities." Drawing on evidence from multiple eras, assess whether this verdict is fair.
