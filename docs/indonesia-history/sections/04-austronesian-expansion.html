Learning Objectives

  • Explain the Austronesian language family — its geographic scope, internal structure, and what the distribution of languages tells us about human migration.
  • Evaluate the "Out of Taiwan" model proposed by Peter Bellwood and Robert Blust, including its linguistic and archaeological foundations and the ways rival hypotheses have challenged it.
  • Trace the routes and approximate timeline by which Austronesian-speaking peoples settled the Indonesian archipelago from around 2000 BCE onward.
  • Describe the outrigger canoe and the navigational techniques that made Austronesian oceanic expansion possible.
  • Identify the "Neolithic package" — rice cultivation, pottery, domesticated animals — and the Sa Huỳnh–Kalanay pottery tradition as markers of Austronesian settlement.
  • Survey the megalithic traditions of Nias, the Pasemah plateau, and Sulawesi, and assess what they reveal about early social organization and ancestor veneration.
  • Account for the spread of Dong Son bronze culture into the archipelago and its role in prestige economies and long-distance trade.
  • Synthesize genetic and linguistic evidence on early Austronesian social structure, including debates over kinship systems and the "house societies" model.

Roughly four thousand years ago, the Indonesian archipelago underwent its most consequential demographic transformation. Speakers of languages belonging to the Austronesian family — a vast linguistic world stretching today from Madagascar to Easter Island and from Taiwan to New Zealand — arrived from the north, paddling outrigger canoes stocked with rice seed, domesticated pigs, and red-slipped pottery. They did not find an empty landscape: ancient Australo-Papuan peoples had inhabited the islands for tens of thousands of years. But the newcomers brought a Neolithic economy of farming and animal husbandry that, combined with superior maritime technology, allowed them to settle and eventually to dominate the archipelago. What followed was not simple replacement but a layered, still-debated process of migration, admixture, and cultural transformation. Its traces survive in language, genetics, the megaliths that punctuate the hills of Nias and South Sumatra, and the bronze drums that circulated across the islands as markers of power and prestige.

The Austronesian World

The Austronesian language family is, by number of languages, one of the largest in the world. Its roughly 1,200 attested members span an enormous arc — from the Malagasy spoken on Madagascar, off the east coast of Africa, eastward across the Indian Ocean, through every major island group of Southeast Asia and the Pacific, to Rapa Nui (Easter Island), the last and most remote inhabited landfall in the Pacific, some 27,000 kilometres from the family's putative homeland in Taiwan.1 The linguist Robert Blust, whose half-century of fieldwork made him the foremost authority on Austronesian comparative grammar before his death in 2022, called the Austronesian expansion "one of the greatest chapters in human prehistory."2 The claim is not hyperbole. No pre-modern people dispersed themselves so widely across the globe using human muscle, wood, and woven sail.

For Indonesia, the Austronesian family is not merely a backdrop — it is the living skeleton of the nation. Of Indonesia's roughly 700 spoken languages, the vast majority are Austronesian, including Javanese, Sundanese, Madurese, Malay (the direct ancestor of Indonesian), Balinese, Buginese, and Acehnese, along with hundreds of smaller tongues from Aceh to Maluku and the Nusa Tenggara islands. Only the non-Austronesian languages of western New Guinea (Papua province) and a handful of languages in eastern Maluku — Ternate, Tidore, and several of their neighbours — stand outside this family. Understanding the Austronesian expansion is, therefore, to understand the deep linguistic foundations of Indonesian identity.

The internal structure of the family offers the first clue to its history. The Formosan languages — the indigenous languages of Taiwan, of which twenty or twenty-five survived into historical times and most are now extinct or endangered — are not a single branch but occupy every primary branching point of the Austronesian family tree that is not Malayo-Polynesian. That is: all the diversity of the Formosan languages taken together is at least as great as the diversity of the entire Malayo-Polynesian branch, which encompasses every Austronesian language spoken outside Taiwan — from Malagasy to Hawaiian, Tagalog to Maori, and Malay to Fijian.3 This pattern — greatest diversity at the smallest geographic point — is precisely what evolutionary and historical linguists expect at an origin. A language family diversifies most at the place where it has been diversifying longest.

Out of Taiwan: Bellwood, Blust, and the Linguistic Evidence

The model that the Austronesian-speaking peoples originated in Taiwan and dispersed southward in a series of rapid migrations beginning around 3500–3000 BCE is most closely associated with two scholars: the archaeologist Peter Bellwood of the Australian National University and the linguist Robert Blust of the University of Hawai'i. Though their disciplinary approaches differed, they arrived at convergent conclusions that have shaped the field for four decades.

Blust's contribution was fundamentally linguistic. By systematically comparing vocabulary and grammar across hundreds of Austronesian languages, he reconstructed Proto-Austronesian (PAn) — the hypothetical common ancestor of all Austronesian languages, spoken in Taiwan around 4000–3500 BCE.4 What emerged from this reconstruction was a vocabulary thick with agricultural and maritime terms: PAn possessed at least three distinct words for rice at different stages of processing, a word for the domesticated pig, a word for the chicken, and directional vocabulary organized around the axes of "inland or upstream" versus "seaward or downstream" — a spatial orientation consistent with a population living along a river-fed coastal plain, not on open ocean islands.5 The reconstructed vocabulary, in Blust's argument, showed that the people who spoke PAn were already rice farmers and pig-keepers before the expansion began. They were not nomadic foragers who picked up agriculture as they moved; they were agriculturalists whose food surplus and farming skill underpinned the entire enterprise.

Bellwood approached the same question from archaeology. His landmark work, Prehistory of the Indo-Malaysian Archipelago, traced the material culture of Austronesian expansion through characteristic artifact assemblages: polished stone adzes (axes), red-slipped pottery, jade ornaments of Taiwanese origin, and the skeletal markers of a rice-farming lifestyle.6 Bellwood argued for what he called the "Early Farming Dispersal Hypothesis": that across many language families and on multiple continents, the expansion of agriculture was driven by population growth among farming communities, which outcompeted or absorbed surrounding foragers. The Austronesian case was, for Bellwood, the maritime version of the same process that had spread Indo-European languages with wheat farmers across Europe and Dravidian languages with millet farmers across South Asia.7 The chain of cause and effect ran: agricultural surplus → population growth → colonization of new territories → spread of the ancestral language.

The Taiwan homeland is supported by the convergence of multiple independent lines of evidence. Linguistically, the depth of divergence among Formosan languages confirms Taiwan as the point of deepest antiquity. Archaeologically, excavated sites in the Batanes Islands between Taiwan and the Philippines, dated to around 2200 BCE, contain pottery whose designs are closely matched to Taiwanese Neolithic assemblages and include nephrite (jade) ornaments that can be traced by geochemical analysis to Taiwan's Hualien source — material proof of southward movement.8 Genetically, as discussed below, ancient and modern DNA studies have consistently shown that all Austronesian-speaking populations outside Taiwan share a distinctive ancestry component most closely related to the aboriginal peoples of Taiwan.

Challenging the Consensus: Alternative Models and Ongoing Debates

The Out of Taiwan model, though dominant, has not gone uncontested. Several alternatives have been proposed, and the debates between them have sharpened the scholarly understanding of what is and is not established.

The most prominent challenge was the "Out of Sundaland" hypothesis, articulated by the British geneticist Stephen Oppenheimer and the geneticist Martin Richards around the turn of the millennium, based primarily on patterns in mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) across Southeast Asian populations.9 Oppenheimer argued that during the Last Glacial Maximum (c. 26,000–19,000 BP), a large land mass called Sundaland united what are now the islands of Sumatra, Java, Borneo, and the Thai-Malay peninsula into a single continental shelf. This was, he proposed, not only a refuge for human populations but the homeland of the ancestors of the Austronesian-speaking peoples. As sea levels rose catastrophically at the end of the Pleistocene — inundating Sundaland over several thousand years — coastal populations were displaced outward, dispersing with them the ancestral Austronesian languages.

The Sundaland hypothesis was compelling as narrative — the "drowned homeland" has an obvious romantic and explanatory power — but it has not withstood subsequent scrutiny. The mtDNA patterns Oppenheimer and Richards cited could be explained as in-situ drift in Southeast Asian populations rather than as evidence of an origin point. More decisively, the genetic studies of the 2010s and 2020s — particularly Lipson et al.'s 2014 analysis of modern Island Southeast Asian populations — showed that all sampled Austronesian groups harbor ancestry "more closely related to aboriginal Taiwanese than to any present-day mainland population," directly contradicting the Sundaland thesis's prediction that Southeast Asian populations should be the most archaic.10 The Out of Sundaland model retains a small number of proponents but is not accepted by mainstream archaeology or historical linguistics.

Other challenges have come from within the Out of Taiwan paradigm itself. The "Slow Boat" model, associated with geneticists Kayser, Kivisild, and colleagues, accepts the Taiwanese origin but argues that the expansion was much slower and involved far more admixture with resident Melanesian and Papuan-related populations than the "Express Train" model (a term coined by the evolutionary biologist Jared Diamond) allowed.11 The Express Train metaphor — a rapid, undiluted sweep from Taiwan to Polynesia — is now widely considered oversimplified. The Slow Boat model better fits the genetic reality of Island Southeast Asia, where Austronesian-speaking populations in western Indonesia show substantial admixture with earlier Australo-Papuan groups, and where the genetic signal of the original Taiwanese migration becomes increasingly diluted as one moves westward. The process of Austronesian settlement in the Indonesian archipelago was not conquest and replacement but a complex and prolonged encounter over many generations.

There is also a productive debate about what drove the expansion. Bellwood's agricultural-determinism thesis emphasizes surplus and population pressure, but it has been challenged by scholars who point out that in Island Southeast Asia, the Austronesian "Neolithic package" arrived in environments already supporting foraging populations who had been there for tens of thousands of years — and the transition to farming was gradual, not instantaneous. The expansion may have been as much a network of maritime exchange and elite emulation as a demographic flood.

Into the Archipelago: Routes, Timelines, and Sites

The broad outline of the Austronesian migration into the Indonesian archipelago is now reasonably well established, even if many details remain contested. The movement proceeded in several overlapping pulses, following two main corridors: a western route through the Philippines, then along the coast of Borneo and southward into Sumatra, Java, and the Malay Peninsula; and an eastern route through the Philippines into Sulawesi, Maluku, and the Lesser Sunda Islands, and from there simultaneously eastward into the Pacific (where it gave rise to the Lapita culture and ultimately to Polynesia) and westward across the archipelago.

The earliest firm dates for Austronesian-speaking peoples in the Philippines come from the Batanes Islands, where Neolithic sites with Taiwanese-affiliated pottery are dated to around 2200 BCE.12 By 3000–2500 BCE, archaeological evidence indicates Austronesian groups had entered Borneo: the Bukit Tengkorak site in Sabah contains obsidian whose geochemical signature traces back to a source in the Admiralty Islands, over 3,500 kilometres away, demonstrating that long-distance maritime exchange networks were already operating in the region by this date.13 Austronesian settlement of Sulawesi and the Maluku islands followed closely, and by around 2000 BCE the movement had extended into the broader Indonesian archipelago — the date preserved in this book's master timeline.

Physical evidence for the encounter between arriving Austronesians and resident populations is provided by the Gua Harimau cave site in southeastern Sumatra, excavated between 2010 and 2014. Among the eighty-four skeletal remains recovered there, the pre-Neolithic burials (dated to roughly 5700–5600 cal BP) cluster morphologically with Australo-Papuan populations — the long-resident indigenous peoples of the pre-Austronesian era — while skeletal remains from the Metal Period (roughly 1860–1720 cal BP) show close affinity with modern Austronesian speakers from Taiwan, the Philippines, and Indonesia. Crucially, the Metal Period individuals' mitochondrial DNA includes haplogroup E1a1a — a well-established genetic marker of Austronesian dispersal — alongside earlier B4 variants and rare archaic haplogroups that signal pre-Austronesian ancestry.14 Gua Harimau is a stratified record of the "two-layer model": an ancient Australo-Papuan substrate overlain by an Austronesian-affiliated population that arrived in the Neolithic and Metal periods.

This picture became more complicated with the 2021 analysis of a 7,300-year-old female skeleton — nicknamed "Besse" by the research team — from Leang Panninge cave in South Sulawesi. Besse's genome did not fit neatly into either the expected Australo-Papuan or the Austronesian cluster. Instead, she represented a previously unknown population lineage that had been present in Wallacea since at least 50,000 BP, carried roughly 2.2 per cent Denisovan ancestry (consistent with archaic hominin admixture events known to have occurred in this region), and whose closest modern descendants are Papuans and Indigenous Australians — but whose genetic signature had no direct equivalent among any living population.15 "Besse" demonstrated that the human history of Wallacea before the Austronesian arrival was far more complex than the simple two-layer model suggested, and that populations of deep antiquity had achieved levels of genetic differentiation invisible in modern people's DNA.

Meanwhile, the eastern branch of the Austronesian expansion reached the Pacific. By 1600–1200 BCE the Lapita culture — distinguished by its finely worked dentate-stamped pottery — had spread from the Bismarck Archipelago (northeast of New Guinea) eastward through Melanesia to Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa, establishing the first human settlement of Remote Oceania.16 Ancient DNA analysis of Lapita individuals from burial sites in Vanuatu showed them to be "almost completely East Asian, with no detectable Papuan ancestry" — a genomic echo of the rapid push eastward out of Taiwan and through the Philippines, bypassing the densely inhabited main islands of eastern Indonesia and moving directly into the relatively open Pacific.17 From Tonga and Samoa, after a "long pause" of roughly two thousand years, Polynesian descendants of these Lapita people pressed further east to settle the Marquesas, Hawaiʻi, New Zealand, and Easter Island — completing the most extensive prehistoric maritime dispersal in human history.

Ships of the First Sailors: Austronesian Maritime Technology

The Austronesian expansion was made possible by a technology without parallel in the ancient world: the outrigger canoe. Scholars have called it "humanity's first true ocean-going ship," and the description is defensible.18 The basic problem of any canoe is stability: a narrow hull capsizes easily, especially in open water. The solution that Austronesian boat-builders discovered — attaching one or two additional floats (outriggers) to the hull by means of projecting booms — transformed the canoe from a sheltered-water craft into a vessel capable of crossing thousands of kilometres of open ocean. Single-outrigger canoes, dominant in Polynesia and Micronesia, provide stability on one side; double-outrigger canoes (effectively trimarans), which became the dominant form in Island Southeast Asia, allow the vessel to tack freely because neither side can capsize.19

The sailing technology was no less important than the hull. Austronesian boatbuilders developed several distinct sail forms, the most distinctively theirs being the "crab-claw" sail — a roughly triangular canvas with both its upper and lower edges supported by spars, forming a V-shape when viewed from the bow, with the apex of the V pointing downward. The crab-claw sail, which appears in the archaeological and ethnographic record by around 1500 BCE, is exceptionally efficient for sailing across the wind and can be reoriented rapidly, making it superior to a square sail in the variable winds of the Pacific and the Indonesian archipelago.20 A later development, the tanja sail — a rectangular sail with spars on both its upper and lower edges, set on a bipod or tripod mast — became the characteristic rig of the Indonesian world and is generally considered a precursor of the lateen sail that revolutionized navigation in the Indian Ocean.

Beyond hull and sail, Austronesian voyagers developed sophisticated systems of open-ocean navigation. Polynesian navigators — whose techniques are the best documented, partly through modern revival efforts in Hawaiʻi and the Carolines — used the rising and setting points of stars on the horizon (which remain constant throughout the year) as directional guides; read ocean swells for direction by lying in the hull of the canoe and feeling the roll through their bodies; observed the flight paths and species distribution of seabirds (a fairy tern, for example, never ranges more than about twenty to thirty nautical miles from land); and noted cloud formations above distant islands, which tend to form differently over land than over open water.21 Whether all of these techniques were employed this far west and this far back in time cannot be demonstrated from the Indonesian archaeological record, but the consistent ability of Austronesian peoples to reach and settle islands at the very edge of the navigable world argues for something like this navigational toolkit.

Outrigger canoe — a vessel in which one or two lateral floats (outriggers) are attached to the main hull by projecting booms, providing stability on open water. Developed by Austronesian-speaking peoples of Southeast Asia, outrigger technology enabled the colonization of the Pacific and Indian Ocean islands and spread beyond Austronesian communities to South Asia, East Africa, and Aboriginal Australia through maritime contact.

The Neolithic Package: Rice, Pottery, and Domestic Animals

The Austronesian migrants who moved into the Indonesian archipelago from around 2000 BCE were not blank-slate settlers. They carried with them what archaeologists call the "Neolithic package" — an ensemble of agricultural practices, domestic animals, material culture, and technologies that had been assembled over centuries in Taiwan and, before that, along the rivers and coasts of southern China and mainland Southeast Asia.22

The centrepiece of the package was rice. Oryza sativa was originally domesticated in the middle Yangtze River valley of China, probably between 8000 and 6000 BP (roughly 6000–4000 BCE), and spread southward through mainland Southeast Asia over the following two millennia. Micro-CT analysis of pottery sherds from Neolithic sites in the Vietnamese Mekong delta — An Son, Loc Giang, and Rach Nui — has identified domesticated rice grains trapped in clay fabric at dates ranging from about 4200 to 3265 cal BP (roughly 2250–1315 BCE), directly demonstrating that the Austronesian communities associated with these sites were rice cultivators.23 The diagnostic feature of domestication in the rice spikelet base — a "vertically asymmetric and deeply recessed" abscission scar — distinguishes these specimens from wild rice, confirming that the crop moving south with Austronesian migrants had already passed through thousands of years of selective cultivation in China.

Alongside rice, the Neolithic package included taro (Colocasia esculenta) and several species of Dioscorea yam — root crops that thrive in the wetter, lower-altitude environments where rice cultivation was difficult — as well as bananas, breadfruit, coconuts, and paper mulberry (used for barkcloth). The domesticated animals were the pig, the chicken, and the dog. The pig was probably the most socially important: in many later Austronesian societies across the archipelago, from the Batak of Sumatra to the peoples of Sulawesi, pigs were the primary medium of ceremonial exchange, bride-wealth payment, and feast-of-merit competition.24

The material culture signature most consistently associated with Austronesian Neolithic settlement is red-slipped pottery. Fired at moderate temperatures and finished with a distinctive red ochre slip before firing, this ceramic tradition was identified and named by the American archaeologist Wilhelm G. Solheim II in a foundational 1964 paper that called it the "Sa Huỳnh–Kalanay tradition" — after a site in central Vietnam and a cave in the Philippines, respectively.25 Solheim traced the distribution of this pottery across coastal Vietnam, the Philippines, Borneo, and the broader Indonesian world from roughly 500 BCE to 1500 CE, arguing that it represented an authentically Austronesian ceramic heritage in continuous development, not a single import event. The Sa Huỳnh culture of central Vietnam (flourishing c. 1000 BCE–200 CE), associated with large jar burials, distinctive penannular jade earrings called lingling-o, and locally worked iron, is now understood as a late and regionally specialized expression of this broader Austronesian Neolithic tradition, likely ancestral to the Cham people whose kingdom of Champa flourished in later centuries.26

Neolithic package — the ensemble of agricultural practices, domestic animals, and material culture introduced by Austronesian-speaking migrants into Island Southeast Asia from around 3500 BCE onward, including rice and root-crop cultivation, domesticated pigs, chickens, and dogs, polished stone adzes, and red-slipped pottery. The "package" metaphor acknowledges that these elements co-occurred consistently enough to suggest a shared cultural origin, though their relative importance varied by environment and community.

Stone and Memory: The Megalithic Traditions

Among the most dramatic material legacies of the early Austronesian world in Indonesia are its megalithic traditions — the widespread practice of erecting large stone monuments as markers of social rank, memorials to the dead, and conduits between the living and ancestral spirits. Though these traditions vary enormously by region, they share a cultural logic rooted in the Austronesian veneration of ancestors and the belief that the dead continue to exercise power over the living. They also share a long durability: some of Indonesia's megalithic traditions were still alive — in modified forms — well into the twentieth century.

Nias

The island of Nias, off the western coast of Sumatra, preserves one of the best-documented megalithic traditions in the archipelago. Stone monuments on Nias served as the material expression of a precisely graded system of noble rank and feast-giving obligation. The most visible type was the behu — a tall vertical column or obelisk erected in front of a noble household to commemorate a feast of merit and to permanently mark the family's status. More elaborate were the osa-osa: anthropomorphic behu topped with stylized animal-head features — commonly a lasara, a hybrid deer-dragon creature associated with ancestral power, or a hornbill — that gave the stone the character of an ancestor figure rooted in the earth before the house.27 The number, size, and elaborateness of a noble family's stone monuments directly indexed their accumulated social standing: a man who had sponsored more Owasa feasts, redistributing more wealth to his community, had a grander stone front yard. The monuments were not merely symbolic; erecting them was a ceremonial and logistical achievement that required coordinating hundreds of labourers and feasting the entire village.

The most celebrated practice associated with Nias megalithic culture is the hombo batu — stone jumping. A standing stone roughly 1.5 to 2 metres high was set up in the village centre; young men of noble aspiration were required to leap over it in a single bound, a feat of strength and coordination that signified readiness for war and adult rank. The practice has survived into the present as a tourist spectacle, but in its original context it was a physical rite of social passage embedded in a world of ancestral stone.

The Pasemah Plateau

In the highlands of South Sumatra, on the Pasemah plateau (also written Besemah), a different megalithic tradition produced some of the most technically accomplished stone sculpture in all of ancient Southeast Asia. The Pasemah monuments are carved from local andesitic stone and are notable for a figurative boldness unusual in the region: warrior figures wearing belted loincloths and heavy bronze bracelets; men wrestling what appear to be buffalo or elephants; and — most remarkably — several statues depicting warriors astride elephants, carrying what art historians have identified as Dong Son-type bronze kettledrums at their hips.28 These last figures provide one of the most vivid visual links between the megalithic and Dong Son traditions: here is a society in which the possession of a prestige bronze drum from northern Vietnam — an object that, as we discuss below, marked political authority across the archipelago — was so central to elite identity that it was carved into the most permanent medium available.

The dating of the Pasemah monuments has been disputed for over a century. European scholars investigated them from the colonial period onward, and recent systematic research by Dominik Bonatz of the Freie Universität Berlin suggests that the Sumatran megalithic tradition as a whole spans from approximately 2,500 years ago to very recent constructions, with the most accomplished figurative sculptures likely falling in the period 300–100 BCE — contemporary with peak Dong Son influence.29 The tradition was not a single episode but a long-lived cultural practice. Bonatz has also noted that local communities later reinterpreted the monuments in Hindu-Buddhist terms, treating figures they no longer recognized as representing "a god from the time when there were still Indian gods in Indonesia" — a reminder that the Austronesian Neolithic world was subsequently overlaid with centuries of Indian religious influence before the region became Islamic.

Sulawesi and the Bada Valley

Central Sulawesi contains one of the most mysterious megalithic concentrations in all of Southeast Asia. Within the Lore Lindu National Park, in a cluster of valleys — Bada, Besoa, and Napu — archaeologists have recorded over four hundred megalithic monuments of several types: carved human-form statues (arca menhir), large cylindrical stone containers called kalamba (apparently used for collective burial), and standing stones of various sizes.30 The thirty or so human-form statues in the Bada Valley are among the most striking prehistoric sculptures in Indonesia: they range from roughly one metre to over four metres in height, depict stylized human figures with enlarged heads, round eyes, and mouths often absent or only suggested, and bear no obvious connection to any known historical culture. Their purpose is debated; most scholars argue they were associated with ancestor veneration and social commemoration, possibly placed at important gathering points in a landscape organized around cycling between settlement and swidden agriculture.

The dating of the Sulawesi megaliths remains a matter of considerable uncertainty, and scholars are frank about this. The kalamba burial jars in the Besoa Valley have yielded radiocarbon dates spanning roughly CE 766–1272, suggesting active use during the medieval period — long after Austronesian settlement of the region.31 Some researchers propose that the tradition began as early as 2,000 to 5,000 years ago, based on parallels with dated megalithic traditions elsewhere in Southeast Asia. European discovery of the monuments in 1908 sparked decades of speculation, and local oral tradition attributes them to a mythical figure called Pahit Lidah ("Bitter Tongue"), who turned moral transgressors into stone. What is clear is that megalith construction in Central Sulawesi ceased around the twelfth century CE, possibly linked to a prolonged regional drought, and that the tradition left no living descendants who remember its full meaning.

Megalith — literally "great stone"; any large stone structure erected by human effort for ritual, commemorative, or social purposes. In the Indonesian context, megalithic traditions include standing stones (menhir), stone seats and tables (dolmen), step-pyramids (punden berundak), stone statues, and burial containers (kalamba, sarcophagi), distributed across Sumatra, Java, Sulawesi, Nias, Sumba, and Flores. Though they vary enormously in form, Indonesian megalithic traditions share a common logic of ancestor veneration and the use of durable stone to anchor social memory in the landscape.

The Bronze World: Dong Son Culture and Its Island Reach

Alongside the megalithic traditions, and sometimes in direct dialogue with them (as the Pasemah warrior-with-drum statues attest), a second great cultural force shaped the Indonesian world from around 500 BCE: the spread of Dong Son bronze-working technology and its most spectacular product, the bronze kettledrum.

The Dong Son culture takes its name from a village in the Thanh Hóa province of northern Vietnam, near the Red River, where French and Vietnamese archaeologists in the 1920s and 1930s excavated a rich assemblage of bronze weapons, tools, and ceremonial vessels. The culture flourished in the Red River Delta from roughly 1000 BCE to 100 CE — the period before and during which the region came under Han Chinese imperial pressure.32 It was associated with wet-rice cultivation, animal husbandry, and a sophisticated maritime technology that used dugout canoes for river and coastal transport. Its metalworkers mastered the lost-wax casting process, producing objects of extraordinary technical precision. The ethnic affiliation of the Dong Son people is debated: they were probably Austroasiatic speakers (ancestors of today's Vietnamese and related Mon-Khmer peoples), though the culture's trade networks reached Austronesian-speaking communities throughout Island Southeast Asia.

The kettledrum — classified under the Heger Type I system established by the Austrian scholar Franz Heger in 1902 — is the Dong Son object that most thoroughly penetrated the Indonesian world. These drums could reach one metre in height and weigh up to one hundred kilograms; they were cast in bronze using the lost-wax technique and decorated with elaborate surface iconography arranged in concentric bands. The central motif on the flat upper face (the tympanum) is typically a multi-rayed sun or star; surrounding it are bands depicting, in highly stylized form, the world the Dong Son people knew: long-hulled boats propelled by paddles and carrying armed warriors in feather headdresses, deer and egrets, houses on stilts, and dancing figures in ceremonial costume.33 The drums were not primarily musical instruments — their acoustic properties are real but secondary — but objects of concentrated political and ritual power. They were used in rain-prayer ceremonies, harvest festivals, funerals, and as signal instruments in warfare. In the political economy of the pre-state polities of Island Southeast Asia, possession of a large and finely worked drum was a direct index of chiefly authority: the more powerful the leader, the larger and more elaborately decorated the drum he could acquire.

More than two hundred Dong Son drums have been discovered across a geographic arc stretching from eastern Indonesia to southern China and northern Vietnam, with significant concentrations in Sumatra, Java, Bali, and the Eastern Indonesian islands as well as in mainland Vietnam, Laos, southern China, and Malaysia.34 Their distribution maps an early interregional exchange network in which prestige goods flowed along maritime routes connecting communities that otherwise spoke different languages and practised different subsistence economies. When the Pasemah plateau sculptors of South Sumatra depicted their warriors holding kettledrums, they were recording the social reality of a world in which these objects were the markers of legitimate authority — the crowns and sceptres of the Austronesian Bronze Age.

The Dong Son culture does not survive into the historical record. The Han dynasty's southward expansion brought northern Vietnam under Chinese administration in 111 BCE, and by the first century CE the Dong Son material culture — its distinctive bronzes, its burial practices, its political forms — had been absorbed into the new Sino-Vietnamese hybrid society of the Red River plain. But the drums had already dispersed. Those that reached the Indonesian islands were kept, accumulated, traded onward, sometimes buried with their owners, and in a few cases treasured into the modern period as heirlooms whose original provenance was entirely forgotten. Several Indonesian communities, as late as the twentieth century, possessed Dong Son drums of unknown origin that they treated as sacred objects of great antiquity — which, in the most literal sense, they were.

Society, Kinship, and the Genetic Record

What can we say about the social organization of the Austronesian-speaking peoples who settled the Indonesian archipelago? Three different disciplines — historical linguistics, comparative ethnography, and, most recently, ancient and modern genomics — have each contributed a partial answer, and the answers are now beginning to converge in instructive ways.

The most systematic linguistic reconstruction of early Austronesian social organization was Robert Blust's 1980 paper in Current Anthropology, which used comparative linguistic evidence to argue that the Proto-Austronesian and Proto-Malayo-Polynesian societies were organized on the principle of "prescriptive alliance" — that is, a system in which each person was expected to marry into a specific social category, not simply prohibited from marrying into others. Blust reconstructed a society divided into two "moieties" on a male-female axis, intersected by a second dual division, producing a quadripartite (four-group) intermarrying system of considerable structural elegance.35 This reconstruction anticipated the bilateral kinship — tracing descent and obligation through both parents equally — that is characteristic of most Austronesian societies in the historical record, distinguishing them from the unilateral descent systems (patrilineal clans, matrilineal lineages) more common in Africa and parts of South Asia.

The comparative ethnographic literature has largely confirmed Blust's reconstruction while adding nuance. The anthropologist F.A.E. van Wouden, working in the mid-twentieth century, and Claude Lévi-Strauss, who theorized "house societies" (sociétés à maison) as a structural type, identified a recurrent social form in Austronesian communities: the "house," a named corporate group occupying a prestige dwelling that perpetuated itself through strategic marriages and the accumulation of sacred heirlooms, regardless of whether its members were strictly related by blood. In a "house society," the physical house and its valuables — including, crucially, bronze drums, sacred textiles, and ritual objects — were the anchors of social continuity, not a named lineage as such. J. Stephen Lansing and colleagues have argued that this house-society structure was the social vehicle of the original Austronesian expansion, providing a flexible, portable template for social organization that could adapt to widely varying environments without losing its basic logic.36

A crucial empirical contribution has come from a 2009 study in the Proceedings of the Royal Society B by Fiona Jordan and colleagues, who applied Bayesian phylogenetic methods to residence patterns in 135 Pacific Austronesian societies while simultaneously modelling the evolution of the Austronesian language tree across more than 400 languages. Their finding was striking: the ancestral Austronesian social organization, at the time of the Taiwan homeland (around 5000–4500 BP), was overwhelmingly matrilocal — women stayed in their natal community after marriage while men moved.37 The transition from matrilocal to patrilocal residence (where the couple settles with the husband's family) occurred in multiple independent events over the subsequent millennia, at twice the rate of the reverse transition — suggesting that as Austronesian communities settled into intensive rice agriculture, the structural logic of the house shifted toward male-centred ownership of land and rice fields. This finding elegantly explains why some Indonesian societies, like the Minangkabau of West Sumatra, retain strong matrilineal institutions while their neighbours do not: the Minangkabau may have preserved, in amplified form, an ancestral social logic common to all Austronesian societies at their origin.

The genomic record has both confirmed and complicated the social picture. As noted above, Lipson et al.'s 2014 study demonstrated that all sampled Austronesian-speaking groups in Island Southeast Asia carry a Taiwan-related ancestry component, ranging from around thirty per cent in populations in western Indonesia (where admixture with earlier residents was substantial) to over ninety per cent in remote Pacific island communities where few non-Austronesian people had lived.38 The pattern of this admixture carries a social signal: in western Indonesia, the genetic mixing between incoming Austronesians and resident Australo-Papuan populations was dated to roughly 2,200 years ago (200 BCE–200 CE) — later than the initial Austronesian arrival, which suggests that the first wave of migrants remained relatively endogamous before later contact and intermarriage became more common. An unexpected Austro-Asiatic genetic component in parts of western Indonesia has also prompted debate: this signal, stronger than can be explained by simple proximity, suggests either a historically significant Austro-Asiatic presence in Island Southeast Asia before the Austronesian arrival, or that some Austronesian migrants passed through mainland Southeast Asia and admixed there before continuing westward.39

The ancient DNA evidence from "Besse" (Leang Panninge, Sulawesi, c. 5300 BCE) adds a further layer of complexity: her genome's closest modern analogues are Papuans and Australian Aboriginals, but her lineage was already differentiated from those groups for some 37,000 years.40 This means that when Austronesian migrants arrived in Wallacea, they were not encountering a single uniform "pre-Austronesian" population but a mosaic of distinct, long-separated human communities, some with no surviving modern descendants. The Austronesian expansion did not simply overwrite this ancient human geography; it wove itself into it, producing the complex genetic patchwork that characterizes Indonesia's peoples to this day.

Bilateral kinship — a system in which descent, inheritance, and social obligation are traced through both the father's and the mother's sides equally, rather than exclusively through one line (as in patrilineal or matrilineal systems). Most Austronesian societies practise bilateral kinship, which facilitates flexible alliance-building and is consistent with the mobile, maritime lifestyle of the initial Austronesian expansion.

Summary

The Austronesian expansion, beginning around 4000–3500 BCE in Taiwan and reaching the Indonesian archipelago by around 2000 BCE, was one of the great demographic and cultural movements of human prehistory. Linguists Robert Blust and Peter Bellwood established the "Out of Taiwan" model on the strength of the Formosan languages' deep antiquity and the convergence of linguistic, archaeological, and genetic evidence; rival models, particularly the "Out of Sundaland" hypothesis, have not survived the scrutiny of ancient DNA studies, which consistently show all Austronesian groups to be closer to aboriginal Taiwanese than to any mainland population. The migrants brought outrigger canoe technology — enabling ocean crossing — and a Neolithic package of rice cultivation, domesticated pigs and chickens, and red-slipped pottery, whose Sa Huỳnh–Kalanay tradition is traceable across the archipelago. Their arrival did not erase the ancient Australo-Papuan peoples already resident in the islands; genetic studies reveal a complex layering of populations, and the 7,300-year-old "Besse" skeleton from Sulawesi complicates the simple two-layer model. From roughly 2000 BCE through the first millennium BCE, Austronesian communities developed distinctive regional megalithic traditions — on Nias, the Pasemah plateau, and in Central Sulawesi — in which stone monuments anchored ancestor veneration and marked social rank. From around 500 BCE, Dong Son bronze drums from northern Vietnam circulated through the archipelago as prestige objects of chiefly authority, linking island communities into a wide exchange network. Linguistically reconstructed early Austronesian social organization was probably matrilocal and organized around "house societies," with the transition to patrilocal farming communities occurring independently across many communities over subsequent millennia.

Review & Discussion Questions

3.1 Why does the internal structure of the Austronesian language family — specifically the relationship between Formosan and Malayo-Polynesian branches — support a Taiwan homeland rather than a mainland Southeast Asian one? What would the family tree look like if the homeland were in Sundaland?
3.2 Compare the "Express Train" and "Slow Boat" models of Austronesian expansion. What kinds of evidence — genetic, archaeological, linguistic — would you need to distinguish between them in a specific region of Indonesia?
3.3 The "Besse" skeleton from Sulawesi predates the Austronesian arrival by several thousand years yet represents a human lineage with no clear modern descendants. What does this imply for how we describe the pre-Austronesian human geography of the Indonesian archipelago?
3.4 Trace the multiple lines of evidence — linguistic, archaeological, genetic — that support the claim that rice farming, not just rice cultivation, was part of the original Austronesian cultural toolkit before the expansion from Taiwan. Where does the evidence become thinner or more contested?
3.5 The Pasemah plateau sculptures show warriors carrying Dong Son kettledrums, yet the Dong Son culture was probably Austroasiatic-speaking and centred in northern Vietnam. What does this iconographic borrowing tell us about how prestige and political authority operated in the prehistoric Indonesian world?
3.6 Jordan et al.'s 2009 study found that matrilocality was ancestral in Austronesian societies and that the shift to patrilocality occurred at twice the reverse rate. Why might intensive rice agriculture accelerate a shift from matrilocal to patrilocal residence?
3.7 The Sulawesi megalithic tradition (Bada Valley, Besoa) died out around the twelfth century CE, while the Nias stone tradition was still living well into the twentieth century. What factors — environmental, social, political, or religious — might explain this difference in longevity between regionally neighbouring megalithic traditions?
3.8 Blust argued that the Austronesian expansion is "one of the greatest chapters in human prehistory." On what grounds might you agree or disagree with this characterization? What criteria are being used to define "greatness" in human migration history?
