Learning Objectives

  • Explain why cloves and nutmeg grew nowhere on earth except a tiny cluster of volcanic islands in the Maluku archipelago, and understand how that biogeographical accident shaped world history.
  • Trace the long arc of the spice trade from its earliest documented exchanges — cloves in Bronze Age Syria, pepper in Roman Italy — through its classical-Islamic and medieval phases.
  • Describe the multi-layered Asian commercial system that carried spices from the islands to China, India, the Islamic world, and the Mediterranean before any European arrived.
  • Analyse the economics of the trade: the successive markups, the structure of merchant networks, and the extraordinary profit margins that made control of Malacca worth more, in Tomé Pires's metaphor, than control of Venice.
  • Understand why medieval and early modern Europeans so intensely desired spices, and evaluate the scholarly debate over whether preservation was the real motive.
  • Connect the "Spice Islands" to the Age of Exploration: how the desire to reach the source directly drove Columbus, da Gama, and Magellan, and set the stage for European intrusion into the archipelago.

In the middle of the second millennium before the Common Era, a ceramic jar was left behind in a burning house in Terqa, a provincial town on the middle Euphrates in what is now Syria. When archaeologist Giorgio Buccellati excavated the site in 1976, he found inside the jar the carbonized remains of clove buds — a spice that could only have been harvested on five tiny volcanic islands at the other end of the known world, more than seven thousand kilometres away in what is today the Indonesian province of North Maluku.1 That one archaeological find encapsulates the central argument of this chapter: centuries before the Portuguese rounded Africa, before Columbus sailed west, before any European set foot in Southeast Asia, the islands of the Indonesian archipelago were already nodes in a world system of commerce whose reach was genuinely global. The spices — cloves, nutmeg, mace, and pepper — that made these islands famous did not simply wait for European discovery. They had been travelling the Indian Ocean for three thousand years.

Islands at the World's Edge: The Biogeography of Spice

The spice trade was, at its root, an accident of botany. Cloves (Syzygium aromaticum), one of the most traded commodities in human history, grew in their natural habitat on exactly five small islands clustered off the western coast of Halmahera in what is today North Maluku province: Ternate, Tidore, Bacan, Makian, and Moti. The genus Syzygium, scientists now know, originated on the ancient supercontinent of Sahul (the merged landmass of Australia and New Guinea) perhaps thirty million years ago and dispersed westward along the island arc over geological time.2 The result was a plant perfectly adapted to — and, for most of its history, inseparable from — a specific equatorial microclimate: high humidity, abundant rainfall, rich volcanic soil, and temperatures that never fell below twenty degrees Celsius. Attempts to transplant clove trees to other islands before the eighteenth century invariably failed; the plant seemed to demand conditions found only in this handful of places.

Nutmeg (Myristica fragrans), which produces both the nutmeg kernel and its lacy red aril (mace) — effectively two spices from a single fruit — was even more narrowly restricted. Its native habitat was the Banda Islands, a volcanic archipelago of ten small islands sitting in the Banda Sea roughly two thousand kilometres southeast of Ternate.3 The Banda Islands lie within the "Banda Sea Islands moist deciduous forests" ecoregion, where the combination of volcanic soil chemistry, reliable monsoon rainfall exceeding 1,500 millimetres annually, and specific diurnal temperature ranges created conditions that the nutmeg tree could not, prior to eighteenth-century European botanical transfers, be persuaded to replicate elsewhere. University of Washington archaeologist Amy J. Jordan has described the Banda Islands as "the world's sole source of nutmeg in the sixteenth century" — a claim that is almost banal in statement but extraordinary in its global consequences.4

Pepper (Piper nigrum) had a somewhat wider native range: the vine is indigenous to the southern Indian subcontinent, but it was cultivated intensively in the hinterlands of Sumatra and later Java, where it grew alongside black cardamom and long pepper from at least the early centuries of the Common Era. Sumatran pepper, which came to market through the ports of Samudra Pasai, Banten, and Palembang, would eventually account for the majority of the world's supply, but pepper was always less rare than cloves or nutmeg, and accordingly commanded a lower price. It was the extreme geographic concentration of cloves and nutmeg — the accident that placed them in the hands of the people of Ternate, Tidore, and the Bandanese — that made the eastern Maluku islands the most commercially consequential real estate on the planet for several centuries.

Myristica fragrans — the nutmeg tree, native to the Banda Islands of central Maluku; its seed is nutmeg and its aril (the lacy red covering) is mace. Before eighteenth-century European botanical transfers, the Banda Islands were the world's only source of both spices.

The consequence of this ecology was a systematic scarcity that was not manufactured but real. Because cloves grew in five small islands and nutmeg in ten, any merchant or power that could control the point of production, or the sea lanes connecting the islands to the rest of the world, occupied a position of extraordinary leverage. The history of the Maluku islands before 1600 is the history of how successive networks of traders — Javanese, Malay, Gujarati, Arab, Chinese, and eventually Portuguese and Dutch — competed for that leverage. The history of world commerce is in large part the history of the price gradient that stretched from those five volcanic peaks to the spice markets of Cairo, Venice, Lisbon, and Amsterdam.

Before the Europeans: Spices in Ancient and Medieval Trade

The Terqa cloves that opened this chapter are not an isolated find. Archaeologists have recovered cloves at Óc Eo in the Mekong delta of what is now Vietnam (first through eighth century CE), at Mantai in Sri Lanka (ninth through eleventh century CE), and in later medieval contexts across the Indian Ocean world.5 Chinese records from the Han dynasty (202 BCE–220 CE) describe the court protocol by which imperial ministers were required to hold cloves in their mouths before addressing the emperor, so as not to offend him with their breath — an early indication that the spice had reached China through the Austronesian maritime networks that connected the eastern and western halves of the Indo-Pacific long before any state formalized the connection.6 The *Periplus Maris Erythraei* ("Periplus of the Erythraean Sea"), a mariner's guide composed by an anonymous Greek-speaking Egyptian merchant around 45–55 CE, describes a well-organized system of seasonal trade in which Roman-era merchants sailed annually from Red Sea ports, utilizing the monsoon winds, to reach the western coast of India, where pepper, ginger, and cotton textiles changed hands for Roman gold and silver.7

The Roman appetite for spices was voracious and, to ancient moralists, alarming. The elder Pliny, writing in the first century CE, lamented that "there is no year in which India does not drain the Roman Empire of fifty million sesterces" in exchange for luxury goods including pepper — a figure, whatever its literal accuracy, that testifies to the scale of the trade in elite Roman consciousness.8 The passion ran deep enough to become geopolitically significant: when Alaric and his Visigoths besieged Rome in 408 CE, their ransom demand reportedly included three thousand pounds of pepper alongside gold and silver, treating spice as currency interchangeable with bullion.8 Cloves, nutmeg, and mace arrived in the Roman world too, though in smaller quantities and at higher prices, carried through a chain of intermediaries that already stretched from Maluku to the Mediterranean.

With the rise of Islam and the expansion of Muslim commercial networks across the Indian Ocean from the seventh century onward, the trade became more regularized and more voluminous. The historian K.N. Chaudhuri, in his landmark study of Indian Ocean commerce, has argued that the ocean constituted not merely a trade route but a "civilisational system" linking societies from the South China Sea to the eastern Mediterranean, with Islamic merchants and jurists providing the legal and ethical frameworks — contracts of partnership (mudaraba), letters of credit, shared cargo risk — that allowed long-distance commerce to function across language and cultural barriers.9 M.N. Pearson has similarly shown that the Indian Ocean spice trade of the medieval period was not a simple pipeline but a web of overlapping regional circuits, in which Gujarati, Arab, Malay, and Javanese merchants each dominated particular segments and commodities, connected by ports that served as nodes of redistribution.10

The Great Entrepôt: Malacca and the Asian Commercial World

By the fourteenth century, the northern coast of Sumatra had emerged as the first major point of articulation between the spice islands and the wider world. The sultanate of Samudra Pasai, founded according to tradition in 1267 CE on the northern tip of Sumatra, controlled hinterlands rich in pepper and gold. A Brill-published study of the pre-European Straits of Malacca describes Samudra Pasai as "the most important centre of commerce in the Straits of Melaka" before the rise of Malacca itself — a stopover that integrated trade communities from Gujarat, Bengal, Arabia, Persia, and the Indonesian archipelago into a single commercial zone.11 Tomé Pires, the Portuguese apothecary who gathered intelligence on the trading world from Malacca between 1512 and 1515, described Samudra Pasai's merchants as including "Turks, Arabs, Persians, Gujaratis, south Indians, Malays, Javanese, Thai, Peguans, and Kedahans" — an enumeration that captures the cosmopolitan reality of the Sumatran spice port before European intervention.12

It was the sultanate of Malacca, however, founded around 1402–1403 on the Malay Peninsula, that became the supreme entrepôt of the pre-European spice trade. Malacca commanded the Strait of Malacca — the narrow passage between the Malay Peninsula and Sumatra through which virtually all sea traffic between the Indian Ocean and the South China Sea had to pass. Anthony Reid has described the rhythm of trade at Malacca as organically tied to the monsoon system: the northeast monsoon (November–March) brought junks from China and traders from the South China Sea; the southwest monsoon (May–September) brought ships from India, Arabia, and the Persian Gulf, carrying textiles, silver, and finished goods in exchange for cloves, nutmeg, mace, and pepper that the Javanese and Malay traders had assembled from across the archipelago.13 At its height, Malacca was the greatest single marketplace in maritime Asia.

Historian Rila Mukherjee has reconstructed the "textiles-against-cloves" trade that was the material foundation of Malacca's commercial supremacy. Merchants from the Bengal region — carrying fine cotton and silk textiles that were highly prized in the archipelago — could exchange their goods at Malacca for cloves and other Maluku spices, then resell them at profit in Indian or Arab ports. The circuit ran in both directions: Maluku cloves moved west while South Asian textiles moved east, with Malacca capturing a share of value at the intersection.14 Pires's testimony on the sheer scale of this multilingual commerce is the most celebrated in the historical literature. He reported that "Malacca is of such importance and profit that it seems to me it has no equal in the world" and that over eighty languages were spoken by the merchants resident in the city at any one time.15 His most famous sentence captures the geopolitical stakes with lapidary force: "Whoever is lord of Malacca has his hand on the throat of Venice."16

Malacca's administrative genius lay in its management of commercial diversity. The port operated through a shahbandar system in which four senior officials, each responsible for supervising a specific community of foreign traders, collected taxes, arbitrated disputes, and managed the port's warehousing and weighing operations.17 One shahbandar served the Gujarati merchants; one the traders from the Bay of Bengal; one the Javanese, Malay, and other Southeast Asian traders; one the Chinese. The system allowed Malacca to profit from a traffic it could not itself have generated — the city produced nothing except services — while providing foreign merchants with enough legal security and institutional predictability to justify the risks of long-distance trade. The Undang-Undang Melaka (Malaccan port law) and the Undang-Undang Laut Melaka (Malaccan maritime law) gave the institutional framework a written codification, making Malacca's commercial regime one of the most sophisticated in the pre-colonial world.

Shahbandar — literally "harbour-master" (Persian: shāh, king; bandar, harbour). In the Malacca sultanate and other Southeast Asian port polities, the shahbandar was the official responsible for managing a specific community of foreign merchants, collecting duties, and mediating disputes. The four-shahbandar system of Malacca was a sophisticated mechanism for governing commercial diversity.

The Asian Commercial System: Networks, Ships, and Margins

The spice trade before European intervention was not a simple two-point exchange but a layered system of nested regional circuits, each with its own dominant merchants, commodity specializations, and transport technologies. Archibald Lewis, in his study of Indian Ocean maritime skills in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, has emphasized the role of Gujarati merchants from the port of Cambay as the linchpin connecting the Indian subcontinent to both the Red Sea and the Southeast Asian islands.18 Gujarati capital and credit financed much of the trade; Gujarati textiles — especially fine cotton cloth — were the principal commodity that the archipelago wanted in return for spices. Pires described Gujarati merchants as "men who understand merchandise" whose network of factors and agents extended from Malacca to the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, and the East African coast.

The Javanese occupied a different but equally critical niche. The great ports of Java's north coast — Tuban, Gresik, Demak, Banten — sent their own large sailing vessels (jong or junk) east to the Maluku islands, collecting cloves and nutmeg directly from Ternate, Tidore, and the Banda communities, and carrying them west to Malacca or selling them directly to Indian and Arab merchants who called at Javanese ports. Leonard Andaya has shown that the commercial organization of the Maluku trade was deeply enmeshed with the political dualism of the two great clove-producing sultanates: Ternate and Tidore, rivals for primacy in the islands, each cultivated alliances with different external trading partners — the one tilting toward Malay and Javanese networks, the other sometimes toward different combinations — such that competition between them was simultaneously political and commercial.19 The inhabitants of the Banda Islands, for their part, maintained their own trading relationships through which Bandanese merchants carried nutmeg north to Maluku's hub ports and south toward Timor and the Sunda Islands, exchanging it for sago (their staple food, imported because the Banda volcanic soils were unsuited to rice cultivation), cloth, and metal.

Anthony Reid's two-volume Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce has argued that the period from roughly 1405 to 1630 constituted a genuine "Age of Commerce" for the region, in which expanding international demand — above all from China and Europe — stimulated urbanization, commercialization, and political consolidation across the islands and mainland.20 The great trading cities of Southeast Asia — Malacca, Ayutthaya, Makassar, later Banten and Aceh — were, on Reid's reading, products not of agricultural surplus but of commercial wealth, their populations sustained by imported food paid for with spice revenues. Denys Lombard's monumental study of Java as a historical crossroads reinforces this picture: the Javanese north coast cities were not peripheral appendages of the Hindu-Buddhist hinterland but autonomous commercial societies oriented outward toward the Indian Ocean system, with their own mercantile cultures, multilingual communities, and financial instruments.21

The economic structure of the trade can be reconstructed, at least approximately, through a comparison of prices at different nodes in the supply chain. K.N. Chaudhuri's analysis of Indian Ocean price data and merchant accounts suggests that spices typically passed through four to six distinct stages of exchange between producer and European consumer, with each stage adding a significant markup for the risks of transport, credit, and storage.22 Cloves purchased at Ternate might be worth a small quantity of cloth or metal; the same cloves, carried to Malacca, would command a price several times higher; transported to Calicut on the Malabar coast, several times higher still; carried by Arab or Egyptian merchants to Aden and then Cairo, higher again; passed to Venetian or Genoese merchants at Alexandria, higher again; and retailed in northern European markets at a price that could be — by the height of the spice trade's profitability in the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries — an order of magnitude or more above the original production price.23 Jack Turner, in his comprehensive history of the spice trade, has suggested that by the time spices reached Italian consumers at the height of the Renaissance, the cumulative markup from the point of production could exceed two thousand percent, with each intermediary extracting a share appropriate to the risks and capital costs of their segment of the route.24

Entrepôt — a port that serves primarily as a centre of redistribution rather than production, where goods from multiple origins are assembled, sorted, and resold. Malacca was the archetypal pre-modern entrepôt: it produced almost nothing itself but commanded the chokepoint through which the Indian Ocean and South China Sea trade systems were linked.

Spices in the European Imagination

Why did Europeans want spices so desperately? The question seems obvious, but the standard answer — that spices were needed to preserve meat in a refrigeration-free era — has been subjected to devastating scholarly criticism. Paul Freedman, in his authoritative study Out of the East: Spices and the Medieval Imagination, has shown that the preservation explanation is historically untenable: if medieval Europeans needed to mask the taste of putrefied meat, they had access to far cheaper and more effective methods — salt, smoking, drying, air-curing, and pickling — that did not require importing luxury goods from the other side of the world.25 More fundamentally, as Freedman points out, spices were expensive luxury items "beyond the reach of ordinary medieval people" and therefore could not have served as general-purpose food preservatives for a population that was mostly poor.25

The real explanation is at once more interesting and more revealing about medieval European culture. Spices were consumed, Freedman argues, for three overlapping sets of reasons: culinary (they were central to an elaborate cuisine that prized complex flavour combinations, particularly the sweet-savoury blends characteristic of medieval haute cuisine), medical (they were held, within the Galenic humoral framework, to have specific therapeutic properties — pepper was "hot and dry," nutmeg "warm and moist" — that made them ingredients in an enormous pharmaceutical repertoire), and symbolic/cosmological (they were believed to emanate from Paradise, their fragrance being literally the breath of Eden carried by the great rivers of the East).26 In a related article, Freedman has analysed how the "scarcity and cost of pepper" — mythologized in medieval bestiaries as guarded by serpents in eastern forests — made spice consumption a mark of social distinction, a "symbol of beauty, affluence, taste, and grace" that the wealthy displayed precisely because others could not afford it.27 This constructed scarcity, interacting with the real biogeographical scarcity of the production zone, produced a demand that was at once rational (spices did taste good and did have mild pharmacological effects) and partly irrational (the mythology of their origins amplified their desirability well beyond any purely functional explanation).

The price of this desire was borne at the point of consumption in quantities that alarmed moralists across the centuries. Pliny the Elder's complaint about fifty million sesterces draining from Rome annually to pay for Indian luxuries was echoed, more than a millennium later, by European statesmen who watched gold flow east to pay for pepper and cloves. In 408 CE, when the Visigoths under Alaric besieged Rome, their ransom demand famously included over three thousand pounds of pepper alongside gold and silver — a measure of how deeply the spice had penetrated Roman economic imagination.28 By the fifteenth century, the same logic was operating on a larger scale: the Italian city-states, particularly Venice, had built much of their commercial empire on the privilege of being the primary gateway through which eastern spices entered the European market. Venice's stranglehold on the Alexandria trade meant that other European states — Portugal, Castile, the nascent nation-states of the Atlantic seaboard — paid Venetian prices. The incentive to find an alternative route to the source was economic, geopolitical, and, in the minds of European monarchs and merchants, urgent.

The Age of Exploration and the Search for the Source

The transformation of the spice trade by European voyaging is one of the defining stories of the early modern world, and it begins not with success but with a spectacular geographical error. When Christopher Columbus sailed west from Spain in 1492, he was seeking the "Spice Islands" — the Maluku archipelago — by what he believed was a shorter westward route. His miscalculation of the earth's circumference, combined with his failure to account for the American landmass, led him to the Caribbean, which he insisted until his death was the outer margin of Asia. The spices he claimed to have found there — particularly what he called "pepper" (actually the unrelated Capsicum genus, now known as chilli peppers) — were commercial and biological substitutes that never fully displaced the Malukan originals. Columbus's error reoriented European agriculture and cuisine but did not solve the problem of spice supply.29

The Portuguese solution was more direct: sail around Africa. Vasco da Gama's voyage of 1497–1499, rounding the Cape of Good Hope and reaching Calicut on India's Malabar coast, proved the feasibility of a direct sea route from Europe to the source of the spice trade. Sanjay Subrahmanyam, the leading scholar of the Portuguese Estado da India, has emphasized that da Gama's arrival was understood by the Indian Ocean's existing commercial communities not as a miraculous discovery but as the intrusion of a well-armed newcomer into a system that had functioned for centuries without European participation.30 The Portuguese strategy that followed was not primarily about peaceful commerce: it relied on naval force, the seizure of key nodes, and the attempt to redirect existing trade flows through Portuguese-controlled channels. The cartaz system — the requirement that all Indian Ocean shipping purchase a Portuguese licence or face seizure — was an attempt to extract a rent from a trade the Portuguese could never fully monopolize by price competition alone, since Asian merchants could produce and carry goods at costs the Portuguese could not match.

The most dramatic single episode in the European search for the spice source was Ferdinand Magellan's circumnavigation of 1519–1522. Magellan, a Portuguese navigator sailing under the Spanish crown, left Sanlúcar de Barrameda in September 1519 with five ships and approximately 270 men, explicitly tasked with reaching the Moluccas by a westward route — thereby establishing a Spanish claim to the islands that would compete with Portugal's eastern approach.31 The expedition's costs were extraordinary: Magellan himself was killed in the Philippines in April 1521; the armada lost three of its five ships to storms, desertion, and combat. When the sole surviving vessel, the Victoria, limped back to Sanlúcar in September 1522 under the command of Juan Sebastián Elcano, only eighteen of the original crew remained aboard — plus a cargo of twenty-six tonnes of cloves and cinnamon collected at Tidore.31 That cargo, according to contemporaries, came close to paying the entire cost of the expedition. The ratio of profit to suffering was, by any human measure, disturbing — but it accurately reflects the economic stakes that spice represented in the early sixteenth century.

The Portuguese Threshold: Malacca 1511 and Its Aftermath

The pivotal moment in European intrusion into the Indonesian spice trade was the Portuguese conquest of Malacca in 1511 — a campaign led by the viceroy Afonso de Albuquerque who had already established Portuguese control of Hormuz (at the mouth of the Persian Gulf) and would go on to seize Goa on India's Malabar coast. Albuquerque's seizure of Malacca, the world's most important spice entrepôt, was intended to redirect the entire eastward flow of the spice trade through Portuguese-controlled channels, and to deny the Islamic commercial world — which had dominated the trade for centuries — its most profitable node.32

The immediate consequences confounded Portuguese expectations. Asian merchants did not simply submit to the new order. As Rila Mukherjee has documented, the wealthy trading communities of Malacca — Gujarati, Javanese, Malay, Tamil, Chinese — dispersed across alternative ports almost immediately after the Portuguese capture, reconstituting their commercial networks in Aceh, Johor, Banten, Ayutthaya, and Pegu.33 The Acehnese sultanate, controlling the northern tip of Sumatra, became the most significant beneficiary: its control of extensive Sumatran pepper hinterlands and its ability to offer Islamic merchants a port free from Portuguese interference made it the dominant commercial power in the western archipelago for much of the sixteenth century. By the 1820s, Aceh would come to produce over half the world's pepper supply — but that position was built on foundations already laid in the century after the Portuguese conquest of Malacca.

In the Maluku Islands themselves, the Portuguese found a commercial world more complex than their maps had suggested. Ternate and Tidore, the twin sultanates of the clove trade, had been in a state of structured rivalry — what Andaya calls a "dualistic" relationship in which competition was balanced by mutual necessity — for generations before the first Portuguese ship arrived.19 The Portuguese allied primarily with Ternate, establishing a fort there in 1522 and attempting to create a monopoly purchase arrangement for cloves. This arrangement was persistently undermined: Javanese traders who had operated in Maluku for generations did not abandon their routes because Portuguese ships occasionally appeared; Tidore aligned with Spain after the Magellan expedition; and the local rulers proved capable of playing European powers against each other in ways that preserved a degree of indigenous commercial agency. The neat monopoly the Portuguese sought was never achieved.

The broader significance of the Portuguese intrusion for this chapter's economic argument is paradoxical. The Portuguese did not replace the Asian commercial system; they inserted themselves into it as a heavily armed, partially integrated participant. They lacked the commercial networks, the capital, the textiles, and the linguistic capacity to displace Gujarati or Javanese merchants from the niches those merchants had occupied for centuries. What they added was violence — the cannon on the caravel and the fort on the headland — as a mechanism for extracting rents from a trade they could partially tax but never fully control. The economic historian K.N. Chaudhuri's characterization of the Portuguese Estado da India as a "predatory state" rather than a true commercial enterprise captures something essential: it was the Dutch, arriving a century later with superior capital, organizational sophistication, and an even more systematic willingness to use violence, who would finally restructure the archipelago's spice economy from the ground up.34

Estado da India — "State of India," the name given to the Portuguese maritime empire in Asia (1505–1961), centred on a network of fortified trading posts (feitorias) stretching from East Africa to Japan. Its commercial strategy combined licensed trade with naval coercion; it was never able to achieve the full monopoly over the spice trade that its founders envisioned.

On the Eve of the VOC: The Commercial World the Dutch Would Inherit

By the closing decades of the sixteenth century, the archipelago's commercial world presented a picture of fragmented intensity rather than monopoly. Pepper from Sumatra and Java continued to flow in vast quantities through Banten, Jambi, and Palembang, carried by a combination of Javanese, Malay, Chinese, Gujarati, and now Portuguese merchants toward Indian and Middle Eastern markets, with some diverted to Lisbon via the Cape route. Cloves from Ternate and Tidore moved through a chaotic mixture of channels — some through Portuguese Malacca's successor ports, some directly in Javanese vessels, some in the hands of the Makassarese, who were emerging as a major commercial power on the southwestern tip of Sulawesi. Nutmeg and mace from the Banda Islands were sold primarily to Javanese traders who carried them to the great markets of Java's north coast, from which they entered the wider Indian Ocean circuit.35

The structures of this commerce — diverse, polyglot, driven by Asian capital and Asian logistics — would pose a formidable challenge to any new entrant seeking a monopoly. The VOC, when it arrived after 1602, understood this challenge in a way the Portuguese had not. Rather than simply inserting itself into the existing system, the VOC set about systematically destroying the Asian commercial networks that had sustained the trade for centuries, replacing them with a vertically integrated extractive monopoly backed by military force and the power of a state-chartered company with the legal authority to make war, negotiate treaties, and administer territory. The Banda massacre of 1621, in which VOC forces under Jan Pieterszoon Coen killed or enslaved the vast majority of the Banda Islands' population of perhaps fifteen thousand people and replaced them with Dutch-managed nutmeg plantations worked by enslaved labour, was the most violent and complete expression of this strategy.36 It was, as this book's subsequent chapters will detail, the hinge between the world of Asian commerce described in this chapter and the colonial order that followed.

The spice trade was not merely a commercial phenomenon. It was, as Paul Freedman argued of its effects on European imagination, a "force that remade the geography, politics, culture, economy, and ecology of the entire globe."37 The five volcanic islands of North Maluku and the ten small islands of the Banda Sea were the involuntary pivot of that remaking — their botany an accident of deep geological time that became, through the logic of global demand, one of the organizing facts of early modern world history. Understanding that trade, and the Asian commercial world it sustained before European violence disrupted it, is essential context for everything that follows in the colonial and post-colonial history of the Indonesian archipelago.

Summary

The spice trade was built on a biogeographical accident: cloves grew only on five small volcanic islands in North Maluku, nutmeg only on the Banda Islands in the Banda Sea, and the entire Indian Ocean world organized itself partly around reaching those points of production. Cloves were already travelling seven thousand kilometres to Syria by 1720 BCE; they were present at Han dynasty courts; they were known to Roman merchants who used the monsoon winds to reach the pepper coast of India. The medieval Indian Ocean trade was a sophisticated civilisational system, analysed by scholars such as K.N. Chaudhuri and M.N. Pearson, in which Gujarati, Arab, Malay, Javanese, and Chinese merchants participated in overlapping networks connected by the great entrepôt of Malacca. Tomé Pires's testimony — over eighty languages at Malacca, "hand on the throat of Venice" — captures the scale of this pre-European commerce. European desire for spices was real but has been persistently misunderstood: Paul Freedman's scholarship demolishes the preservation myth and shows that spices were prized for cuisine, medicine, and their cosmological status as gifts of Paradise. The search for a direct sea route to the source drove Columbus (misdirected), da Gama (successful, 1498), and Magellan (victorious at devastating cost, 1522). Portuguese seizure of Malacca in 1511 inserted European power into the existing Asian commercial system without replacing it; Asian merchants simply rerouted through Aceh, Banten, and other ports. The full destruction of the indigenous Malukan commercial world awaited the VOC and the Banda massacre of 1621 — the threshold between the world described in this chapter and the colonial order that followed.

What does this imply about the depth and complexity of pre-modern commercial networks? How does this challenge the common assumption that global trade was a product of European expansion?
