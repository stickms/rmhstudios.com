Learning Objectives

  • Trace the centuries-long Chinese presence in the archipelago from early trade sojourners to permanent settlers, distinguishing the peranakan and totok communities.
  • Explain how the VOC era shaped Chinese-Indonesian economic and political roles, and analyse the causes and consequences of the 1740 Batavia massacre.
  • Describe the colonial legal category of Vreemde Oosterlingen ("Foreign Orientals") and the kapitan system, and assess how they entrenched Chinese Indonesians as economic middlemen.
  • Understand the twentieth-century split between China-oriented and Indonesia-oriented identities, and evaluate the significance of 1965–66 and the New Order's assimilation campaign.
  • Analyse the relationship between Chinese-Indonesian conglomerates, crony capitalism, and the scapegoating violence of May 1998.
  • Assess the post-Reformasi revival of Chinese-Indonesian culture and politics, including Gus Dur's reforms and the Ahok case.
  • Survey the other minority diasporas of the archipelago — Hadhrami Arabs, Indians, and Eurasians — and compare their trajectories with the Chinese-Indonesian experience.

No minority community in Indonesian history has been more central to the country's economic life, more contested in its legal status, or more violently scapegoated in moments of crisis than the ethnic Chinese. Over seven centuries of settlement they built cities, financed colonial administrations, dominated retail and wholesale commerce, and erected conglomerates that shaped the modern economy — yet they spent much of their history classified as legally alien, culturally suspect, and politically disposable. This chapter traces their presence from the first sojourning merchants of the Song and Yuan dynasties to the post-Reformasi politicians who finally won seats in Jakarta's cabinet; it also surveys the archipelago's smaller but historically significant diasporas — the Hadhrami Arabs, Indian Tamils, and Eurasian Indos — whose stories intersect with both the Chinese experience and the larger drama of Indonesia's long struggle with diversity.

Sojourners and Settlers: Early Chinese Presence in the Archipelago

Chinese merchants were sailing the waters of the Indonesian archipelago at least as early as the seventh and eighth centuries CE, when the Buddhist kingdom of Srivijaya served as a major entrepôt for Indian Ocean trade and Chinese monks like Yijing stopped at Palembang on their way to and from India. The growth of the Song dynasty's maritime trade (960–1279 CE) — which for the first time exceeded overland commerce in total value — dramatically intensified Chinese commercial engagement with the southern seas. Chinese porcelain, silk, and bronze began appearing in ever-larger quantities at the same sites where Indian textiles and Middle Eastern glass had previously dominated exchange.1

Chinese emigration to the archipelago was officially discouraged, even banned, by the Ming dynasty (1368–1644), which sought to restore the tributary system and restrict private trade. The great Ming maritime expeditions under the Muslim eunuch admiral Zheng He (1405–1433) were state ventures, not settler expeditions, and the seas fell quiet after they ended. Yet despite official prohibitions, merchants from Fujian and Guangdong provinces continued to sail south, some settling permanently at Javanese and Sumatran port cities. By the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries there were established Chinese quarters at Tuban, Gresik, Demak, and Banten — ports that were simultaneously becoming centres of the new Islamic order. The early Chinese settlers were overwhelmingly Hokkien (Minnan) speakers from Fujian; later waves brought Hakka, Cantonese, and Teochew migrants.

The founding of Batavia in 1619 transformed the scale of Chinese presence in the archipelago. The VOC's first Governor-General, Jan Pieterszoon Coen, recognised that his tiny Dutch garrison could not build or run a colonial capital without outside labour and commercial expertise. Coen actively recruited Chinese migrants, writing that they were "the most industrious and best traders in these parts."2 Within a generation, Chinese immigrants constituted the essential workforce of Batavia: they built its warehouses and canals, operated its sugar mills, traded its markets, and collected revenues on its behalf. The pattern established in Batavia was replicated across the colonial archipelago — Chinese became the structural economic intermediary between the European colonial administration and the indigenous population, a role whose advantages were always shadowed by danger.

Nanyang — literally "Southern Seas," the Chinese term for the maritime Southeast Asian world. The phrase captures how Chinese emigrants imagined their destination: not as a foreign land to be assimilated into, but as a southern extension of the Chinese commercial world.

Peranakan and Totok: Two Chinese Worlds

The Chinese community in Indonesia was never homogeneous. The most important internal distinction — one that has structured Chinese-Indonesian politics from the seventeenth century to the present — is between the peranakan and the totok. The peranakan ("locally born") were descended from earlier male migrants who had married indigenous women, usually Javanese or Malay. Over generations they developed a syncretic hybrid culture: they spoke Malay or Javanese rather than Chinese, wore batik clothing, ate Chinese dishes enriched with local spices, and lived within the social rhythms of their Javanese neighbours while maintaining Chinese surnames, ancestral altars, and certain ceremonial practices. By the seventeenth century the peranakan community of Java was several generations deep.3

The totok ("pure-blooded" or "newcomer") were more recent arrivals who had come as adults from China and maintained Chinese language, dress, and cultural orientation. They were more likely to regard their sojourn in the archipelago as temporary, to remit money to families in Fujian or Guangdong, and to follow political developments in China closely. The distinction was never absolute — intermarriage and cultural borrowing blurred the line continuously — but it corresponded, broadly, to a difference in political orientation: peranakan generally identified with the local society in which they had grown up, while totok maintained Chinese nationalist or (later) Communist sympathies.4

In the early twentieth century the distinction gained political salience. The 1911 Xinhai Revolution that overthrew the Qing dynasty galvanised totok communities and some peranakan who had been drawn into the Chinese nationalist (Kuomintang) orbit. But peranakan intellectuals were simultaneously engaging with Indonesian nationalism, and a growing stream of Chinese-language and Malay-language newspapers debated whether Chinese Indonesians' future lay in China or in a free Indonesia. The scholar Leo Suryadinata, in his landmark studies of peranakan identity, identified this as the "great divide" of twentieth-century Chinese-Indonesian history: the choice between Chineseness (with its emotional pull toward the ancestral homeland and its practical expression in Chinese-language schooling) and Indonesianness (with its promise of civic equality in a future independent state).5

Peranakan — "locally born"; in Indonesian usage, ethnic Chinese who have been in the country for several generations and have adopted substantial aspects of local (particularly Javanese or Malay) culture, language, and dress, while retaining Chinese surnames and certain ceremonial practices. The term is also used in Malaysia and Singapore. Distinct from totok (more recent or China-oriented migrants).

The VOC Era: Collaboration, the Kapitan System, and the 1740 Massacre

The relationship between the VOC and the Chinese community in Batavia was, for its first century, a productive if unequal partnership. Chinese entrepreneurs operated the sugar mills that were Batavia's primary export industry, farmed out the collection of taxes and tolls on behalf of the colonial administration, and supplied the labourers, artisans, and small traders without whom the city could not function. The VOC institutionalised this relationship through the kapitan system: a hierarchy of Chinese community leaders — the Kapitein der Chinezen, his lieutenants (luitenant), and assistants (wijkmeester) — who served as intermediaries between the colonial government and the Chinese population, enforcing internal discipline, registering residents, resolving disputes under Chinese customary law, and delivering tax revenues.6

The first Kapitein, Souw Beng Kong, was appointed in Batavia in 1619. Subsequent kapitans were invariably wealthy peranakan merchants whose commercial interests aligned with the colonial order. The position was lucrative — kapitans held monopoly contracts on opium sales, gambling houses, markets, and river ferries — but it was also dangerous: kapitans stood between two worlds and could be blamed by either side when things went wrong. Charles Coppel has observed that the system, while it gave the Chinese community a measure of self-governance, also made its leaders complicit in colonial revenue extraction from the indigenous Javanese, deepening the structural resentments that would periodically explode into violence.7

By the 1730s, deep structural stresses had accumulated. Sugar mill profits had collapsed, partly because the surrounding land had been exhausted and partly because European demand was shifting to Caribbean sugar. Thousands of Chinese labourers, no longer needed on the mills, had drifted into Batavia's hinterland, where they scraped a living as itinerant traders and workers. The colonial administration, alarmed by this unregulated mass, began rounding up unemployed Chinese for deportation to Ceylon and the Cape Colony. Rumours spread — that the deportation ships were actually drowning their passengers, that the Dutch planned to massacre the Chinese. On the night of 7–8 October 1740, Chinese in the suburbs attacked a Dutch outpost. The colonial authorities declared martial law, and for the next several days Dutch soldiers and local auxiliaries systematically killed Chinese residents throughout Batavia. Contemporary estimates suggest between 5,000 and 10,000 were killed in what became known as the Batavia massacre, or the Chinezenmoord.8

In his magisterial reconstruction of the event, the Leiden historian Leonard Blussé argues that the massacre was not the inevitable product of Dutch colonial brutality but the catastrophic consequence of institutional breakdown — a century of productive collaboration undone by a toxic convergence of ecological decline, mass unemployment, administrative incompetence, and panic.9 The distinction matters: it resists both a narrative of Dutch genocide and a narrative of Chinese victimhood, presenting instead a contingent tragedy in which multiple actors' failures compounded. Whatever the causes, the consequences were permanent. The surviving Chinese were confined to a designated quarter outside the city walls — the Pecinan, the neighbourhood now known as Glodok — and placed under stricter surveillance. The massacre reverberated across the Indian Ocean world; even the Emperor of China protested, though his government took no retaliatory action.

Colonial Law, "Foreign Orientals," and Economic Middlemen

The transition from VOC to Dutch Crown rule in 1799–1800 brought no improvement in the legal status of Chinese Indonesians. If anything, the nineteenth century saw that status more precisely and disadvantageously codified. The Netherlands Indies operated under a strict three-tier legal classification: Europeanen (Europeans), Vreemde Oosterlingen ("Foreign Orientals"), and Inlanders (Natives). The middle category — covering Chinese, Arabs, and Indians — was defined from 1818 onward to encompass Asians from outside the archipelago who occupied an intermediate social and economic position: above natives in certain legal privileges, but firmly below Europeans.10

In practice the category was a mechanism of containment. Chinese were required to live in designated pecinan (Chinatown) quarters in each colonial city. A passenstelsel (pass system) restricted their movement outside these areas. They were subject to separate courts and different taxation regimes. They could not own agricultural land. Charles Coppel, in his analysis of the legal transitions experienced by Chinese Indonesians, argues that the "Foreign Orientals" category was deliberately ambiguous: the colonial state found Chinese commercial skills indispensable but wanted to ensure those skills remained controllable, channelled through officially sanctioned structures rather than allowed to develop autonomously.11

Within these constraints, Chinese Indonesians became essential to colonial economic functioning. They dominated retail trade throughout Java and the Outer Islands, serving as the final link in commodity chains that moved agricultural surplus from Javanese villages to European export houses. They operated the pacht system — the revenue farms by which the colonial government auctioned monopoly rights to collect taxes on opium, gambling, pawnbroking, slaughter, and markets to the highest bidder. Chinese entrepreneurs typically won these auctions, generating revenue for the colonial treasury while accumulating private capital. Ethnic Chinese entrepreneurs also came to dominate the tin mines of Bangka and Belitung, the tobacco plantations of Deli in North Sumatra (where tens of thousands of Chinese contract labourers worked under harsh koelie ordinances from the 1880s onward), and urban credit and wholesale networks across the archipelago.

This economic position made Chinese Indonesians structurally visible as instruments of colonial extraction in the eyes of indigenous Indonesians, even as it simultaneously made them subordinate and legally vulnerable in colonial eyes. The double marginality — privileged relative to natives in some legal and economic respects, but alien, suspect, and unprotected relative to Europeans — was the defining condition of Chinese Indonesian life under colonialism. It would persist, in various forms, through independence.

Vreemde Oosterlingen — "Foreign Orientals"; the Dutch colonial legal category that grouped Chinese, Arabs, and Indians in the Netherlands East Indies into an intermediate legal tier, subject to different laws and residential requirements than either Europeans or indigenous Indonesians (Inlanders).
Kapitan Cina — the Chinese community headman appointed by colonial authorities in the Netherlands East Indies (and earlier by the VOC). The first Kapitein der Chinezen was Souw Beng Kong, appointed in Batavia in 1619. Kapitans mediated between the colonial government and the Chinese community, maintaining order, registering residents, and managing revenue collection. They typically held monopoly concessions and were chosen from the wealthiest peranakan merchants.

Identity in Crisis: Twentieth-Century Politics and the 1965–66 Watershed

The opening decades of the twentieth century transformed Chinese-Indonesian politics. The fall of the Qing dynasty in 1911 electrified totok communities and parts of the peranakan elite who had been drawn into Chinese nationalist networks; Kuomintang (KMT) branches opened across the archipelago, and a network of Chinese-language schools (sekolah Tionghoa) aligned with either the KMT or, later, the Chinese Communist Party educated a generation in Chinese cultural and political identity. Simultaneously, peranakan intellectuals and organisations were engaging with Indonesian nationalism, with some prominent Chinese Indonesians contributing to the nationalist press and organisations from the 1910s onward.12

The Japanese occupation (1942–45) and the subsequent revolution (1945–49) forced choices. Some Chinese Indonesians joined the republican armed forces or supplied the revolution with money and logistics; others maintained neutrality; a small number collaborated with the Dutch. In general, peranakan communities were more likely to cast their lot with the Republic, while totok and KMT-oriented families either returned to China or waited to see which way the political winds blew. The founding of the People's Republic of China in 1949 added a new complication: dual nationality. The Indonesian Republic and the PRC concluded a Dual Nationality Treaty in 1955, requiring Chinese Indonesians to choose either Indonesian or Chinese citizenship within two years. Many chose Indonesia; others left for China, joining the "return to the motherland" (huiguo) movement that the PRC actively encouraged.13

Within Indonesian politics, Chinese Indonesians organised through several bodies, most prominently the Baperki (Badan Permusyawaratan Kewarganegaraan Indonesia, "Consultative Body on Indonesian Citizenship"), founded in 1954. Baperki advocated an "integrationist" policy: Chinese Indonesians should be full citizens without being required to abandon their cultural identity. Baperki was aligned with President Sukarno and over the following decade drifted into the orbit of the PKI (Indonesian Communist Party). The association was partially ideological — some Chinese Indonesian leftists genuinely identified with the PKI's anti-colonial, anti-capitalist platform — but was also structural: Baperki's welfare and education network overlapped with PKI-affiliated institutions.14

When the political crisis of 1965–66 shattered Sukarno's Guided Democracy, Chinese Indonesians were caught in a devastating triple bind. The PRC had been the PKI's principal external patron; Baperki was associated with the PKI; and anti-communist propaganda relentlessly conflated Chinese ethnicity with communist sympathy. In the aftermath of the alleged coup of 30 September/1 October 1965, Baperki was dissolved, Chinese-Indonesian institutions were attacked, and Chinese Indonesians throughout Java and Sumatra experienced harassment, property destruction, and violence. Coppel's careful research, however, distinguishes this from the mass killings of the period: the vast majority of the half-million or more people killed in 1965–66 were Javanese, Balinese, and Sundanese PKI members and sympathisers, not Chinese Indonesians. Anti-Chinese violence was widespread and traumatic but significantly less lethal than the anti-communist killings more broadly.15 The PRC's diplomatic ties with Indonesia were suspended in 1967 and not fully restored until 1990, a fact that further complicated the position of Chinese Indonesians who wanted to demonstrate their loyalty to Indonesia while maintaining any private connection to Chinese cultural heritage.

The New Order's Assimilation Campaign, 1966–1998

The New Order government that Suharto consolidated from 1966 onward approached the "Chinese problem" with systematic rigour. The regime's ideology combined two seemingly contradictory impulses: it subjected Chinese Indonesians to comprehensive cultural and political restrictions while simultaneously allowing — and structurally depending upon — Chinese-Indonesian dominance of the private business sector. The result was a paradox: a community that was economically essential and politically invisible, materially enriched in certain sectors and culturally suppressed everywhere.

Presidential Instruction No. 14 of 1967 banned all forms of Chinese cultural, religious, and ceremonial expression from public spaces. Chinese-language schools — some two hundred and fifty institutions that had educated generations of totok and peranakan children — were shuttered. The Chinese-language press, which had operated continuously since the early twentieth century, was closed. Public celebration of Chinese New Year, the performance of lion dances, the display of Chinese characters on shop fronts — all were prohibited. Even within temples, Chinese-language rituals had to be kept behind closed doors. Confucianism was removed from the list of officially recognised religions; Chinese Indonesians who practised the blended Confucian-Buddhist-Daoist folk religion of their ancestors had to register officially as Buddhist, Christian, or (rarely) Muslim.16

The administrative pressures were compounded by a citizenship bureaucracy designed to remind Chinese Indonesians of their precarious belonging. The Surat Bukti Kewarganegaraan Republik Indonesia (SBKRI, "Certificate of Proof of Indonesian Citizenship") was required specifically of people of Chinese descent — not of other Indonesians — to access schools, banks, notarial services, and many forms of employment. It was expensive, time-consuming to obtain, and had to be renewed; every interaction with this system reinforced the message that Chinese Indonesians were citizens only provisionally. Chinese Indonesians were effectively barred from the military and civil service, further concentrating them in the private sector.17

The pressures on naming were less formal but pervasive. From the late 1960s onward, Chinese Indonesians were strongly encouraged — and in many practical contexts required — to adopt Indonesian-sounding names. The practice was semi-voluntary, but for those seeking employment, admission to government schools, or dealings with government offices, a Chinese surname became an obstacle. Tens of thousands of families adopted two-name personas: a Chinese name used in the community, an Indonesian name for official purposes. Charlotte Setijadi has argued that this double naming was symptomatic of a broader condition of unbelonging — an internalized split between the identity one maintained in private and the identity one was permitted to express in public — whose psychological legacy would shape Chinese-Indonesian politics long after the New Order's fall.18

Business Empires, Crony Capitalism, and the Architecture of Resentment

The New Order's paradox — cultural suppression combined with economic enablement — produced the most concentrated Chinese-Indonesian business empires in the country's history. Suharto's system of cukong (business patron) relationships functioned as a kind of economic franchise: Chinese entrepreneurs provided capital, commercial networks, and management expertise; Suharto's family and military cronies provided political protection, access to state contracts and monopolies, and immunity from regulation. The result was a small number of Chinese-controlled conglomerates that came to dominate the commanding heights of the Indonesian private economy.

The most studied example is the Salim Group, built by Liem Sioe Liong (Anthony Salim's father), who had arrived in Java from Fujian as a penniless migrant in 1938 and became Suharto's principal business partner over the following decades. At its apogee, the Salim Group controlled Indonesia's largest private bank (Bank Central Asia), the country's dominant cement producer, its monopoly flour miller, and through the Indofood subsidiary the world's largest instant noodle manufacturer. Estimates of the group's total scope ran to some six hundred affiliated companies. As Richard Borsuk and Nancy Chng document in their exhaustive history, Salim's growth was inseparable from presidential patronage: state contracts, subsidised inputs, and monopoly rights flowed to Salim enterprises in exchange for financial support for Suharto family ventures and New Order political operations.19

Other Chinese-Indonesian conglomerates — the Sinar Mas Group (Eka Tjipta Widjaja), the Lippo Group (Mochtar Riady), the Gajah Tunggal Group (Sjamsul Nursalim) — operated on similar principles. By the 1990s, studies suggested that ethnic Chinese Indonesians, perhaps 3 percent of the national population, controlled a disproportionate share of private corporate assets — sometimes cited at 70 percent or more, though such figures were contested and difficult to verify precisely. The structural explanation for this concentration was rarely heard in public discourse: colonial history had excluded Chinese from land ownership and civil service employment, channelling accumulated skills and capital into commerce; the New Order then excluded Chinese from politics while relying on them for private investment. But the popular narrative was simpler and more dangerous: the Chinese were exploiting Indonesia. Periodic episodes of anti-Chinese violence from the 1960s onward tapped this resentment, as Human Rights Watch documented in a 1998 report that traced years of scapegoating rhetoric by military officials who blamed Chinese entrepreneurs for hoarding foreign currency and profiteering from the poor.20

May 1998: Violence, Exodus, and the Failure of the State

The Asian financial crisis of 1997–98 struck Indonesia with unusual severity. The rupiah lost more than eighty percent of its value against the US dollar between mid-1997 and mid-1998; prices of basic foodstuffs — rice, cooking oil, sugar — soared beyond the reach of urban poor families. As mass unemployment spread, the scapegoating of Chinese Indonesians intensified. Senior military officials made inflammatory public statements; market traders spread stories of Chinese merchants hoarding goods; the mass media amplified outrage. By early 1998 sporadic attacks on Chinese-owned shops were occurring across Java and Sumatra.21

The worst violence erupted in Jakarta on 13–15 May 1998, just days before Suharto's resignation. Rioters attacked, looted, and burned hundreds of Chinese-owned businesses and homes across the capital; several shopping malls were set alight with people inside, killing an estimated 1,188 individuals, most of them rioters trapped in burning buildings. In Medan, Solo, and other cities, similar patterns unfolded. The violence was not spontaneous in any simple sense: investigations by the National Commission on Human Rights and subsequent inquiries found evidence that elements of the military had organised or facilitated the rioting, possibly as part of a factional struggle within the armed forces in which ethnic Chinese property served as an instrument of destabilisation.22

Among the most disturbing aspects of the May 1998 violence was the sexual violence directed specifically at Chinese-Indonesian women. The Volunteer Team for Humanity (Tim Relawan untuk Kemanusiaan) documented at least 168 cases of sexual violence in Jakarta alone during 13–15 May, the great majority targeting women identified as ethnic Chinese. Human Rights Watch, in a September 1998 report, described the rapes as "widespread, systematic, and sadistic," and the National Human Rights Commission subsequently confirmed that mass rape had occurred while the security forces failed to intervene.23 The perpetrators were never prosecuted; the government's initial response combined denial and minimisation. A 2022 scholarly study by Himawan, Pohlman, and Louis, drawing on survey data from Indonesian civilians, confirmed that the 1998 riots remain a deeply unprocessed trauma in Indonesian public memory, with many survivors and witnesses still hesitant to speak publicly about what they witnessed.24

The immediate consequence of the May 1998 violence was a massive haemorrhage of Chinese-Indonesian people, capital, and talent. Tens of thousands of ethnic Chinese left Indonesia for Singapore, Australia, Hong Kong, and Taiwan. Estimates of capital flight attributable to the crisis and its ethnic dimension ran to tens of billions of dollars. Many who left did not return; those who stayed faced the question of how to rebuild trust in a country that had, in the most visceral way, demonstrated their fragile citizenship. Setijadi argues that the May 1998 violence became the foundational trauma narrative around which post-Reformasi Chinese-Indonesian identity politics organised: not the quieter daily humiliations of the New Order, but the explicitness of murder and rape and arson, witnessed by the world and acknowledged only reluctantly by the Indonesian state.25

Reformasi and Revival: The Post-1998 Era

The fall of Suharto on 21 May 1998 — the same week as the worst of the anti-Chinese violence — opened a period of rapid, if uneven, reversal. The first substantive shift came under President Abdurrahman Wahid ("Gus Dur"), who served from October 1999 to July 2001 and who has been called, with good reason, the "father of Chinese-Indonesian pluralism." Wahid issued Presidential Instruction No. 6 of 2000, which revoked the 1967 ban on Chinese cultural expression in public. The practical changes were immediate and vivid: the sound of firecrackers on Chinese New Year returned to Indonesian streets, lion and dragon dances resumed in shopping-mall forecourts, and Chinese-language signage began reappearing on shop fronts for the first time in three decades.26

Wahid also pushed for the formal recognition of Confucianism (Konghucu) as Indonesia's sixth official religion, alongside Islam, Protestantism, Catholicism, Hinduism, and Buddhism — a recognition that had political and practical implications for Chinese Indonesians who identified with the blended folk religious tradition that colonial-era regulations had forced underground. Chang-Yau Hoon's analysis of what he calls "the politics of Imlek" (Chinese New Year) shows that even these gains were contested: Confucian organisations strategically repackaged Imlek as a religious holiday marking Confucius's birth, which secured its status as a state-recognised holiday under President Megawati Sukarnoputri (who formally gazetted it in 2002) — but this framing was resented by Chinese Indonesians who viewed Imlek as a purely cultural celebration and who felt excluded by the Confucian institutional claim.27 The politics of cultural revival, Hoon argues, remained entangled with questions of identity: increased public Chineseness paradoxically reinforced New Order-era suspicions that Chineseness and Indonesianness were incompatible.

Further legislative changes followed. The 2006 Law on Indonesian Citizenship formally abolished the SBKRI requirement, making citizenship documentation the same for all Indonesians regardless of ethnic origin. Chinese-language teaching was permitted in schools, and Chinese-language media — newspapers, television stations, internet portals — was revived. The post-Reformasi period saw Chinese Indonesians more willing to participate openly in politics: as candidates in regional elections, as ministers, and most prominently in the career of Basuki Tjahaja Purnama, known as Ahok.

Ahok, an ethnic Chinese Christian from Bangka island, served as a regional parliamentarian, then as Deputy Governor and Governor of Jakarta (2012–2017). His tenure as governor was marked by bold and often controversial policies — clearing illegal settlements, fighting corruption in city procurement, improving services — and by a bluntness of manner that won both admiration and fierce opposition. In 2016, an edited video of a speech in which Ahok cited the Quranic verse Al-Maidah 51 (often quoted to argue that Muslims should not be governed by non-Muslims) was spread on social media with doctoring that implied he had insulted the Quran. Mass protests by conservative Islamic organisations — the "Aksi 212" demonstrations of November–December 2016, drawing hundreds of thousands of participants — demanded his prosecution. In May 2017 the North Jakarta District Court convicted Ahok of blasphemy under Article 156a of the Criminal Code and sentenced him to two years in prison, one month after he lost Jakarta's gubernatorial election.28

Legal scholar Simon Butt's analysis of the verdict identifies serious procedural deficiencies: the court adopted prosecution witnesses' conclusions wholesale without independent evaluation, largely ignored evidence that the video had been doctored, and convicted Ahok under a more serious charge than the prosecutors themselves had requested — a procedural irregularity without parallel in comparable cases.29 The Ahok case drew international attention as a test of Indonesian pluralism, and the outcome was widely interpreted as a signal that ethnic Chinese Christians could be prosecuted for alleged religious offences in ways that Muslims could not be. Ahok was released in January 2019; he subsequently served as president commissioner of the state oil company Pertamina and remained a significant figure in Indonesian civic life. His case demonstrated both how far Chinese-Indonesian political participation had come since the New Order and how far it remained from full civic equality.

Other Diasporas: Arabs, Indians, and Eurasians

The Chinese were the most numerous and most politically fraught of the archipelago's overseas communities, but they were not alone. The Hadhrami Arabs, the Tamil and Gujarati Indians, and the Eurasian Indos each constituted distinct diasporic communities with their own histories, trajectories, and relationships to the colonial and post-colonial state.

The Hadhrami Arabs

Arab merchants from the Hadramawt region of southern Yemen had been sailing the Indian Ocean for centuries; by the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries they were well established in the port cities of Java, Sumatra, and the eastern archipelago, where they combined trade with Islamic scholarship and proselytisation. The genealogical tradition of the Hadhrami diaspora centred on the sayyid class — those who claimed descent from the Prophet Muhammad through his grandson Husayn — whose religious authority was portable across the Indian Ocean world. The inclusion of Hadhrami sayyids among the legendary Wali Songo ("Nine Saints") credited in Javanese tradition with Islamising Java reflects the deep integration of Hadhrami religious networks into Indonesian Islam.30

The anthropologist Engseng Ho, in his landmark study of Hadhrami genealogy and mobility, argues that the Hadhrami diaspora achieved a distinctive form of globalisation: by marrying local women and embedding their descendants in local societies while maintaining genealogical and textual connections across the Indian Ocean, Hadhrami sayyids became "cosmopolitan locals" — simultaneously at home everywhere and connected to a transoceanic community of faith and descent.31 This dynamic produced an Indonesian Arab community that was, by the colonial period, substantially peranakan in character: descended from Hadhrami men and local women, speaking Malay or Javanese, culturally Indonesian in most respects, yet claiming religious prestige through maintained genealogical connection to Hadramawt.

Like Chinese Indonesians, Arab Indonesians were classified as Vreemde Oosterlingen under colonial law — legally subordinate to Europeans but above Natives, and subject to residential and movement restrictions. In the early twentieth century, the Hadhrami community engaged actively with the Indonesian nationalist movement. The Persatoean Arab Indonesia (PAI, Arab Indonesian Union), founded in 1934, advocated assimilation into the Indonesian national community rather than maintenance of a separate Arab identity; it produced several figures who participated in the independence movement. The sociologist Frode Jacobsen, in a study of contemporary Hadhrami Arabs in Indonesia, describes the community as "an Indonesia-oriented group with an Arab signature" — thoroughly Indonesian in political identity and daily life, yet maintaining Islamic religious practices, Arabic-derived names, and a sense of genealogical connection to the Arabian Peninsula that distinguishes them from the broader Muslim Indonesian population.32 The contemporary Arab Indonesian community numbers perhaps three to four million, concentrated in Java, though precise figures are difficult to establish since many have assimilated so thoroughly that they no longer self-identify as Arab-descended.

The Indian Diaspora

Indian merchants — particularly Tamil Chettiars and Nagori traders, and Gujarati Muslims — had participated in the Indian Ocean trading world for centuries, leaving traces in the religious and cultural vocabulary of classical Indonesian civilisation. The Tamils who would form modern Indonesia's Indian community, however, arrived mostly in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries as contract labourers on the Deli tobacco and rubber plantations of North Sumatra. Dutch plantation companies recruited Tamil workers from South India through a system of indenture that, despite reform efforts, retained features of debt bondage well into the twentieth century. Conditions on the Deli plantations were notoriously harsh, and the coolie ordinances that governed Tamil (and Chinese) contract labour were among the most controversial instruments of colonial labour policy.33

Today, between forty and seventy-five thousand Indian-Indonesians remain in the country, concentrated in North Sumatra's cities — Medan, Binjai, Lubuk Pakam — where a Tamil neighbourhood known as Kampung Madras preserves temples, shops, and cultural institutions. The Indian-Indonesian community is significantly smaller and less economically prominent than either the Chinese or Arab communities, and has largely avoided the political turbulence that marked those groups. Many Tamil descendants have retained their Hindu religious identity; others have converted to Islam or Christianity. Gujarati Muslim traders, whose descendants became fully absorbed into the broader Indonesian Muslim merchant class centuries ago, are effectively invisible as a distinct community.

Eurasians and Indos

The Eurasian community — known as Indos (from "Indo-European") — was the product of centuries of intimate contact between European men and Asian women in the colonial setting. VOC soldiers and merchants, many of whom had no prospect of marrying Dutch women, took indigenous concubines or wives; their children occupied an ambiguous but legally significant position. By the nineteenth century a substantial "Eurasian" or "Indo" population existed throughout the Netherlands East Indies, legally classified as "European" if their European father had acknowledged them, but culturally and economically distinct from full-blooded ("totok") Dutch colonials.34

Ann Laura Stoler's influential scholarship on intimacy and colonial rule demonstrates that the management of these mixed-race families was not merely a private matter but a central preoccupation of colonial governance. As racial hierarchies hardened in the late nineteenth century, defining the boundaries of "Europeanness" became urgent: Indo children had legal claims to European rights that the administration found expensive and politically inconvenient. The result was a complex of legal and social mechanisms — from school fees to poor-relief policies to citizenship laws — that acknowledged Indo European legal status while marginalising Indos economically and socially within the colonial order.35 Many Indos found employment in the lower rungs of the colonial civil service, the railways, and the army, forming a distinct middle-class community that identified more strongly with "being Dutch" than with the indigenous Indonesian societies from which their mothers came.

The end of the colonial period brought the greatest rupture. After Indonesian independence, most Dutch and Indo families "repatriated" to the Netherlands — a process that continued through the 1950s and early 1960s, eventually bringing some three hundred thousand people to a country many had never seen. Those who remained in Indonesia chose Indonesian citizenship and, over subsequent generations, largely assimilated into the broader Indonesian population. Today, Indonesians of partial European descent number in the hundreds of thousands by some estimates, but they have no recognised collective political identity comparable to the Chinese-Indonesian community; individual families retain or ignore their European heritage according to personal circumstance.

Contested claims — the 1998 sexual violence. The scale and nature of sexual violence against Chinese-Indonesian women during the May 1998 riots remains contested between human rights organisations, which have documented hundreds of cases and described systematic organisation, and successive Indonesian governments, which have acknowledged violence while disputing its scope and coordination. The National Human Rights Commission's 1998 report confirmed mass rape occurred; military and government officials continued to dispute figures and characterise findings as exaggerated for political purposes. As of 2026, no perpetrators have been prosecuted for the sexual violence, and the government has not issued a formal apology. Scholars across the political spectrum agree that establishing definitive numbers is impossible given the breakdown of security infrastructure and the reluctance of many survivors to speak; what is not in dispute is that targeted sexual violence against women identified as ethnically Chinese occurred during the riots.

Contested claims — Chinese share of private corporate wealth. Figures claiming that ethnic Chinese Indonesians controlled seventy percent or more of private corporate assets during the Suharto era circulated widely and are still cited, but they rest on contested methodological foundations. Distinguishing "Chinese-owned" from jointly-owned, nominee-held, or military-partnered enterprises is methodologically difficult, and the figures were often generated by or for political purposes. Scholars including Coppel and Suryadinata have cautioned against their uncritical use. What is less contested is that Chinese-Indonesian entrepreneurs were disproportionately represented at the upper end of the private business hierarchy — a concentration that had deep structural roots in colonial economic history, not simply in cultural traits or conspiratorial organisation.

Summary

Chinese Indonesians have been present in the archipelago for centuries, forming two broadly distinct communities — the locally-rooted peranakan and the China-oriented totok — whose internal debates over identity and loyalty have shaped their politics from the colonial period onward. Under the VOC the Chinese were essential economic partners, operating the kapitan system and the revenue farms, but they were also vulnerable: the 1740 Batavia massacre demonstrated how violently a century of collaboration could collapse. Colonial law formalised their intermediate legal position as Vreemde Oosterlingen ("Foreign Orientals"), entrenching them as economic middlemen while excluding them from political life. The twentieth century brought difficult choices between Chinese nationalist and Indonesian nationalist identities; the events of 1965–66 associated Chinese Indonesians with the PKI and the PRC, providing the ideological foundation for the New Order's comprehensive assimilation campaign — closing schools, banning the press, requiring name changes, and conditioning citizenship on periodic bureaucratic humiliation. Simultaneously, the New Order enabled a handful of Chinese-Indonesian conglomerates to grow into dominant economic forces through crony relationships with Suharto's family, creating a visible wealth gap that fuelled popular resentment. The May 1998 violence — looting, arson, murder, and mass rape targeting Chinese-Indonesian communities — was the crisis this resentment produced. The post-Reformasi era brought rapid reversals: Gus Dur's 2000 presidential instruction, the recognition of Confucianism, the return of public Chinese New Year celebrations, and new legislative citizenship protections. The career and prosecution of Ahok (Basuki Tjahaja Purnama) showed both how far Chinese-Indonesian political participation had advanced and how fragile those gains remained. The Hadhrami Arab, Indian, and Eurasian diasporas each traced different trajectories — the Arabs deeply integrated into Indonesian Islamic life; the Tamil-descended Indians small and concentrated in North Sumatra; the Indos largely repatriated to the Netherlands after independence — but all shared with the Chinese the experience of legal ambiguity, colonial categorisation, and the challenge of belonging in a state whose definition of citizenship was never entirely settled.

Review & Discussion Questions

47.1 The distinction between peranakan and totok Chinese Indonesians has been described as the "great divide" of twentieth-century Chinese-Indonesian history. What made this divide so politically consequential, and how did it shape responses to Indonesian nationalism?
47.2 Leonard Blussé argues that the 1740 Batavia massacre was not inevitable but the product of contingent institutional breakdown. How does this framing change our understanding of Dutch colonial responsibility for the event?
47.3 How did the Vreemde Oosterlingen legal category simultaneously benefit and disadvantage Chinese Indonesians in the colonial period? What structural consequences did it have for inter-ethnic relations under and after colonialism?
47.4 The New Order simultaneously suppressed Chinese-Indonesian cultural identity and allowed Chinese-Indonesian business empires to flourish. How do you explain this apparent contradiction, and what were its political consequences?
47.5 Human rights organisations documented widespread sexual violence against Chinese-Indonesian women during the May 1998 riots, but the Indonesian government has never prosecuted perpetrators or issued a formal apology. What does this impunity reveal about the relationship between ethnic violence, state accountability, and historical memory in Indonesia?
47.6 Chang-Yau Hoon argues that the post-Reformasi revival of public Chineseness paradoxically reinforced the New Order premise that "Chineseness" and "Indonesianness" are incompatible. Do you find this argument persuasive? What evidence would you need to evaluate it?
47.7 How do the trajectories of the Hadhrami Arab and the ethnic Chinese diasporas in Indonesia compare? In what ways did they face similar challenges, and how did their different religious status shape their relationship to the Indonesian state?
47.8 The Ahok blasphemy case has been interpreted both as evidence of resurgent religious intolerance and as evidence of the increasing political prominence of Chinese Indonesians. Which interpretation do you find more persuasive, and why?
47.9 Compare the post-independence trajectories of the three "Foreign Oriental" communities — Chinese, Arabs, and Indians — under successive Indonesian governments. What factors explain the differences in how these groups were treated and how they fared?
