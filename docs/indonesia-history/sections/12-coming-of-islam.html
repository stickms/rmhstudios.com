Learning Objectives

  • Trace the arrival and gradual spread of Islam across the archipelago from the thirteenth century onward, and explain why historians describe conversion as a process rather than an event.
  • Weigh the earliest material and textual evidence — the Leran and Samudra Pasai gravestones, Marco Polo's report of Perlak, and Ibn Battuta's visit of 1345–46 — and assess what such sources can and cannot prove.
  • Compare the competing theories of Islamization (Gujarati/Indian, Arab, Persian, Bengali, and Chinese-Muslim origins) and identify the scholars who advanced them.
  • Distinguish the principal proposed mechanisms of conversion — merchant trade networks, royal conversion and the prestige economy, and Sufi missionary activity — and follow the debate between A. H. Johns and his critics.
  • Explain the role of the Wali Songo in the Islamization of Java and the blending of Islamic and pre-Islamic cultural forms.
  • Evaluate Clifford Geertz's abangan/santri framework, the anachronism it risks, and the major scholarly critiques and revisions of it.

No army carried Islam to the Indonesian archipelago. There was no single conquest, no founding battle, no decisive date that historians can point to and say: here the islands became Muslim. Instead Islam arrived in the holds of trading ships, in the marriage contracts of port-city dynasties, in the devotional poems of wandering mystics, and in the shadow-play performances of saints who told old stories in a new key. It came slowly, unevenly, and along the same maritime roads that had earlier carried Hindu and Buddhist ideas into the islands. Today Indonesia is the most populous Muslim-majority nation on earth, yet the manner of its conversion remains one of the most debated questions in Southeast Asian history. This chapter follows the coming of Islam — the scraps of evidence, the rival theories, the saints and sultans and Sufis — and is honest about how much we still do not know.

A faith carried by trade

The single most important thing to grasp about the Islamization of Indonesia is that it was a process, not an episode. As the historian Peter Sluglett put it in a concise survey of the question, conversion across the region unfolded through "a series of long drawn out and incremental developments," beginning with the arrival of Muslim merchants and accelerating between the thirteenth and the fifteenth centuries, moving in general from coast to interior and from ruler to subject.1 Muslim traders were present in the waters of Southeast Asia for centuries before any local ruler adopted their faith. The shipping lanes that linked the Red Sea and the Persian Gulf to the ports of India, and India in turn to the Strait of Malacca and the South China Sea, had been busy since well before the rise of Islam; once the merchants who plied them were Muslims, Islam travelled with their cargoes.

The Dutch scholar J. C. van Leur, in a set of essays that reshaped the study of early Indonesian commerce, insisted that this trade be understood on its own terms rather than as a mere prelude to European expansion. He characterized pre-modern Asian commerce as a "peddling trade" conducted by small operators, and he argued, in a famous and much-quoted phrase, that Islam in its first centuries in the islands was little more than a "thin and flaking glaze" over a deeply Indic society — a description that captured the superficiality of early conversion even as it provoked decades of disagreement about how thin that glaze really was.2 Whatever its depth, the link between commerce and conversion is not in doubt. The Australian historian Anthony Reid, in his two-volume study Southeast Asia in the Age of Commerce, 1450–1680, set Islamization inside a larger transformation: a boom in maritime trade, urbanization, and state-building during which Islam and Christianity "made rapid gains in alliance with the new states." For Reid the period witnessed an intense "religious revolution," in which world faiths competed for the allegiance of commercializing coastal societies and Islam won decisively across the archipelago.3

Islamization — the long, uneven process by which Islam spread through the archipelago and was woven into local society, law, and culture. Historians stress that it was gradual, regionally variable, and never simply replaced what came before.

The earliest evidence: gravestones and travellers

What is the hardest evidence for the presence of Islam in the islands, and how early does it go? The answer comes from two kinds of source — inscribed gravestones and the reports of foreign travellers — and each must be handled with care.

The oldest dated Muslim gravestone yet found in the archipelago lies far from the centres usually associated with early Islam. At Leran, a hamlet near Gresik on the north coast of East Java, a stone records the death of a woman named Fatimah binti Maimun, in Arabic Kufic script, with a date corresponding to 1082 CE.4 The stone is genuinely puzzling: it sits in the heartland of Hindu-Buddhist Java, two centuries before any local conversion is otherwise attested, and scholars disagree sharply over what it means. It may mark the grave of a member of a small foreign merchant community — a settled but isolated pocket of Muslims doing business at a Javanese port — rather than any evidence of Javanese conversion. Indeed, the medievalist Elizabeth Lambourn has cautioned that gravestones in general have been made to bear too much interpretive weight in this field. In a study of the Samudra Pasai stones she argued for treating tombstones as "integral cultural products" in which material, image, and text matter equally, and she showed that some of the most famous stones may be later replacements set up to manufacture a dynastic pedigree rather than contemporary records of the deaths they commemorate.5 A gravestone proves that someone Muslim was buried at a place; it does not, by itself, prove that the surrounding society had become Muslim.

With that caution in mind, the decisive cluster of evidence appears in the late thirteenth century in northern Sumatra. In 1292, on his homeward voyage from China, the Venetian Marco Polo touched at the small kingdom of Perlak (which he called "Ferlec") on the northern tip of Sumatra and recorded that its townspeople, through constant contact with Muslim merchants, had abandoned their old beliefs: "the people of Ferlec used all to be idolaters, but owing to contact with the Saracen merchants, who continually resort here in their ships, they have all been converted to the law of Mahomet" — while, he noted, the hill peoples of the interior remained "as beasts."6 Polo's report is the earliest European notice of a Muslim polity in the islands, and it captures the essential pattern: conversion in the trading town, persistence of older ways in the highlands behind it.

Close to Perlak lay the kingdom that became the archetype of the first Indonesian sultanate: Samudra Pasai. Its founding ruler, Sultan Malik al-Saleh, died in 1297; his gravestone, inscribed entirely in Arabic and dated to Ramadan 696 in the Muslim calendar, is the earliest firmly dated evidence of a Muslim ruler anywhere in the archipelago.7 Half a century later the kingdom received its most celebrated visitor. In 1345–46 the great Moroccan traveller Ibn Battuta, on his way to China, stopped at Samudra Pasai for about two weeks as the guest of its sultan, al-Malik al-Zahir. He found a thriving Muslim court whose ruler was, in his telling, "a most illustrious and open-handed" prince, devout, learned, given to waging campaigns against non-Muslim neighbours, and a follower — like the Indian Muslims of the Malabar coast whom Ibn Battuta had already met — of the Shafi'i school of law (madhhab).8 Crucially, Ibn Battuta regarded Samudra Pasai as the easternmost outpost of Dar al-Islam, the domain of Islam; beyond it, he understood, lay lands ruled by no Muslim prince. Taken together, Marco Polo and Ibn Battuta bracket the establishment of Islam at the apex of Sumatran society within the space of about fifty years, between roughly 1290 and 1345.

That Java was not far behind is suggested by the cemetery at Tralaya, near Trowulan, the very capital of the Hindu-Buddhist empire of Majapahit. The French epigrapher Louis-Charles Damais published a study of the Muslim gravestones found there, some bearing dates in Old Javanese numerals rather than Arabic ones, indicating Muslims — quite possibly Javanese Muslims — living and dying at the heart of the Majapahit realm in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, while the court itself remained Hindu-Buddhist.9 Islam, in other words, was present inside Majapahit long before Majapahit became Muslim.

Where did Islam come from? The competing theories

If Islam arrived with merchants, which merchants, from where? This apparently simple question has generated a century of scholarly argument, and the rival answers have come to be known as the theories of Islamization. None has won universal assent, and most historians now regard them less as mutually exclusive alternatives than as overlapping descriptions of a genuinely plural process.

The Gujarati (or Indian) theory was the orthodoxy of Dutch colonial scholarship. Its classic formulation came from the great Leiden Islamicist Christiaan Snouck Hurgronje, who reasoned that once Muslims controlled the port cities of western India, Indian Muslims from Gujarat and the Deccan became the natural "middlemen" propagating the faith eastward to the archipelago. Evidence was found in the style of the imported marble gravestones at Pasai, which appeared to match Gujarati workshops, and in the documented dominance of Gujarati merchants at later trading centres.10 The theory has a notorious weakness, however: Gujarat itself was not brought under firm Muslim rule until around 1298 — a year after the death of Malik al-Saleh — which makes it awkward to treat Gujarat as the fountainhead of an Islam already established at Pasai by 1297.11

The Arab theory holds that Islam came directly, and early, from Arabia, carried by Arab — and especially later Hadhrami (Yemeni) — traders and scholars. Its most influential early exponent was the British Orientalist Thomas Walker Arnold, whose The Preaching of Islam (1896; revised 1913) argued across a chapter on the Malay archipelago that the faith had spread overwhelmingly by peaceful commercial contact rather than by the sword.12 Supporters point to the prevalence of the Shafi'i school of law — the same school followed in Yemen and South Arabia — and to the steady migration of Hadhrami sayyids and scholars in later centuries.

The Persian theory, associated above all with the Indonesian scholar P. A. Hoesein Djajadiningrat, looks instead to Iran. Djajadiningrat pointed to the striking parallels between Javanese mystical doctrine — especially the teaching of union between servant and Lord (manunggaling kawula gusti) attributed to the heterodox saint Siti Jenar — and the ecstatic formulas of Persian Sufism, above all al-Hallaj's notorious "I am the Truth." He noted, too, the Javanese observance of the tenth of Muharram and certain ritual foods that seemed to echo Iranian Shi'a practice.13 The Bengali theory, argued by S. Q. Fatimi in Islam Comes to Malaysia (1963), made a related move on different evidence, contending from the form of early tombstones that the decisive impulse came not from Gujarat but from Bengal in eastern India.14

Finally, a more recent and frankly contested proposal is the Chinese-Muslim theory, which credits the Muslim Chinese admiral Zheng He (Cheng Ho) and his treasure-fleet voyages of 1405–33 with seeding Islam in Java's north-coast ports. The Chinese scholar Kong Yuanzhi has assembled the case that Zheng He helped establish Chinese-Muslim communities at Palembang, Semarang, Gresik, and elsewhere and appointed Muslim agents to oversee them.15 But Kong himself concedes that Chinese archives contain "no relevant record" of any missionary activity, and that the detailed account of the voyages by Zheng He's interpreter Ma Huan, written in 1433, mentions Muslim communities in Java without crediting the fleet with creating them. The theory remains a minority position, useful chiefly as a reminder of the Chinese-Muslim presence on the Java coast rather than as a settled explanation of Islamization.

The richest single window onto the merchant world that actually carried Islam is the Suma Oriental of the Portuguese apothecary Tomé Pires, compiled at Malacca between 1512 and 1515. Pires described a cosmopolitan port in which roughly a thousand Gujarati merchants resided, served by their own harbour-master (syahbandar), advising the sultan and receiving great annual cargoes from Cambay.16 His account is the foundational primary-source evidence that embedded Muslim merchant communities, Gujarati prominent among them, preceded and underwrote the formal conversion of rulers — the mechanism that all the origin theories, however they differ on geography, ultimately share.

Why convert? Trade, courts, and Sufis

Knowing where the merchants came from does not explain why local people — and especially local rulers — chose to become Muslim. Three broad mechanisms have dominated the scholarship: the pull of the trade networks themselves, the logic of royal conversion, and the appeal of Sufi mysticism.

The first is structural. Anthony Reid's "age of commerce" thesis ties conversion to the explosive growth of maritime trade after about 1450: as port-states grew rich on pepper, cloves, and the carrying trade, adopting the religion of the wider commercial world brought a ruler into a vast network of co-religionists, shared law, and prestige, and Islam advanced precisely "in alliance with the new states" that the commercial boom threw up.17 The second mechanism explains why the ruler converted first. The historian O. W. Wolters, in his influential reflections on Southeast Asian statecraft, described indigenous polities as built around charismatic "men of prowess" who attracted followings by demonstrating superior spiritual potency, and he coined the term localization for the way Southeast Asian societies selectively absorbed foreign ideas and recast them as local statements.18 In such a world, a ruler who embraced Islam gained access to Arabic literacy, a sophisticated legal tradition, and the glamour of a cosmopolitan faith — and his subjects, by the logic of the prestige economy, tended to follow him. Conversion moved from the top down.

prestige economy — the dynamic by which a ruler's adoption of Islam, and the cosmopolitan prestige, literacy, and trade connections it conferred, drew subjects and rivals to follow. It helps explain why Islamization characteristically began with courts and ports rather than with the peasantry.

The third mechanism is the most debated. In a landmark paper of 1961, the Australian scholar A. H. Johns proposed that the decisive agents of mass conversion were wandering Sufi mystics whose flexible, experiential spirituality could accommodate the animist and Hindu-Buddhist sensibilities of the islanders, easing them into Islam without demanding an abrupt rupture with the past.19 The Sufis, claiming miraculous powers (karamat) and offering initiation into orders, resembled the holy men the islands already knew. Johns developed the argument through his editions of Sufi texts that had circulated in the Malay-Indonesian world, such as the seventeenth-century devotional treatise The Gift Addressed to the Spirit of the Prophet.20 The thesis was elegant and enormously influential — and it drew sustained criticism. The Dutch Javanologist G. W. J. Drewes, in a sharp 1968 essay pointedly titled "New Light on the Coming of Islam to Indonesia?", argued that the manuscript record did not in fact support a picture of early mass conversion by Sufi preaching, and that organized Sufi orders (tarekat) appear in indigenous Indonesian sources only from the seventeenth century onward.21 Johns himself returned to the question repeatedly over more than three decades, conceding by 1975 and again in 1993 that Islamization "could never have been as simple" as his first formulation implied, and that mysticism, trade, and royal politics must all have worked together.22 The episode is a model of how scholarship corrects itself; it is also a warning against any single-cause explanation.

Whatever its precise role in the first conversions, the ongoing connection to the heartlands of Islam was real and durable. The Indonesian historian Azyumardi Azra has documented the dense networks of scholars (ulama) linking the archipelago to Mecca and Medina (the Haramayn) in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, demonstrating that Indonesian Islam was never an isolated local growth but remained plugged into the intellectual currents of the wider Muslim world from early on.23

The Wali Songo and the Islamization of Java

Java, the demographic heart of the archipelago, has its own foundational tradition of conversion: the story of the Wali Songo, the "Nine Saints" credited with bringing Islam to the island. The tradition is part history, part hagiography, and historians treat its details warily; but it preserves a genuine memory of how Islam was domesticated on Javanese soil. The nine — among them Sunan Gresik (Malik Ibrahim), Sunan Ampel, Sunan Bonang, Sunan Drajat, Sunan Giri, Sunan Kudus, Sunan Muria, Sunan Gunung Jati, and the most beloved, Sunan Kalijaga — are remembered as a loosely connected network of teachers, many of them kin, active along Java's north coast in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries.24

What makes the Wali Songo tradition historically illuminating is its emphasis on cultural translation. Rather than condemning the inherited Hindu-Buddhist and animist culture of Java, the saints are remembered for converting it. Sunan Kalijaga above all is credited with adapting the shadow theatre, wayang kulit, to carry Islamic meaning — recasting the Pandawa heroes of the Mahabharata as exemplars of monotheism and using the gamelan orchestra to draw crowds to whom he could preach.25 Sunan Bonang and Sunan Drajat are likewise remembered as composers of devotional songs set to gamelan. The image is of a faith that entered Java not by displacing the wayang but by inhabiting it. That a recognizably Sufi Islam was being taught in Javanese by the sixteenth century is confirmed by surviving texts: G. W. J. Drewes edited and translated one such work, The Admonitions of Seh Bari, a sixteenth-century Javanese Muslim treatise attributed to Sunan Bonang, which transmits Islamic doctrine in an unmistakably mystical register.26

Wali Songo — the "Nine Saints" (Javanese wali, "friend [of God]," from Arabic walī; songo, "nine") credited by tradition with the Islamization of Java. They are remembered for spreading Islam through Javanese cultural forms — wayang, gamelan, and song — rather than against them.

The institutional base of this effort was the sultanate of Demak, the first Muslim state on Java, established on the north-central coast late in the fifteenth century by Raden Patah, by tradition a son of the last Majapahit king. Supported by the wali and the Muslim port-lords of the pasisir (the north coast), Demak became the spearhead of Islam against the waning Hindu-Buddhist interior; the traditional date for the final collapse of Majapahit before Demak is 1527, though the fall of the old empire was in reality a drawn-out affair whose chronology historians still debate.27 Demak's Great Mosque, by tradition raised by the saints themselves, survives as the oldest mosque on Java and as the architectural emblem of the island's conversion — its tiered meru-like roof echoing the very Hindu-Javanese forms the new faith had supplanted.

Syncretism and the abangan–santri debate

Because Islam came to Java through translation rather than rupture, the religion that resulted was famously syncretic — layered over, rather than scrubbed clean of, the island's Hindu-Buddhist and animist inheritance. The most influential attempt to map that complexity came from the American anthropologist Clifford Geertz, whose The Religion of Java (1960), based on fieldwork in an East Javanese town in the early 1950s, divided Javanese society into three "currents" (aliran): the abangan, nominal Muslims whose practice was suffused with animist and Hindu-Buddhist elements; the santri, pious and observant Muslims; and the priyayi, the aristocratic-bureaucratic elite whose culture drew on the old Hindu-Buddhist court traditions.28 For a generation this trichotomy supplied the basic vocabulary of Indonesian studies, and the abangan/santri contrast in particular became almost unavoidable.

abangan — in Clifford Geertz's framework, nominally Muslim Javanese whose religious life blends Islamic observance with animist and Hindu-Buddhist practice. santri — by contrast, devout and orthoprax Muslims. The pair has been enormously influential and heavily criticized.

That influence has not protected the framework from serious challenge, and a student of the subject must treat it critically. Two problems stand out. The first is anachronism: Geertz drew his categories from the highly politicized Java of the 1950s, when the terms mapped onto rival political streams, and projecting them back onto the fifteenth or sixteenth century risks importing twentieth-century divisions into a past that did not know them. The second is empirical. The anthropologist Robert Hefner argued, on the basis of fieldwork in rural East Java, that Geertz's categories were too static to capture real religious change — that the balance had shifted markedly toward orthodox piety in the decades after 1965, so that the trichotomy described a moment rather than an enduring structure.29 Hefner's study of the Tengger highlanders, who preserved a non-Islamic priestly tradition descended from Hindu clergy, further showed that "abangan" was no monolith and that Islamization remained ongoing rather than complete.30 The most frontal critique came from Mark Woodward, whose Islam in Java (1989) attacked Geertz's implication that Islam had "never really taken hold" in Java outside a merchant minority. Woodward argued the reverse: that normative Islam lay at the very heart of the court culture of Yogyakarta which Geertz had filed under Hindu-Buddhist priyayi tradition, and that even Javanese mysticism was better understood as a local form of Islam than as a survival of something pre-Islamic.31

The most satisfying synthesis of this debate belongs to M. C. Ricklefs, who proposed the idea of a mystic synthesis: a stable accommodation, consolidated in the seventeenth century under the Mataram sultan Agung, in which Javanese were "deeply committed to Islam, observant of its ritual life, but admitting indigenous, non-Islamic, spiritual forces to the faith."32 This is not a halfway house between Islam and Hinduism but a genuinely Islamic settlement on Javanese terms — and Ricklefs showed that it was contested by reform-minded Muslims from the late seventeenth century, a tension still alive today. Its intellectual texture is preserved in the suluk, the corpus of Javanese Sufi devotional poetry analysed by P. J. Zoetmulder, which fused the Islamic doctrine of the "unity of being" (wahdat al-wujud) with older Indic notions of pantheism and monism into something distinctively Javanese.33

Beyond Java and Sumatra: Aceh, the Spice Islands, and adat

Islam did not stop at Sumatra and Java. From the late fifteenth century it advanced through the eastern archipelago along the same commercial arteries. In Maluku, the clove-rich Spice Islands, the ruling families of Ternate and Tidore adopted Islam through contact with Muslim traders from Java, Malacca, and beyond; under Sultan Baabullah in the later sixteenth century Ternate projected Muslim influence across eastern Sulawesi, Ambon, and the Papuan islands.34 Sunan Giri and his successors are credited in Javanese tradition with carrying Islam to Lombok, Makassar, and Borneo. The pattern, as Sluglett emphasized, was everywhere the same: a coastal, top-down, commercially driven conversion that radiated only slowly into the interior.35

At the western gateway of the archipelago, Aceh rose in the sixteenth century to become both a great pepper power and the most fervently Islamic state in the islands — the "Veranda of Mecca" (Serambi Mekah), the embarkation point for pilgrims and a magnet for scholars. Under Sultan Iskandar Muda (r. 1607–36) Aceh dominated the trade of northern Sumatra and the Strait, and its court became a centre of Malay Islamic letters.36 It was here that the Sufi poet Hamzah Fansuri, the first major literary voice of Malay mysticism, articulated the doctrine of wahdat al-wujud in Malay verse late in the sixteenth century — and here, too, that the Gujarat-born scholar Nuruddin ar-Raniri arrived in 1637 to denounce Hamzah's teachings as heretical, an early sign of the recurring tension between mystical accommodation and reformist orthodoxy that the historian Peter Riddell traces through the intellectual history of the Malay-Indonesian world.37

The encounter between Islam and local custom is captured with particular clarity among the Minangkabau of West Sumatra, the world's largest matrilineal society. Islamic inheritance law, which favours male heirs, sat awkwardly with a matrilineal adat in which ancestral property passed through women; the Minangkabau resolved the friction by distinguishing inherited communal property, which descended in the female line, from individually earned wealth, which followed Islamic rule. Their famous maxim — "custom founded upon Islamic law, Islamic law founded upon the Qur'an" (adat basandi syara', syara' basandi Kitabullah) — expresses the negotiated settlement itself.38 That settlement was hard-won. In the Padri War (1803–37), reformist ulama returning from Mecca, inspired by Wahhabi ideas, sought to enforce a stricter Qur'anic order against the adat chiefs, who eventually called in the Dutch; the long conflict ended with adat reshaped to rest more firmly on Islamic precept.39

adat — the customary law and tradition of local communities, varying greatly from region to region. Across the archipelago Islam did not abolish adat but entered into a long negotiation with it; the balance between the two remains a live question in Indonesian life and law.

A gradual and uneven conversion

Why did Islamization take so long and remain so uneven? The answer lies in everything this chapter has described. Conversion began in the cosmopolitan ports and the courts, where commerce, literacy, and prestige gave the new faith its purchase, and it spread only gradually inland, where older Hindu-Buddhist institutions and deeply rooted adat held their ground. It accommodated rather than erased what it found, taking on the wayang in Java, the matriliny of the Minangkabau, the mysticism of the Sumatran courts. The result was — and is — a religion of great regional variety, from the scripturalist rigor of Aceh to the mystic synthesis of the Javanese interior. And the process never truly ended: the reform-versus-accommodation tension that flared in seventeenth-century Aceh and in the Padri War runs on into the present, even as scholars such as Julia Day Howell have documented a vigorous revival of Sufi practice among educated urban Muslims in our own time.40 The coming of Islam to Indonesia is best understood not as an arrival with a date but as a conversation, conducted over centuries, that is still going on.

Summary

Islam reached the Indonesian archipelago not by conquest but along the maritime trade routes that had earlier carried Hindu and Buddhist ideas, and it spread through a slow, uneven process rather than a single event. The earliest hard evidence is fragmentary and contested: the 1082 gravestone at Leran, the 1297 tomb of Sultan Malik al-Saleh at Samudra Pasai, Marco Polo's report of Muslim Perlak in 1292, and Ibn Battuta's account of the devout Pasai court in 1345–46 — sources that bracket the establishment of Islam at the top of Sumatran society around 1290–1345 but that, as scholars such as Elizabeth Lambourn warn, cannot by themselves date mass conversion. Historians have proposed Gujarati, Arab, Persian, Bengali, and Chinese-Muslim origins for the faith, championed respectively by Snouck Hurgronje, T. W. Arnold, Hoesein Djajadiningrat, S. Q. Fatimi, and (most contentiously) Kong Yuanzhi; most now treat these as overlapping rather than rival. Conversion is variously attributed to the structural pull of Anthony Reid's "age of commerce," to royal conversion within O. W. Wolters's prestige economy, and to the Sufi missionary thesis of A. H. Johns — a thesis Johns himself tempered after criticism from G. W. J. Drewes. On Java, the tradition of the Wali Songo, the sultanate of Demak, and figures such as Sunan Kalijaga embody an Islam spread through, not against, Javanese culture. The syncretic result was famously schematized in Clifford Geertz's abangan/santri/priyayi trichotomy, since heavily criticized for anachronism and rigidity by Robert Hefner and Mark Woodward and resynthesized in M. C. Ricklefs's idea of a "mystic synthesis." Beyond Java and Sumatra, Islam advanced through Maluku, Sulawesi, and Borneo and negotiated lasting accommodations with local adat, as among the matrilineal Minangkabau — a negotiation, like Islamization itself, that continues into the present.

Review & Discussion Questions

11.1 Historians insist that Islamization was "a process, not an event." What specific evidence in this chapter supports that claim, and why does the distinction matter for how we read the earliest sources?
11.2 Elizabeth Lambourn cautions that a gravestone proves only that a Muslim was buried somewhere, not that the surrounding society was Muslim. Apply this caution to the Leran stone of 1082 and the Tralaya graves. What can each actually demonstrate?
11.3 Compare the Gujarati, Arab, Persian, Bengali, and Chinese-Muslim theories of Islam's origin. What kind of evidence does each rest on, and why is the 1297 date of Malik al-Saleh's death a problem for the Gujarati theory?
11.4 Distinguish the three proposed mechanisms of conversion — trade networks, royal conversion, and Sufi mission. Are they competing explanations or complementary ones? Use Reid, Wolters, and Johns to argue your case.
11.5 Trace how A. H. Johns's Sufi thesis changed between 1961 and 1993 in response to G. W. J. Drewes's criticism. What does this exchange illustrate about how historical knowledge is revised?
11.6 Why are the Wali Songo, and Sunan Kalijaga in particular, remembered for using wayang and gamelan? What does this method of conversion suggest about the relationship between Islam and pre-existing Javanese culture?
11.7 Clifford Geertz's abangan/santri distinction has been criticized as anachronistic. Explain the anachronism, then summarize how Hefner, Woodward, and Ricklefs each responded to or revised Geertz's framework.
11.8 How did the Minangkabau reconcile matrilineal adat with Islamic inheritance law, and what did the Padri War (1803–37) change about that balance? What does the case reveal about the relationship between Islam and local custom across the archipelago?
11.9 Aceh became known as the "Veranda of Mecca," yet it was also the site of bitter disputes over mysticism, as in Nuruddin ar-Raniri's attack on Hamzah Fansuri. How can the same society be both a centre of scholarship and a battleground over orthodoxy?
11.10 The chapter closes by calling the coming of Islam "a conversation … that is still going on." What evidence justifies treating Islamization as ongoing rather than completed?
