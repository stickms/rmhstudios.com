Learning Objectives

  • Explain how and why the federal Republic of the United States of Indonesia (RIS) was dissolved in 1950 and what constitutional framework replaced it.
  • Identify the major political parties of the parliamentary era and characterize their ideological orientations and social bases.
  • Analyze the causes of cabinet instability — seven governments in seven years — and assess whether that instability alone explains parliamentary democracy's failure.
  • Describe the 1955 general elections: their conduct, results, and significance as Indonesia's first national democratic exercise.
  • Explain Indonesia's foreign policy during the period, especially the Bandung Conference of 1955 and the logic of non-alignment.
  • Evaluate the Konstituante's debates over the Jakarta Charter and assess why the body failed to produce a permanent constitution.
  • Trace the economic policies of the era, particularly the Benteng programme, and evaluate their successes and failures.
  • Understand the roots of regional discontent and military restiveness that contributed to parliamentary democracy's collapse.

In the early years of independence, Indonesia made a sustained and serious attempt at parliamentary democracy. Between August 1950, when the short-lived federal Republic of the United States of Indonesia was dissolved and a unitary state proclaimed, and March 1957, when the last parliamentary cabinet collapsed amid martial law and regional rebellion, the archipelago experienced something remarkable among decolonizing nations: a functioning multi-party system, a vigorous free press, open parliamentary debate, and — in September 1955 — what Herbert Feith called the most orderly national election in postcolonial Asia. Yet these same years were also marked by crippling cabinet instability, an unresolved constitutional question about the role of Islam in the state, an economy still dominated by Dutch and Chinese capital, and a military institution that would never fully accept civilian supremacy. The decade's end proved that the real question was not whether Indonesian parliamentary democracy had achieved things of genuine worth — it had — but whether those achievements were sufficient to survive the extraordinary pressures bearing down on them from every direction.

From Federation to Republic: The Dissolution of the RIS, 1950

The unitary Republic that emerged in August 1950 was born of a paradox. The Round Table Conference negotiations of late 1949, which ended Dutch military campaigns and transferred sovereignty, had produced not the unitary Indonesia that nationalists had proclaimed in August 1945 but a federated "Republic of the United States of Indonesia" (Republik Indonesia Serikat, or RIS), composed of sixteen constituent states.1 Most of these states were Dutch creations — the product of the Netherlands' policy of building up autonomous territories as counterweights to the Yogyakarta-based revolutionary Republic — and they commanded little popular legitimacy outside the narrow circles of their Dutch-sponsored leaderships. The Republic of Indonesia itself, with its capital at Yogyakarta, was only one member of the federation, albeit by far the most politically vital, having led the independence struggle and claimed the moral authority of the Proclamation.

The collapse of the RIS was remarkably swift. Nationalist sentiment in the Dutch-created states ran strongly against the federal arrangement, and a rapid series of mergers and unification votes through the early months of 1950 progressively eliminated the constituent states. The process was not entirely bloodless: in January 1950, the Dutch-trained KNIL (Koninklijk Nederlands Indisch Leger, Royal Netherlands Indies Army) captain Raymond Westerling launched a coup attempt in West Java — the "APRA affair" — aimed at forcing the preservation of the West Java state and precipitating the RIS government's collapse. The attempt was suppressed, but the episode illustrated the fragility of the transition and the degree to which Dutch colonial instruments had not yet been dismantled.2 By July 1950, only three of the sixteen original states survived as independent entities, and the momentum was wholly toward dissolution. Mohammad Hatta, who served as Prime Minister of the RIS while Sukarno was its ceremonial President, later conceded that he had presided over the federation's own liquidation — a paradox that Anne Booth's scholarship notes directly, observing that Hatta was in fact "a convinced federalist" who nevertheless "played the central role in dissolving" the federal structure he had helped negotiate.3

On 17 August 1950 — the fifth anniversary of the Proclamation of Independence — the RIS was formally dissolved and the unitary Republic of Indonesia proclaimed in its place. The date was deliberate, chosen to lend the new arrangement the symbolic authority of the independence moment and to emphasize continuity with the revolutionary Republic that had fought the Dutch for four years. A new Provisional Constitution (Undang-Undang Dasar Sementara, UUDS) came into effect simultaneously, having been drafted in the preceding months. Unlike the original 1945 Constitution, which had reflected Soepomo's "integralist" philosophy of the state as a unified family-organism requiring strong executive authority — a philosophy that, as Abdurrachman Satrio has argued, explicitly rejected Western individual rights and power limitations — the UUDS drew heavily on liberal-democratic principles: it established cabinet government responsible to parliament, codified an extensive bill of civil and political rights, provided for an independent judiciary, and designated itself explicitly as temporary, pending a permanent constitution from a popularly elected Constituent Assembly.4 The transformation from the integralist framework to this liberal-parliamentary one was substantial and its fragility would soon become apparent.

UUDS 1950 — the Undang-Undang Dasar Sementara, or Provisional Constitution of 1950, which governed Indonesia from August 1950 until July 1959. It established a parliamentary system with cabinet responsibility to parliament, a comprehensive bill of rights, and an independent judiciary, while designating itself temporary pending a permanent constitution from an elected Constituent Assembly.

The Architecture of the New Democracy

Under the UUDS 1950, the political system took a shape that was genuinely parliamentary in character. The President — Sukarno, who had been proclaimed President of the revolutionary Republic in 1945 — became a largely ceremonial head of state: he formally appointed prime ministers, opened parliamentary sessions, and represented the Republic in state functions, but the government's authority depended on maintaining the confidence of the elected parliament (Dewan Perwakilan Rakyat, DPR).5 The prime minister and cabinet bore day-to-day executive authority. This was a radical departure from the 1945 constitutional model, which had envisioned a strong presidential executive; Sukarno accepted the ceremonial role in 1950 as politically necessary but never reconciled himself to it, and his increasing impatience with the parliamentary arrangement would become one of the defining dramas of the decade.

The parliament that the cabinets faced through 1955 was an appointed transitional body carried over from the RIS arrangements, giving it only qualified democratic legitimacy and making the eventual elections of September 1955 all the more symbolically crucial. The system was further complicated by a high degree of party fragmentation — not two or three parties but a dozen or more competing for influence, reflecting every shade of opinion from Islamic modernism to secular nationalism, agrarian socialism, and Stalinist communism. Coalition arithmetic was perpetually unstable. A cabinet could generally be formed only through multi-party bargains, and those bargains were always susceptible to the withdrawal of a coalition partner over a single policy dispute or a rival party's electoral calculation.6

Scholars have disagreed about how much weight to assign cabinet instability as an explanatory variable. Justus van der Kroef, writing in 1960, catalogued the three constitutional frameworks through which Indonesia had passed since 1945 and pointed to the colonial legacy — the Dutch Volksraad (People's Council) had only been established in 1918 and gave Indonesians minimal legislative experience before independence — as one source of the system's fragility.7 Herbert Feith, in his landmark 1962 study The Decline of Constitutional Democracy in Indonesia, offered a more nuanced reading: the system produced reasonably stable governance when the major parties cooperated and fell apart when they did not; the real question was what caused the parties to stop cooperating, not the mechanics of parliamentarism itself.8

Cabinet Politics: The Revolving Door, 1950–1957

Seven cabinets in approximately seven years: the rhythm of parliamentary democracy in Indonesia had something in common with France's unstable Fourth Republic, though the Indonesian context made the instability more consequential. Each cabinet change meant not merely a reshuffling of ministers but a full renegotiation of the coalition, a redistribution of patronage networks, and frequently a reversal of policy priorities. The cumulative effect was to exhaust political energies, erode administrative continuity, and wear out public confidence in the system itself.

The first cabinet of the unitary Republic was formed by Mohammad Natsir of Masyumi in September 1950. Natsir — a disciplined, religiously devout modernist intellectual from West Sumatra — led a government that deliberately excluded the Partai Nasional Indonesia (PNI), the party most closely identified with Sukarno and the Javanese bureaucratic establishment. The exclusion of the PNI proved fatal: unable to sustain a stable parliamentary majority, the Natsir cabinet lasted only until March 1951, when it fell on a motion concerning regional autonomy and outer-island representation.9

The Sukiman cabinet (April 1951 – February 1952) attempted a different formula, bringing Masyumi and the PNI together in coalition under Masyumi's Sukiman Wirjosandjojo. It fell, however, over what became one of the most revealing episodes of the period: Foreign Minister Ahmad Subardjo, without consulting cabinet colleagues, signed a Mutual Security Act agreement accepting American economic assistance under terms that critics argued compromised Indonesia's proclaimed neutrality in the Cold War. The episode exposed a raw nerve — Indonesian leaders across all parties had repeatedly declared non-alignment as a cornerstone of foreign policy, and the perception that a cabinet minister had secretly tilted toward the United States was politically devastating.10

The Wilopo cabinet (April 1952 – June 1953), led by PNI's Wilopo, confronted crises of a different character. It faced the notorious "October 17 Affair" of 1952, when senior army officers — including General A.H. Nasution — brought armored vehicles to the presidential palace and demanded that Sukarno dissolve parliament and govern by presidential decree. Sukarno refused, but the episode marked a watershed: it revealed that elements of the military were prepared to intervene directly in constitutional politics, and that the civilian order could not be taken for granted.11 The Wilopo cabinet also fell over the Tanjungmorawa affair in North Sumatra, where plantation land claims had produced violent confrontations between squatters and state authorities — an early sign of the agrarian tensions that would fuel political conflict for years. Herbert Feith's detailed study of the Wilopo cabinet characterized it as "a turning point in post-revolutionary Indonesia," the moment when the democratic trajectory first bent decisively downward.12

Ali Sastroamidjojo's first cabinet (August 1953 – July 1955) was, paradoxically, both the most activist and the most internationally prominent of the parliamentary era's governments. Led by the PNI with Nahdlatul Ulama support and facing Masyumi opposition, the Ali I cabinet pursued an aggressively anti-colonial foreign policy, culminating in its organization of the Asian-African Conference at Bandung — a diplomatic triumph discussed below. Its domestic record was more mixed: charged with preparing the long-overdue elections, it also became entangled in the patronage politics that increasingly defined party competition. It resigned in July 1955 as the elections approached, having served somewhat longer than most of its predecessors.13

The caretaker cabinet formed by Burhanuddin Harahap of Masyumi (August 1955 – March 1956) presided over the two critical elections of September and December 1955 — an achievement that should not be underestimated. The very fact that free and fair elections were held and their results accepted by all parties was a genuine accomplishment for a government of limited political base. Yet the Burhanuddin cabinet failed to consolidate power after the elections; its coalition was too narrow for the new parliament's arithmetic, and in March 1956 it yielded to a second Ali Sastroamidjojo cabinet.

Ali II (March 1956 – March 1957) governed as the parliamentary system began visibly to unravel. Regional military commanders in Sumatra and Sulawesi were moving toward open defiance of central authority; the Konstituante was deadlocked; Sukarno was openly hostile to the multi-party system; and the army was increasingly restive. J. Eliseo Rocamora's study of the PNI notes that by this period the party had shifted from a vehicle for radical nationalism to an instrument of bureaucratic patronage — a symptom of the exhaustion afflicting the entire parliamentary order.14 The Ali II cabinet resigned in March 1957, whereupon Sukarno declared a state of war and siege (Staat van Oorlog en Beleg, SOB) and appointed a non-party "business cabinet" under Djuanda Kartawidjaja — the parliament-based system's effective obituary as a governing mechanism.

The Party System: Masyumi, PNI, NU, and PKI

Indonesian political competition during the parliamentary era was structured by a tension that ran deeper than electoral strategy: the clash between those who conceived of the Indonesian state as fundamentally secular-nationalist, grounded in the Pancasila principles that Sukarno had articulated in June 1945, and those who believed the world's most populous Muslim nation should reflect Islamic law in its constitutional foundations.15 This tension mapped imperfectly but persistently onto party lines. The sociologist Clifford Geertz, working in Java during this period, famously described the underlying social divide as one between abangan Indonesians — nominally Muslim, Javanese, blending Islamic practice with Hindu-Buddhist and animist traditions — and santri Indonesians — more consciously and observantly Muslim, oriented toward Islamic scholarship and law. While Geertz's framework has been debated and qualified by subsequent scholars, it captures a real fault line that organized much of party competition during the parliamentary decade.16

abangan / santri — a distinction drawn by the sociologist Clifford Geertz to describe a cleavage within Javanese Muslim society. Abangan denotes those whose Islam was nominal and syncretized with Javanese Hindu-Buddhist and animist tradition; santri denotes the more observant, Islamic-law-oriented segment. The distinction became a major analytical framework for understanding Indonesian party politics during the parliamentary era, though subsequent scholarship has complicated and partially challenged Geertz's original formulation.

Masyumi (Majelis Syuro Muslimin Indonesia, Council of Indonesian Muslim Associations) was the preeminent Islamic political party of the era — a coalition of Muslim organizations dominated by the modernist Muhammadiyah movement. Intellectually disciplined and reform-oriented, Masyumi was particularly strong in Sumatra, Sulawesi, and other outer islands, as well as among urban educated Muslims. Its leadership included figures of considerable stature — Mohammad Natsir, Mohammad Roem, Sjafruddin Prawiranegara — and it consistently advocated for the incorporation of Islamic principles into the constitutional framework. When Nahdlatul Ulama, the traditionalist Islamic mass organization, broke away from Masyumi in 1952 to form its own independent party, Masyumi lost its claim to represent all Indonesian Muslims and became more narrowly associated with the modernist-reformist current of Islam.17

The Partai Nasional Indonesia (PNI), the party most closely identified with the secular nationalist tradition and with Sukarno personally (though Sukarno as president formally remained above party), drew its strength from the Javanese bureaucratic class — the priyayi — and from the nationalist intelligentsia. It represented the abangan pole of Indonesian politics more than any other major party. Daniel Lev observed that the PNI functioned less as an ideological vehicle than as a patronage network, distributing government jobs and contracts to its supporters through control of the civil service — a characteristic that made it powerful but also made it resistant to programmatic reform and vulnerable to accusations of corruption.18

Nahdlatul Ulama, having broken from Masyumi, became the party of the rural Javanese santri — the world of the pesantren (Islamic boarding school), the village kiai (Islamic scholar), and the dense networks of traditionalist Islamic piety. NU's constituency was enormous — Java's rural population was the country's largest — but it was culturally conservative and focused on protecting the institutional interests of traditionalist Islamic education and practice rather than on reshaping the constitutional order. In electoral terms, NU proved its independent strength in the 1955 elections, winning approximately 18.4 percent of the national vote — a result that fully vindicated the decision to leave Masyumi and compete independently.19

The most surprising story of the parliamentary era was the rise of the Partai Komunis Indonesia (PKI). Shattered by the failed Madiun revolt of 1948 — when PKI-aligned forces clashed fatally with the Republican army during the revolution — and led briefly thereafter by discredited figures, the party recovered under the leadership of Dipa Nusantara Aidit, who seized control in 1951 at the age of 28. Aidit rebuilt the PKI into a disciplined mass-mobilization machine through a network of affiliated organizations spanning peasant farmers (Barisan Tani Indonesia), industrial workers (SOBSI), women's groups, and youth associations. By 1955 the PKI had emerged as the fourth-largest party in the country, winning approximately 16.4 percent of the vote — an achievement that astonished foreign observers and alarmed Washington.20 Lev noted that the PKI received disproportionate scholarly attention relative to the other parties, yet its organizational discipline and mass-mobilization strategy were genuinely distinctive — a model the other parties conspicuously failed to match.21

September 1955: The Elections Indonesia Got Right

If one moment in the parliamentary decade stands as an unambiguous achievement, it is the general election of 29 September 1955, followed by the Constituent Assembly election on 15 December of the same year. Delayed since independence in 1945 by the revolution, subsequent cabinet instability, and the considerable logistical challenges of designing an electoral system for a sprawling archipelago of tens of millions of mostly rural voters, the elections were finally conducted a decade after independence, under a system of proportional representation that treated the whole country as a single national constituency for seat-allocation purposes.

Feith, who had observed the campaigns closely, described the conduct of the 1955 elections as remarkably orderly for a country holding its first nationwide democratic exercise: the balloting was administered with considerable competence by the newly established Electoral Committee; campaigning was vigorous and largely peaceful; voters in remote areas accessible only by small boat or jungle track turned out in high numbers; and the results, when announced, were accepted by all major participants.22 In an era when many newly independent countries either postponed elections indefinitely or staged them as theatrical legitimations of single-party rule, Indonesia's 1955 vote was genuinely competitive and genuinely consequential — a fact remarked upon by international observers at the time and affirmed by subsequent scholarship.

The results themselves were both clarifying and complicating. Four parties emerged as roughly equal giants: the PNI led with approximately 22.3 percent of the national vote; Masyumi followed with approximately 20.9 percent; NU won approximately 18.4 percent; and the PKI — the great surprise of the election — took approximately 16.4 percent.23 Dozens of smaller parties and independent candidates shared the remaining votes. No party came anywhere near a majority; the four-way near-parity ensured that governing would require the same complex, fragile coalitions that had plagued the appointed parliaments of 1950–1955. Leo Suryadinata has characterized the result as crystallizing rather than resolving the fundamental tension between secular nationalism and political Islam: with neither tendency achieving a decisive mandate, the upcoming Constituent Assembly would have to confront the basis-of-state question on contested democratic ground.24

The December 1955 elections for the Constituent Assembly (Konstituante) produced a broadly similar distribution of seats, with 520 members elected specifically to draft a permanent constitution. The political forces that would deadlock over Islam and the state were now democratically mandated — and essentially equally matched. The stage was set for a constitutional confrontation that would prove the parliamentary period's ultimate undoing.

Bandung 1955: Indonesia as Champion of Decolonization

Between the two 1955 elections, from 18 to 24 April, the West Java city of Bandung hosted an event that briefly placed Indonesia at the center of world attention. The Asian-African Conference — hosted by Prime Minister Ali Sastroamidjojo and opened with a celebrated address by President Sukarno — brought together twenty-nine nations spanning Asia and Africa, representing more than half of the world's population, under the twin banners of anti-colonialism and peaceful coexistence. It was, in the immediate assessment of George McT. Kahin — who attended as a scholar-observer — a watershed moment for the newly decolonized world: a gathering at which the countries that had been the objects of European imperialism announced themselves as subjects of world politics on their own terms.25

Sukarno's opening address set the rhetorical register that the conference's subsequent proceedings would sustain. "This is the first intercontinental conference of coloured peoples in the history of mankind!" he declared, framing Bandung as a civilizational event — the moment when "the peoples of Asia and Africa woke up" — as much as a diplomatic one. The gathering brought together leaders as ideologically varied as Jawaharlal Nehru of India, the great advocate of non-alignment; Zhou Enlai of the People's Republic of China, pursuing international rehabilitation after the Korean War; Gamal Abdel Nasser of Egypt, newly emboldened after the 1952 revolution; and leaders of African nations on the eve of independence.26

Naoko Shimazu, in a study of Bandung as theatrical performance, has argued that the conference should be understood not only as a summit of diplomatic substance but as a carefully staged display of postcolonial sovereignty — that Bandung city itself, its hotels, its streets, and its ceremonial pageantry functioned as a "theatrical stage" on which the new nations of Asia and Africa performed their arrival as global actors.27 This interpretive framing captures something the conventional diplomatic record tends to obscure: Bandung mattered not only for the Ten Principles adopted in its final communiqué — non-aggression, non-interference in internal affairs, equality of races and nations, peaceful coexistence, and active opposition to colonialism in all its forms — but for the image it projected of Asian and African nations acting in concert, with dignity and seriousness of purpose.

The conference's historiography is itself contested. Sally Wood's survey of recent scholarship notes that Bandung has been read simultaneously as a continuation of anti-imperialist struggle, as a Cold War episode (the United States was alarmed by communist China's participation and the implicit challenge to SEATO), as a protest against racial hierarchy spanning centuries, and even, by hostile commentators of the time, as a "communist plot."28 Wood argues persuasively that all of these readings contain elements of truth and that the conference was capacious enough to sustain multiple, partly incompatible interpretations. For Indonesia specifically, Bandung represented the high-water mark of the parliamentary era's foreign-policy ambitions: the unambiguous demonstration that an independent, non-aligned Indonesia could play a leading role on the world stage — an achievement that no subsequent diplomatic success of the parliamentary period would quite match.

Non-alignment — the foreign-policy orientation adopted by Indonesia and many other newly independent states during the Cold War, refusing formal military or political alignment with either the United States-led Western bloc or the Soviet Union-led Eastern bloc. The Bandung Conference of 1955 gave non-alignment its most celebrated early expression; the Non-Aligned Movement was formally institutionalized at Belgrade in 1961. Sukarno was among its most prominent international advocates throughout his presidency.

The Konstituante and the Jakarta Charter Question

When the elected Constituent Assembly (Konstituante) convened in November 1956, it faced a task simultaneously technical and profound: to draft a permanent constitution for a country that had spent its first six years governed by a provisional document, while resolving questions of fundamental political identity that independence had long deferred. The body's 520 elected members were among the most democratically legitimate representatives Indonesia had yet convened. Its failure over the following three years to agree even on the most basic constitutional principle — the basis of the state — would prove fatal not only to the Konstituante itself but to the entire parliamentary order.

The core question was one that Indonesian nationalism had wrestled with since before independence: should the state rest on Pancasila, the five secular-pluralist principles that Sukarno had articulated in June 1945 (belief in one God; a just and civilized humanity; the unity of Indonesia; democracy guided by wisdom; social justice for all), or should it incorporate the "seven words" of the Jakarta Charter — the phrase dengan kewajiban menjalankan syariat Islam bagi pemeluk-pemeluknya ("with the obligation for adherents of Islam to practice Islamic law") — that had briefly appeared in the constitutional preamble draft in June 1945 before being removed on 18 August 1945 to accommodate non-Muslim communities in eastern Indonesia?29

R.E. Elson's careful re-examination of the Jakarta Charter controversy has shown that the removal of the "seven words" in August 1945 was a deliberate act of statecraft — a recognition by both nationalist and Islamic leaders that mandating Islamic law in the constitution would alienate Christian minorities and fracture the tenuous nationalist coalition at the moment of maximum peril. The compromise was accepted as provisional by all parties; the Islamic parties entered independence expecting that once democratic elections were held, the Muslim majority would endorse a more explicitly Islamic constitutional framework. The Konstituante was the arena in which that expectation was tested.30

Mirjam Künkler's analysis of the Konstituante debates identifies three broad blocs among its 520 members: those supporting Pancasila as the basis of the state (a coalition including the PNI, PKI, the small secular socialist PSI, and other non-Islamic parties); those supporting Islam as the basis of the state (Masyumi, NU, and smaller Islamic parties); and a third group primarily concerned with socioeconomic rights. The Islamic and Pancasila blocs were roughly equal, with the Islamic parties consistently unable to muster the two-thirds supermajority required under the UUDS to decide the constitutional basis.31

Three successive votes on the basis of the state — held in 1957 and 1959 — produced the same result each time: a majority for the Islamic option but not the required two-thirds. Neither side was willing to compromise on a question it regarded as constitutive of the state's identity. Adnan Buyung Nasution's comprehensive study of the Konstituante concludes that the body was not inherently dysfunctional: it had made considerable progress on many constitutional questions and contained members of genuine ability and good faith. The deadlock was specific to the basis-of-state question, which engaged identities too deep for ordinary legislative bargaining to bridge.32 Simon Butt's work on religious freedom in the Indonesian constitutional tradition confirms that the fundamental ambiguity established by the 1945 compromise — a state "based on the belief in Almighty God" without specifying Islam or any other religion — was an inherently unstable foundation that the Konstituante was expected to resolve but structurally could not.33

Jakarta Charter (Piagam Jakarta) — a document drafted by a subcommittee of Indonesian nationalist leaders on 22 June 1945, setting out principles for the independence declaration. It included the phrase "with the obligation for adherents of Islam to practice Islamic law" in the preamble — the "seven words" removed from the final constitution on 18 August 1945 to accommodate non-Muslim communities in eastern Indonesia. The Charter's status remained a central contested issue throughout the parliamentary era, at the heart of the Konstituante's deadlock.
Konstituante — the Constituent Assembly of Indonesia, elected in December 1955 with 520 members and charged with drafting a permanent constitution to replace the provisional UUDS 1950. It convened from November 1956 but deadlocked over the question of whether the state should be based on Pancasila or on Islam. President Sukarno dissolved it by presidential decree on 5 July 1959, simultaneously restoring the original 1945 Constitution by executive fiat.

The Konstituante's failure had ramifications far beyond constitutional law. It handed Sukarno — who had grown increasingly impatient with parliamentary bickering and was elaborating his concept of Demokrasi Terpimpin (Guided Democracy) — a decisive argument: that Western-style liberal parliamentarism was unsuitable for Indonesia and that a different, more culturally rooted system was required. His famous "Konsepsi" speech of 21 February 1957 called for a "cabinet of all parties" (a gotong royong, or mutual-cooperation government, invoking the Javanese cooperative-labor tradition) and for the "burial" of the opposition system as such, citing the country's inability to produce stable governance or a permanent constitution as evidence of systemic failure.34

Economic Travails: The Benteng Programme and Development Failure

The parliamentary era's political instability was matched by an economy whose structural challenges would have tested any government. Indonesia in 1950 inherited a colonial economic structure in which the commanding heights — large plantations, oil wells, major trading firms, import-export houses — remained overwhelmingly in Dutch and ethnic-Chinese hands. The new nation's leaders confronted the central paradox of decolonization: political sovereignty had been won, but economic sovereignty was largely a fiction. The question was how to transform the colonial economy into a genuinely national one without precipitating capital flight or the collapse of the export earnings on which government revenues depended.

The Benteng (fortress) programme was the parliamentary era's primary response to this challenge. Introduced by the Natsir and Sukiman cabinets in the early 1950s, the programme reserved import licences and preferential credit for indigenous (pribumi) Indonesian entrepreneurs, explicitly excluding ethnic Chinese businessmen from sectors the government designated for Indonesianization. As Kian Wie Thee has shown in a detailed study, the Benteng programme was "Indonesia's first affirmative action initiative" — an attempt to nurture an indigenous capitalist class capable of competing with the established Chinese and Dutch commercial networks, and to convert what Thee describes as the "colonial economy into a national economy."35

The programme's results were discouraging. Many of the indigenous entrepreneurs who received Benteng licences lacked the business experience, capital base, and commercial networks to exploit them effectively; others engaged in what became known as "Ali Baba" arrangements — formally holding licences while functioning as fronts for the ethnic Chinese businessmen the programme was designed to displace. Corruption was pervasive. Import licences became patronage resources distributed through party networks rather than economic incentives driving genuine business formation. By the late 1950s, Thee concludes, the programme was quietly terminated as a failure, without having fundamentally altered the ethnic composition of Indonesian business or dismantled the colonial inheritance.36

Structural problems compounded the Benteng failure. The export economy depended on rubber, oil, and tin — commodities whose prices fluctuated with global markets beyond Indonesian control. The foreign-exchange earnings generated disproportionately by Sumatra's rubber and oil and Sulawesi's copra financed the imports that the import-substitution strategy required, but those revenues were captured by Jakarta while the producing regions felt systematically shortchanged. Goh Cheng Teik's analysis of parliamentary democracy's failure emphasizes this regional economic grievance as a root cause of political instability: military officers and civilian leaders in the outer islands complained that despite independence, their regions "continue to live in deplorable conditions and poverty" while resources concentrated in Jakarta with inadequate return investment.37 Inflation was chronic, the budget deficit persistent, and the administrative capacity of the state stretched well beyond what it could reliably deliver. The relationship between economic dysfunction and political instability was mutually reinforcing: weak governments could not implement sustained development strategies, and failed development discredited the governments that pursued it.

Regional Discontent, Military Restiveness, and the Collapse

If the parliamentary system's internal contradictions made it fragile, external pressures made it brittle. The two most consequential pressures — regional resentment and military politics — reinforced each other in a spiral that successive parliamentary governments were ultimately unable to contain.

The regional problem had its roots in the economic geography described above but was compounded by the political geography of decolonization. The outer islands — Sumatra, Sulawesi, Kalimantan, Maluku — had been economically productive under Dutch rule and possessed distinct cultures, political traditions, and religious characters. Sumatran and Sulawesi leaders, many of them Masyumi supporters, viewed Javanese political dominance with mounting suspicion. The PNI's grip on the central bureaucracy meant that outer-island positions were filled by Javanese officials; revenue transfers to Jakarta were experienced as extraction without adequate compensation; and the army's territorial structure, which gave divisional commanders in the outer islands considerable local autonomy, provided a vehicle for regional political mobilization.38

The army itself was a deeply divided institution. The revolutionary generation of officers, who had built the military from scratch during the independence struggle, resented the Dutch-trained KNIL officers integrated into the new national army after 1949. There were ideological divisions between secular nationalists and Islamic-oriented officers, territorial divisions between the Java-based central command and outer-island divisional commanders, and personal rivalries that frequently mapped onto these other cleavages. Ulf Sundhaussen's survey of the scholarly literature on the Indonesian military cautions that early Western scholarship overemphasized factional divisions while underestimating the degree to which a shared institutional identity — and shared resentment of civilian political disorder — held the officer corps together even in its most contentious moments.39

General A.H. Nasution occupied a pivotal position throughout the period. After being effectively sidelined following the October 17, 1952 affair, he was reappointed Army Chief of Staff in 1955 — a decision reflecting Sukarno's recognition that Nasution was indispensable for managing the army's growing restiveness. Nasution articulated what he called the army's "Middle Way": it would "neither seek to take over the government nor remain politically inactive" but would act as a "social-political force" in Indonesian life — a doctrine that Sundhaussen identifies as the intellectual precursor of the dwifungsi (dual function) principle later institutionalized under the New Order.40

From late 1956, the constitutional system began openly to break apart. In December 1956, Colonel Maludin Simbolon, commanding the North Sumatra military division, broke with central military command and aligned himself with a regional council (dewan) movement demanding fiscal autonomy and greater regional representation. Similar councils emerged in Central and South Sumatra, and in North Sulawesi, under Colonel Ventje Sumual. These movements were not straightforwardly secessionist — most of their leaders explicitly affirmed Indonesian national unity while demanding structural reform — but their challenge to the authority of Jakarta was unmistakable and their connections to Masyumi politicians gave the crisis an added ideological dimension.41

Harold Crouch's authoritative study of the army and politics identifies this period as the critical moment at which civilian political authority — already weakened by the Konstituante's paralysis, the Ali II cabinet's exhaustion, and Sukarno's openly expressed hostility to the parliamentary system — effectively ceased to function as a constraint on military behavior.42 When Ali Sastroamidjojo resigned in March 1957 and Sukarno declared a state of war and siege, the parliamentary system had already lost the institutional capacity to resolve its own crises. Sukarno's appointment of the non-party Djuanda cabinet, while formally within constitutional bounds, was substantively a declaration that parliament could no longer govern. Crouch's theoretical reading — drawing on Weber's concept of patrimonialism — suggests that the parliamentary system's failure reflected not merely inexperience or institutional weakness but the structural difficulty of establishing impersonal, rule-governed authority in a political culture whose networks were built on personal loyalty, kinship, and patronage rather than on formal institutional roles.43

The historical debate over whether parliamentary democracy's failure was inevitable deserves careful attention. Goh Cheng Teik's 1972 analysis explicitly rejects a deterministic reading, noting that the elections of 1955 were successfully held, that several cabinets achieved meaningful policy outcomes, and that the Konstituante came closer to resolving the constitutional question than its critics acknowledged.44 Feith himself, the most searching analyst of the period, argued that the system's decline was the result of specific choices made by identifiable political actors — choices that could, in principle, have been made differently — rather than the mechanical consequence of structural forces.45 Against these relatively optimistic readings, van der Kroef emphasized the sheer novelty and difficulty of the enterprise: a country with minimal experience of representative government, an inexperienced electorate, a fragmented party landscape, and a colonial economy suddenly required to produce stable democratic governance under Cold War pressures.46 The weight of evidence suggests that both sets of factors were operative: the system faced genuine structural challenges that made success difficult, and it was also undone by particular choices — Sukarno's impatience with democratic pluralism, the military's willingness to intervene, the parties' preference for factional advantage over institutional sustainability — that were not structurally determined.

The parliamentary era's end did not arrive cleanly in 1957. The Djuanda cabinet governed without formal parliamentary confidence; the Konstituante continued to meet, futilely, until Sukarno's presidential decree of 5 July 1959 restored the 1945 Constitution and formally inaugurated Guided Democracy. The PRRI-Permesta rebellions of February 1958 — when Sumatran and Sulawesi officers proclaimed a Pemerintah Revolusioner Republik Indonesia (Revolutionary Government of the Republic of Indonesia) in direct defiance of Jakarta — were the violent denouement of the regional tensions that the parliamentary era had failed to resolve.47 These events are treated in Chapter 30; what belongs to the parliamentary chapter's final reckoning is the recognition that the seeds of those conflicts were planted in the coalition offices and on the parliament floor of 1950 to 1957 — and that the parliamentary era's genuine achievements, above all the 1955 elections and the Bandung Conference, coexisted with structural failures from which Indonesia's democratic experiment could not recover.

PRRI-Permesta — two overlapping regional rebellions launched in 1958 by military officers and civilian leaders in Sumatra (Pemerintah Revolusioner Republik Indonesia, Revolutionary Government of the Republic of Indonesia, proclaimed February 1958) and Sulawesi (Piagam Perjuangan Semesta Alam, Charter of Universal Struggle, launched March 1957). Both were demands for structural political and fiscal reform rather than full separatism; both were suppressed by the Indonesian army by 1961. They represented the violent culmination of the regional and military discontent building throughout the parliamentary era.

Summary

The years from 1950 to 1957 represented Indonesia's sustained attempt at liberal parliamentary democracy. The dissolution of the federal RIS in August 1950 produced a unitary state governed under the Provisional Constitution (UUDS 1950), which established cabinet government, civil liberties, and the expectation of an elected Constituent Assembly. Seven cabinets in seven years demonstrated the instability endemic to a fragmented multi-party system, though the period also produced genuine achievements: the remarkably free 1955 general elections, the Bandung Conference's diplomatic triumph, and serious — if inconclusive — constitutional deliberation in the Konstituante. The fundamental fault line between secular nationalism (Pancasila) and political Islam (centered on the Jakarta Charter's "seven words") structured both party competition and the Konstituante's fatal deadlock. The Benteng programme failed to dismantle the colonial economic inheritance. Growing regional resentment and military restiveness, exploited by Sukarno's impatient advocacy for a Guided Democracy that would "bury the parties," set in motion forces that ended parliamentary government in 1957 and led to Sukarno's decree of 1959. Feith's account of "decline" remains the dominant scholarly framework, but historians now also insist on recognizing the real democratic achievements of these years alongside their structural failures.

Review & Discussion Questions

29.1 Why was the federal RIS dissolved so quickly after its creation in 1949, and what does the speed of its dissolution reveal about the limits of Dutch-sponsored federalism in Indonesia?
29.2 Herbert Feith described the parliamentary period as one of "decline" rather than simple failure. What does this distinction mean, and how does it affect the way we evaluate Indonesian democracy in the 1950s?
29.3 Compare the social bases and political orientations of Masyumi, the PNI, NU, and the PKI. What does the roughly equal electoral strength of these four parties in 1955 reveal about Indonesian society?
29.4 The 1955 elections were widely praised as Indonesia's finest democratic moment. What made them possible, and why did the favorable electoral outcome fail to resolve the country's political crises?
29.5 Evaluate the Bandung Conference of 1955 as both a diplomatic achievement and a historical moment. What did it accomplish for Indonesia specifically, and how have historians disagreed about its broader significance?
29.6 Why did the Jakarta Charter question prove so intractable in the Konstituante? Could a constitutional compromise on Islam and the state have been reached, or was deadlock structurally inevitable given the democratic arithmetic?
29.7 Assess the Benteng programme as an economic policy. What was it designed to achieve, and why did it fail? What does its failure reveal about the challenges of post-colonial economic development more broadly?
29.8 How much responsibility for the collapse of parliamentary democracy should be attributed to Sukarno personally, to the military, to the political parties, and to structural factors beyond any individual actor's control? Can the collapse be considered avoidable?
