  • Describe the founding, character, and historical significance of Budi Utomo (1908), and critically assess scholarly debates about its status as the "start" of the national awakening.

  • Assess Kartini's correspondence as a historical document and her contested symbolic legacies.
  • Analyze how the vernacular press and the Malay/Indonesian language became instruments of nationalist consciousness-building.
  • Distinguish the reformist (Muhammadiyah) and traditionalist (Nahdlatul Ulama) strands of Islamic organisation and their roles in the pergerakan.
  • Evaluate how pan-Islamism, the Russo-Japanese War, and other global anti-colonial currents shaped Indonesian nationalist thought.

In the first decade of the twentieth century a new kind of meeting was being held in the Netherlands East Indies. In the Dutch-language classrooms of the Batavia Medical College, students who had grown up in Javanese kraton households were debating — in Dutch — whether science or cultural revival should lead their people's regeneration. In the batik markets of Surakarta, Muslim merchants were forming mutual-aid societies whose economic grievances were shading into political demands. In the correspondence of a young regent's daughter confined to her father's house in Jepara, a vision of women's freedom and human dignity was being articulated in letters to Dutch friends. These strands were not coordinated; they did not share a single vocabulary or a common goal. Yet within a generation they would converge — incompletely, tensely, but irresistibly — into the idea of Indonesia. This chapter traces how that convergence began: from the colonial schools and newspapers of the early 1900s through the founding organisations of the 1910s and 1920s to the threshold of the great ideological contests that would define the movement in its maturer phase.

The Colonial Womb of Nationalism

The paradox at the heart of Indonesian nationalism is that the Dutch colonial state, largely by accident, made it possible. The Ethische Politiek — the Ethical Policy — proclaimed in Queen Wilhelmina's throne speech of 17 September 1901 was not designed to produce nationalists. Its author, the liberal jurist Conrad van Deventer, framed it as the repayment of a "debt of honor" (eereschuld) that the Netherlands owed to the Indies after decades of ruthless extraction under the Cultivation System.1 The policy rested on three programmatic pillars — irigatie (irrigation), emigratie (resettlement of Javanese to less densely populated outer islands), and educatie (education) — and of these it was education that most profoundly transformed colonial society.2

Expansion of the school network was fitful and underfunded; later critics would note that by 1928, even after nearly three decades of the policy, only about 75,000 Indonesians had completed primary school and roughly 6,500 had finished secondary education, while overall literacy remained below ten per cent of the population.3 Yet the creation of even a small educated class had effects out of all proportion to its numbers. Dutch-language HBS secondary schools and specialist colleges — above all the STOVIA (School tot Opleiding van Inlandsche Artsen, the school for training native doctors) in Batavia — gave a cohort of young men access to European political thought, comparative history, and scientific knowledge. They read about the French Revolution, the Greek independence struggle, and the founding of the Indian National Congress. They encountered Social Darwinism, liberalism, and socialism. They were, in the historian Robert Van Niel's formulation, an extension of the old priyayi aristocratic stratum rather than a wholly new social formation — for the most part conservative, pragmatic, and colonial-accommodating as long as Dutch authority seemed unshakeable.4 But the Ethical Policy generated expectations it could not fulfill. Graduates of Dutch schools found that no amount of education translated automatically into professional equality or political standing; the color line remained; the promises of uplift rang hollow. That frustrated expectation was the first fuel of nationalism.

Ethische Politiek (Ethical Policy) — The Dutch colonial development program proclaimed in 1901 and nominally operative until the Japanese occupation of 1942, resting on the three pillars of irrigation, emigration, and education. Its expansion of Western schooling produced the educated elite that would challenge colonial rule.
priyayi — The Javanese aristocratic and administrative class, whose members occupied the lower and middle rungs of the colonial civil service. The Ethical Policy primarily educated their sons, making the priyayi the seedbed of early nationalism while also binding many of them to the colonial order.

The historian Hans Pols has noted a further paradox: the earliest nationalist intellectuals from STOVIA were, culturally speaking, "the least Javanese individuals on the island of Java." They spoke Dutch with one another, dressed in Western clothes, and "were more impressed by modernity and by the 'West' than they were by Javanese culture."5 Their nationalism was not, initially, a rediscovery of indigenous essence; it was fueled by their cosmopolitanism and their sense of exclusion from the modernity they had been taught to prize. As Chapter 23 has shown, the Ethical Policy's educational expansion created this cohort; the present chapter traces what they and their contemporaries made of their frustration.

Early Voices: Kartini, Tirto, and the World Before 1908

Two figures active before the founding of the first formal organisations deserve particular attention, because they represent vectors of proto-nationalist consciousness that operated outside the organizational frame: Raden Adjeng Kartini, whose letters mapped the contours of emancipation, and Tirto Adhi Soerjo, whose journalism forged the native press as a political instrument.

Raden Adjeng Kartini (1879–1904) was the daughter of the Bupati (regent) of Jepara in north-central Java — a priyayi family of high standing. She was permitted to attend a local Dutch-language elementary school until the age of twelve, when custom required her return to the family compound to await marriage. Confined, she turned to letter-writing: between 1899 and 1904 she conducted a rich correspondence, primarily in Dutch, with a circle of sympathetic Dutch liberals including Rosa Abendanon-Mandri and the feminist activist Stella Zeehandelaar. In those letters she produced one of the earliest sustained critiques, written from within Indonesian society, of the entanglement of colonial hierarchy, Javanese feudal patriarchy, and the exclusion of women from education.6

Kartini died in childbirth in September 1904 at the age of twenty-five, before she could act on her convictions. It was only in 1911 that her correspondence reached the wider world, when the colonial official J.H. Abendanon compiled and edited a selection under the title Door Duisternis tot Licht (Out of Dark Comes Light). The edition was heavily shaped by its editor: Abendanon removed passages critical of colonialism and polygamy, presenting a Kartini useful as a poster child for the Ethical Policy.7 The Indonesian title, Habis Gelap Terbitlah Terang, appeared in the 1922 Balai Pustaka Malay edition. An English translation, Letters of a Javanese Princess, serialised in the Atlantic Monthly in 1919, gave her an international readership. The most complete scholarly edition, restoring the deleted passages and contextualising the letters within Dutch imperial arts policy debates, is Joost Coté's Kartini: The Complete Writings 1898–1904 (2014).8

Jean Gelman Taylor's foundational 1989 article in the Bijdragen tot de Taal-, Land- en Volkenkunde places Kartini squarely within "the material and political conditions of the late colonial Indies," resisting both the Dutch liberals' use of her as a symbol of beneficent colonial tutelage and the later nationalist lionisation that stripped her of context.9 Paul Bijl has read the letters as exercises in "legal self-fashioning" — an early articulation of human rights claims within the constraints of colonial law and aristocratic convention.10 What is beyond dispute is that her posthumous publication transformed a private correspondence into a political text, and that by the 1920s Kartini had become both the primary female symbol of Indonesian nationalism and one of the most contested figures in its historiography.

Tirto Adhi Soerjo (c. 1880–1918) worked at the opposite end of the social spectrum from Kartini's reflective solitude: he was the founding figure of the Indonesian-operated vernacular press. A Javanese priyayi educated in Dutch schools, Tirto founded Soenda Berita in 1903 and then Medan Prijaji (Field of the Priyayi) in Bandung in 1907 — a newspaper eventually recognised by historians as "the first truly Indonesian newspaper" in the sense that it was owned, operated, and written by indigenous Indonesians for an indigenous readership.11 Where Indo-European and Chinese-Indonesian proprietors had already built a vernacular press infrastructure across the archipelago since the 1850s, Tirto turned that infrastructure toward explicitly anti-colonial purposes: raising, in Takashi Shiraishi's formulation, "issues of racial injustice, discrimination, and inequality that were deliberately silenced by colonial-owned media."12 Tirto would also play a role as co-founder and secretary-advisor of Sarekat Dagang Islam. He died in poverty and colonial disgrace in 1918, largely forgotten until the novelist Pramoedya Ananta Toer immortalised him in his Buru Quartet novels in the 1970s and 1980s.

Budi Utomo, 1908: The Symbolic Beginning

On 20 May 1908, at the STOVIA medical college in Batavia, a group of students led by a twenty-year-old named Soetomo formally established Budi Utomo (Noble Endeavour). The meeting gave institutional form to an idea that had been circulating since the previous year, when the retired physician Wahidin Sudirohusodo (1852–1917) had toured Java seeking backers for a scholarship fund to enable talented young Javanese to receive Western education. Wahidin's vision was cultural and ameliorative rather than political; he wanted to revive Javanese civilisation by coupling its classical arts with modern science. Soetomo and his STOVIA classmates were more impatient, but the organisation they founded was, at least initially, culturally conservative and socially restricted: it drew its membership predominantly from Javanese priyayi students and civil servants and was explicitly Javanese in orientation rather than archipelago-wide.13

Budi Utomo grew quickly: it reached around 10,000 members in 40 branches by 1910, mostly students and civil servants. Its first congress in October 1908 at Yogyakarta drew 300 delegates from across Java and Madura. Yet the organisation remained divided over its purpose. The moderate faction led by older priyayi officials, who quickly dominated the executive, wanted collaboration with the colonial government. A younger radical wing, represented by the physician Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo, wanted political confrontation. When K.R.T. Radjiman Wediodiningrat took control and steered Budi Utomo toward Javanese cultural revival as a "spiritual alternative to Western materialism," the political radicals drifted away.14

Akira Nagazumi's dedicated study of Budi Utomo's first decade shows the organisation contributing to nationalist consciousness less through direct political action than by normalising the idea that indigenous Indonesians could organise in their own name, debate public questions, and claim a stake in the colony's future.15 The Indonesian state later designated 20 May as Hari Kebangkitan Nasional (National Awakening Day), cementing Budi Utomo's foundational status. But historians have raised sharp questions about this official narrative. Ariel Heryanto challenged "the relevance of national awakening today" as a frame, noting that an organisation whose membership was overwhelmingly Javanese priyayi and whose cultural vision was explicitly Javanese does not self-evidently represent the awakening of a multi-ethnic Indonesian nation.16 Sartono Kartodirdjo argued in an influential 1962 article that the "genesis of nationalism" was a longer, more structural process that could not be dated to a single founding meeting, and that the 1908 periodisation reflected political convenience more than historical analysis.17 These debates have not settled; the 1908 date remains conventionally accepted while its meaning continues to be contested.

Budi Utomo — "Noble Endeavour"; the cultural and social organisation founded by Javanese priyayi students at STOVIA on 20 May 1908, conventionally treated as the symbolic start of the Indonesian national awakening. Its membership was largely Javanese and its orientation initially cultural rather than explicitly political.

The First Mass Movement: Sarekat Islam

The organisation that first brought Indonesian nationalism to the masses was not Budi Utomo but Sarekat Islam — and it grew from a commercial dispute, not a school. In 1911, Haji Samanhudi, a batik merchant in Surakarta, organised Sarekat Dagang Islam (SDI, Islamic Traders' Association) to defend indigenous and Arab batik producers against what he and they perceived as unfair competition from Chinese-Indonesian intermediaries who controlled much of the textile distribution network. The combination of economic grievance and Islamic identity gave the association an unusually broad popular base from its inception. Tirto Adhi Soerjo served as secretary-advisor, helping to give the association organisational coherence.18

The transformation from a traders' guild into a mass political movement was rapid and decisive. On 19 September 1912 the organisation formally renamed itself Sarekat Islam, and its new central chairman was Oemar Said Tjokroaminoto (1882–1934) — a charismatic Javanese priyayi who had absorbed Islamic education, Dutch schooling, and sharp political instincts in equal measure. Under Tjokroaminoto, SI mushroomed at a speed that astonished colonial officials: from some 4,500 members in April 1912 to 150,000 by April 1913, to 350,000 by 1916, and to a claimed two million by 1919.19 Its first national congress in January 1913 was attended by tens of thousands; colonial observers described Tjokroaminoto being "hailed as a messianic figure" by crowds who had never seen indigenous leadership on such a scale.

What made SI's growth possible was the breadth of the grievances it channeled. At one level it was a defence of indigenous economic interests against Chinese competition; at another, it offered Muslim solidarity as a counter to the hierarchical social order of the colonial state; at yet another, it provided a vehicle for the vague but powerful sense among ordinary Javanese that something was wrong and change was overdue. Takashi Shiraishi, in his foundational study of Java's "age in motion," argues that this popular radicalism from below — not the elite nationalism of the educated priyayi — was the defining feature of the 1912–1926 period.20 Timur Jaylani's study of SI noted that while Muslim masses showed willingness for sacrifice, they "lacked the technical expertise needed to match the influence of Western-educated nationalist leaders," creating a persistent tension between the organisation's popular base and its educated leadership.21

SI's very success generated internal contradictions that would soon fracture it. The organisation attempted to hold together Islamic reformers, secular nationalists, and, from around 1917, Marxist radicals associated with the Indische Sociaal-Democratische Vereeniging (ISDV), the colonial socialist organisation that would eventually become the PKI (Communist Party of Indonesia). Agus Salim, who joined the SI leadership in 1915, represented the pan-Islamic modernist wing and grew increasingly alarmed by communist influence. The strains between "SI Putih" (White SI, Islamic) and "SI Merah" (Red SI, communist) would eventually split the organisation at the Madiun congress of 1921, when Tjokroaminoto accepted the principle of "discipline" that prohibited dual SI-PKI membership. The mass movement that had seemed, around 1913–1919, to be the unstoppable engine of Indonesian independence thus fragmented — but not before it had demonstrated that millions of ordinary Indonesians could be mobilised around the idea of political change.22

Sarekat Islam — "Islamic Union"; originally Sarekat Dagang Islam (Islamic Traders' Association), founded 1911 in Surakarta, renamed 1912. Under Tjokroaminoto it became the colony's first mass movement, claiming two million members by 1919. Internal tensions between Islamic and communist factions fractured it in the early 1920s.

Indies for Indies People: The Indische Partij

If Budi Utomo represented cultural nationalism and Sarekat Islam mass Islamic mobilisation, the Indische Partij (Indies Party), founded on 25 December 1912 in Bandung, represented something more radical still: the first political organisation in the Netherlands East Indies to declare independence as its explicit goal.23 Its founders — the "Three Alliance" (Tiga Serangkai) — crossed the racial boundaries that other organisations observed. Ernest François Eugène Douwes Dekker (1879–1950, later Danudirja Setiabudi) was an Indo-European journalist, grandnephew of the novelist Eduard Douwes Dekker (Multatuli), who brought to the party a fierce critique of the racial hierarchies that degraded both Indo-Europeans and indigenous Indonesians. Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo (1886–1943) was a Javanese physician who had already clashed with the conservative Budi Utomo leadership. Soewardi Soerjaningrat (1889–1959, later Ki Hajar Dewantara) was a Javanese journalist and educator whose satirical pamphlet Als ik eens Nederlander was (If I Were a Dutchman, 1913) — mocking Dutch plans to celebrate a century of independence from Napoleon while still ruling an unwilling colony — became one of the most celebrated texts of the movement.

The party's programme was "Indie voor Indiers" — the Indies for those who identify as Indies people, regardless of ancestry. This territorial nationalism was explicitly multiracial: it welcomed Javanese, Sundanese, Batak, Indo-Europeans, and any other resident of the colony who identified their future with it rather than with the Netherlands. By early 1913 the party had around 7,000 members in 30 branches, of whom over 5,000 were Indo-Europeans and roughly 1,000 were indigenous Indonesians — a ratio that both reflected the party's Indo-European leadership and limited its mass appeal.24

The colonial government moved swiftly. The Indische Partij was denied legal recognition in March 1913 on the grounds that it sought to "undermine the authority of the Dutch government." Soewardi's satirical pamphlet prompted his arrest and that of his two co-founders; all three were exiled to the Netherlands, where they continued their activism in a Dutch environment that offered, paradoxically, more freedom of speech than Batavia. In exile, Soewardi immersed himself in educational thought — particularly the ideas of the Italian educator Maria Montessori and the German philosopher Friedrich Fröbel — and returned to Indonesia in 1919 to found the Taman Siswa (Garden of Students) independent school movement, which would become one of the most significant non-state educational institutions of the colonial period. The Indische Partij itself did not survive the exile of its founders. But its declaration of independence as a goal, its multiracial framing, and the fame of Soewardi's pamphlet fixed markers that later nationalist organisations would find indispensable.25

Indische Partij — The first political organisation in the Netherlands East Indies to declare independence as its explicit goal, founded 25 December 1912 by Douwes Dekker, Tjipto Mangoenkoesoemo, and Soewardi Soerjaningrat (Ki Hajar Dewantara). Denied legal recognition and its founders exiled in 1913, it nonetheless set the template of territorial, multiracial Indonesian nationalism.

Words as Weapons: The Vernacular Press and the Indonesian Language

Underpinning all the organisations described above was a revolution in communication. The vernacular press — newspapers, pamphlets, and periodicals in Malay, Javanese, Sundanese, and other local languages — was both the infrastructure of nationalist mobilisation and the crucible in which the idea of a single Indonesian nation was imagined into being.

Ahmat B. Adam's foundational study of the vernacular press challenges the standard 1908 periodisation by showing that "a Malay-speaking colonial urban society" with its own press infrastructure had been developing since the 1850s, and that Indonesian-language political journalism was possible by 1913 because of a "long gestation period of a public Malay voice" stretching back through earlier Indo-European and Chinese-Indonesian print culture.26 The colonial city produced print audiences before it produced nationalist organisations; the organisations then used the existing print infrastructure to reach those audiences. By 1912–1913 Batavia, Surabaya, Semarang, Bandung, and Medan each had multiple Malay-language newspapers addressing indigenous readers on political topics that Dutch-language papers ignored.

The language question was as significant as the press infrastructure itself. Bazaar Malay (bahasa Melayu pasar), long the lingua franca of the archipelago's inter-island trade, was a language of commercial convenience rather than cultural identity. What the press did — and what Budi Utomo, SI, and the Indische Partij all contributed to in different ways — was to begin transforming Malay into something more: a language with ideological and political charge, eventually capable of carrying the weight of a national claim. When the 1928 Youth Pledge declared "one language — Indonesian" (satu bahasa — Indonesia), it was not creating a new language from nothing; it was elevating and renaming a vernacular that the press and the movement had spent two decades politicising.27 The colonial government's own intervention, the Balai Pustaka (People's Reading House) established in 1917 to publish "safe" vernacular literature, simultaneously spread Malay literacy and, inevitably, spread the capacity for reading nationalist texts alongside approved ones.

R.E. Elson notes that the appropriation of the word "Indonesia" itself — a mid-nineteenth-century ethnographic coinage by European scholars — for explicitly political purposes was an act accomplished in large part through the press: Soewardi Soerjaningrat named his Dutch exile publication an Indonesisch Pers-bureau in 1913, and by the early 1920s young radicals in student organisations in the Netherlands and in Batavia were deploying "Indonesia" and "Indonesian" as deliberate counters to "Netherlands East Indies" and "Inlander" (a colonial term for native inhabitants with a derogatory edge).28 Words, as much as organisations, were the instruments of national construction.

Global Winds: Pan-Islamism and the Russo-Japanese War

The national awakening did not develop in a colonial vacuum. Three global currents fed Indonesian nationalist consciousness in the decade before the First World War: the pan-Islamic movement centred on the Ottoman caliphate, the shock of Japan's military victory over Russia, and the wider anti-colonial ferment of the early twentieth century.

Pan-Islamism arrived in the Indies primarily through two channels: the annual Hajj pilgrimage, which brought tens of thousands of Indonesian Muslims to Mecca and exposed them to modernist Salafi ideas as well as to news of Muslim struggles against European imperialism; and the Malay-Arabic press, which circulated Ottoman pan-Islamic propaganda across the Indian Ocean world. Justus van der Kroef's early analysis of Islam in Indonesian nationalism identified the Hajj network as a crucial conduit for political as well as religious ideas, noting that returning pilgrims carried both a renewed sense of Islamic solidarity and a sharpened awareness of their subordination within the colonial order.29 Deliar Noer's comprehensive study of the modernist Muslim movement traces how ideas generated in the reformist circles of Mecca, Cairo, and Istanbul filtered into the intellectual formation of both Muhammadiyah and the pan-Islamist wing of Sarekat Islam.30 The SI leader Agus Salim was the most articulate advocate of an Islamic internationalism that linked the Indonesian struggle to Muslim resistance movements from Egypt to India.

The Russo-Japanese War of 1904–05 struck contemporary Asian observers with an impact that is difficult to recapture in retrospect. Japan's comprehensive defeat of Russia constituted, as EBSCO Research Starters summarises, "the first time in modern history that a major European power was decisively and completely defeated by a non-European nation," raising "the hopes of nationalists in Asia and Africa that the days of European colonialism were numbered."31 In the Indies, the news was received across all communities. Indigenous students in Dutch schools who had been taught that European civilisation represented an unassailable pinnacle of human achievement were confronted with a vivid counter-demonstration: an Asian power had out-organised, out-fought, and out-strategised a European empire. Colonial officials noted with alarm the circulation of pro-Japanese sentiments. Nationalist historians would later identify the war alongside the Philippine independence struggle (1898–1902), the founding of the Indian National Congress (1885), and the Russian Revolution (1917) as the four principal external catalysts for Indonesian nationalist consciousness.32

The wider anti-colonial world was present in the reading rooms and discussions of every nationalist organisation. The Indian National Congress, the Egyptian nationalist movement under Mustafa Kamil, and the Young Turk revolution of 1908 all demonstrated that colonial subjects could organise, demand, and in some cases win. The First World War (1914–18) intensified the lesson: European powers that had presented themselves as bearers of civilisation proceeded to slaughter one another in industrial quantities, while demanding military and economic sacrifices from their colonies. Pols notes that for the STOVIA physician-nationalists, the war demonstrated that the science they had been taught to revere could be turned to mass destruction, accelerating the radicalism of a younger generation.33

Islam Reformed and Organised: Muhammadiyah and Nahdlatul Ulama

Two of the most durable organisations of the entire period were not primarily political parties at all, but Islamic social and educational movements — and their relationship to nationalism was always complex, indirect, and contested.

Muhammadiyah was founded on 18 November 1912 in Yogyakarta by Ahmad Dahlan (1868–1923), a court official of the Yogyakarta Sultanate who had studied in Mecca under the reformist scholar Ahmad Khatib Al-Minangkabawi. Ahmad Dahlan returned from Mecca convinced that Indonesian Islam had become encrusted with Javanese syncretic practices — elements of Hindu-Buddhist cosmology, ancestor veneration, and local spirit beliefs — that deviated from what he regarded as authentic Qur'anic teaching. Muhammadiyah's mission was to purify these accretions through ijtihad (independent reinterpretation of scripture), to modernise Muslim social life through hospitals, clinics, and Dutch-language schools, and to train women as well as men to participate in Islamic public life.34

ijtihad — Independent interpretation of Islamic scripture by a qualified scholar, as opposed to taqlid (unquestioning conformity to established legal rulings). Muhammadiyah's advocacy of ijtihad was central to its modernist Islamic vision; Nahdlatul Ulama's defence of traditional legal schools was, in part, a response to Muhammadiyah's challenge.
pesantren — A traditional Islamic boarding school in which students (santri) live with and study under a senior Islamic scholar (kyai). The pesantren network was the organisational and social base of Nahdlatul Ulama.

Muhammadiyah grew steadily: approximately 2,600 men and 724 women at Ahmad Dahlan's death in 1923; 10,000 members by 1928; 250,000 by 1938.35 It built a nationwide infrastructure of schools, clinics, and mosques, operating on a parallel track to the colonial state's own institutions — and offering a vision of modernity that was Islamic rather than Dutch. Deliar Noer identifies Muhammadiyah as the principal vehicle of the modernist Muslim movement, carefully distinguishing it from Sarekat Islam (principally political) while noting that the modernists "rejected on principle any clear distinction between politics and religion, because they felt that Islam did not allow that distinction."36

Nahdlatul Ulama (NU, Renaissance of the Scholars) was founded on 31 January 1926 in Surabaya by Hasyim Asy'ari (1875–1947), head of the Tebuireng pesantren in East Java — and, like Ahmad Dahlan, a former student of Ahmad Khatib Al-Minangkabawi in Mecca, illustrating the remarkable way in which the same Mecca-trained teacher produced the founders of both rival organisations. NU was explicitly traditionalist: it sought to defend the authority of the classical Ash'ari theological tradition and the Shafi'i legal school against Muhammadiyah's reformist challenge, and to protect the pesantren network and the social world of the rural kyai from being displaced by modernist institutions.37

In its early decades NU was cautious about explicitly political engagement, preferring to focus on religious education and social welfare. But its relationship to nationalism was never merely passive: it represented, in the sociological analysis of Ajid Thohir and colleagues, a "santri" Islamic identity — devout, learned, rooted in rural Java — that coexisted uneasily with the "abangan" syncretic religious practice of many Javanese, and provided an independent base of authority from which to resist colonial pressure.38 When independence came, NU would prove to be one of the largest and most consequential organisations in the new republic; its founding in 1926 closed out the period of organisational formation that had begun with Budi Utomo eighteen years earlier.

Muhammadiyah — Islamic modernist social and educational organisation, founded 1912 in Yogyakarta by Ahmad Dahlan. Advocates ijtihad, condemns syncretic practice, and built a nationwide network of schools and clinics. Now one of the world's largest Islamic organisations.
Nahdlatul Ulama — "Renaissance of the Scholars"; traditionalist Islamic organisation founded 1926 in East Java by Hasyim Asy'ari. Defends classical Ash'ari theology, the Shafi'i legal school, and the pesantren network against modernist reform. Now claims over 40 million members.

Contested Ground: Historiographical Debates and Legacies

The history of the national awakening is as contested as the period itself. Several interlocking debates structure the scholarship and deserve explicit attention.

When did nationalism begin? The Indonesian state's official answer — 20 May 1908 — is a political choice as much as a historical verdict. Ahmat B. Adam's work on the vernacular press suggests that "modern Indonesian consciousness" was being formed in Malay-speaking colonial cities from the 1850s, making the 1908 date an institutional convenience rather than an intellectual origin. Sartono Kartodirdjo argued in his foundational 1962 article that the "genesis of nationalism" was a structural process whose conditions included urbanisation, the money economy, the decline of traditional authority, and the development of print culture — none of which were inaugurated by a single meeting at STOVIA.39 R.E. Elson's comparative study of the "idea of Indonesia" traces a conceptual genealogy running from the 1850 ethnographic coinage through to the early twentieth century, showing that "Indonesia" as a political claim was the product of gradual appropriation rather than sudden inspiration.40

Elite nationalism or popular movement? Robert Van Niel's influential thesis — that the modern Indonesian elite was essentially a conservative extension of the old priyayi stratum, colonial-accommodating so long as Dutch authority held firm — has been challenged by Shiraishi's insistence on popular radicalism from below, and by Kahin's portrayal of nationalism as a genuine liberation struggle with broad social roots.41 The tension between these readings is not merely academic: it shapes assessments of which organisations mattered most (elite Budi Utomo vs mass Sarekat Islam), which social groups drove the movement (educated professionals vs urban workers and peasants), and whether the eventual achievement of independence represented the triumph of an elite project or a genuine popular revolution.

Was "Indonesia" the frame from the start? Adrian Vickers has noted that as late as c. 1920, what would become Indonesian nationalism was more commonly conceived in ethnic territorial terms — as a Javanese movement, a Balinese movement, a Sumatran movement — than as a single Indonesian one. The progressive substitution of "Indonesia" for those ethnic frames was itself a political achievement, accomplished through the press, student organisations in the Netherlands, and youth congresses, culminating in the 1928 Youth Pledge.42 The question of whether the "national awakening" created Indonesian national identity or merely expressed it is, in Benedict Anderson's terms, about imagined community: the nation had to be imagined before it could be fought for, and the imagining was work that took decades and never fully resolved its internal tensions.

What kind of Indonesia? The three principal streams that coursed through the national awakening — secular territorial nationalism (Indische Partij), Islamic nationalism (Sarekat Islam, Muhammadiyah, NU), and socialist/communist radicalism (emerging in the SI's left wing by 1917) — were not merely tactical disagreements about methods; they were incompatible visions of the kind of state and society Indonesia should be. These incompatibilities would generate the great ideological contests of the 1920s–1930s, the period covered in Chapter 25. The world Budi Utomo, Sarekat Islam, and the Indische Partij had made was a world of competing nationalisms, each claiming to speak for the same archipelago, each with different answers to the question of what — beyond independence itself — Indonesia was for.

Summary

Indonesian nationalism emerged from an unlikely convergence: the Western-educated priyayi elite produced by the Dutch Ethical Policy (1901), the mass Islamic mobilisation of Sarekat Islam (1911–12), the explicitly independence-seeking multiracial programme of the Indische Partij (1912), and the Javanese cultural nationalism of Budi Utomo (1908). These were not a unified movement; they were competing visions of who "Indonesia" was and what it should become. Running beneath and through all of them was a revolution in print and language — the vernacular press transforming Malay from a trade pidgin into a political weapon — and a set of global stimuli that included pan-Islamism, the Russo-Japanese War, and the worldwide ferment of anti-colonial thought. Two major Islamic social organisations — the modernist Muhammadiyah (1912) and the traditionalist Nahdlatul Ulama (1926) — built parallel civil societies that would outlast both the colonial state and most of the period's political parties. By 1928, when the Youth Pledge (Sumpah Pemuda) declared one Indonesian homeland, nation, and language, the idea of Indonesia had been argued into existence; the struggle over what that idea meant had scarcely begun.

Review & Discussion Questions

24.1 The Dutch Ethical Policy is often described as containing a paradox: it created the elite that would ultimately challenge colonial rule. What were the limits of this paradox — that is, in what ways did the Ethical Policy succeed in producing a loyal, colonial-accommodating elite rather than a revolutionary one?
24.2 Budi Utomo (1908) has been designated the symbolic start of the national awakening, yet it was an explicitly Javanese organisation. What does the Indonesian state's choice of this date over alternatives (say, the founding of Sarekat Islam in 1912) reveal about the politics of national memory?
24.3 Kartini's letters were edited by a Dutch colonial official before publication, removing passages critical of colonialism. How should historians use a source whose content was shaped by its editor? Does this editorial intervention change the significance of Kartini as a historical figure?
24.4 Compare the nationalism of the Indische Partij ("Indies for Indies people," a territorial and multiracial frame) with the nationalism of Sarekat Islam (rooted in Islamic solidarity). What did each vision gain and sacrifice relative to the other?
24.5 Muhammadiyah and Nahdlatul Ulama were founded by two men who studied under the same teacher in Mecca, yet they represent opposing tendencies within Indonesian Islam. What does this reveal about the relationship between pan-Islamic education networks and local religious diversity?
24.6 The Russo-Japanese War (1904–05) is often cited as a catalyst for Asian nationalist movements. What specific mechanisms might explain how a military conflict between two foreign powers accelerated nationalist consciousness in the Netherlands East Indies?
24.7 Takashi Shiraishi argues that popular radicalism from below, not elite nationalism, drove the "age in motion." Robert Van Niel argues the early nationalist elite was essentially conservative and colonial-accommodating. How would you assess these competing views in light of Sarekat Islam's extraordinary growth?
24.8 By 1926, three distinct visions of Indonesia were already in competition: secular territorial nationalism, Islamic nationalism, and socialist radicalism. To what extent is the history of independent Indonesia (1945–present) a continuation of the competition that began in this period?
