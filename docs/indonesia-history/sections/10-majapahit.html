Learning Objectives

  • Trace the founding of Majapahit from the collapse of Singhasari through Raden Wijaya's strategic exploitation of the Mongol invasion of 1293.
  • Analyse the apogee of the empire under Queen Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi, Gajah Mada's Palapa Oath, and the reign of Hayam Wuruk (1350–1389).
  • Evaluate the principal primary sources — the Nagarakretagama and the Pararaton — and understand how historians read them critically rather than literally.
  • Assess the contested debate over Majapahit's territorial extent: genuine empire, tributary network, mandala state, or nationalist myth.
  • Describe the capital at Trowulan, the empire's syncretic Hindu-Buddhist religion, the causes of decline, and Majapahit's long afterlife in modern Indonesian nationalism.

In November 1293, a Javanese prince called Raden Wijaya received the homage of what remained of a Mongol expeditionary army — and then turned on his allies and drove them into the sea. From that audacious reversal of fortune was born Majapahit, the empire whose territorial claims would one day become the template for Indonesia itself. For roughly two centuries, from a capital in the Brantas River delta of East Java, Majapahit projected power, prestige, and culture across the maritime world of Southeast Asia. Its golden age — when the court poet Mpu Prapanca composed the Nagarakretagama and the chief minister Gajah Mada swore his Palapa Oath — remains the most celebrated chapter in pre-colonial Indonesian history. Yet historians have never ceased arguing about exactly what Majapahit was: a genuine empire with nearly a hundred tributaries, a loosely connected maritime trade network, or a nationalist myth retroactively enlarged to justify modern Indonesia's borders. Answering that question honestly requires reading the sources as the political documents they were, weighing the archaeology at Trowulan, and understanding why a fourteenth-century Javanese kingdom still carries such weight in twenty-first-century Indonesia.

The Founding of Majapahit: Raden Wijaya and the Mongol Opportunity, 1292–1293

The empire that became Majapahit rose directly from the wreckage of its predecessor. Singhasari, the East Javanese kingdom founded by the legendary Ken Arok in 1222, had reached its greatest ambitions under King Kertanagara (r. c. 1268–1292) — a ruler described in later tradition as simultaneously a master of Tantric ritual and an incorrigible drunkard, but unquestionably an energetic imperialist who launched seaborne expeditions as far as Sumatra and the Malay Peninsula.1 His downfall came from within: in 1292, Jayakatwang, the Adipati (duke) of the vassal kingdom of Kediri, rose in rebellion while Kertanagara's forces were occupied in Sumatra and killed the king. Kertanagara's son-in-law, the prince Raden Wijaya, escaped the massacre and fled to Madura, where he allied with the regional governor Arya Wiraraja and began to plan his return.2

The instrument of his recovery was the great Yuan fleet. Kertanagara had made the catastrophic diplomatic error of mutilating the face of Kublai Khan's envoy, who arrived demanding tribute, and sending him back to China. The Khan dispatched a punitive expedition estimated at twenty to thirty thousand men carried aboard five hundred to a thousand vessels from southern China.3 When this armada landed on the north coast of Java in 1293, its original target was already dead. Wijaya, with Wiraraja's backing, struck an alliance with the Yuan commanders: he would guide them against Jayakatwang in exchange for assistance restoring his own position. The joint campaign was swift. Jayakatwang's forces were routed; Kediri fell. But the moment Jayakatwang surrendered, Wijaya executed the reversal that makes his story memorable. On 26 May 1293 he launched a surprise attack on his Mongol allies: weakened by tropical heat, ambushed in unfamiliar terrain, and facing a monsoon season that would have stranded their fleet for months, the Yuan commanders chose to withdraw, suffering casualties estimated in the tens of thousands. The fleet never returned.4

On 10 November 1293 — the fifteenth day of the month of Kārttika in the Śaka year 1215 — Raden Wijaya was crowned at the village of Majapahit in the Brantas River delta, taking the regnal name Kertarajasa Jayawardhana.5 The kingdom's name derived from the maja tree (Aegle marmelos) that grew in abundance at the site — pahit meaning "bitter" in Javanese, a reference to the fruit's taste. To legitimate his new dynasty, Wijaya married four daughters of the murdered Kertanagara, as well as Indreswari from the Dharmasraya kingdom of Sumatra, binding the new line genealogically to its Singhasari predecessor.6 When he died in 1309, he was interred at Candi Simping near Blitar and posthumously deified as Harihara — the composite deity formed by the fusion of Shiva (right half) and Vishnu (left half) — inaugurating the pattern of royal apotheosis that would characterize the Majapahit dynasty for the next century.6

Harihara — the composite Hindu deity formed by the fusion of Shiva and Vishnu; at Majapahit (and before it, Singhasari) used as the posthumous divine form assigned to deceased rulers in their mortuary temples, expressing the court's syncretic theological vision.

Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi and the Rise of Gajah Mada

Kertarajasa's death in 1309 was followed by a turbulent succession. His son Jayanegara, who reigned until his murder in 1328 by a court physician, presided over an era punctuated by military revolts. The decisive pivot came when Jayanegara's step-sister, Tribhuwana Wijayatunggadewi — daughter of Kertarajasa and the queen-mother Gayatri Rajapatni — assumed the throne at her mother's direction in 1328–1329. Tribhuwana (r. c. 1329–1350) is perhaps the most consequential of all Majapahit's rulers, not for her own military campaigns — though she led successful expeditions against Bali and Kalimantan — but for a single administrative decision: in approximately 1334, she appointed Gajah Mada as Mahapatih, the chief minister of the realm.7

Gajah Mada's origins are obscure. He may have risen from humble birth through the ranks of the elite royal guard (Bhayangkara) by force of ability alone; the first recorded act attributed to him is the suppression of a dangerous plot against Jayanegara, which he accomplished by barricading the king in a fortified compound and outwaiting the conspirators.8 What is certain is that his appointment as Mahapatih transformed Majapahit's ambitions. At his inauguration — recorded not in the Nagarakretagama but in the later chronicle the Pararaton — Gajah Mada swore what became the most famous oath in Indonesian history: the Sumpah Palapa, the Palapa Oath.9

The original Old Javanese text, as preserved in the Pararaton, reads: "Lamun huwus kalah Nusantara isun amukti palapa... lamun kalah ring Gurun, ring Seram, Tanjungpura, ring Haru, ring Pahang, Dompo, ring Bali, Sunda, Palembang, Tumasik, samana isun amukti palapa."9 The translation is approximately: "If I have conquered the Nusantara archipelago... if [I have conquered] Gurun, Seram, Tanjungpura, Haru, Pahang, Dompo, Bali, Sunda, Palembang, Tumasik — only then shall I amukti palapa." The precise meaning of amukti palapa is debated: some scholars understand it as "enjoy rest" or "enjoy pleasure," others as "eat spices" — meaning Gajah Mada was vowing to abstain from earthly pleasures until he had unified the entire archipelago under Majapahit.10 Either reading carries the same political meaning: total regional domination before any personal reward.

Mahapatih — the chief minister of the Majapahit state, equivalent to a prime minister; the most powerful administrative official below the ruler. Gajah Mada held this office c. 1334–1364 and used it to direct Majapahit's wars of expansion.
Nusantara — Old Javanese for "outer islands" (from Sanskrit nusa, island, and antara, other/outer); used by Gajah Mada in his Palapa Oath to designate the archipelago he pledged to unify. The term was adopted in 2022 as the name of Indonesia's new capital city in East Kalimantan, illustrating the medieval concept's modern political potency.

Over the following three decades, Gajah Mada orchestrated a sustained programme of military expansion. By 1343, Majapahit forces had conquered Bali — the last significant independent Hindu polity in the island chain — and brought Lombok and Sumbawa under control.11 Campaigns pushed further: Palembang, Malayu (Jambi), the Muslim sultanate of Samudra Pasai in northern Sumatra, Bintan, and territories of the Malay Peninsula came into Majapahit's orbit. The Nagarakretagama's enormous list of tributaries — compiled two decades into Hayam Wuruk's reign — is the partial fruit of these campaigns, even if, as we shall see, it claims considerably more than Gajah Mada actually administered.

Tribhuwana abdicated in 1350 upon the death of her mother Gayatri, whose spiritual authority had underwritten the reign, ceding the throne to her sixteen-year-old son Hayam Wuruk. She was posthumously deified as the goddess Parvati.7

The Golden Age: Hayam Wuruk's Realm, 1350–1389

Hayam Wuruk (born 1334; formal title Sri Rajasanagara; r. 1350–1389) presided over what contemporaries and historians alike recognize as Majapahit's apogee.12 The portrait that emerges from the Nagarakretagama — composed in 1365, the fifteenth year of his reign — depicts a king accomplished in martial arts, mask-dancing, music, and the religious scriptures of both Hindu and Buddhist tradition, presiding over a court of extraordinary refinement. Mpu Prapanca describes Hayam Wuruk's birth as a divine manifestation of Shiva himself: the king was literally the god incarnate on Earth.13

The great Mahapatih Gajah Mada served Hayam Wuruk as he had Tribhuwana, until the catastrophe of the Bubat Incident of 1357. The king of the Sunda Kingdom in West Java had arrived at the Majapahit court with his daughter, Princess Dyah Pitaloka Citraresmi, intended as a bride for Hayam Wuruk in a match that would have constituted a diplomatic alliance between peers. Gajah Mada intervened: he demanded the Sundanese delegation submit as vassals rather than as diplomatic equals, insisting the princess be surrendered as tribute rather than offered in marriage. The Sundanese king refused. A confrontation broke out at the courtyard called Bubat; the Sundanese king and his retinue were slaughtered, and the princess reportedly died by suicide. Hayam Wuruk's own courtiers blamed Gajah Mada for the provocation.14 The Mahapatih was stripped of several administrative responsibilities; he died in approximately 1364, his oath formally unfulfilled — Sunda had never been subjugated — and Hayam Wuruk thereafter distributed the office's vast powers among four separate officials, unwilling to allow any single minister to accumulate such authority again.14

Yet the reign's cultural achievements were genuine. Hayam Wuruk's annual royal progresses (napak tilas) through his realm — described in loving detail across more than a dozen cantos of the Nagarakretagama — were elaborate state ceremonies touching scores of holy sites, vassals, and religious foundations, weaving the realm into a single sacred geography. Diplomatic missions from China, the Malay kingdoms, Champa, Cambodia, and Siam arrived at Trowulan. The poet Mpu Tantular composed his Kakawin Sutasoma in these years, the work that would furnish modern Indonesia with its national motto. And in 1365, Mpu Prapanca completed the text that is simultaneously our richest source and our greatest interpretive challenge. Hayam Wuruk died in 1389 without having arranged an unambiguous single successor, setting off the disputes that would eventually consume the empire.15

Reading the Sources: Nagarakretagama and Pararaton

No scholar can approach Majapahit without first reckoning with its two principal literary sources — and without understanding how historians read them critically rather than at face value.

The Nagarakretagama (Desawarnana)

The Nagarakretagama — its proper Old Javanese title is Deśawarṇana, meaning "Description of the Districts/Regions," a name preferred by its modern English translator Stuart Robson — is an Old Javanese kakawin (a court epic in Sanskrit metres) composed in 1365 by Mpu Prapanca.16 Prapanca was a Buddhist monk who served as Dharmadhyakṣa ring kasogatan (head of Buddhist ceremonial affairs) at the Majapahit court, a position he had inherited from his father.17 The poem comprises 98 cantos and performs multiple functions simultaneously: it is a eulogy of Hayam Wuruk, a descriptive gazetteer of the realm's holy places and administrative units, a record of the royal progresses, a genealogy of the dynasty, and an account of diplomatic relations with neighbouring states.18

kakawin — a genre of Old Javanese court poetry composed in Sanskrit quantitative metres; the prestige literary form of the Majapahit court. The Nagarakretagama, the Kakawin Sutasoma, and earlier works such as the Arjunawiwaha are major examples. The author Mpu Prapanca and the poet Mpu Tantular were the form's greatest Majapahit-era practitioners.

The manuscript's subsequent history was turbulent. Written on lontar (palm leaf), it was held in the Lombok royal palace for centuries and remained entirely unknown to Western scholarship until Dutch forces seized that palace in 1894 during a military expedition. The scholar J.L.A. Brandes helped preserve it; T.G.Th. Pigeaud produced a monumental Dutch edition with commentary in five volumes (1960–1963). The standard modern English translation is Stuart Robson's Deśawarṇana (Nāgarakṛtāgama) (Leiden: KITLV Press, 1995), which Jacob Ensink, reviewing it in the Journal of Southeast Asian Studies, recognized as a milestone bringing the full apparatus of Old Javanese philology to an Anglophone readership.19 In 1970, the manuscript was returned to Indonesia during President Suharto's state visit to the Netherlands. UNESCO inscribed it as a Memory of the World record in 2013.20

What does the text claim? Most famously, it names some 98 tributaries of Majapahit spread across an enormous arc from Sumatra in the west to the eastern islands of the archipelago — including territories corresponding to parts of modern Malaysia, Brunei, the southern Philippines, Timor, and coastal New Guinea.18 For nationalist readers, this list is proof of Majapahit's imperial reach anticipating modern Indonesia's boundaries. For historians, however, the list requires a different kind of reading. As a royal eulogy, the text was designed to magnify its patron, not to provide an administrative inventory. Prapanca lists as tributaries many polities that were, at most, states that had sent occasional tribute missions or maintained trading relationships with the Majapahit court. The Nagarakretagama is indispensable — without it we would know almost nothing about Majapahit's internal life — but it must be read as a political document, a performance of suzerainty as much as a record of it.

The Pararaton

The Pararaton ("Book of Kings"; also known as Katuturanira Ken Angrok, "Story of Ken Arok") is a very different kind of text. Written in Kawi (Old Javanese) on 32 lontar folios totalling approximately 1,126 lines, it was composed — or more likely compiled from earlier oral and written sources — between approximately 1481 and 1600, well after the fall of the empire it describes.21 Anonymous and retrospective where the Nagarakretagama is named and contemporary, the Pararaton blends legend with chronicle: its first half is dominated by the mythological adventures of Ken Arok, the founder of Singhasari, who appears as a figure of divine parentage, supernatural weaponry, and cursed daggers. Its later sections, covering Majapahit's rulers, are more historically grounded, though still filtered through the conventions of Javanese court literature.

Critically, the Palapa Oath of Gajah Mada appears in the Pararaton, not in the Nagarakretagama.21 This is a fact of considerable significance: the most celebrated event associated with Majapahit's imperial ambition is recorded only in a text compiled at least a century after that ambition was expressed. The Nagarakretagama records Gajah Mada's administrative role and campaigns in general terms, but the oath formula itself — with its specific list of polities and its dramatic vow — is a Pararaton-only tradition. Whether this makes the oath historically unreliable is debated; the Pararaton's later sections contain details corroborated by Chinese records and inscriptions, suggesting that its compilers drew on authentic earlier sources even when encrusting them with legendary elaboration.

A methodological caution: The historian confronting Majapahit must work from an unusually thin and tendentious source base. The picture of the kingdom's greatness in the second half of the fourteenth century rests overwhelmingly on a single court eulogy (the Nagarakretagama) and on a retrospective chronicle compiled at least a century later (the Pararaton). Chinese diplomatic records and stone inscriptions provide partial corroboration of dates and events, but neither the Mongol invasion records nor the Ming dynasty sources give an independently detailed view of Majapahit's internal arrangements. Every specific claim about the empire's administration, religion, and territorial reach should be understood as passing through this narrow, politically committed source tradition.

Empire, Trade Network, or Nationalist Myth? The Territorial Debate

The question of what Majapahit actually was — in terms of territory and the exercise of power — is one of the most actively contested issues in Indonesian historiography, touching as it does on contemporary politics.

The Nationalist Reading

The nationalist interpretation, powerfully articulated in the mid-twentieth century by Indonesian scholars and politicians such as Mohammad Yamin, reads the Nagarakretagama's 98-tributary list as proof that Majapahit was a genuine empire coextensive with modern Indonesia — and indeed somewhat larger. In this reading, Gajah Mada completed the Palapa Oath; the empire ruled the entire archipelago; and the Republic of Indonesia is the legitimate heir to a pre-colonial territorial unity that was indigenous, not colonial, in origin.22 Yamin's 1945 book on Gajah Mada — written as much as a nationalist manifesto as a work of scholarship — went so far as to identify a terracotta figurine fragment from Trowulan as a portrait of the great Mahapatih himself, a claim archaeologists have since questioned given the impossibility of verifying a personal likeness from any known depiction.23

C.C. Berg's Radical Skepticism

At the opposite extreme stands the Dutch scholar Cornelis Christiaan Berg (1900–1990). In his 1962 monograph Het Rijk van de vijfvoudige Buddha (The Kingdom of the Fivefold Buddha), published by the Royal Netherlands Academy of Sciences, Berg advanced the startling thesis that the Old Javanese textual record — including the Nagarakretagama — was not a historical record at all, but a supernatural instrument for determining and shaping the future.24 In Berg's reading, texts such as the Nagarakretagama functioned as magical prognostications; their apparent historical content was constructed to produce cosmological effects rather than to record actual events. The conclusion was that Majapahit as conventionally described was essentially a scholarly projection onto a body of cosmological literature.

This position has found little support in the scholarly mainstream. As the New World Encyclopedia summarizes, "most scholars do not accept this view."25 Berg's radical claims are now generally regarded as overreaching. Nevertheless, his work performed a lasting service by forcing historians to think rigorously about the political and ritual functions of these texts — rather than accepting them as transparent windows onto the past. The best subsequent scholarship navigates between naive literalism and Berg's wholesale skepticism: treating the Nagarakretagama as a genuine historical document about courtly life and Majapahit's self-image, while reading its territorial claims as ideological assertions rather than administrative inventories.

The Mandala-State Consensus

The prevailing scholarly consensus, represented by historians including M.C. Ricklefs, draws on O.W. Wolters's influential Southeast Asian "mandala" model to view Majapahit as what one might call a mandala state: a polity defined by its brilliant centre rather than by fixed territorial boundaries, in which outer dependencies were connected to the core through personal relationships between rulers, trade networks, and periodic tribute rather than through bureaucratic integration.26 In concrete terms:

  • Direct administration by Majapahit extended primarily over East Java and Bali.
  • Outer territories were organized in a three-tier structure: the palace and its immediate hinterland; directly administered East Java and Bali; and a broad outer ring of nominally tributary polities enjoying substantial internal autonomy.
  • Geographic and logistical constraints made it impossible for the empire to exercise consistent coercive control over the scattered islands of the outer archipelago — the sea that connected them also created enormous distances between any central authority and the far periphery.
  • The 98-tributary list in the Nagarakretagama reflects an ideological claim — the aspiration of a mandala state whose prestige radiated outward — not an administrative inventory.27

This does not mean Majapahit was a fiction. Its power was real: its navy patrolled trade routes, its prestige drew tribute missions from distant polities, its cultural influence spread Hindu-Buddhist art and court culture across the archipelago, and its merchants participated in a vibrant maritime economy. But the difference between direct imperial administration and a hegemonic trade-and-prestige network is substantial — and the political implications, especially for modern Indonesian territorial claims, are considerable.

mandala state — a concept from Southeast Asian historical studies, associated with O.W. Wolters, describing a polity whose power radiates outward from a brilliant centre through personal, ritual, and commercial relationships rather than through fixed territorial borders. The mandala state is defined by the prestige of its core, not the firmness of its edges; outer dependencies drift in and out of its orbit as the centre's power waxes and wanes.

Capital, Economy, and Religion: Trowulan and Its World

The Capital at Trowulan

Trowulan, located in what is now Mojokerto Regency, East Java, approximately 50 kilometres southwest of Surabaya, is the largest pre-colonial urban site in Southeast Asia. Archaeological surveys estimate the settled area at approximately 100 square kilometres, crisscrossed by an extensive network of brick-lined canals that served both hydraulic management and commercial circulation.28 Thomas Stamford Raffles, who first systematically documented the site in 1815, called it "this pride of Java." The Dutch engineer Henri Maclaine Pont conducted the first systematic modern excavations in 1921–1924, comparing his findings against the Nagarakretagama's descriptions of the royal precinct and its surrounding settlements.29

The principal surviving structures speak to both the capital's physical grandeur and the depth of the Hindu-Buddhist world it embodied:

  • Wringin Lawang: a 14th-century candi bentar (split gateway) of red brick standing 15.5 metres high — one of the oldest surviving split gates in Java, its twin halves representing the mountain-gate of the cosmic realm.
  • Bajang Ratu: an elegant paduraksa (roofed gateway) standing 16.5 metres, associated in tradition with commemorating King Jayanegara.
  • Candi Tikus (the "Mouse Temple"): a ritual bathing pool with concentric turrets modelled on the mythological cosmic mountain, Mahameru — a microcosm of the Hindu universe in red brick and water.
  • Segaran Pool: a massive reservoir measuring approximately 800 by 500 metres, serving as both freshwater supply and possibly ceremonial space.29

In October 2009, Indonesia submitted Trowulan to UNESCO's World Heritage tentative list as "The Trowulan Archaeological Site of the Majapahit Capital," recognising both the site's importance and the threats it faces from brick-making industries and construction activity that consume its archaeological deposits.30 The Museum Majapahit (Trowulan Museum), established in 1980, now houses thousands of terracotta figures, glazed ceramics, stone sculpture, and architectural fragments from the site.

Economy and Trade

Majapahit's wealth rested on the intersection of agrarian productivity and maritime commerce. East Java's fertile volcanic soils, irrigated by the Brantas and Solo river systems, supported the dense wet-rice agriculture that gave the court the surplus necessary to sustain a complex bureaucracy, a military establishment, and an elaborate religious infrastructure. But the maritime dimension was equally fundamental. The Nagarakretagama describes trading partners from China, India, Cambodia, Siam, Annam, and the Malay world visiting the capital; Chinese porcelain and Islamic glassware found at Trowulan testify to these connections.31 Majapahit's position astride the Java Sea made it a natural intermediary in the spice trade flowing westward from the Maluku islands. Royal merchants (saudagar) moved between Trowulan and the outer ports, reinforcing both commercial ties and political suzerainty.

The Śiva-Buddha Synthesis

Majapahit's official religion is most accurately described as a syncretic fusion of Shaivite Hinduism and Mahayana Buddhism, known in scholarship as the Śiva-Buddha (or Siwa-Buddha) synthesis.32 This was not a blending of the two traditions into a single theology but a structured coexistence in which the court simultaneously maintained both Buddhist and Shaivite establishments. Raden Wijaya's posthumous deification as Harihara (Shiva-Vishnu) set the pattern; subsequent rulers received similar dual-tradition honours in their mortuary temples. Mpu Prapanca, as Dharmadhyakṣa ring kasogatan (head of Buddhist affairs), served alongside the Dharmadhyakṣa ring kasaiwan (head of Shaivite affairs) in a deliberate institutional parallelism. Hayam Wuruk's court ceremonies wove both traditions together in elaborate annual festivals.32

Śiva-Buddha — the syncretic religious tradition of the Majapahit court, combining Shaivite Hinduism and Mahayana Buddhism in a structured coexistence. Both traditions were institutionally maintained; rulers were often posthumously identified with deities of both faiths, and Buddhist and Shaivite sanctuaries stood in close proximity throughout the realm.
devaraja — Sanskrit for "god-king"; the doctrine identifying the ruler with a Hindu deity (especially Shiva or Vishnu) and expressing this through posthumous deification in mortuary temple statues. At Majapahit, the first king Raden Wijaya was deified as Harihara; subsequent rulers were identified with Shiva, Vishnu, and their divine consorts.

A remarkable piece of additional evidence complicates — and enriches — this picture. The Troloyo/Tralaya Islamic burial complex near Trowulan was in use from approximately 1368 CE through the early seventeenth century, demonstrating that Muslim traders and perhaps courtiers maintained a resident community at the very heart of the Majapahit capital during the golden age — well before Islam became a political force in Java.33 The Nagarakretagama is entirely silent about Islam, but the archaeological record makes clear that Majapahit's syncretic pluralism had room, in practice, for a Muslim community within its walls.

The theoretical expression of this theological pluralism is found not in the Nagarakretagama but in the roughly contemporary Kakawin Sutasoma by Mpu Tantular (fl. c. 1365–1389). Tantular — whose name itself suggests "uninfluenced" in Old Javanese — was a Buddhist court poet who composed his masterwork during Hayam Wuruk's reign.34 The Sutasoma tells the story of Prince Sutasoma, a Bodhisattva who achieves enlightenment by converting demons through compassion rather than force, and in so doing articulates the most famous verse in Old Javanese literature: in Canto 139, stanza 5, Tantular writes — in a passage addressing the relationship between Buddhist and Shaivite truth — the phrase bhinneka tunggal ika tan hana dharma mangrwa: "They are indeed different, but they are of the same kind, as there is no duality in Truth."34 The phrase Bhinneka Tunggal Ika — "Different yet One," conventionally rendered as "Unity in Diversity" — embedded in this verse would become, six centuries later, the motto of the Republic of Indonesia.

Decline and Fall: Civil War, the Rise of Islam, and the End of an Era, 1389–1527

The empire's unravelling was at once rapid and slow — rapid in the sense that the Paregreg civil war of 1405–1406 permanently crippled Majapahit's authority over its outer dependencies, and slow in the sense that a rump state persisted in East Java for more than a century after that catastrophe.

Succession Crisis and the Paregreg War

Hayam Wuruk's death in 1389 left Majapahit without a clear single successor. The legitimate heir, Kusumawardhani — his daughter by his primary queen — married Wikramawardhana, who became the next king. But Hayam Wuruk had also fathered a son, Bhre Wirabhumi, by a concubine; this prince administered the eastern territories of Blambangan as a semi-autonomous domain. The coexistence of two courts — the western court at Trowulan under Wikramawardhana, and the eastern court at Blambangan under Wirabhumi — was inherently unstable, and the trigger for open conflict was a quarrel over the prestigious title of Bhre Lasem, which both factions claimed for their consorts.35

In 1403, Wirabhumi went so far as to seek recognition and military backing from the Ming Dynasty court in China, which acknowledged his province's effective autonomy. Active fighting broke out in 1405. In 1406, Wikramawardhana's forces won the decisive battle; Wirabhumi fled by boat but was overtaken and killed by the general Raden Gajah. The civil war's political consequences were severe: approximately 170 Chinese merchants died as collateral damage, and the Yongle Emperor demanded sixty thousand tahil of gold in compensation from Wikramawardhana (a debt he eventually pardoned). More consequentially, Majapahit's outer tributaries — sensing weakness at the centre — began to reassert their independence. Palembang, Malayu, and the emerging Malacca Sultanate all ceased effective tribute payments.36

The Rise of Islam and the Pasisir Cities

The fifteenth century saw the commercial geography of the Java Sea redrawn against Majapahit's interests. The Malacca Sultanate, founded on the Malay Peninsula around 1400, grew rapidly under Ming Dynasty patronage and became the dominant entrepôt of maritime Southeast Asia. Its conversion to Islam — accelerated by the arrival of Muslim merchants from Gujarat, Bengal, and the Arabian Sea — made Malacca the hub of a new Islamic commercial network that progressively displaced the Majapahit-centred trading system.37 By the 1430s, Muslim merchant communities from China, the Middle East, and the Malay world had established themselves in the north Javanese coastal cities. The pasisir (coastal) towns — Demak, Gresik, Tuban, Cirebon — developed their own Islamic identities, their prosperity increasingly decoupled from the inland Hindu-Buddhist court.

The most consequential was Demak. Founded on the north coast of Java by Raden Patah — a figure whose genealogy later tradition traced to Majapahit itself, though this claim is debated — Demak became the first Islamic sultanate in Java. Its maritime commerce, its patronage of Muslim scholars arriving from Arabia and Gujarat, and its alliance with the Malacca network gave it advantages the Hindu-Buddhist inland court could not easily match.

The End-Date Question

The formal end date of Majapahit is contested between two events, and historians have defended both.37

In 1478, forces commanded by Girindrawardhana attacked Trowulan and killed King Bhre Kertabhumi. The Javanese chronogram Sirna ilang kertaning bhumi — "Gone is the glory of the earth" — encodes the Śaka year 1400 (= 1478 CE) and records this event. After 1478, Demak operated as a fully sovereign polity; the Majapahit court never regained meaningful authority over the pasisir cities.

However, a successor state survived. Girindrawardhana himself continued to claim the Majapahit title; his descendants ruled from Daha (Kediri). The final military extinction of any Majapahit successor came in 1527, when the Sultanate of Demak conquered Daha and killed its last ruler, Prabu Udara. A large exodus followed: courtiers, Brahmin priests, artists, and members of the royal family moved eastward to Bali, carrying with them the textual, artistic, and religious traditions of the Majapahit court. The extraordinary Hindu-Balinese culture that survives on Bali today — temple architecture, wayang traditions, court literature, music — is in substantial measure a transplanted and creatively transformed Majapahit inheritance, preserved across the Bali Strait while Java was absorbed into the Islamic world. This migration is the subject of the following chapter.37

The Long Afterlife: Majapahit and Modern Indonesian Nationalism

Few medieval empires have cast as long a political shadow as Majapahit. The reasons are both historical and ideological: the Nagarakretagama's list of tributaries traced an arc that corresponds, loosely but suggestively, to the territory of modern Indonesia; Mpu Tantular's verse furnished the republic with its motto; and Gajah Mada's Palapa Oath supplied the concept of Nusantara — the united archipelago — that has served nationalist discourse for more than a century.

The Nationalist Construction

The foundational move was made in the early twentieth century, when Indonesian nationalist thinkers reached back past four centuries of Dutch colonialism to the most impressive pre-colonial polity in the archipelago. Majapahit — as the Nagarakretagama described it — offered proof that the diverse peoples of the islands had once been politically unified, and that this unity was not a Western imposition but an indigenous achievement. The historical narrative was attractive precisely because it was usable: it provided a pre-colonial predecessor whose claimed territory roughly matched the territory nationalists sought to inherit.

Mohammad Yamin (1903–1962), the poet, historian, and politician, was the most energetic early weaver of this myth. He introduced Bhinneka Tunggal Ika to the deliberations of the Investigating Committee for Preparatory Work for Independence (BPUPK) in May–June 1945, proposing the Old Javanese phrase as the motto of the new republic.38 The phrase was incorporated into the state emblem — the Garuda Pancasila — and formally proclaimed the national motto on 15 February 1950. It received constitutional enshrinement in the second constitutional amendment of 2000.38

Sukarno's Pancasila, proclaimed on 1 June 1945, implicitly drew on the Majapahit tradition of political pluralism. The five founding principles of the Indonesian state — belief in one God, just and civilised humanity, Indonesian unity, consultative democracy, and social justice — have often been contextualized against the backdrop of Majapahit's syncretic order, which had accommodated Shaivites, Buddhists, and Muslims simultaneously within a single polity.39 The connection is partly rhetorical and partly genuine: the Majapahit court's institutional pluralism offered a pre-colonial precedent for the kind of religiously diverse but politically unified nation that Sukarno was trying to build.

Suharto's New Order and the "Palapa" Satellite

Under President Suharto (r. 1966–1998), Majapahit symbolism was deployed more explicitly in service of national unity and territorial control. In 1976, Suharto named Indonesia's first domestic telecommunications satellite "Palapa" — directly evoking Gajah Mada's oath to unify the archipelago before allowing himself any personal pleasure.40 The naming was deliberate: where the fourteenth-century Mahapatih had pledged to connect the islands through military and political subjugation, the twentieth-century state would connect them through the electromagnetic spectrum. Successor satellite systems — Palapa B, C, and D — continued the naming tradition. This use of Majapahit symbolism was part of a broader New Order project of legitimating Suharto's centralizing nationalism through appeal to indigenous pre-colonial precedent, presenting modern state-building as the fulfilment of a medieval oath.

The New Order also frequently cited the Nagarakretagama's territorial map to undergird Indonesia's claim to all the territories between Sabang (northernmost Aceh) and Merauke (southern New Guinea). This use of a fourteenth-century court poem as a geopolitical argument for a twentieth-century state reveals both the power and the problem of Majapahit's modern afterlife: the ideological claims of Prapanca's royal eulogy have been recruited, selectively and anachronistically, to settle questions the poem's author never imagined.

The Scholarly Critique

Historians have been careful to distinguish the historical Majapahit from its nationalist reconstructions. M.C. Ricklefs, in the standard Anglophone history of Indonesia, situates Majapahit as a significant East Javanese polity that exercised genuine regional hegemony while resisting the maximalist reading that would make it a direct administrative predecessor to the modern Indonesian state.41 The mandala-state framework implies that the Nagarakretagama's territorial claims were ideological assertions, not administrative facts; to build modern territorial arguments on those assertions is to import a court poem's celebratory boasting into geopolitics.

The most striking recent evidence of Majapahit's continuing political vitality is Indonesia's decision to name its new capital city — under construction in East Kalimantan since 2022 — "Nusantara": the very word Gajah Mada swore to unify in his 1334 Palapa Oath.42 The medieval Mahapatih's vocabulary is now the address of a twenty-first-century capital. Whether this represents a legitimate continuity of historical identity or a selective use of pre-colonial precedent to lend authority to a contemporary political project is a question scholars and citizens continue to debate. What is certain is that Majapahit — the actual fourteenth-century mandala state with its brilliant court at Trowulan, its Buddhist poets and Shaivite priests, its commercial network and its ambitious but contested territorial claims — has achieved an afterlife more enduring than its historical reign.

Summary

Majapahit was founded in 1293 by Raden Wijaya, who exploited a Mongol punitive expedition to destroy his Javanese rival Jayakatwang and then expelled his Mongol allies through surprise attack. It reached its apogee under Hayam Wuruk (1350–1389) and his formidable chief minister Gajah Mada, whose Palapa Oath pledged to unify the entire archipelago (Nusantara) under Majapahit's suzerainty before accepting any personal reward. The empire's two principal literary sources — the Nagarakretagama (1365) of Mpu Prapanca and the later Pararaton — are indispensable but demand critical reading: the former is a royal panegyric whose 98-tributary list reflects ideological aspiration rather than direct administration, and the latter is a retrospective chronicle blending legend with chronicle, written at least a century after the events it describes. Modern historians view Majapahit as a mandala state whose direct control extended over East Java and Bali, while a broader zone of tributary relations connected it to the outer archipelago through trade, prestige, and personal relationships between rulers rather than bureaucratic integration. The great capital at Trowulan covered approximately 100 square kilometres; the court practised a syncretic Hindu-Buddhist (Śiva-Buddha) synthesis, and the Buddhist poet Mpu Tantular composed the phrase Bhinneka Tunggal Ika — "Unity in Diversity" — that would become Indonesia's national motto. Decimated by the Paregreg civil war (1405–1406), outcompeted by the Islamic Malacca Sultanate and the north Javanese pasisir cities, and ultimately conquered by the Sultanate of Demak in 1527, Majapahit sent its court culture eastward to Bali, where it survived in transformed form. The empire's afterlife in Indonesian nationalism — as pre-colonial proof of a unified archipelago — has been as politically potent as its actual history, though scholars distinguish the historical mandala state from the nationalist myth it has become.

9.2 Evaluate the contrasting characteristics of the Nagarakretagama and the Pararaton as historical sources. What can each tell us that the other cannot, and what are the principal limitations of each?
9.3 C.C. Berg argued that the Nagarakretagama was not a historical document but a supernatural instrument for shaping the future. Why has mainstream scholarship rejected this view while still finding value in Berg's insight?
9.4 What does the mandala-state model imply for the historical debate over Majapahit's territorial extent, and what does it imply for modern Indonesian territorial claims that invoke Majapahit as a precedent?
9.5 Describe Majapahit's Śiva-Buddha synthesis and its institutional expression at the court. How does the existence of an Islamic burial complex at Trowulan from 1368 CE complicate — or enrich — that picture?
9.6 What were the structural causes of Majapahit's decline after 1389? Distinguish between internal causes (the succession dispute, the Paregreg civil war) and external causes (the rise of Malacca, the spread of Islam along the pasisir).
9.7 Trace the journey of the phrase Bhinneka Tunggal Ika from Mpu Tantular's fourteenth-century Buddhist poem to Indonesia's twentieth-century national motto. What did the phrase originally mean, and what work has it been made to do in modern nationalism?
