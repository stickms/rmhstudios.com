Learning Objectives

  • Explain why the Indonesian archipelago occupied a strategic position in ancient maritime trade between India, China, and the wider Indian Ocean world.
  • Describe the spice and aromatics trade and evaluate the evidence for its antiquity.
  • Compare the major theories of how Indian cultural, religious, and political influences spread to Southeast Asia — the kshatriya, vaishya, and brahmana models — and understand why each has attracted support and criticism.
  • Explain O.W. Wolters' concept of "localization" and why it shifted attention toward Southeast Asian agency.
  • Define the mandala as a political form and distinguish it from European concepts of territorial sovereignty.
  • Analyse the Yupa inscriptions of Kutai and the prasasti of Tarumanagara as the earliest surviving evidence of Indic influence in the archipelago.
  • Understand how the developments traced in this chapter set the stage for the classical Hindu-Buddhist kingdoms of Part II.

Sometime in the first centuries of the Common Era, a king named Mulavarman ruled over a river-delta polity on the eastern coast of Borneo. He donated thousands of cattle to Brahmin priests, performed the Vedic horse-sacrifice, and had his generosity commemorated in Sanskrit verse on tall stone pillars. Everything about those pillars — the script, the language, the ritual, the very concept that a king's piety deserved such commemoration — was rooted in the civilization of the Indian subcontinent. Yet Mulavarman ruled not in India but in a tropical rainforest nearly four thousand kilometres across the sea. The question of how, why, and on whose initiative Indian culture reached him — and was then adapted, transformed, and made distinctly Indonesian — is the central problem of this chapter, and one of the richest debates in the historiography of Southeast Asia.

The Archipelago at the Crossroads of Ancient Commerce

Geography made the Indonesian archipelago a natural meeting point of the ancient world's most important long-distance trade systems. The chain of islands stretching more than five thousand kilometres from Sumatra in the northwest to New Guinea in the east lay precisely at the juncture where the Indian Ocean's westerly monsoon winds gave way to the easterly winds of the South China Sea. Mariners from India, Arabia, and Persia who rode the southwest monsoon across the Bay of Bengal arrived at the northern shores of Sumatra or Java; Chinese and mainland Southeast Asian traders who followed the northeast monsoon down from the South China Sea encountered the same coasts from the other direction. The Strait of Malacca, between the Malay Peninsula and northeastern Sumatra, became one of the great chokepoints of the ancient maritime world — whoever controlled it could tax, facilitate, or disrupt the flow of goods between the Indian and Pacific oceans.1

The islands themselves supplied commodities that the rest of the world craved and could obtain nowhere else. The Maluku Islands (the Moluccas) were the world's sole source of cloves and nutmeg, spices so valued in the ancient and medieval world that they moved through multiple intermediary hands — Malay, Javanese, Indian, Arab — before reaching the dinner tables of Rome or the medicine chests of China.2 Sumatra produced camphor, benzoin resin, and gold; the forests of Borneo yielded camphor of the highest quality and aromatic woods; the coasts of Java offered pepper. These were not trivial luxuries. In the ancient world, aromatics and spices served religious, medicinal, and preservative functions that made them as economically important as industrial raw materials are today.

The trade was ancient. Austronesian-speaking peoples — ancestors of the modern Malay, Javanese, Sundanese, and hundreds of other Indonesian peoples — had developed sophisticated seafaring technology over millennia, building sewn-plank outrigger vessels capable of crossing open ocean. By around 1500 BCE, Austronesian sailors had established trade contacts with South India and Sri Lanka, exchanging Maluku spices and other forest products for Indian textiles and metalwork.3 The maritime jade networks that moved Taiwanese nephrite ornaments through the Philippines and down into Borneo and Sulawesi between roughly 2000 BCE and 500 CE attest to still older trans-archipelagic exchange.4 Well before the first Sanskrit inscription was carved in Borneo, the islands of the Indonesian archipelago were already embedded in regional and trans-oceanic commercial systems.

Spices, Aromatics, and the Maritime World Before Inscriptions

The contact between the Indonesian archipelago and the Indian subcontinent long preceded any written evidence. Indonesian vessels were plying routes to the African coast by at least the first century CE, as Chinese and Roman sources indirectly attest.5 Ancient Indian texts offer some of the earliest literary references to the islands: Sanskrit epic literature refers to Yavadvipa (the "Barley Island" or "Island of Millet"), a wealthy island realm to the east, and to Dvipantara ("islands in between"), suggesting awareness of a great archipelago that was a source of gold and forest products.6 Chinese records from the Han dynasty era mention tribute missions and merchants from polities in the southern seas. The Roman world, too, felt the archipelago's influence: the cloves found in the household goods of Roman-era Syria and the spices demanded by Roman cuisine had passed through a chain of Indonesian, Indian, and Arab middlemen.7

The bronze-working traditions of the Dong Son culture, originating in mainland Southeast Asia around 500 BCE, reached the archipelago along these same commercial channels — with bronze drums, wet-field rice cultivation techniques, and new ritual practices crossing from the mainland into Borneo, Java, Sulawesi, and the eastern islands.8 Trade, in other words, was already the mechanism through which cultural practices, technologies, and ideas moved across the archipelago. When Indian religious and political ideas arrived in the early centuries CE, they traveled along routes and through social networks that were already well established.

Precisely who was trading — and on whose ships — is a question that has generated significant scholarly debate. Early scholarship, particularly that produced by Dutch and French colonial-era historians, tended to depict the Indonesian islanders as relatively passive recipients of goods and culture brought by more dynamic foreign merchants. Later historians, notably the Dutch scholar Jacob Cornelis van Leur in his posthumously collected essays Indonesian Trade and Society (1955), challenged this picture vigorously, arguing that indigenous Indonesian traders were sophisticated actors in their own right, and that foreign trade with the archipelago was characterized by a kind of "peddling" commerce — many small-scale transactions with a wide range of goods — rather than the organized bulk commodity trade that colonial-era scholars had imagined. Van Leur's intervention helped break the habit of reading Indonesian history backwards from the Dutch period, but later scholars found his portrait of a fragmented, small-scale trade equally misleading.9

What "India" Brought: The Bundle of Influences

When scholars speak of the "Indianization" of Southeast Asia, they mean a broad, centuries-long process by which elements of Indian civilization — religion, script, political philosophy, law, art, architecture, and ceremonial culture — came to shape the societies of the archipelago and mainland Southeast Asia. The process was not uniform, not instantaneous, and emphatically not identical to what is sometimes called "colonization." No Indian army conquered Kutai or Tarumanagara. Instead, a constellation of cultural elements arrived, was selectively adopted, and then transformed by local societies into something distinctly their own.

The bundle of influences is worth enumerating. Sanskrit arrived as the language of prestige, religion, and royal inscription — the Latin of the Indic world, a language that conferred legitimacy on inscriptions and ritual texts regardless of whether the population at large could read it. With Sanskrit came the Pallava script, the writing system developed by the Pallava dynasty of southern India in the fourth and fifth centuries CE, which spread to Southeast Asia and became the ancestor of most of the scripts used in the archipelago today: Javanese, Balinese, Sundanese, Batak, and Bugis scripts all descend from Pallava traditions.10 The arrival of Sanskrit and its script represents a revolutionary moment in Indonesian history, because it transformed societies that had previously relied on oral tradition and perishable materials into ones capable of producing durable inscribed records.

Along with language and script came two great religious traditions: Hinduism and Buddhism, often arriving not as competitors but as complementary elements within a shared Indic symbolic vocabulary. The Hindu pantheon — especially Shiva and Vishnu — provided powerful metaphors for royal authority: a king who identified himself with Vishnu claimed to be the preserver and protector of the cosmic order; one associated with Shiva claimed to channel the energy of the great destroyer-creator. The devaraja concept — the idea that the king was a divine universal ruler, a manifestation of a deity on earth — provided an extraordinarily effective tool for centralizing political power in societies where authority had previously rested on more dispersed, genealogical, or military bases.11 Buddhism, with its emphasis on merit, compassion, and universal spiritual aspiration, offered complementary resources: Buddhist monks could serve as royal chaplains, teachers, and diplomats, and Buddhist monuments could project royal generosity and piety across vast distances.

Devaraja — from Sanskrit deva (god) and rāja (king): the concept, adopted by Indianized Southeast Asian rulers, that the king was a manifestation of a deity — often Vishnu or Shiva — on earth. The divine essence of kingship was conferred through Brahmin ritual, typically involving the sacred linga. The concept provided theological legitimation for royal authority and underpinned the construction of temple-mountains that embodied the cosmic mountain Meru.

Indian political theory also contributed the concept of the mandala as a cosmological model of power — which Southeast Asian rulers adapted into a political form (discussed separately below) — and the great Sanskrit epics, the Rāmāyaṇa and the Mahābhārata, which furnished rulers, artists, and storytellers with inexhaustible narrative resources for expressing moral, political, and social ideals. In Java and Bali these epics were not merely imported; they were rewritten, elaborated, and made distinctly local, generating traditions like the wayang kulit shadow puppet theatre that remain living art forms today.

The Scholars Argue: Theories of How Indianization Happened

The question of how Indian culture came to exercise such deep influence over Southeast Asian societies has generated one of the most productive debates in the historiography of the region. Several major positions have been advanced, associated with key scholars of the twentieth century.

Coedès and the Hinduized States

The foundational scholarly framework was established by the French historian and epigraphist George Coedès (1886–1969), whose monumental work Les états hindouisés d'Indochine et d'Indonésie (1948), translated into English as The Indianized States of Southeast Asia (1968), defined the field for a generation. Coedès defined an "Indianized" state as one founded upon "the Indian conception of royalty, characterized by Hinduist or Buddhist cults and expressed in the Sanskrit language."12 For Coedès, Indianization was primarily a political and religious phenomenon: Indian kings, priests, and adventurers brought to Southeast Asia the organizational templates — divine kingship, Sanskrit epigraphy, court ritual, temple architecture — that transformed loosely organized chieftaincies into coherent polities.

Coedès proposed that Indianization could have resulted from several different mechanisms: the displacement of populations by Central Asian incursions into northern India; high-caste adventurers seeking fortunes abroad; or the natural expansion of commercial contacts along the trade routes. He emphasized the role of Brahmin specialists — scholars, priests, astrologers, and ritual experts — who could offer Southeast Asian rulers the technologies of sacred kingship in exchange for patronage. This reading placed Indian agents at the center of the story and Southeast Asians in a somewhat passive role as recipients of a superior civilization.

The Three Caste Theories

Coedès' framework prompted subsequent scholars to identify three broad hypotheses about which class of Indian society was primarily responsible for transmitting Indian culture to Southeast Asia. These hypotheses, sometimes called the "caste theories" of Indianization, each have strengths and weaknesses.

The kshatriya (warrior caste) theory holds that Indian warriors conquered parts of Southeast Asia and established themselves as ruling dynasties, bringing their courts' culture with them. This theory is supported by the martial language of early inscriptions and the legends of hero-adventurers that appear in some foundation myths. But it has attracted little sustained scholarly support because there is very little credible literary or archaeological evidence for Indian military conquest of any Indonesian polity.13 The absence of conquest narratives in Southeast Asian sources, the absence of Indian colonies of the kind found in the Mediterranean world, and the overwhelmingly peaceful character of the archaeological record all argue against a conquest model.

The vaishya (merchant caste) theory locates the main transmission mechanism in commerce. Indian traders who settled permanently in Southeast Asian ports formed mixed-heritage communities, married local women, and gradually diffused Indian cultural practices into local elites and populations. A variant of this theory, associated with the early twentieth-century Dutch scholar N.J. Krom, suggested that Indian merchants, after establishing themselves as permanent settlers, introduced Hindu practices that then spread to local ruling groups. The merchant theory captures something real — commerce was unquestionably the channel through which contact intensified — but critics note that it cannot explain why Indianization permeated elite culture so comprehensively and so specifically in the domain of Sanskrit learning, ritual, and royal ideology, domains that merchants would have had less knowledge of and less incentive to transmit.14

The brahmana (Brahmin priest-scholar) theory, associated with the Dutch scholar F.D.K. Bosch, is generally considered the most compelling explanation for the depth and character of Indian cultural influence. This theory holds that Brahmin specialists were invited to Southeast Asian courts by local rulers who recognized the value of Indian ritual and ideological expertise for their own political projects.15 Brahmins could perform the rituals that consecrated a king, compose the Sanskrit inscriptions that proclaimed royal achievements, design temples that embodied cosmic order, and serve as astrologers, lawyers, and advisers. From the perspective of a local chief trying to construct a more powerful and legitimate monarchy, a Brahmin scholar was an extraordinarily useful acquisition. The Brahmin theory places the initiative with Southeast Asian rulers themselves — they were not passive recipients but active consumers of imported ideological technology.

Van Leur's Critique

J.C. van Leur (1908–1942) — whose career was cut short when he was killed at the naval Battle of the Java Sea — offered a trenchant critique of all versions of the Indianization narrative that placed Indians in the driving seat. In Indonesian Trade and Society, van Leur argued that Indian cultural influence on Southeast Asia amounted to no more than a "thin and flaking glaze" laid over deeply indigenous social structures that remained essentially intact beneath.16 Where Coedès saw a wholesale transformation of Southeast Asian civilization by Indian culture, van Leur saw a superficial elite fashion — court ceremonies in Sanskrit, royal inscriptions in Pallava script, Hindu cosmology in royal architecture — that scarcely touched the lives of ordinary village populations who continued to live according to their own customary (adat) laws, worship their own ancestral spirits, and organize their social life according to pre-Indic principles.

Van Leur's intervention was valuable in several ways: it insisted on the resilience and sophistication of indigenous Indonesian cultures, pushed back against the Eurocentric and Indocentric assumptions of colonial-era scholarship, and directed attention toward the question of how deeply Indianization actually penetrated. But later historians found his "thin glaze" metaphor ultimately too dismissive of real and lasting transformations. The Sanskrit vocabulary absorbed into Old Javanese, the Pallava-derived scripts that became the writing systems of the archipelago, the Indic cosmology embedded in Javanese and Balinese temple architecture — these are not superficial phenomena.

Localization: Southeast Asian Agency in Transformation

The scholar who most successfully transcended the limitations of both the Indianization model and van Leur's thin-glaze counter-narrative was the British historian Oliver William Wolters (1915–2000). A former Malayan civil servant who became a professor at Cornell University, Wolters spent decades studying early Southeast Asian commerce and politics, producing foundational works including Early Indonesian Commerce: A Study of the Origins of Srivijaya (1967) and History, Culture, and Region in Southeast Asian Perspectives (1982, revised 1999).

The central concept that Wolters contributed to the debate was localization. Rather than seeing Southeast Asian societies as either passive recipients of Indian civilization (the Coedès view) or as maintaining a separate, essentially unchanged indigenous base beneath a thin Indian veneer (van Leur), Wolters argued that Southeast Asians actively engaged with Indian materials, selecting elements that resonated with their own pre-existing values and social structures, and then reworking those elements into distinctly local cultural statements that contained "something else" — something irreducibly Southeast Asian.17 Localization was not merely adaptation; it was creative transformation. Indian ideas "did not move into a vacuum," as Wolters put it; they entered societies with their own deep traditions, and what emerged from the encounter was neither purely Indian nor simply indigenous, but something new.

Localization — O.W. Wolters' term for the process by which Southeast Asian societies absorbed, translated, and re-contextualized foreign (Indian, Chinese, later Islamic and European) cultural elements, fitting them into local traditions and values to produce distinctly regional cultural forms. Localization implies active Southeast Asian agency, as opposed to passive reception of foreign civilization.

Wolters also introduced into the debate the concept of "men of prowess" — local leaders whose authority derived from demonstrated personal power, courage, and the ability to attract followers. Such leaders were often eager to adopt Indian ritual and symbolic resources precisely because those resources offered new idioms in which to express and legitimate their indigenous authority. A Javanese or Bornean chief who adopted Sanskrit titles, invited a Brahmin to perform royal rituals at his court, and constructed a temple in the form of the cosmic mountain Meru was not submitting to Indian colonization; he was exploiting Indian cultural technology to enhance his own position within his own society. The initiative, on this reading, lay with the Southeast Asian rulers themselves.

The localization paradigm has been enormously influential. It explains, for instance, why Borobudur and Prambanan — the great Buddhist and Hindu temple complexes of Central Java, built in the eighth and ninth centuries CE — are at once deeply Indic in their cosmological conception and unmistakably Javanese in their stylistic execution: the carving traditions, the narrative programmes, the aesthetic sensibility are distinct from anything produced in India itself.18 It explains why the caste system was adopted in Indianized courts but was never applied universally, being restricted mainly to a restricted noble and priestly stratum rather than defining the whole of social life as in its Indian homeland.19 And it explains why the Sanskrit epics — the Rāmāyaṇa and Mahābhārata — were not merely imported but were thoroughly localized, generating distinctly Javanese and Balinese variants with local characters, local landscapes, and local moral concerns.

A further refinement came from the American scholar Ian Mabbett, who argued that the three "caste theories" of Indianization were not mutually exclusive: warriors, merchants, and Brahmin specialists all likely played roles at different times and in different contexts, and the process of Indian influence should be understood as historically plural rather than reducible to a single mechanism.20 More recently, the Sanskrit scholar Sheldon Pollock has proposed the concept of the "Sanskrit cosmopolis" — a transregional literary and political culture spanning South and Southeast Asia, united not by political power but by shared aesthetic norms, Sanskrit literary learning, and the practices of court writing — which suggests that participation in this cosmopolis was itself a form of prestige that Southeast Asian rulers actively sought.

The Mandala Polity: Power Without Fixed Borders

One of Wolters' most important contributions to the study of Indonesian political history was his analysis of the mandala as a specifically Southeast Asian political form. The term mandala, borrowed from Sanskrit (where it designates a ritual diagram of the cosmos, with a sacred center surrounded by concentric zones), was applied by Wolters and, independently, by the anthropologist Stanley Tambiah (who used the phrase "galactic polity"), to describe a pattern of political organization profoundly different from the European territorial state.

In a mandala polity, political authority did not radiate outward to a fixed territorial boundary demarcated on maps and defended at borders. Instead, it radiated outward from a sacred center — the royal palace and its associated temples — in gradually weakening concentric rings. Close to the center, subordinate chiefs were direct clients of the king, present at court, integrated into the royal ritual system, subject to royal taxation and military calls. Further out, local rulers acknowledged the king's suzerainty through periodic tribute payments and ceremonial attendance, but governed their own territories largely autonomously. At the margins, polities might simultaneously acknowledge the overlordship of two or more competing centers, playing one great kingdom off against another — a practice that made classical Southeast Asian politics extraordinarily fluid.

Mandala (Southeast Asian polity) — a political form characterized by a sacred center whose authority radiates outward in concentric rings rather than outward to fixed boundaries. Named by O.W. Wolters and comparable to Stanley Tambiah's "galactic polity," the mandala model explains why Southeast Asian states appear to expand and contract dramatically, and why their "borders" were zones of overlapping allegiance rather than hard lines.

This explains much that is puzzling about the classical Indonesian kingdoms if one approaches them with European territorial assumptions. When inscriptions boast that a king's power extended over the whole of the Nusantara — a term that first appears in the Mula Malurung inscription of 1255 CE and was famously invoked by the Majapahit minister Gajah Mada in his Palapa oath — they are claiming not that the king administered every village from his capital but that his prestige radiated far enough to draw tribute from distant polities.21 The historian who tries to draw a fixed map of "Majapahit territory" analogous to the map of a European kingdom is likely to produce anachronistic cartography.

The mandala model was not itself an Indian import; it was a creative Southeast Asian synthesis. The cosmological concept of the mandala came from India, the political technology was Southeast Asian. A Brahmin could consecrate a king's linga and compose his Sanskrit inscriptions; but the art of managing a network of personal relationships, tributary obligations, strategic marriages, and selective military campaigns — the actual political work of maintaining a mandala — was the work of the Southeast Asian ruler and his own bureaucratic traditions.22 Here, again, localization is the key: Indian symbolic resources were taken up and adapted to serve distinctly Southeast Asian political purposes.

Mandalas expanded and contracted with the personal charisma and military success of individual rulers. A brilliant king could attract new tributaries from great distances; a weak successor would find the peripheries peeling away. This volatility made the classical kingdoms appear unstable by European standards, but it reflected a different logic: power was relational and personal, not territorial and bureaucratic. The mandala structure persisted, in various forms, throughout the Hindu-Buddhist classical period, shaped Islamic sultanates after the fifteenth century, and left deep traces in Indonesian political culture that scholars have argued are still visible in the patron-client networks of the post-independence era.

Stone and Scripture: The Yupa Inscriptions of Kutai

The earliest surviving evidence of Indic influence in the Indonesian archipelago is a set of seven stone pillars discovered near the Mahakam River in East Kalimantan (Borneo) and now housed in the National Museum of Indonesia in Jakarta. These are the yūpa inscriptions of Kutai — ritual sacrificial posts bearing Sanskrit texts in Pallava script, dated to the last half of the fourth century CE, making them among the oldest Sanskrit inscriptions ever found in Southeast Asia.23

The inscriptions record three generations of a ruling dynasty. The first king was Kudungga, described as a narendra ("lord of men") — a title that carries some Sanskrit resonance but whose personal name is widely believed to be of local Dayak or Bugis origin rather than Indian, suggesting he himself was a pre-Indianized chief whose descendants adopted the new religious and political culture.24 His son Aśwawarman is called vaṁśakartṛ, "founder of the [Indian-style] dynasty" — the one who, in other words, made the decisive step of adopting the apparatus of Indic kingship. It is Aśwawarman's Indianization that the inscriptions celebrate, not a conquest from outside.

The third and greatest king was Mulavarman, titled rājendra ("lord of kings"), who is the principal subject of the yūpa texts. The inscriptions describe his victories over neighbouring peoples, his great wealth, and above all his extraordinary piety and generosity: he donated "twenty thousand cows" to Brahmin priests, performed the ancient Vedic Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice), a ritual marking a king's claim to universal sovereignty, and gave lavish gifts of gold at a sacred place called "Waprakeśvara."25 The language in which this royal achievement is commemorated — the formal Sanskrit of Vedic ritual, inscribed in the literate tradition of the Pallava dynasty of southern India — speaks of a court that had thoroughly mastered the cultural technology of Indic kingship.

Several aspects of the Kutai inscriptions deserve particular attention for what they reveal about the localization process. First, the dynasty's founder bore a non-Indian name, while his son adopted the Sanskrit suffix -warman ("shield" or "protection") common to Pallava-influenced dynasties — a deliberate choice to announce participation in the Indic royal tradition. Second, the Ashvamedha, a ritual of extreme antiquity associated with the highest form of Vedic kingship, implies that Mulavarman's court included Brahmin scholars capable of performing rituals that had been archaic even in India itself for centuries. Third, nowhere do the inscriptions suggest that this culture was imposed from outside; they describe a local ruler's achievements in an imported idiom. The impression is of deliberate adoption for internal political purposes — exactly the pattern that Wolters' localization model would predict.

The geographic location of Kutai is itself suggestive. East Kalimantan lay along the sea routes connecting the Maluku spice islands to the Malay Peninsula and the wider Indian Ocean. The Mahakam River was a natural corridor for interior forest products — camphor, aromatic woods, rattan — to reach coastal entrepôts where Indian and Chinese merchants traded. The political elite of this riverine kingdom would have had sustained commercial contact with Indian Ocean traders long before Aśwawarman's generation made the decisive step of adopting Brahminic ritual and Sanskrit epigraphy as the language of royal power.

Purnawarman and Tarumanagara: The Hindu Kingdom of West Java

Roughly contemporary with the flourishing of Kutai, and perhaps slightly later in its Indian-style inscriptions, was the kingdom of Tarumanagara in western Java. Tarumanagara is conventionally dated to approximately 358–669 CE and occupied the territory of what is now the greater Jakarta-Bogor region — the fertile lowlands watered by the Cisadane and Citarum rivers draining from the Sundanese highlands into the Java Sea.26 It is known primarily from seven stone inscriptions (prasasti), all dating to the fifth century CE, all composed in Sanskrit in the Pallava script, and all connected with the figure of its most celebrated ruler, King Purnawarman.

Prasasti — an inscribed stone or copper-plate monument, typically in Sanskrit (and later in Old Javanese or Old Malay), recording royal achievements, religious donations, land grants, or territorial claims. Prasasti are one of the primary sources for early Indonesian history.

The Tarumanagara prasasti display a range of royal concerns that illuminate the political culture of early Indic Java. The Ciaruteun inscription, discovered in 1863 near the Ciaruteun River west of Bogor, equates the king's footprints with those of the god Vishnu: "The powerful, illustrious, and brave King, the famous Purnawarman, whose footprints are like [those of] God Vishnu."27 The use of footprints — a mark of presence, ownership, and divine authority — and the invocation of Vishnu as the deity whose terrestrial incarnation the king embodies, reflects the devaraja concept in practice. Purnawarman was not merely claiming to worship Vishnu; he was claiming to be Vishnu's earthly form.

The Kebon Kopi inscription records two large elephant footprints alongside a Sanskrit verse praising the king's elephant; the Jambu inscription describes Purnawarman as equal to the god Indra in prowess. But perhaps the most revealing is the Tugu inscription, which records that Purnawarman ordered the construction of a canal — the Candrabāga canal, apparently redirecting the course of the Cakung River — and then organized a ritual feast for Brahmin priests to consecrate the hydraulic project.28 This is a striking detail: it shows Indian royal ideology being applied not merely to martial achievement or religious donation, but to an infrastructure project of direct economic benefit to the population. Brahmin priests, on this reading, served not only as ritual specialists but as legitimators of state power over territory and its resources.

External confirmation of Tarumanagara's connections to the wider world comes from Chinese sources. The Chinese Buddhist monk Faxian, traveling between India and China via the sea routes in approximately 412–413 CE, recorded a stopover in "Ye-po-ti" — almost certainly Java — where he found Buddhism poorly established but the country populous and prosperous.29 Tang dynasty annals record Tarumanagara sending tribute missions to the Chinese court in 666 and 669 CE, placing the kingdom within the tributary diplomatic network that China maintained with neighboring states and polities along the maritime routes.

The kingdom of Tarumanagara declined in the later seventh century, apparently under pressure from Srivijaya's expanding maritime power. The great Palembang-based Buddhist kingdom, which the Chinese monk Yijing visited in 671 CE and described as a thriving center of Sanskrit Buddhist learning, offers a glimpse of what Tarumanagara's successor world looked like — and of how thoroughly the Indian-influenced polity model had taken root in the archipelago by the time the classical kingdoms proper emerged.30

Before the Inscriptions: Older Connections

The yūpa inscriptions of Kutai and the prasasti of Tarumanagara are the earliest surviving written records of Indic influence in Indonesia. But they are evidence of an already-developed tradition, not of first contact. By the time Aśwawarman adopted Sanskrit titles and Purnawarman compared himself to Vishnu, generations of commercial and cultural exchange had already created the conditions for these appropriations to make sense to local audiences.

The archaeology of the pre-inscription period in Indonesia reveals sustained Indian Ocean contact. Indian glass beads, carnelian and agate ornaments of South Asian manufacture, and distinctive Indian pottery types appear at sites across the archipelago in the centuries before the first Sanskrit inscriptions — transported along the same spice-trade routes that Austronesian sailors had established centuries earlier.31 Roman artifacts, including coins and ceramics, found at the trading port of Oc Eo in the Mekong Delta (part of the earliest major Indianized kingdom, Funan, which flourished from roughly the first to the seventh centuries CE) demonstrate the remarkable reach of Indian Ocean commercial networks in the early Common Era, extending from the Mediterranean world to the archipelago through Indian merchant intermediaries.32

Indian traders and pilgrims crossing the Bay of Bengal to Southeast Asian ports in the early centuries CE found societies already organized around sophisticated chieftaincies, skilled maritime commerce, and complex ritual traditions centering on ancestor veneration, bronze ceremonialism, and wet-rice agriculture. These were not blank cultural slates. What they lacked — or what at least their elites evidently found attractive in Indian civilization — was the specific combination of royal ideology, literate administrative culture, and cosmic symbolism that Sanskrit Brahmanism and Buddhism offered. The decisive moment was not when Indian culture arrived but when local rulers chose to make use of it.

It is also worth noting the role of the sea itself as a cultural highway. Unlike overland transmission of culture — which tends to be slow, contiguous, and geographically constrained — maritime transmission can leap vast distances in a single voyage, connecting remote ports while bypassing the spaces between them. A Brahmin scholar or Buddhist monk boarding a merchant vessel at Kanchipuram or Mahabalipuram on the Coromandel Coast could reach the Malay Peninsula, Sumatra, Java, or Borneo in a matter of weeks. The monsoon winds that connected these ports also connected their ideas, texts, and artistic traditions. Southeast Asia's Indianization was, in a literal sense, made possible by the sea.

The Bridge to the Classical World

Chapter 4 covers the foundations; Part II of this book (Chapters 5–10) examines the classical edifice built upon them. By the late seventh century CE, the transformation catalysed by the processes described in this chapter was producing kingdoms of a new order of complexity and ambition. Srivijaya, the Buddhist maritime empire centred at Palembang in southern Sumatra, had emerged by 683 CE as a major regional power commanding the Strait of Malacca and serving as the most important centre for Sanskrit Buddhist learning in the eastern Indian Ocean world.33 The Sailendra dynasty in Central Java was beginning what would culminate in the construction of Borobudur — the world's largest Buddhist monument — and the great Shaivite temples at Prambanan, in the early ninth century. In East Java, Singhasari and then Majapahit would carry the mandala polity to its greatest extent. All of these classical kingdoms grew from the seeds planted in the early centuries CE, when local rulers along the spice and aromatic trade routes chose to make Indian civilization their own.

What connects the Yupa pillars of Kutai, the Ciaruteun footprints of Purnawarman, and the spires of Borobudur is not imitation but creative adaptation — the localization process that Wolters identified. Mulavarman's Sanskrit was not Indian Sanskrit; it was a Bornean chief's Sanskrit, deployed to legitimate a local dynasty within an emerging trans-regional prestige culture. Purnawarman's Vishnu was not the Vishnu of Tamil Nadu; he was a West Javanese king's Vishnu, whose divine footprints authorized control over irrigated rice land and river-born trade. The process of making Indian ideas Indonesian — begun in those earliest inscriptions and continued through a thousand years of Hindu-Buddhist civilization — is one of the great creative achievements of world history.

Scholarly caution: The three "caste theories" of Indianization (kshatriya, vaishya, brahmana) are often presented as competing hypotheses; contemporary scholars tend to see them as complementary, with different agents playing dominant roles in different times and places. The Brahmin-invitation model is most widely invoked for the Indonesian evidence, but this should not be read as settled consensus. Summary

By the beginning of the Common Era, the Indonesian archipelago was already integrated into a vast maritime trade system connecting it to India, China, and ultimately the Mediterranean world. The spice and aromatics trade, pursued by Austronesian sailors for at least fifteen centuries before the first Sanskrit inscription, created the human and commercial networks along which Indian cultural influences traveled. Scholars have debated vigorously whether Indian culture was brought by warriors (kshatriya theory), merchants (vaishya theory), or Brahmin specialists invited by local rulers (brahmana theory — the most widely accepted view). O.W. Wolters' concept of "localization" has fundamentally reoriented the debate: rather than seeing Southeast Asian societies as passive recipients of Indian civilization, it insists on their creative agency in selecting, adapting, and transforming Indian elements to serve distinctly local purposes. The mandala, a Southeast Asian political form adapted from Indian cosmological concepts, organized classical Indonesian kingdoms around radiating centers of personal prestige and tributary relationship rather than fixed territorial boundaries. The two earliest monuments to Indic influence in Indonesia — the Sanskrit yūpa inscriptions of King Mulavarman at Kutai (c. 400 CE) and the prasasti of King Purnawarman at Tarumanagara (c. 450 CE) — display this localization process already at work: Indian royal ideology, Sanskrit learning, and Pallava script are deployed by local rulers to legitimate their own dynasties within their own societies. These foundations support the great classical kingdoms of Part II.
