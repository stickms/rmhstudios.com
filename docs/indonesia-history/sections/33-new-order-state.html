Learning Objectives

  • Explain the doctrine of dwifungsi and how it embedded the military in Indonesian civilian governance, from provincial administration to parliament.
  • Describe how Golkar and the "floating mass" policy turned general elections into managed rituals of regime legitimation rather than contests of real political choice.
  • Analyse the role of Pancasila as an enforced state ideology — through the P4 indoctrination programme and the 1985 asas tunggal law — in suppressing political pluralism.
  • Evaluate the tension between the Berkeley Mafia technocrats and economic-nationalist rivals, and its consequences for patronage and corruption.
  • Assess the scale and mechanisms of the Suharto family's business empire and the KKN (Korupsi, Kolusi, Nepotisme) system more broadly.
  • Identify the major instruments of New Order repression — Kopkamtib, the Petrus killings, the Tanjung Priok massacre, press censorship — and situate them within present scholarly debates about the nature of the regime.

Between 1966 and 1998 Suharto presided over one of the twentieth century's most durable authoritarian states. The Orde Baru — New Order — was not simply a military dictatorship. It was a carefully engineered political architecture: a managed electoral system that produced ritual affirmations of regime support rather than genuine competition; an ideology of national unity, enforced through indoctrination and law, that rendered dissent literally anti-constitutional; a technocratic economic programme that generated real growth while funnelling its rewards upward to the president's family and allies; and a repressive apparatus that operated in both open and clandestine registers, from the overt authority of a security command with extraordinary powers to the death squads that dumped bodies on city streets in the 1980s. Understanding how these elements fitted together — and how scholars have argued over what they add up to — is the central task of this chapter.

The Architecture of the New Order State

When Suharto received the infamous Supersemar — the Order of Eleventh March 1966 transferring executive authority from President Sukarno — he moved with deliberate speed to construct a new political system rather than simply substitute one ruler for another. The New Order, as he and his allies christened it, was explicitly presented as the antithesis of Sukarno's "Old Order": disciplined where Sukarno had been theatrical, technocratic where he had been ideological, developmental where he had presided over economic collapse.1 But if the rhetoric was novelty, much of the structure built on existing foundations — the territorial military apparatus inherited from the revolution, the bureaucratic state enlarged under Guided Democracy, and the ideological framework of Pancasila that Sukarno himself had articulated in 1945.

The state Suharto constructed rested on three institutional pillars: the armed forces (ABRI — Angkatan Bersenjata Republik Indonesia), the civil bureaucracy, and the regime's electoral vehicle Golkar. These three structures interlocked in a system scholars have variously described as "bureaucratic authoritarianism," "bureaucratic pluralism," and "patrimonial military rule" — labels that capture different emphases, as this chapter's closing section examines.2 What is not disputed is that real power resided in a very small circle around Suharto himself, who was simultaneously Supreme Commander of the Armed Forces, head of government, and chairman of Golkar's functional board — and who used each role to reinforce the others.

The ideological genius of the New Order lay in making this concentration of power appear not as a personal ambition but as a national necessity. The 1965 coup attempt and the subsequent mass killings of alleged communists (covered in the preceding chapter) furnished an ever-present founding trauma: the PKI had nearly destroyed Indonesia, and only firm, unified leadership — under the military, guided by Pancasila — could prevent a recurrence. This was the New Order's constitutive myth, repeated in school curricula, state ceremonies, and annual commemorations of Hari Kesaktian Pancasila ("Pancasila Sacred Day," 1 October) until 1998. Ben Anderson's essays in Language and Power analyse how the New Order used Javanese political cosmology — in which legitimate power is indivisible, concentrated, and self-evidently correct — to naturalize what was in fact a constructed and contested authority.3

Dwifungsi: Soldiers in Suits and Offices

The ideological keystone of military authority was the doctrine of dwifungsi ("dual function"), which held that the Indonesian armed forces had a legitimate — indeed a constitutionally mandated — role not only in defence but also in socio-political governance. The concept had roots in the independence struggle, when the army had no formal government behind it and officers therefore assumed civilian administrative roles by necessity. General Abdul Haris Nasution formalized this into a theoretical position in 1958, proposing a "Middle Way" between full military dictatorship and purely professional subordination to civilian authority — the armed forces as a "social-political force" alongside their military role.4

Suharto institutionalized Nasution's concept far beyond its original scope. At the Second Army Seminar in August 1966 — a gathering of over one hundred Army Staff College participants — military doctrine was formally revised to mandate that ABRI "participate in every effort and activity of the people in the field of ideology, politics and economics."5 The operational expression of this mandate ran through every layer of Indonesian society. By 1969 military officers served as governors in nearly seventy percent of Indonesia's provinces and as mayors or district heads in more than half of the country's cities and regencies.6 By the early 1980s, active and retired military personnel occupied positions as cabinet ministers, ambassadors, heads of state corporations, directors of universities, and judges.

In the national legislature the military presence was literal and substantial. The armed forces maintained a dedicated parliamentary faction (Fraksi ABRI) with up to one hundred appointed seats in the People's Representative Council (DPR) and the People's Consultative Assembly (MPR), the body that formally elected the president every five years.7 These representatives were not elected but appointed by the military command; they owed their position to institutional loyalty rather than to constituents. Combined with Golkar's electoral dominance (below), the arrangement ensured that the New Order's legislative chambers were instruments of ratification, not deliberation.

Undergirding civilian penetration was the territorial command structure — the most pervasive instrument of New Order social control. The army's territorial hierarchy mirrored the national administrative structure at every level: the regional military command (Kodam) shadowed each province; the resort command (Korem) mirrored each regency cluster; the district command (Kodim) mirrored each regency; the subdistrict command (Koramil) mirrored each subdistrict; and the village-level Non-Commissioned Officer (babinsa) sat in every village office, gathering intelligence and mediating local disputes. Harold Crouch, whose 1978 monograph The Army and Politics in Indonesia remains the foundational study of ABRI's political role, described this system as creating "a parallel government" through which the military monitored, channelled, and where necessary suppressed civilian political activity at every level of society.8

Dwifungsi — literally "dual function," the New Order doctrine that assigned the Indonesian Armed Forces (ABRI) a permanent socio-political role alongside their defence function, institutionalized through parliamentary seats, territorial commands, and appointments to civil administrative posts.

Golkar and the Theater of Elections

Unlike most single-party authoritarian states, the New Order maintained the form of competitive multi-party elections throughout its existence. General elections were held in 1971, 1977, 1982, 1987, 1992, and 1997. Two non-Golkar parties — Partai Persatuan Pembangunan (PPP, the United Development Party) and Partai Demokrasi Indonesia (PDI, the Indonesian Democratic Party) — were permitted to contest seats, campaign, and win limited representation. Yet the elections were so comprehensively managed that they served primarily as rituals of affirmation rather than genuine contests of political choice.

The vehicle for this managed democracy was Golkar (Golongan Karya, "Functional Groups"), which had originated in the early 1960s as a loose federation of non-party organisations but was transformed after 1968 by Ali Murtopo into an electoral machine at the regime's direct disposal.9 The 1971 election — the first of the New Order — produced a result that set the pattern for the next quarter century: Golkar received 62.80 percent of the vote and 236 of 360 contested seats. Subsequent elections saw the share fluctuate only within a narrow band of managed variation: 62.11 percent (1977), 64.34 percent (1982), a conspicuous jump to 73.11 percent (1987), 68.10 percent (1992), and 74.51 percent (1997).10

Several interlocking mechanisms produced these outcomes. First, the civil service was made an instrument of Golkar mobilisation. Government Regulation No. 6 of 1970 introduced the principle of monoloyalitas, requiring civil servants to channel their political aspirations exclusively through Golkar; in 1971 the all-civil-servant corps KORPRI (Korps Pegawai Republik Indonesia) was established, with compulsory membership for all approximately 1.5 million state employees, who were expected to campaign for and vote for Golkar under pain of dismissal.11 Second, the military's territorial apparatus was deployed to generate the required vote shares from village upward: local commanders received explicit directives about targets, and those who delivered them were rewarded with career advancement.

A third mechanism was structural and more subtle: the "floating mass" (massa mengambang) policy, which held that the rural population required state guidance rather than partisan mobilisation and should therefore be "floated" above competitive politics. In practice, this meant that Golkar could establish branches and conduct political activity all the way down to the village level, while the two opposition parties were restricted to organisational activities at the subdistrict level and above. In the village — where the vast majority of Indonesians lived in the 1970s and 1980s — only Golkar had a presence, and the village headman, himself a civil servant subject to monoloyalitas, served as its effective agent.12

The very existence of two opposition parties had been engineered by the regime. On 5 January 1973, the government compulsorily merged Indonesia's four Islamic parties — the Nahdlatul Ulama, Parmusi, PSII, and Perti — into the PPP (Partai Persatuan Pembangunan), and merged the five secular nationalist and Christian parties — PNI, IPKI, Murba, Parkindo, and Partai Katholik — into the PDI (Partai Demokrasi Indonesia).13 The PPP was required to remove Islamic symbols from its banner and adopt the Pancasila as its ideological basis. The regime then intervened in both parties' internal leadership selections to ensure compliant leaderships. When the PDI under Megawati Sukarnoputri showed signs of genuine popular mobilisation in the early 1990s, Suharto engineered a leadership coup that ousted her in 1996 — triggering the Jalan Sabang riots that were a foreshock of the 1998 upheaval.

Donald Emmerson coined the term "bureaucratic pluralism" to describe this arrangement: multiple parties exist, some genuine competition occurs at the margins, bureaucratic elites hold competing positions on policy — yet the range of permissible contestation is tightly bounded and the outcome of any fundamental challenge is never in doubt.14 The label captures both the New Order's distinctive sophistication (it was not simply a blank authoritarian façade) and its fundamental constraint on political life.

Massa mengambang — "Floating mass," the New Order policy that held rural Indonesians should be insulated from partisan politics, used to justify restricting opposition party organisation to subdistrict level and above, while Golkar retained village-level access.

Pancasila as State Ideology: P4 and the Asas Tunggal

The five principles of Pancasila — belief in one God, humanitarianism, Indonesian unity, democracy guided by deliberation and representation, and social justice — had been articulated by Sukarno in 1945 as a deliberately capacious ideological formula, designed to bridge the tensions between Islam, secular nationalism, and Marxism. The New Order did not invent Pancasila but transformed it: from a broad umbrella under which diverse political forces could shelter into a mandatory consensus that allowed no shelter to those outside its regime-defined terms.

The main vehicle for this transformation was the P4 programme — Pedoman Penghayatan dan Pengamalan Pancasila (Guide to Experiencing and Practising Pancasila) — established by MPR Resolution No. 2 of 1978. Initially directed at civil servants, P4 was extended in the early 1980s to university students, who were required to complete a P4 orientation before their first semester and a full two-semester Pancasila Education course before graduation.15 The Human Rights Watch Indonesia Report of 1998 documented the content in detail: at least forty hours of classroom instruction organised around rote memorisation of thirty-six formulaic precepts, presenting a "static vision of Indonesian society" that emphasised economic growth, the armed forces' stabilising role, and the impermissibility of social conflict. The curriculum explicitly treated criticisms that might "threaten government stability" as anti-Pancasila, providing ideological cover for the prosecution of political opponents.16

If P4 was the pedagogical arm of Pancasila enforcement, the asas tunggal ("sole foundation") laws of 1983–1985 were its legal arm. MPR Resolution No. II/MPR/1983 required all political parties and mass organisations to adopt Pancasila as their sole ideological basis — displacing Islam, Christianity, or any other ideological commitment to a secondary position. Law No. 3 of 1983 applied this to the three political parties; Law No. 8 of 1985 on Public Organisations extended the requirement to every organisation in Indonesian civil society, from professional associations to religious bodies.17 The consequences for Muslim organisations were far-reaching. Those that resisted faced dissolution; those that complied — including the two largest, Nahdlatul Ulama and Muhammadiyah — had to reconfigure their self-understanding to place Pancasila formally above Islamic doctrine as the foundation of their public activity. Resistance was possible but costly: Abu Bakar Baasyir, later prominent as a jihadist leader, chose seventeen years of self-imposed exile in Malaysia rather than sign a declaration of compliance.

The New Order's use of Pancasila was, as scholars have noted, deliberately paradoxical. The ideology's very vagueness — five anodyne principles that almost no one could oppose in the abstract — was its most effective attribute. Any opposition could be labelled "anti-Pancasila" without the regime having to specify exactly what it was objecting to. This vagueness also meant that Islamic political forces, secular nationalists, and even former communists had all claimed Pancasila as their own: the New Order's achievement was to monopolise the interpretation of the symbol while presenting that monopoly as mere orthodoxy.18

Asas tunggal — "Sole foundation," the New Order principle, enacted in Law No. 8 of 1985, requiring all Indonesian social and political organisations to adopt Pancasila as their only ideological basis, displacing Islamic or other foundations.

The Technocrats and Their Rivals

Among the most consequential decisions of the early New Order was Suharto's recruitment of a cohort of US-trained economists to manage economic policy. The group — collectively known as the "Berkeley Mafia" — comprised five core figures: Widjojo Nitisastro, Mohammad Sadli, Emil Salim, J.B. Sumarlin, and Ali Wardhana, all of whom had completed graduate training at the University of California, Berkeley, through Ford Foundation scholarships in the 1950s and 1960s. They occupied the commanding heights of Indonesian economic institutions: the finance ministry, the central bank, and above all the National Development Planning Agency (Bappenas), where Widjojo served as chairman for most of the 1970s and 1980s.19

The technocrats' approach was explicitly orthodox by the standards of the period: fiscal austerity to bring down the ruinous inflation inherited from Sukarno (which had reached several hundred percent per annum by 1965), openness to foreign investment, reliance on oil revenues to fund development, and export diversification over time. Working alongside the Inter-Governmental Group on Indonesia (IGGI) — a Western-dominated aid consortium chaired by the Netherlands — they designed the five-year development plans (REPELITA) that organised New Order economic policy: Repelita I (1969–74) prioritised food production and infrastructure; subsequent plans moved progressively toward manufacturing and industrialisation.20

The technocrats were not without rivals. A distinct economic-nationalist current, rooted in the military's network of state enterprises and in Suharto's personal patronage relationships, consistently resisted their orthodoxy. The most dramatic confrontation came with the Pertamina crisis of 1975. The state oil company, placed under the direction of General Ibnu Sutowo, had expanded into tankers, steel, construction, and petrochemicals with spectacular recklessness, borrowing heavily on short-term foreign credit to finance ventures unrelated to oil extraction. When oil prices surged after 1973 Sutowo borrowed still more aggressively. By early 1975 the company's foreign debt was officially acknowledged to exceed ten billion US dollars — roughly thirty percent of Indonesia's entire GNP. The government was forced to intervene in March 1975; on 3 March 1976 Suharto formally discharged Sutowo and seven other Pertamina directors.21 Peter McCawley's analysis in the Journal of Southeast Asian Studies was prescient: the crisis was not simply personal corruption but the structural consequence of a state enterprise system without institutional checks — a condition that would recur wherever military-connected figures controlled economic assets outside technocratic oversight.

A second nationalist challenge came in the 1980s and 1990s from B.J. Habibie, Suharto's protégé and eventual successor, who championed a "high-tech leap" strategy for Indonesia — investment in aircraft manufacturing (the IPTN plant in Bandung), shipbuilding, and strategic industries — that the Berkeley Mafia regarded as economically irrational and fiscally ruinous. The strategic-industry programme consumed disproportionate resources and yielded expensive products that international markets did not want; the technocrats' critique of it was largely vindicated by the 1997–98 financial crisis, which exposed the vulnerability of an economy still substantially dependent on political connections rather than competitive efficiency.

The tension between technocrats and nationalists was never fully resolved; it was managed by Suharto, who used both factions strategically, playing them against each other when either grew too powerful. Richard Robison's Indonesia: The Rise of Capital (1986) identified the underlying dynamic: the New Order was not a developmental state in the East Asian sense, with a Weberian bureaucracy insulated from rent-seeking, but a hybrid in which technocratic competence and patrimonial distribution operated simultaneously and at times in creative tension.22

KKN: Corruption, Collusion, Nepotism

The acronym KKN — Korupsi, Kolusi, Nepotisme — crystallised for Indonesians in 1998 as a shorthand for three decades of systemic elite enrichment under the New Order. But the phenomenon it described was not incidental to the regime; it was structural. Corruption was not a parasitic growth on a developmental state: it was, as Robison and Vedi Hadiz have argued, the means by which a political-business oligarchy reproduced its dominance.23

At the apex of the system was the Suharto family. The president's six children constructed corporate empires that by the late 1990s spanned banking, telecommunications, cement, toll roads, clove trading, oil, timber, and automotive assembly. Transparency International, in a press release issued days after Suharto's resignation in May 1998, estimated that the family had accumulated "over US$30 billion" in ill-gotten assets; TI's 2004 Global Corruption Report ranked Suharto as the most corrupt leader in modern history, with an estimated $15–35 billion stolen.24 Individual family portfolios were staggering: Tommy Suharto held the national clove-trading monopoly, a share of Lamborghini, and the Kia Motors assembly contract; Bambang Trihatmodjo's Bimantara Citra conglomerate comprised some ninety companies; and eldest daughter Siti "Tutut" Hardiyanti Rukmana built a toll-road and infrastructure empire worth hundreds of millions of dollars.25

Five mechanisms enabled this accumulation, as documented by investigative reporting subsequently reviewed in detail. First, the yayasan (charitable foundation) system: dozens of foundations ostensibly devoted to education and social welfare functioned as mandatory collection vehicles, requiring financial institutions to contribute portions of their annual profits and individuals in senior positions to donate fractions of their salaries. Second, monopoly transfer: state-managed industries were privatised into family hands, or private companies were granted monopolistic licences in exchange for kickbacks disguised as foundation donations. Third, compulsory partnership: companies seeking resource exploitation licences — timber, mining, fisheries — were required to take Suharto relatives as equity partners, who contributed no capital but extracted a share of profits. Fourth, state bank lending without repayment expectations. Fifth, commission extraction on state contracts: infrastructure and procurement deals built in percentage payments to family intermediaries.26

Parallel to the family system was the cukong network — the ethnic-Chinese business partners whose capital and commercial acumen complemented the family's political access. The most important was Liem Sioe Liong (Sudono Salim), a Fujian-born entrepreneur who had supplied Suharto's Diponegoro Division during the independence struggle and subsequently built the Salim Group into the largest conglomerate in Southeast Asia. Richard Borsuk and Nancy Chng's meticulously researched study of the Salim Group traces how its banking, cement, flour-milling, and noodle-manufacturing franchises — obtained through preferential state licensing — constituted the operational core of the New Order's cukong economy.27 Other prominent figures included Mohammed "Bob" Hassan, who held the national timber-plywood export monopoly, and Prajogo Pangestu, who controlled vast logging concessions in Kalimantan. All operated within what Jakarta correspondent Maggie Farley described as "a system ground in nepotism, sanctioned skimming and economic dislocation...where a Suharto family member or friend acts as the overlord of an industry, squeezing out all competition."

The cukong system had a specific racial dimension that the New Order exploited and reproduced. Ethnic-Chinese Indonesians were barred from military and top bureaucratic careers, confined to business; their vulnerability to expropriation, anti-Chinese violence, and the withdrawal of business licences gave them powerful incentives to purchase protection through partnership with politically connected figures. The result was a structure that benefited both the indigenous political elite and the Chinese business community while perpetuating the racial stratification of economic life — and, critically, providing a convenient scapegoat whenever popular resentment of inequality needed redirection.

KKN — Korupsi, Kolusi, Nepotisme: the triad of corruption, collusion, and nepotism that characterised the Suharto regime's political economy. The acronym became the central slogan of the 1998 reform movement.
Cukong — an ethnic-Chinese business partner of a politically connected indigenous official or military officer; the term describes a structural role in the New Order's patronage economy rather than a simple pejorative.

The Father of Development

Suharto did not rule by fear alone. The New Order also constructed a positive legitimating narrative centred on economic achievement, and for much of the regime's life this narrative was grounded in measurable reality. Indonesia's per capita income rose from approximately $50 in 1965 to $1,300 by 1997; the poverty rate fell from roughly forty-five percent in 1970 to around eleven percent by 1996; the literacy rate climbed from forty-three to ninety percent over the same period; manufactured goods grew to fifty-three percent of exports by 1997; and infant mortality was cut by more than sixty percent.28 These gains were real, and they created constituencies for the regime — especially among the Javanese lower-middle class that benefited most directly from rural development programmes.

The centrepiece of the developmental narrative was rice self-sufficiency. In the 1970s Indonesia was the world's largest rice importer; by 1984 the country had achieved self-sufficiency. The BIMAS (Bimbingan Massal, mass guidance) programme distributed high-yielding varieties, fertilisers, and pesticides to village farmers under the direction of agricultural extension agents who were themselves part of the New Order's penetrating state machinery. The Food and Agriculture Organisation awarded Suharto a medal for the achievement — a rare moment of international recognition that the regime exploited extensively for domestic propaganda.29

In 1982 the People's Consultative Assembly formally conferred on Suharto the title Bapak Pembangunan — Father of Development — institutionalising a cult of presidential identity that had been cultivated for over a decade through imagery, rhetoric, and the state-directed curriculum.30 Official portraits of Suharto hung in every government office, school, and many private businesses. The New Order state apparatus — from the ministry of information's censorship bureau to the territorial command's village liaisons — reinforced the message that political stability, development, and Suharto were inseparable; to oppose the first was to threaten the second and third. Anderson's analysis identifies this fusion as characteristically Javanese in its logic — power as concentrated, indivisible, and creative — transplanted into a modern nation-state and reproduced through bureaucratic rather than courtly mechanisms.31

The Repressive Apparatus: Kopkamtib, Petrus, and Tanjung Priok

The developmental legitimacy and managed consensus of the New Order rested, ultimately, on a capacity for violence and surveillance that was never far from the surface. The primary security instrument was Kopkamtib — Komando Operasi Pemulihan Keamanan dan Ketertiban (Operational Command for the Restoration of Security and Order), established ten days after the 30 September 1965 coup attempt, with Suharto as its first commander.32 Kopkamtib operated outside the normal framework of Indonesian law, exercising powers of arrest, interrogation, and indefinite detention without charge or trial. In the aftermath of 1965 alone it oversaw the detention of approximately 200,000 persons in connection with the coup attempt, "most of whom were never charged or tried"; total political prisoner (tapol) estimates for the entire period 1965–1980 range from 600,000 to 750,000 detainees across prisons and island detention camps, most prominently Buru Island in the Maluku archipelago.

Kopkamtib screened candidates in the 1971 elections, disqualifying approximately twenty percent for political reasons, and maintained a "List A/B" (daftar A/B) system categorising individuals as politically suspect on the basis of family relationships to former PKI members or sympathisers — a category that could encompass millions of people and be activated at any bureaucratic moment.33 Suharto served as Kopkamtib commander twice (1965–69 and 1974–78) and appointed a succession of loyalists thereafter; the command was formally dissolved in September 1988 and replaced by Bakorstanas (Coordinating Body for Assistance in Improving National Stability), which assumed essentially identical functions.

The most internationally scrutinised episode of New Order repression in the early 1980s was the Petrus campaign — a portmanteau of Penembakan Misterius, "Mysterious Shootings." Beginning in 1983, military death squads and undercover police units began killing suspected criminals, primarily identified by their tattoos, in cities across Java. Bodies were left in public locations, sometimes bound and with marks of execution. Official silence alternated with attribution to "gang wars." In 1988, in the extraordinary candour of his co-authored autobiography Pikiran, Ucapan, dan Tindakan Saya, Suharto acknowledged his direct authorisation: "Those who resisted had to be shot. They were gunned down because they fought back."34 Death toll estimates vary widely, from approximately 300 in 1983 alone (the lowest credible figure) to over 10,000 over the campaign's course, with Indonesian criminologist Mulyana W. Kusumah estimating over 2,000 and Dutch Foreign Minister Hans van den Broek citing approximately 3,000 by 1984. The campaign was framed by the regime as necessary "shock therapy" to restore social order in cities destabilised by crime; critics identified it as an exercise in intimidation aimed at demonstrating the state's unlimited coercive capacity to the population at large.

A year after Petrus reached its peak, the New Order committed what has come to be regarded as one of its most egregious acts of mass violence in an urban setting. On the evening of 12 September 1984, at the port of Tanjung Priok in North Jakarta, a crowd of several thousand Muslim residents — assembled to protest the arrest of congregants from the As-Saadah Mosque following an altercation with a military sergeant who had entered the mosque without removing his shoes — marched toward the North Jakarta Military District Command. Shortly before midnight, personnel from the 6th Air Defence Artillery Battalion opened fire on the crowd. The official government figure was twenty-four killed and fifty-four injured; survivor testimonies and independent investigations have consistently placed the death toll above one hundred, with some accounts estimating four hundred or more killed or subsequently disappeared.35

The Tanjung Priok massacre is significant not only for its scale but for what it revealed about the New Order's management of political Islam. The immediate trigger — a military officer desecrating a mosque — combined with the shooting of unarmed protesters to inflame the most politically sensitive fault line in the regime's social base. The government's response was to suppress information about casualties, prosecute survivors who spoke publicly, and wait out international criticism. A 2003 trial held under crimes-against-humanity provisions convicted two mid-ranking officers (Colonel Sutrisno Mascung to three years' imprisonment; Major General R.A. Butar-Butar to ten years) but exempted from prosecution the senior officers most directly implicated, including Jakarta military commander Try Sutrisno and Armed Forces commander Benny Moerdani, who had supervised the removal of victims that night.36

The New Order's management of information — through censorship and the threat of publication bans — was the civilian complement to these coercive instruments. Reporting that embarrassed the government, aired cabinet disagreements, or touched on military operations could result in the revocation of a publication's Surat Izin Usaha Penerbitan Pers (SIUPP), the mandatory publishing licence without which no periodical could operate. The most prominent casualty was Tempo, Indonesia's most respected newsweekly, founded in 1971 by Goenawan Mohamad. On 21 June 1994, Information Minister Harmoko revoked the publishing licences of Tempo, Editor, and DeTik, citing national stability; the immediate provocation was a Tempo article reporting disagreement within the cabinet over Habibie's purchase of thirty-nine former East German warships — a decision that airing an internal government dispute publicly evidently enraged Suharto.37 The bannings prompted the formation of the Alliance of Independent Journalists (Aliansi Jurnalis Independen) and foreshadowed the political crisis gathering beneath the regime's apparently serene surface.

Interpreting the New Order: Scholarly Debates

Scholars have never agreed on what kind of state the New Order was, and the disagreement is not merely academic — different framings yield different verdicts on what was achieved, what was lost, and what legacy was bequeathed to democratic Indonesia after 1998.

The earliest and most optimistic framing was the developmental state interpretation: the New Order achieved genuine socioeconomic transformation comparable to the East Asian "tigers," using state capacity and technocratic competence to accelerate industrialisation and poverty reduction. This view was implicit in much World Bank reporting of the 1980s and early 1990s and was not entirely wrong — the statistics cited in the previous section are real. Its weakness, identified by later scholars, was its tendency to take the regime's own developmental claims at face value and to underestimate the degree to which growth was fuelled by oil rents and foreign borrowing rather than sustainable productivity gains, and the degree to which the distribution of those gains was distorted by the patronage system.

A second framework, influential since Harold Crouch's 1979 essay "Patrimonialism and Military Rule in Indonesia" in World Politics, is the patrimonial state interpretation.38 Crouch argued that the New Order's political logic was not Weberian-bureaucratic — governed by rules, offices, and impersonal authority — but neo-patrimonial: organised around personal loyalty to Suharto, who distributed resources, appointments, and protection as personal favours rather than institutional entitlements. This model illuminates the regime's extraordinary centralisation of decision-making, the way in which technically capable technocrats were tolerated only insofar as their advice suited presidential preferences, and the manner in which all significant economic resources were ultimately subject to Suharto's personal dispensation. Its limitation is that it may over-personalise what was also a systemic and institutional arrangement.

The third and currently most influential framework is the oligarchic capitalism interpretation, associated above all with Richard Robison's Indonesia: The Rise of Capital (1986) and developed with Vedi Hadiz in Reorganising Power in Indonesia: The Politics of Oligarchy in an Age of Markets (2004).39 Robison and Hadiz argue that the New Order created — and was sustained by — a distinctive oligarchy: a fusion of politico-military elites and capitalist interests (both indigenous and ethnic-Chinese) whose common interest lay in controlling the state apparatus to extract economic rents. After Suharto's fall, this oligarchy did not dissolve; it reconstituted itself within the new democratic institutions, explaining why Reformasi produced more genuine political competition but only limited redistribution of economic power.

A fourth strand, prominent since Suharto's fall, has foregrounded violence and exclusion as the regime's structural core rather than incidental features. Ben Anderson, in essays written after 1998, and human rights scholars examining the record of Kopkamtib, Petrus, Tanjung Priok, East Timor, Papua, and Aceh, have argued that the coercive foundation of the New Order was not merely an ugly addition to an otherwise developmental or managerial state but its constitutive condition: growth happened within and through a structure of violence.40 Edward Aspinall, acknowledging this critique's force, has nonetheless warned against a "new orthodoxy" in which violence and exclusion become the only lens, at the cost of understanding how the New Order also co-opted potential opponents and maintained genuine popularity in significant sectors of society.41

Vedi Hadiz's parallel study of labour politics under the New Order, Workers and the State in New Order Indonesia (1997), offers a related but distinct emphasis: the regime's systematic suppression of independent labour organisation — through the state-backed SPSI union federation and the criminalisation of wildcat strikes — was central to the "growth without distribution" model that benefited the oligarchy while leaving workers politically marginalised. Industrial unrest in the early 1990s, especially the murder of activist Marsinah in 1993, signalled the limits of this containment strategy even before the financial crisis made those limits catastrophic.42

These debates are not merely historical. They shape contemporary Indonesian politics: how the legacy of military business empires is addressed, whether post-1998 democratic institutions have genuinely transformed the power structures the New Order created, and how Indonesia's development model can be reformed to reduce the structural inequality the Suharto system entrenched. The New Order ended in May 1998; its structural legacies, as Part IX of this book examines, proved far more durable.

Summary

Suharto's New Order (1966–1998) combined managed electoral politics, enforced ideological consensus, technocratic developmentalism, and systematic repression into one of the twentieth century's most durable authoritarian systems. The doctrine of dwifungsi gave the military a permanent presence in civilian governance, from provincial administration to parliament. Golkar's electoral dominance was sustained by the "floating mass" policy, the KORPRI civil-service loyalty mechanism, and the forced merger of all opposition parties into two emasculated vehicles (PPP and PDI). Pancasila was transformed from a broad ideological umbrella into a mandatory consensus through the P4 indoctrination programme and the 1985 asas tunggal law. A cohort of Berkeley-trained technocrats produced genuine economic development — per capita income rising from $50 to $1,300, poverty halved, rice self-sufficiency achieved — but operated alongside a patronage economy that funnelled vast resources to the Suharto family and its cukong partners; Transparency International estimated the family had extracted over $30 billion. The repressive apparatus — Kopkamtib, the Petrus extrajudicial killings of the 1980s, the 1984 Tanjung Priok massacre, systematic press censorship — coexisted with the developmental narrative and enforced the cult of Bapak Pembangunan. Scholars have debated whether the New Order is best understood as a developmental state, a patrimonial system, an oligarchic capitalist formation, or a structure of organised violence; most now combine elements of these frameworks rather than adopting any single model.

Review & Discussion Questions

32.1 How did the doctrine of dwifungsi differ from a conventional military dictatorship, and what advantages did this difference give the New Order in sustaining itself over thirty-two years?
32.2 The New Order held six general elections yet is classified as authoritarian. What specific mechanisms made those elections compatible with authoritarian rule? Were there any respects in which the elections placed genuine constraints on the regime?
32.3 How did the New Order's use of Pancasila differ from Sukarno's use of it? What does this difference reveal about the relationship between ideology and power?
32.4 The "Berkeley Mafia" produced real economic development while operating within a system of systematic corruption. How should historians evaluate this coexistence? Is it possible to separate the achievements from the pathologies?
32.5 Evaluate the KKN system. Was it primarily an expression of personal greed by Suharto and his family, or was it a structural feature of how the New Order maintained political stability?
32.6 Compare the Petrus killings of 1983–85 with the Tanjung Priok massacre of 1984. What do the different contexts, targets, and subsequent legal proceedings reveal about how the New Order deployed violence?
32.7 Which of the four scholarly frameworks for interpreting the New Order — developmental state, patrimonial state, oligarchic capitalism, or violence/exclusion — do you find most persuasive, and why? What evidence would you need to adjudicate between them?
32.8 Robison and Hadiz argue that the New Order's oligarchy reconstituted itself within democratic institutions after 1998. What evidence from the New Order period supports or complicates this argument?
