Learning Objectives

  • Describe the sequence of events on the night of 30 September–1 October 1965 (the G30S) and explain what the movement's leaders claimed they were doing.
  • Understand the major scholarly interpretations of who was responsible — the army's official narrative, the Cornell Paper of Anderson and McVey, John Roosa's analysis of the Sjam documents, and the question of Suharto's foreknowledge — and why the question remains contested.
  • Analyze how Suharto used the crisis to dismantle the PKI, sideline Sukarno, and consolidate military power within a matter of months.
  • Assess the scale and character of the anti-communist mass killings of 1965–66, including the range of scholarly death-toll estimates, regional variation, and the roles of the army and civilian militias.
  • Evaluate the evidence for Western (US and UK) knowledge and complicity in the killings, drawing on declassified documents and the scholarship of Brad Simpson and others.
  • Trace the fate of the mass detainees — particularly on Buru Island — and understand the Supersemar of 11 March 1966 as the legal instrument of Suharto's final seizure of power.
  • Examine the politics of memory and silence that surrounded 1965 under the New Order, and the halting efforts at reckoning that followed Reformasi — including the 2012 film The Act of Killing and the 2015 International People's Tribunal.

In the small hours of 1 October 1965, death squads fanned out across Jakarta. By dawn, six of the seven generals they had been sent to seize were dead or dying, their bodies destined for a well at the edge of the city called Lubang Buaya — the Crocodile Hole. Within hours, a little-known major general named Suharto had taken command of the crisis. Within months, the Indonesian Communist Party (PKI) — with some three million members, the largest communist party outside the Soviet Union and China — had been annihilated. Within a year, somewhere between five hundred thousand and over a million people were dead, and the republic that Sukarno had proclaimed in 1945 had been quietly replaced by a military autocracy. The events of that single night, and the violence they unleashed, remain the most contested and consequential in modern Indonesian history — a knot of evidence, interpretation, ideology, and silence that scholarship has been untangling, with incomplete success, for six decades.1

The Perfect Storm: Indonesia on the Eve of Crisis

To understand the night of 30 September 1965 it is necessary to understand how far the Indonesian republic had deteriorated from the optimism of independence. By mid-1965, Sukarno's Guided Democracy had produced a country in deep structural crisis. Inflation was running above 500 percent annually, the foreign debt was unserviceable, export revenues had collapsed, and the development projects Sukarno favoured — stadiums, monuments, grandiose public buildings — were adding to the debt without producing economic growth.2 The army, bloated and corrupt, had developed its own economic interests through a web of state enterprises and regional fiefdoms; military commanders supplemented their inadequate salaries through smuggling, extortion, and business dealings that had little to do with national defense. M.C. Ricklefs, whose standard survey of modern Indonesian history devotes particular attention to the structural roots of the 1965 crisis, identifies the combination of economic collapse, ideological polarization, and Sukarno's declining health as the three interlocking conditions that made catastrophe possible.3

The most dangerous polarity was between the army and the PKI. Sukarno's formula for stability — NASAKOM, the forced unity of nationalism, religion, and communism — required the army to tolerate the party's rapid growth. Under Guided Democracy the PKI had flourished, growing from perhaps one million members in 1955 to approximately three million by 1965, with a wider orbit of affiliated trade unions, peasant organizations, and youth groups numbering perhaps twenty million people. PKI Chairman D.N. Aidit had pursued a legal, parliamentary road to power, working within the system and cultivating Sukarno's favour. Yet the party's mass mobilizations in the early 1960s — particularly the unilateral land-seizure campaigns in Central Java and Sumatra — had generated intense antagonism from Muslim landowners, Islamic organizations, and anti-communist officers who regarded the PKI's gains as an existential threat.4

Against this backdrop of polarization, there circulated in mid-1965 a rumour that intensified the anxieties of both sides: that Sukarno, who had been ill for much of the year and whose personal physician had reportedly given him a bleak prognosis, might not survive much longer. For the PKI, a Sukarno death would remove the protective political umbrella that had allowed the party to grow while its enemies seethed. For the army's most senior generals, it created an opportunity for a reckoning. Harold Crouch's authoritative study of the army and politics, drawing on extensive interviews conducted in Indonesia in the late 1960s, noted that each side in the polarized polity was aware that the other was making plans for the post-Sukarno transition — and each was tempted to strike first.5 The PKI, according to John Roosa's later reconstruction, believed that a coterie of right-wing generals — whom PKI propaganda labelled a "Dewan Jenderal" (Council of Generals) — was preparing a coup to seize power after Sukarno's death or incapacitation. It was this belief, Roosa argues, that supplied the motivation for what happened on the night of 30 September.6

The Night of the Generals: 30 September–1 October 1965

At approximately 3:15 in the morning of 1 October 1965, seven detachments of troops — drawn from units based at Halim Air Force Base on the southern outskirts of Jakarta — moved out in trucks and buses toward the homes of seven of the army's most senior generals. Their mission was to abduct the generals, alive if possible, and bring them to Halim, where, according to the plan, they would be confronted and placed in custody pending the formation of a "Revolutionary Council" that the movement intended to announce over the radio.7

The operation was only partly successful. Three generals — Chief of Staff Lieutenant General Ahmad Yani, Major General M.T. Haryono, and Brigadier General D.I. Pandjaitan — were killed at their homes, either because they resisted or because the abduction squads lost control of the situation in the darkness and confusion. Three others — Major General Soeprapto, Major General S. Parman, and Brigadier General Sutoyo Siswomiharjo — were taken alive, brought to Halim, and subsequently killed there. The seventh target, General Abdul Haris Nasution — the army's powerful Chief of Staff and one of the most prominent anti-communist officers — escaped by jumping over a low wall into the garden of the neighbouring Iraqi embassy. His aide, Lieutenant Pierre Tendean, was killed in his place, evidently mistaken for Nasution in the darkness. Nasution's young daughter was also shot and mortally wounded. The six bodies were eventually dumped down the well at Lubang Buaya.8

At 7:15 AM on 1 October 1965, Radio Republic Indonesia broadcast an announcement. The movement — calling itself the Gerakan 30 September (30 September Movement, abbreviated G30S, or pejoratively by the army as "Gestapu") — declared that it had taken control of strategic locations in Jakarta "to forestall a coup attempt" by a "Council of Generals" that was operating with CIA support against President Sukarno. It announced that Sukarno was under its protection, that all military ranks above lieutenant colonel were abolished, and that a "Revolutionary Council" of forty-five figures had been formed to exercise power. Similar announcements and brief seizures of radio stations and other facilities occurred in Yogyakarta and Central Java, but the movement gained no significant traction outside Jakarta.9

G30S (Gerakan 30 September) — the "30 September Movement," the name used by the group itself for the coup attempt of the night of 30 September–1 October 1965. The army's pejorative contraction "Gestapu" (Gerakan September Tigapuluh) was widely used during the New Order to associate the movement with the German SS. Scholarly convention favours the neutral abbreviation G30S. The movement claimed to be protecting Sukarno from a right-wing generals' coup rather than staging one itself.

Who Did It? A Historiographical Reckoning

No question in modern Indonesian history has generated more scholarship, more controversy, or more political danger than the question of who organized the G30S and why. For three decades under the New Order, the official answer was unchallengeable: the PKI had masterminded the coup as a bid for power, the generals were murdered in cold blood by communist assassins, and Suharto had heroically saved the nation. Challenging this narrative was, in the literal sense, life-threatening — scholars who questioned it were imprisoned, banned, or silenced. The real historiographical debate was conducted mostly abroad, by scholars in the United States, Australia, and the Netherlands, who worked from a distance on incomplete evidence. That evidence has expanded considerably since Suharto's fall in 1998, but the core questions remain contested.

The Army's Official Narrative

The New Order's account, elaborated in official histories, school textbooks, and the annual mandatory screening of the film Pengkhianatan G30S/PKI (The Treachery of the 30 September Movement/PKI, first shown on state television in 1984), held that the PKI under Aidit had planned the coup as a direct bid for state power, exploiting Sukarno's illness to strike before the anti-communist generals could act. Central to this narrative was a lurid account of torture and mutilation at Lubang Buaya, allegedly carried out by female PKI members — a story that served to mobilize public revulsion and to delegitimize not only the PKI but any defence of its members. The historian Katharine McGregor has analyzed how this official memory was institutionalized through monuments, textbooks, and ritual commemoration, creating what she terms a "politics of memory" that effectively replaced historical scholarship with state mythology for the duration of the New Order.10

The Cornell Paper: An Internal Army Affair?

The most influential counter-analysis of the official narrative was produced within weeks of the events themselves. Benedict Anderson and Ruth McVey, Southeast Asian specialists at Cornell University, working with Frederick Bunnell, completed a preliminary analysis on 10 January 1966 — later known simply as the "Cornell Paper" — that circulated among fewer than two dozen scholars before being formally published by Cornell's Modern Indonesia Project in 1971.11 Anderson and McVey examined the publicly available Indonesian press coverage and noticed a series of anomalies in the army's account. The troops involved in the G30S were not random PKI militiamen; they were regular army units, drawn from the Palace Guard and from the Diponegoro and Brawijaya divisions stationed in Central Java. The movement's nominal leader, Lieutenant Colonel Untung, was a career army officer, a battalion commander in the Palace Guard. The G30S had operated from Halim Air Force Base — a military installation. The targets, moreover, were specific senior generals, not a general seizure of state power.

On the basis of these observations, Anderson and McVey proposed that the G30S was best understood as "an internal army affair" — a conspiracy by junior officers, potentially motivated by resentment of a corrupt and overprivileged general staff, rather than a PKI coup attempt. In their reading, PKI involvement was peripheral rather than central; the party may have been aware of the operation but did not organize it. Their analysis was systematically discredited by the New Order — possession of the Cornell Paper in Indonesia was dangerous — and was also criticized by other Western scholars who felt it understated the PKI's organizational capacity and Aidit's known radicalism.12

Roosa and the Sjam Documents

The most consequential new evidence came from an unlikely source. After Suharto's fall, John Roosa, a historian at the University of British Columbia, gained access to the trial testimony and a lengthy unpublished manuscript left by Sjam Kamaruzaman — the head of the PKI's clandestine Special Bureau, the unit responsible for the party's covert relations with sympathizers within the military. Sjam had been the key link between the PKI leadership and the military conspirators in the G30S. His testimony and manuscript, analysed in Roosa's *Pretext for Mass Murder* (University of Wisconsin Press, 2006), provided an account from inside the conspiracy itself.13

Roosa's central argument, derived from the Sjam documents, is a carefully calibrated middle position. The PKI leadership — specifically Chairman Aidit and Sjam himself — was closely and knowingly involved in planning the G30S. But the aim, Roosa argues, was not to seize state power. The PKI believed, perhaps correctly, that a group of right-wing generals was planning a post-Sukarno coup, and the G30S was intended as a pre-emptive decapitation strike against those generals, after which Sukarno would be able to govern without the threat of a military takeover. It was, in Roosa's formulation, a "pretext" — or rather, a gamble based on a misread of the political situation — not a revolutionary seizure of power. The plan was operationally amateurish: the radio announcements were contradictory, the Revolutionary Council list was absurd, and the movement had no coherent plan for what to do after the initial phase. Aidit flew to Halim the night of the operation but seems to have been largely passive, a witness rather than a commander.14

The Question of Suharto's Foreknowledge

Among the most persistent and politically explosive questions in the historiography is whether Suharto knew about the G30S in advance. Several facts have struck scholars as anomalous. Suharto, who by October 1965 commanded KOSTRAD (the Army Strategic Reserve Command) and was one of the most senior generals in Jakarta, was conspicuously absent from the list of targets — despite being by any measure as prominent an anti-communist officer as those who were killed. He was personally acquainted with Lieutenant Colonel Untung, the G30S's nominal commander, having served in the same unit in the early 1950s. And he moved with extraordinary speed and self-assurance on the morning of 1 October, assuming command of the situation within hours of learning of the kidnappings.15

Roosa, examining the Sjam documents carefully, notes that the PKI's Special Bureau had spent years cultivating contacts within the military. The omission of Suharto from the target list suggests either that the PKI did not regard him as part of the anti-Sukarno generals' faction, or — more troublingly — that he had some channel of communication with the conspirators. Roosa stops short of asserting that Suharto organized or controlled the G30S; the evidence does not support that conclusion. But he does argue that the official New Order portrayal of Suharto as a shocked and heroic defender of the republic cannot be taken at face value, and that the question of his prior knowledge "has never been satisfactorily resolved."16

Other scholars have taken stronger positions. The Dutch historian Wim Wertheim, writing in the 1970s, proposed the hypothesis of a "dalang" — a puppet-master behind the scenes, a reference to the wayang tradition — suggesting Suharto himself had orchestrated the whole affair. The American journalist and scholar Peter Dale Scott argued for significant CIA involvement in facilitating Suharto's seizure of power. These interpretations have not found widespread acceptance in mainstream scholarship, which generally considers them unsubstantiated by available evidence. What the scholarly mainstream does accept, following Crouch, Roosa, and Robinson, is that Suharto was neither the simple victim of the narrative nor an innocent bystander: he was a sophisticated political actor who understood the situation he found himself in and exploited it with ruthless skill.17

Suharto's Countermove and the Onset of Mass Violence

Whatever Suharto knew in advance, his response on 1 October 1965 was decisive to the point of appearing pre-planned. By approximately 6 AM on 1 October — before the G30S radio announcements had fully circulated — he had contacted KOSTRAD's headquarters and begun to assess the situation. He reportedly told officers around him that he "knew" what was happening, a phrase that has attracted scholarly attention. Through the morning, using KOSTRAD's communications network, he contacted the commanders of units that the G30S had attempted to mobilize, persuading them one by one to stand down or defect. By the afternoon, the rebel battalions that had occupied Merdeka Square had withdrawn to Halim. By nightfall, Suharto was able to broadcast over Radio Republic Indonesia that he had assumed command of the army and that the "counter-revolutionary" movement was being suppressed. By 2 October, government forces had taken Halim and the G30S had collapsed as a military operation.18

What followed the military collapse of the G30S was not simply a return to order but a carefully orchestrated campaign of destruction. Suharto and the army leadership — working in the first instance through the new para-commando unit RPKAD under Colonel Sarwo Edhie Wibowo — moved to identify, arrest, and ultimately eliminate the PKI and its associated organizations. The propaganda apparatus that the army controlled was immediately deployed to frame the events as a communist atrocity, complete with fabricated or exaggerated accounts of the generals' torture and the sexual mutilation of their bodies by PKI women (medical examinations would later show the generals had been shot, not tortured, and that there was no sign of sexual mutilation). These claims, broadcast relentlessly over army-controlled radio, were designed to inflame public anger and to justify extrajudicial killing on a mass scale.19

The military moved rapidly on multiple fronts simultaneously. PKI offices were ransacked and burned. Party leaders were arrested; Aidit was captured in Central Java and executed without trial on or around 22 November 1965. Newspapers, trade unions, and youth organizations associated with the left were shut down. In the army's language, the operation was a "cleaning-up" (pembersihan) of communist influence — a bureaucratic euphemism that would echo through subsequent decades of scholarship on the mechanics of genocide.20

The Anatomy of Mass Killing: Scale, Method, and the Question of Command

The anti-communist killings that swept Indonesia between October 1965 and the first months of 1966 constitute one of the largest and swiftest episodes of mass murder in the twentieth century. Precisely because they occurred under conditions of deliberate suppression and silence — the perpetrators in power for three decades, the survivors terrorized, the documentation mostly destroyed or hidden — the death toll is a matter of genuine scholarly uncertainty. The range of estimates is itself historically significant: it reflects not only the difficulty of counting in the chaos of widespread violence but also the political stakes attached to the numbers.

The most widely cited range is 500,000 to 1,000,000 deaths. Robert Cribb, whose edited volume *The Indonesian Killings, 1965–1966* (Monash University, 1990) remains one of the essential reference works, concluded after systematic survey that the figure of 500,000 was a credible minimum but that the actual total was probably higher; he noted that some regional estimates alone, added together, produced numbers approaching one million.21 A 1968 CIA report, subsequently declassified, acknowledged that the killings ranked "among the worst mass murders of the twentieth century, along with the Soviet purges of the 1930s, the Nazi mass murders during the Second World War, and the Maoist bloodbath of the early 1950s."22 More recent scholarship by Geoffrey Robinson and Jess Melvin has pushed toward the higher end of the range, while some earlier estimates of 2–3 million have generally been considered excessive by the scholarly community. The honest answer is that nobody knows with precision how many died.

What is increasingly clear from the most recent scholarship is the centrality of the army in directing the violence. This has been one of the key revisionist findings of the post-New Order generation of historians, and it runs directly against the official New Order narrative, which portrayed the killings as a spontaneous uprising by a Muslim population defending itself against communist aggression. Geoffrey Robinson's *The Killing Season* (Princeton University Press, 2018), the most comprehensive single-volume study of the violence, argues on the basis of extensive archival research and fieldwork that the killings were not spontaneous but systematically organized. The army selected targets, supplied weapons, provided logistical support, and in many cases transported and supervised the killing. Civilian and religious organizations — particularly Nahdlatul Ulama's youth wing Ansor and the Pemuda Pancasila youth organization associated with the army — did much of the actual killing, but Robinson's central argument is that "without the army's initiative, direction, and support, the mass killings would not have happened."23

Jess Melvin's *The Army and the Indonesian Genocide: Mechanics of Mass Murder* (Routledge, 2018) adds a crucial dimension to this picture. Drawing on over three thousand pages of classified documents she discovered in the Aceh Government Library and Archives in 2010, Melvin documents the army's command-and-control structure in Aceh in forensic detail. Her research shows that army orders to kill PKI members were issued as early as 1 October 1965 — before any "spontaneous" popular violence could have developed — and that the killing was planned, documented, and executed through a clear military chain of command. Her findings are specific to Aceh, but her methodology and conclusions have shaped the broader field: the 1965–66 killings were not mob violence that the army struggled to control; they were, at least in significant part, a military operation that used civilian auxiliaries as instruments.24

The role of civilian religious organizations nonetheless remains a genuinely complex dimension of the violence that cannot be reduced to army instrumentalization. In many parts of Central and East Java, the antagonism between Muslim communities (especially santri — observant, orthodox Muslims) and PKI-affiliated communities (many of them associated with abangan — syncretic, less orthodox Javanese Islam) had deep roots in the land reform campaigns and political mobilizations of the Sukarno era. The army's anti-PKI propaganda fell on terrain that had been prepared by years of genuine fear and mutual antagonism. In some localities, killings began before army units arrived; in others, Islamic organizations led the violence with apparent conviction rather than mere instrumentalization.25 Distinguishing between army-directed killing and civilian initiative that the army exploited and amplified is, in many instances, not possible with surviving evidence.

Regional Anatomy: Java, Bali, and Sumatra

The violence was not uniform across Indonesia. Its timing, intensity, and social character varied enormously by region, reflecting both the differing political landscapes of different areas and the different degrees to which army units were present, active, and willing to coordinate with or suppress civilian violence.

Java

The killing began in Jakarta and spread rapidly to Central and East Java, the PKI's organizational heartland. In Central Java, where the Diponegoro division had units that had participated in the G30S, the army moved to purge its own ranks as well as destroying the PKI externally. The killings in East Java were particularly severe and often followed cultural fault-lines between santri and abangan communities: Nahdlatul Ulama's youth organization, Ansor, played a major role in identifying and executing PKI members and sympathizers, sometimes in collaboration with army units. In some areas of East Java, killing continued sporadically through 1967 and 1968, as local vendettas merged with the anti-communist campaign.26

Bali

The Balinese killings of December 1965 through early 1966 were, in proportion to population, the most catastrophic anywhere in Indonesia. Geoffrey Robinson's *The Dark Side of Paradise: Political Violence in Bali* (Cornell University Press, 1995), a detailed historical study of political violence in Bali, documented a death toll of approximately 80,000 — roughly five percent of the island's entire population at the time.27 Bali, with its complex caste-based social structure and the PKI's significant inroads among landless peasants, had been a site of intense social mobilization in the preceding years. The killing was catalysed partly by the arrival of Colonel Sarwo Edhie Wibowo's RPKAD para-commandos from Java, who provided the organizational spine for what Robinson portrays not as spontaneous communal violence but as coordinated mass murder. The extraordinary scale of the Balinese killing relative to population has made it a subject of particular scholarly and journalistic attention, though it is important to note that proportional figures can obscure the absolute severity of the Java killings, where the aggregate numbers were far higher.28

Sumatra

In Sumatra, killings were heaviest in areas with significant PKI presence: North Sumatra, where plantation workers had long been organized by PKI-affiliated unions, saw large-scale violence; Aceh, as Melvin's research demonstrates, saw systematic army-directed killing beginning almost immediately after 1 October. South Sumatra saw what Robinson describes as the "slaughter en masse" of Javanese migrant labourers, who were associated with PKI support regardless of their individual affiliations. The Acehnese case is particularly significant because Melvin's document discovery allows for a level of precision — the army's operational orders, after-action reports, and kill tallies — that is simply not available for most other regions. That Aceh produced such documentation suggests that similar command-and-control structures existed elsewhere but the records have not survived or have not yet been found.29

Cold War Complicity: The United States, Britain, and the Purge

The question of Western governments' knowledge of and complicity in the 1965–66 killings has been substantially clarified by the declassification of archival documents over the past three decades, though it remains a subject of historical and political contention. The picture that has emerged is not one of external control — neither the United States nor Britain organized or directed the violence — but of active assistance and deliberate encouragement to those who did.

The United States had powerful strategic reasons to want the PKI destroyed. By 1965, the Johnson administration was escalating its commitment in Vietnam, and Indonesia — the world's fifth most populous country, rich in oil and strategic minerals, and sitting astride the sea lanes between the Indian and Pacific oceans — was seen in Washington as the crucial test case for whether Asia could be held against communist advance. The PKI's strength under Sukarno's protection, and Sukarno's increasingly strident anti-American rhetoric, had made Indonesia a source of intense anxiety in Washington. When the army moved against the PKI, US officials — from the CIA station in Jakarta to Ambassador Marshall Green and the State Department in Washington — greeted the news with barely concealed relief and moved to assist.30

The most documented form of that assistance was the supply of information. Robert J. Martens, a political officer at the US Embassy in Jakarta, compiled lists of PKI members — drawing on public sources including party newspapers and congress proceedings — that were provided to an intermediary connected to Adam Malik, one of the Indonesian politicians aligned with Suharto. In a 1990 interview with journalist Kathy Kadane, Martens acknowledged that "we provided them the names, the addresses, the telephone numbers, whatever we had" — up to five thousand names in total. He also acknowledged that Embassy officials subsequently checked off names on the lists as individuals were killed or captured, tracking the progress of the army's campaign.31

Bradley Simpson's *Economists with Guns: Authoritarian Development and U.S.–Indonesian Relations, 1960–1968* (Stanford University Press, 2008), based on systematic research in declassified State Department, CIA, and NSC records, documents the broader pattern of US support. Simpson shows that Ambassador Green's requests to Washington for covert, non-attributable financial and material assistance were approved, and that the US provided "money, communications equipment, and arms" to support the army's operations. Green's cables to Washington during October and November 1965, since released, express enthusiasm for the army's actions and anxiety only that the generals might be "too cautious" or "too much under Sukarno's thumb."32

The National Security Archive's 2017 release of nearly 30,000 pages from the US Embassy Jakarta files (1964–1968) provided the most detailed documentary picture yet of American awareness and involvement. The cables show Embassy officials documenting mass killings in real time — a November 1965 consular cable from Surabaya reported "PKI being slaughtered in many areas of East Java" and estimated "the largest slaughter had taken place at Tulungagung where reportedly 15,000 Communists killed" — and making no apparent attempt to intervene or protest. A December 1965 Embassy report estimated that "at least 100,000 people have been killed," a figure the Embassy considered an undercount.33

British complicity has attracted less scholarly attention but was also significant. Britain's strategic interest lay partly in the Konfrontasi — Sukarno's undeclared war against Malaysia, which was being actively fought in Borneo. British special operations, psychological warfare units, and intelligence services had been conducting covert operations against Indonesia since Konfrontasi began in 1963, including "black propaganda" designed to discredit Sukarno and intensify army-PKI tensions. The destruction of the PKI and the resulting end of Konfrontasi served British strategic interests directly.34

Note on Western complicity: The documentary record establishes that the US government had detailed knowledge of the mass killings from their beginning and provided active assistance to the army conducting them. The documentary record does not support the stronger claim — advanced by some writers — that the US or CIA organized or initiated the G30S itself. The distinction matters: the US was complicit in genocide by knowledge and assistance, not by origination.

Detention, the Supersemar, and the Transfer of Power

The killing of 1965–66 was one dimension of the anti-communist campaign; detention was another. Alongside those who were killed outright, hundreds of thousands of people were arrested and held without trial in what became a vast and permanent carceral system. The New Order classified detainees into three categories: Category A (those accused of direct involvement in the G30S, subject to trial and often execution); Category B (those deemed PKI members or sympathizers but not directly involved, subject to indefinite detention without trial); and Category C (those with looser PKI associations, subject to surveillance and restrictions on employment, marriage, and travel that would last for decades. The stigma of "Category C" status was hereditary, passed from parents to children, effectively creating a class of second-class citizens that numbered in the millions.35

The most notorious site of long-term detention was Buru Island, a remote island in the Maluku (Moluccas) archipelago. From 1969, the New Order shipped approximately 12,000 Category B political detainees — many of them writers, artists, teachers, and intellectuals — to Buru, where they were forced to clear jungle, build their own barracks, and grow their own food under armed guard. The conditions were brutal; many died of disease and malnutrition. The detainees were held without charge or trial. The most celebrated among them was Indonesia's greatest novelist, Pramoedya Ananta Toer, who was held on Buru from 1969 until 1979. Deprived of writing materials, he composed his *Buru Quartet* — four historical novels about the Indonesian national awakening — by reciting them aloud to fellow prisoners, who memorized and later transcribed the texts. The Quartet, smuggled out and eventually published, became one of the masterpieces of twentieth-century world literature and a monument to the human cost of the New Order's foundational violence.36

The formal transfer of executive power from Sukarno to Suharto was accomplished through a document of enduring ambiguity. On 11 March 1966 — a date whose initials in Indonesian, Sebelas Maret, gave the document its name, Supersemar (Surat Perintah Sebelas Maret, the Order of Eleventh March) — Sukarno signed an authorization granting Suharto sweeping powers to restore order. The circumstances of the signing are disputed. Three generals had arrived at the Bogor Palace where Sukarno was meeting his cabinet, reportedly with armed troops surrounding the grounds. Some accounts claim the generals placed pistols on the table; the army has always denied this. What is not disputed is that Sukarno signed and that the document he signed granted Suharto authority that went far beyond any conventional interpretation of a security mandate.37

The original Supersemar document subsequently disappeared — no copy held by the National Archives is considered certainly authentic — and at least four different versions with significant textual variations circulate, including differences in Sukarno's name spelling, grammar, and signature placement. The disappearance of the original has been interpreted by critics as evidence of alteration or fabrication, though proof is lacking. What is certain is how the document was used: within days, Suharto employed the Supersemar's authority to ban the PKI and arrest a hundred senior ministers and officials associated with Sukarno. The Provisional People's Consultative Assembly (MPRS), now packed with Suharto's allies, formally stripped Sukarno of the presidency in March 1967 and named Suharto Acting President; he assumed the full presidency in March 1968. The process had taken less than two and a half years from the night of the generals to the complete installation of the New Order.38

Supersemar — the Surat Perintah Sebelas Maret (Order of Eleventh March), a document signed by President Sukarno on 11 March 1966 granting Lieutenant General Suharto authority to restore order. It became the legal foundation of Suharto's transfer of power. Its authenticity is disputed: the original has disappeared, and multiple versions with significant textual differences circulate. The circumstances of its signing — whether Sukarno acted freely or under military coercion — remain contested.

The Long Silence: Memory, Impunity, and Reckoning

The New Order's survival depended, in no small part, on the maintenance of a particular story about 1965. That story — the PKI coup, the heroic army, the grateful nation saved from communism — was institutionalized at every level of Indonesian public life for thirty-two years. It was taught in every school. It was reinforced every year on 1 October (designated "Sacred Pancasila Day," Hari Kesaktian Pancasila) with ceremonies at the Lubang Buaya monument, mandatory viewing of the state propaganda film, and the ritual denunciation of communism. Former political prisoners and their children were required to carry documentation of their status, which barred them from government employment, the military, and certain professions. The very language of discussion was policed: using the neutral term "G30S" rather than the charged "G30S/PKI" was itself a political act, and a potentially dangerous one.39

After Suharto's resignation in May 1998 — itself the product of economic collapse and mass protest — the silences of the New Order began, slowly and unevenly, to break. Newspapers could discuss 1965 more openly. Some survivors began to testify. Scholars, particularly those from the younger generation who had grown up after 1965, began to research the events with access to Indonesian archives and communities that had previously been closed. But the reckoning remained incomplete, partial, and contested. No truth and reconciliation commission was established. No prosecutions occurred. The military officers who had directed the killings were never charged. A formal presidential apology was discussed in 2000 by President Abdurrahman Wahid ("Gus Dur"), who expressed sympathy for the victims of 1965, but his government was short-lived and the apology was never formalized or institutionalized. Subsequent governments retreated from the subject.40

The most unexpected intervention in the memory politics of 1965 came from a documentary filmmaker. Joshua Oppenheimer, a British-American director who had spent years working with survivors' communities in North Sumatra, released *The Act of Killing* in 2012. The film took the radical approach of asking the perpetrators — men like Anwar Congo, a former death squad leader in Medan who had personally killed hundreds of people — to recreate their killings in whatever cinematic genre they chose. The result was one of the most disturbing and formally innovative films of its era: perpetrators performed their murders as gangster films, musicals, and Westerns, dressed in elaborate costumes, laughing and boasting, entirely free from legal consequence or social stigma. *The Act of Killing* won or was nominated for dozens of international awards including an Academy Award nomination for Best Documentary Feature. More significantly for Indonesia, it was seen clandestinely by millions of Indonesians and sparked a public conversation about 1965 that no amount of academic scholarship had managed to ignite.41

Oppenheimer followed *The Act of Killing* with *The Look of Silence* (2014), in which a survivor's younger brother — an optometrist named Adi Rukun — confronts some of the men who killed his brother in 1965. The contrast between the two films is itself historically instructive: where the perpetrators of *The Act of Killing* performed their crimes with theatrical pride, the confrontations of *The Look of Silence* reveal a different register — men who are visibly uncomfortable being asked to account for actions they have never previously been required to justify, protected as they are by decades of legal impunity and social power.

In November 2015, a different kind of reckoning occurred in The Hague. The International People's Tribunal (IPT) 1965 — a non-governmental "tribunal of inquiry" convened by Indonesian and international civil society — held four days of hearings at the Nieuwe Kerk, receiving testimony from more than twenty witnesses and reviewing documentary evidence. The tribunal's judges, drawn from international human rights law, found that the Indonesian government bore responsibility for crimes against humanity committed in 1965–66, including murder, enslavement, imprisonment, torture, sexual violence, and enforced disappearance. The tribunal also found that the United States, the United Kingdom, and Australia had been complicit in these crimes. The IPT held no legal jurisdiction and issued no enforceable rulings; the Indonesian government rejected its findings and declined to participate. But the tribunal's Final Report constitutes a significant attempt at documentation and symbolic accountability for events that the Indonesian state has yet formally to acknowledge.42

As of the mid-2020s, the position in Indonesia remains deeply unresolved. Scholarly research — particularly by Indonesian historians of the post-Suharto generation — has substantially expanded what is known about the events of 1965–66. Organizations of survivors and their families, such as the Foundation for Research into the Victims of the Killings of 1965 (YPKP 65), continue to document cases and advocate for recognition. Periodic official statements have approached but never crossed the threshold of formal acknowledgment, let alone apology or reparation. The question of 1965 remains, in Indonesia, less a settled history than a live political wound.

tapol — abbreviation of tahanan politik ("political prisoner/detainee"), used for the hundreds of thousands of Indonesians detained after 1965 on suspicion of PKI membership or sympathy. Tapol were classified into Categories A, B, and C, with differing levels of legal jeopardy. Category C status — the broadest and most loosely defined — was often hereditary, stigmatizing not only individuals but their children and grandchildren.

Summary

The events of 30 September–1 October 1965 set off the most consequential and contested crisis in modern Indonesian history. The G30S — a shadowy operation involving regular army units, PKI clandestine operatives, and the killing of six generals — was swiftly suppressed by Major General Suharto, who used the crisis to dismantle the PKI and sideline Sukarno. The subsequent anti-communist killings of 1965–66 claimed between 500,000 and more than one million lives; the scholarly consensus, reinforced by Geoffrey Robinson's *The Killing Season* and Jess Melvin's archival discoveries in Aceh, increasingly identifies the army as the central organizing force, using civilian and religious organizations as instruments. Western governments — particularly the United States — had detailed knowledge of the killings and provided active assistance. The question of who organized the G30S remains genuinely contested: John Roosa's analysis of the Sjam documents establishes PKI leadership involvement with limited objectives; the Cornell Paper's "internal army affair" hypothesis has been substantially modified by subsequent research; and the question of Suharto's foreknowledge has never been resolved. The New Order suppressed all alternative accounts for three decades; after 1998, limited public discussion became possible but no formal reckoning has occurred. The 2012 film *The Act of Killing* and the 2015 International People's Tribunal at The Hague represent the most visible efforts to force accountability for crimes that the Indonesian state has yet formally to acknowledge.

Review & Discussion Questions

31.1 The G30S targeted seven specific generals but spared Suharto, who commanded KOSTRAD. What are the different scholarly explanations for this omission, and what does each explanation imply about who was responsible for the G30S?
31.2 Compare the "Cornell Paper" hypothesis of an "internal army affair" with John Roosa's argument derived from the Sjam documents. What new evidence did Roosa have access to that Anderson and McVey did not? How significantly does this change the historical picture?
31.3 Death-toll estimates for the 1965–66 killings range from 500,000 to over one million. What factors make it so difficult to establish a more precise figure, and what does this uncertainty itself tell us about the nature of the violence and its political aftermath?
31.4 The US Embassy provided lists of PKI members to the Indonesian army and documented the subsequent killings in real time. Does this constitute "complicity"? What distinction, if any, should be drawn between knowledge, assistance, and origination in assessing foreign responsibility for atrocity?
31.5 How did the New Order institutionalize a particular memory of 1965 — through what mechanisms, and to what ends? What were the human costs of this official memory for survivors and their descendants?
31.6 Joshua Oppenheimer's The Act of Killing (2012) reached Indonesian audiences who had never engaged with the scholarly literature on 1965. What does this suggest about the role of art and documentary film in historical reckoning, compared to academic scholarship?
31.7 The International People's Tribunal of 2015 had no legal jurisdiction and the Indonesian government rejected its findings. Was it nonetheless a meaningful historical or political event? What is the purpose of a "people's tribunal" that lacks enforcement power?
31.8 Jess Melvin's research in Aceh shows army kill orders issued on 1 October 1965 itself. How does this documentary evidence change the debate about whether the anti-PKI killings were "spontaneous" communal violence or a military operation?
