## Our Take

The Royal College of Art — one of the most prestigious art and design institutions in the world — hosting an AI festival is a statement in itself. This isn't a tech conference with an art track bolted on; it's an art institution grappling directly with the technology that's reshaping creative practice.

The festival, which ran February 16–20, brought together researchers, artists, designers, and technologists under a framework that neither uncritically embraces AI nor dismissively rejects it. The keynote, "Art and the Atomic Human," explored vulnerability and intelligence in the AI age — a theme that acknowledges AI's capabilities while centering the irreducibly human elements of creative work.

## Why This Matters

The art world's relationship with AI has been contentious. On one side, generative AI tools have been used to displace working artists. On the other, artists are finding genuinely novel ways to use AI as a creative medium — not to replace human expression, but to explore territories that weren't accessible before.

The RCA festival represents the measured middle ground: institutions and practitioners taking AI seriously enough to engage with it critically, asking hard questions about:

- What does "human creativity" mean when AI can generate technically proficient outputs?
- How should creative education evolve to prepare students for AI-augmented practice?
- Where are the meaningful boundaries between tool use and creative replacement?

## The Somerset House Connection

Running concurrently in London, Somerset House's "Infinite Bodies" exhibition by choreographer Wayne McGregor explores the human body's meaning in the age of AI — specifically, how movement, data, and "physical intelligence" interact. It's a reminder that AI's impact on art extends far beyond visual media into dance, performance, and embodied experience.
