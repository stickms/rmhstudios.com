## The Metaphor at Vega's Core

Project Vega is, on its surface, a tower defense game about protecting a glowing core from waves of enemies. But the language we use tells a different story. The core is the *Memory Core*. The towers are neural constructs — synapses, suppressors, echoes. The enemies are named after mental health struggles. And the central mechanic — replaying the same timeline, learning from past mistakes, building better defenses each time — is a metaphor for **therapy itself**.

This wasn't accidental. It was the first design decision I made, before a single line of code was written. And it's the decision I've been most careful about throughout development.

## Why Mental Health?

I've dealt with anxiety for most of my adult life. The cyclical nature of it — the same intrusive thoughts replaying, the same triggers, the slow work of building coping mechanisms that gradually reduce their power — maps eerily well onto a time-loop game structure.

When I realized that connection, I knew Vega had to explore it. Not as a gimmick or an afterthought, but as the **thematic foundation** that every other design decision grows from.

Games have enormous potential to create empathy and understanding around mental health. *Celeste* did it with platforming as a metaphor for climbing through anxiety. *Hellblade* did it with psychosis rendered as gameplay. I want Vega to do it with the slow, iterative work of building defenses against your own destructive patterns.

## The Enemy Roster as Mental Health Archetypes

Each enemy type in Vega represents a different facet of mental health struggle. This isn't just flavor text — each enemy's **mechanical behavior** reflects the experience of dealing with that particular challenge.

### Anxiety Sprites

**Appearance:** Small, jittery, semi-transparent blobs that flicker erratically
**Behavior:** Fast movement with random direction changes. They dart unpredictably, making them hard to target.

Anxiety is fast, scattered, and hard to pin down. Anxiety Sprites capture that feeling — they're not individually dangerous, but they swarm in numbers that overwhelm your defenses if you're not prepared. They slip through gaps. They show up when you least expect them. And killing one doesn't mean much because three more are right behind it.

**Design intent:** Players should feel the scattershot, overwhelming quality of anxious thoughts. The solution isn't to target them individually — it's to build systems (Synapse clusters, Suppressor zones) that handle them in aggregate. Just like real anxiety management: you don't fight individual thoughts, you build frameworks that reduce their power.

### Depression Hulks

**Appearance:** Large, dark masses that move slowly and absorb enormous punishment
**Behavior:** Slow movement, huge HP pool, immune to the first 2 seconds of slow effects.

Depression doesn't dart around. It *settles*. Depression Hulks lumber toward the Memory Core with an inevitability that can feel crushing. They absorb shot after shot, seemingly unaffected. They resist crowd control — you can't just slow depression down; it adapts.

**Design intent:** Players should feel the weight and persistence of depression. A single Hulk can survive an entire corridor of Synapse fire. The answer is the Lobotomizer — targeted, high-intensity intervention. In the metaphor: depression often requires focused, specialized treatment rather than the general coping mechanisms that work for anxiety.

### Manic Spikes

**Appearance:** Bright, erratic, crackling energy forms that pulse with intensity
**Behavior:** Alternating between very fast bursts and brief pauses. Unpredictable speed.

Mania is all about oscillation — surges of energy followed by crashes. Manic Spikes capture this with their stop-start movement pattern. During their fast phase, they can outrun Suppressor zones entirely. During pauses, they're extremely vulnerable. The challenge is timing your defenses to exploit the vulnerability windows.

**Design intent:** The key to managing Manic Spikes is *patience and positioning*. Placing Suppressors at locations where Spikes tend to pause (which the player learns across loops) creates reliable kill zones. The loop system is the mechanic here: you learn the pattern, then build defenses around it. Progress through understanding.

## Treating the Theme with Respect

This is the section I've rewritten the most. Vega uses mental health as a gameplay metaphor, and that comes with responsibility. Here are my guiding principles:

### 1. No Trivializing

I never want a player to feel like the game is making light of their struggles. The enemy names are clinical, not mocking. "Anxiety Sprite" is descriptive, not dismissive. There are no jokes about mental health in the game's writing. The tone is serious and respectful — this is a clinical space, a place of treatment, not entertainment at someone's expense.

### 2. The Player Is the Healer

In Vega's metaphor, the player isn't fighting *against* a person with mental health issues. The player *is* that person, building internal defenses. The towers are coping mechanisms. The Memory Core is the self. The enemies are the intrusive patterns. The entire framing positions mental health management as **active, strategic work** — because it is.

### 3. Progress Is Non-Linear

The loop system reflects a truth about mental health recovery: it's not a straight line. You relive the same battles. You face the same triggers. But each time, you're a little better prepared. Your Ghost Protocols — the defenses you built last time — are still there, still helping. Recovery is cumulative even when it feels circular.

### 4. No "Cure" Narrative

Vega doesn't end with the enemies being permanently defeated. The game's structure (repeatable matches, escalating difficulty) reflects the reality that mental health management is ongoing. You get better at it. You build stronger defenses. But the enemies always return. That's not pessimistic — it's honest, and it respects the lived experience of people managing chronic mental health conditions.

### 5. Community Consultation

I've shared Vega's design documents with mental health professionals and people with lived experience of the conditions depicted. Their feedback has been invaluable. One therapist told me the loop mechanic is "remarkably close to how exposure therapy actually works." A friend with depression said seeing the Hulk slowly whittled down by sustained fire was "weirdly validating." These responses tell me we're on the right track.

## The Clinical Aesthetic as Emotional Language

Vega's visual language — the CRT scanlines, the medical grid, the terminal fonts — isn't just cool-looking. It serves the mental health theme. The game feels like a medical monitoring system, a clinical tool. You're not playing a *game* — you're operating a diagnostic interface for a mind under siege.

The glitch effects that intensify when Sanity drops aren't just visual flair. They represent the **destabilization of perception** that comes with acute mental distress. The screen tears. Colors shift. Text corrupts. It's unsettling by design, because that's what losing your mental footing feels like.

## The Responsibility of Representation

I won't pretend Vega is therapy. It's a game. It's entertainment. But entertainment has power — the power to normalize conversations, to create understanding, to make someone feel seen. If a player recognizes their own anxiety in the behavior of those jittery sprites and thinks "yeah, that's exactly what it feels like," then Vega has done something meaningful.

The bar for mental health representation in games is rising, and it should. I want Vega to meet that bar — not by being preachy, not by being a PSA disguised as a game, but by being a genuinely good tower defense experience that happens to speak honestly about the human mind.

Mental health isn't a theme I'll ever treat lightly. It's the heart of Project Vega, and I intend to honor it with every design decision.
