## Why We Build for the Browser

Every technology decision is, at its core, a decision about who gets access. When you build a console game, you're building for people who can afford a $500 machine. When you build a PC game, you're building for people with dedicated gaming hardware. When you build a mobile game, you're building for people willing to navigate an app store, surrender storage space, and accept a platform's terms.

When you build a browser game, you're building for everyone with an internet connection.

That's not a marketing slogan. It's a technical reality. The browser is the most universally deployed application runtime in human history. It runs on every operating system, every device class, every price point. A student on a school Chromebook, a commuter on a phone, a developer on a Linux workstation, a retiree on an iPad — they all have a browser, and they can all play the same game from the same URL.

**This is what RMH Studios means: a bet that the most democratic platform deserves the best games.**

## What We've Built

Over the course of our existence, we've shipped a portfolio that demonstrates the breadth of what browser games can be:

**RMHbox** is a multiplayer party game platform with 16+ minigames — proof that real-time, social, laughter-inducing gaming works in a browser tab. No downloads, no installs. Send a link, everyone plays.

**Signal Forge** is a roguelike deckbuilder with procedural encounters, persistent progression, and deep strategic mechanics — proof that complex, systems-driven single-player games don't need a native client.

**Synapse Storm** is a micro-challenge juggler that tests cognitive agility — proof that games requiring split-second timing and multitasking can run in a browser with the necessary precision.

**Temple of Joy** is a philosophical idle clicker — proof that games can be contemplative, weird, and meaningful without sacrificing accessibility.

**Echoes of the Spire** is a narrative extraction thriller — proof that storytelling and mystery work in the browser, with layered narrative design that rewards careful reading and investigation.

**Slice It!** is a rhythm game built on the Web Audio API — proof that audio-timing-critical gameplay is achievable with browser-native technology.

**Laundry Sort** is a physics-based sorting game — proof that tactile, satisfying physical interactions translate to the browser.

**Neon Driftway** is an endless racer with Three.js-powered 3D rendering — proof that visually ambitious, fast-paced 3D games run in the browser.

**RMHTube, RMH Type, and RMH Study** extend our platform beyond games into social entertainment, competitive productivity, and focused work — proof that the same technology stack that powers games can power shared digital experiences of all kinds.

**RMHdle and RMHConnections** are Discord-native daily games — proof that gaming habits can integrate into existing social platforms without requiring players to leave their communities.

None of these are tech demos. They're products people use, communities people join, experiences people remember. That's the difference between proving a concept and building a reality.

## The Philosophy Behind the Studio

Three convictions drive everything we do at RMH Studios:

### Honesty Is Non-Negotiable

We show real gameplay. We acknowledge real limitations. We don't fake screenshots, inflate metrics, or promise features we can't deliver. When we make mistakes — and we do — we tell our community what happened and what we're doing about it.

This isn't altruism. It's strategy. In an industry plagued by misleading marketing (E3 downgrades, launch-day disasters, broken promises), honesty is a competitive advantage. Players who trust us try our new games. Players who've been burned by other studios hesitate.

Trust compounds. Every honest interaction builds credibility for the next one. Every deception erodes it. We're playing the long game on trust, and the returns are real.

### Fact-Checking Matters

When we write about technology, we cite real benchmarks and real specifications. When we discuss industry trends, we reference real data and real events. When we make claims about our games' capabilities, they're verifiable — open a browser and check.

This extends to our blog, our community communications, and our social media. We don't share unverified information. We don't amplify hype without evidence. We don't claim credit for innovations we didn't make.

In an era of misinformation and manufactured narratives, a studio that insists on factual accuracy stands out. We'd rather say "we don't know" than say something wrong.

### The Web Is the Platform

We're not building for the browser because it's easy. It's not — browser game development has real constraints around performance, storage, offline access, and hardware integration. We're building for the browser because its advantages — universal access, zero friction, cross-platform reach, open distribution — align with our values.

The web was designed to be open. Anyone can create a website. Anyone can visit one. No gatekeeper decides what gets published. No platform holder takes a 30% cut for the privilege of existing. The web is the closest thing to a level playing field that technology has produced.

Building games on this platform isn't just a technical choice. It's a statement about who games are for.

## The Technical Innovations

Our technical approach has evolved through hard-won experience:

**Real-time multiplayer over WebSocket** powers RMHbox, RMH Type, and RMHTube. We've built infrastructure for latency compensation, state synchronization, connection recovery, and room management that handles the messy realities of consumer internet connections.

**Progressive loading and code splitting** through Next.js ensure that our games load in seconds, not minutes. First meaningful interaction happens almost immediately; additional content streams in the background.

**Web Audio API integration** gives our games sophisticated sound design — spatial audio, dynamic music, precise rhythmic timing — using browser-native capabilities.

**Three.js 3D rendering** powers our visually ambitious games, with performance optimizations tuned through months of profiling on real devices.

**Discord bot integration** for RMHdle and RMHConnections demonstrates that browser gaming technology extends beyond the browser itself, meeting players where they already spend time.

These aren't revolutionary technologies individually. The innovation is in combining them into cohesive, polished experiences that challenge the assumption that browser games are inherently inferior to native ones.

## The Case for Web Gaming

Here's the argument, laid out plainly:

**The web reaches more people than any other platform.** Billions of devices run browsers. No console, no PC gaming ecosystem, no app store comes close to that reach.

**The web has zero distribution friction.** A URL is the lowest-friction distribution mechanism ever invented. Click and play. No download, no install, no account required for first play.

**The web is open.** No gatekeeper. No platform tax. No approval process. Developers own their distribution, their player relationships, and their revenue.

**The web's technology is now sufficient.** WebGPU, WASM, Web Audio API, WebSocket, WebRTC, WebXR — the browser's capability set supports rich, real-time, visually impressive, socially connected gaming experiences.

**The web is getting better, fast.** Every browser release brings performance improvements, new APIs, and better tooling. The gap between browser and native narrows with every update.

The question isn't whether browser games *can* rival native experiences. They can — we've demonstrated it. The question is whether developers and players will embrace the browser as a legitimate gaming platform. We believe the answer is yes, and we're building the evidence.

## What Comes Next

RMH Studios is not a finished project. It's an ongoing argument — demonstrated through games, tools, and community — that the web deserves better games and that better games deserve the web's reach.

In 2026 and beyond, we're expanding our game catalog, building platform infrastructure for accounts and cross-game progression, adopting WebGPU for next-generation visual experiences, and growing a community that shares our conviction that games should be accessible to everyone.

We don't know exactly where this path leads. We know the direction: toward a future where the best games aren't locked behind expensive hardware, walled gardens, or multi-gigabyte downloads. A future where you click a link and enter a world.
