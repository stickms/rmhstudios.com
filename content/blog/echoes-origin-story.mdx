## The Spark That Started It All

Every game starts with a feeling. For me, it was that electric rush of clearing a room in **Hades** at two in the morning, heart pounding, fingers sore, immediately hitting "one more run." I'd been chasing that feeling for years — through **Dead Cells**, through **Enter the Gungeon**, through countless roguelikes that consumed my weekends and taught me what good game design tastes like. At some point, the question shifted from "what should I play next?" to "what if I built something that gave me that same feeling?"

That's the honest origin of **Echoes of the Spire**. Not a business plan. Not a market analysis. Just a developer who loved dungeon crawlers so much that he had to make one.

## Why a Dungeon Crawler?

I considered a lot of genres early on. I've always been drawn to systems-heavy games — the kind where you can feel the gears turning underneath the surface. RPGs, strategy games, sim builders. But roguelike dungeon crawlers hit a sweet spot that nothing else does: **every run is a contained story**. You start with nothing, build power, make decisions, and either triumph or die trying. There's a narrative arc baked into the mechanics themselves.

What sealed the deal was thinking about the web as a platform. Browser games have come an incredible distance, but they still carry a stigma — people expect simple, throwaway experiences. I wanted to challenge that. I wanted someone to open a browser tab and find something that genuinely surprised them. A dungeon crawler with real depth, real class systems, real progression — running right there in Chrome.

## Early Prototyping: The Ugly Phase

The first prototype of Echoes was *ugly*. I'm talking programmer-art rectangles on a black canvas. The player was a white square. Enemies were red squares. Walls were gray squares. It was a world of squares, and it was beautiful — to me, at least.

But even in that primitive state, something clicked. I had basic movement, collision detection, and a simple attack. You could walk into a room, swing at enemies, and watch them disappear. The feedback loop was there, buried under layers of placeholder everything.

```typescript
// The very first "combat" system - embarrassingly simple
function attack(player: Entity, enemies: Entity[]) {
  const range = 48;
  enemies.forEach(enemy => {
    const dist = distance(player.pos, enemy.pos);
    if (dist < range) {
      enemy.hp -= player.damage;
    }
  });
}
```

I spent about two weeks in this phase, just proving to myself that the core loop could work in a browser. Could Canvas handle dozens of entities updating at 60fps? Could I build pathfinding that didn't chug? Could combat feel responsive with keyboard and mouse input processed through JavaScript event handlers?

The answer to all of those was yes — with caveats. Performance was fine for basic scenarios but fell apart with too many particles. Pathfinding worked but needed optimization. Combat was responsive but lacked *juice*. Each of those caveats became a project unto itself, and honestly, solving them has been the most rewarding part of this entire journey.

## The Inspirations, Dissected

I want to be transparent about what I borrowed and what I tried to make my own.

From **Hades**, I took the philosophy of *narrative through repetition*. Supergiant proved that dying and restarting doesn't have to feel punishing — it can feel like turning the page. I wanted Echoes to have that same sense of forward momentum even in failure. Every run teaches you something. Every death sets up the next attempt.

From **Dead Cells**, I studied the *movement feel*. Motion Twin made a game where just running and jumping feels incredible before you ever swing a weapon. That tactile responsiveness became a north star for me. If the basic movement doesn't feel good, nothing else matters.

From **Enter the Gungeon**, I learned about *variety as a design pillar*. Dodge Roll packed that game with hundreds of guns, items, and synergies. While I can't match that volume as a solo dev, the principle stuck: each run should feel distinct. The player should encounter combinations they've never seen before.

### Making It My Own

Where Echoes diverges from these inspirations is in its **class system** and its **dual rendering modes**. Most roguelikes give you a character and let items define your build. I wanted the opposite — your class choice should fundamentally change how you play, with upgrades enhancing your chosen identity rather than replacing it. And the 2D/3D toggle? That was born from a technical experiment that turned into a genuine feature. But those are stories for future posts.

## The Tech Stack Decision

Choosing to build on **Next.js with TypeScript** was deliberate. I wanted Echoes to live on the web as a first-class citizen, not a ported afterthought. The game itself runs on **HTML5 Canvas** for 2D mode and a custom **Three.js-inspired pipeline** for 3D. State management goes through **Zustand**, which has been a revelation — reactive state without the boilerplate of Redux, perfectly suited for game state that changes sixty times a second.

The web platform introduces real constraints. There's no direct GPU access in the way a native engine provides. Memory management is at the mercy of garbage collection. Audio has browser-specific quirks that will drive you slowly insane. But constraints breed creativity. Every limitation I've hit has forced me to find a solution that's often more elegant than what I would have built with unlimited resources.
