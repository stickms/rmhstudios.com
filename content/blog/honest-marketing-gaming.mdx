## The Trust Deficit

The gaming industry has a marketing credibility problem, and it's been building for over a decade.

In June 2012, Ubisoft showed Watch Dogs at E3 with visuals that stunned audiences — dynamic lighting, detailed environments, lifelike animations. When the game shipped in May 2014, players immediately noticed the downgrade. The retail version looked markedly worse than the E3 demo. Modders later discovered that the E3-quality graphical settings were still in the game files, disabled for the retail release. Ubisoft never fully explained why.

This wasn't an isolated incident. The pattern repeated: impressive vertical slice at a trade show, diminished product at launch. Other Ubisoft titles like The Division saw similar discrepancies. The practice became so common that "E3 downgrade" entered the gaming vocabulary as a specific form of deception.

More recently, **The Lord of the Rings: Gollum** (May 2023) launched to devastating reviews after a marketing campaign that promised a meaningful narrative experience. The game was visually poor, mechanically broken, and bore little resemblance to its promotional materials. Developer Daedalic Entertainment apologized, but the damage was done — to players who bought the game based on marketing promises and to the studio's reputation.

These aren't just PR failures. They're breaches of trust. And in an industry where players pre-order and back projects based on marketing materials, trust is the currency that makes everything else work.

## The Economics of Dishonesty

Misleading marketing works in the short term. An impressive E3 trailer generates pre-orders, media coverage, and wishlist additions. A polished screenshot gets clicks. A carefully staged "gameplay" video builds hype.

But the economics of dishonesty are deteriorating rapidly:

**Social media amplifies backlash.** When No Man's Sky launched in August 2016 with missing features that had been implied in marketing, the backlash was immediate and global. Every unfulfilled promise was catalogued, compared to interviews and trailers, and amplified across Reddit, Twitter, and YouTube. The game eventually recovered through years of free updates — a redemption arc that cost Hello Games enormous time and resources.

**Refund policies have teeth.** Steam's refund policy (less than two hours of playtime, within 14 days of purchase) means that if your game doesn't match its marketing, players can and do get their money back. Cyberpunk 2077's troubled December 2020 launch led to an unprecedented move: Sony removed the game from the PlayStation Store entirely and offered full refunds.

**Community memory is long.** Players remember. Studios that develop reputations for misleading marketing — regardless of the quality of individual games — face an uphill battle with every subsequent release. Trust, once broken, takes years to rebuild.

## Studios That Get It Right

Not everyone in the industry optimizes for deception. Some studios have built their brands on the opposite approach:

**Larian Studios** shipped Baldur's Gate 3 in August 2023 after three years of early access where players could see the actual game evolve in real time. No hidden builds, no carefully curated demos — the game players tested was the game players bought. The result: universal acclaim, massive commercial success, and a reputation for integrity that will benefit everything Larian builds next.

**FromSoftware** consistently ships games that match or exceed their marketing promises. Elden Ring's trailers showed actual gameplay. The launch product delivered. FromSoftware has built an audience that trusts them implicitly, and that trust translates directly to day-one sales.

**Supergiant Games** has maintained a spotless reputation across Bastion, Transistor, Pyre, Hades, and Hades II by showing real gameplay, setting honest expectations, and delivering polished products. Their marketing is understated, their games speak for themselves, and their audience grows with every release.

The common thread: **under-promise and over-deliver.** Show real gameplay. Be transparent about the state of development. Let the product's quality do the marketing.

## RMH Studios' Marketing Principles

We're a small studio. We don't have the budget for E3 demos or cinematic trailers. But even if we did, we'd follow these principles:

### Show Real Gameplay, Always

Every screenshot, every video, every GIF we share shows the actual game running in an actual browser. No mock-ups. No "target renders." No Photoshop touch-ups. If a game looks rough, we either wait until it looks better to share it, or we share it with the context that it's work in progress.

This is easier for browser games than for traditional games, because anyone can verify our claims by opening a URL. Our marketing materials are never more than one click away from the real product.

### Be Transparent About Development State

When we share something that's in progress, we say so explicitly. "This is a prototype." "This feature is in testing." "This design might change." Players are more forgiving of rough edges when they know what they're looking at. What they don't forgive is being misled about the state of a product.

### Never Fabricate Screenshots

This seems obvious, but the gaming industry regularly publishes screenshots with enhanced lighting, higher-resolution textures, removed UI elements, or staged compositions that don't represent actual gameplay. We don't do this. Our screenshots are captured from live games at the resolution and quality our players actually experience.

### Acknowledge Limitations Honestly

Our games run in browsers. They have performance constraints. They don't look like Unreal Engine 5 showcases. We don't pretend otherwise. When we discuss our games' technical capabilities, we're specific about what the technology can and can't do.

This honesty isn't a marketing disadvantage — it's a credibility advantage. Players who try our games knowing what to expect are pleasantly surprised. Players who arrive with inflated expectations are disappointed. We'd rather have the former.

### Don't Manufacture Urgency

We don't use countdown timers, limited-time availability, or artificial scarcity to pressure players into engagement. Our games are free and available whenever you want them. FOMO-driven marketing might boost short-term metrics, but it poisons the relationship between studio and player.

## The Content Creator Relationship

An honest marketing approach changes how you work with content creators — streamers, YouTubers, and journalists.

We don't offer exclusive early access in exchange for positive coverage. We don't require creators to follow a script or avoid discussing bugs. When someone plays our games on stream, we want their honest reaction. If they encounter a bug, that's feedback we need. If they don't enjoy a game, that's information we can learn from.

The best marketing our games have received has come from creators who discovered them organically and shared genuine enthusiasm. You can't buy that kind of authenticity, and trying to manufacture it always backfires.

## Why This Matters More Than Ever

The gaming industry in 2026 faces a trust crisis driven by multiple converging factors:

**AI-generated marketing content** makes it trivially easy to produce screenshots, trailers, and promotional materials that don't represent any real product. As AI tools proliferate, players will become increasingly skeptical of all marketing materials. Studios that can demonstrate authenticity — "this is a real screenshot from a real game you can play right now" — will have a structural advantage.

**The subscription and live-service model** means players commit to ongoing relationships with games and studios, not one-time purchases. Relationships require trust. A studio that deceives players at the point of sale poisons a relationship they need to maintain for months or years.

**Players have more choices than ever.** Steam alone has over 70,000 games. The browser adds millions more. Player attention is the scarcest resource in gaming. Earning and keeping that attention requires trust, and trust requires honesty.

## The Competitive Advantage of Truth

Here's the counterintuitive reality: **honest marketing is a competitive advantage, not a sacrifice.**

When we show real gameplay and a player tries the game, their experience matches their expectations. Satisfaction is the gap between expectation and reality — and honest marketing narrows that gap from the positive side.

When we acknowledge a game's limitations, players who choose to play do so with informed consent. Their feedback is more useful because it's about the actual game, not about unmet expectations.

When we're transparent about development state, our community becomes collaborative rather than adversarial. They're partners in development, not customers waiting to be disappointed.

We're not honest because it's noble. We're honest because it works. In a sea of manufactured hype and broken promises, authenticity is the rarest and most valuable marketing asset a studio can have.

The games speak for themselves. We just make sure no one expects them to say something they don't.
