# Designing Signal Forge: Rhythm Meets Roguelike

Signal Forge emerged from a simple observation: **roguelike deckbuilders are the best framework for exploring new game mechanics**. Games like Slay the Spire proved players love:

- Discovering card synergies
- Making meaningful choices under pressure
- The story of one amazing run
- High replayability with emergent strategy

But what if we added a timing/rhythm element? What if your deck had to *execute correctly*, not just exist optimally?

## The Evolution: From Idea to MVP

### Phase 1: "What If You Had to Perform Your Deck?"

Initial concept: A game where you play cards from your hand in a specific sequence. If you misorder them, you get punished. This became our core insight: **imperfect execution creates interesting decisions**.

### Phase 2: "What About Musical Patterns?"

We'd been listening to a lot of synthwave and lo-fi beats. The idea hit: what if matching patterns *feels like conducting an orchestra*? Instead of "clicking a sequence," you're arranging your cards into a harmonic pattern.

This is where *Pulse, Sine, Saw, and Noise* emerged. Not arbitrary names—each waveform has mechanical meaning:

- **Pulse**: Direct, damage-focused. Like a metronome, reliable
- **Sine**: Smooth, defensive, scaling. Like a sustained note
- **Saw**: Chaotic, draw-focused. Like a sawtooth wave building
- **Noise**: Random, chaotic, high-risk. Like white noise interference

### Phase 3: "What Are the Failure States?"

Early playtesting revealed:
- If players always knew the pattern, it became rote (boring)
- If sequences were random, they felt unfair (frustrating)
- But if enemies *announced their intent and the player responded*, it felt like call-and-response music

This is why every enemy turn shows their target sequence. It rewards attention and planning.

## Key Design Decisions

### Decision 1: Why Tempo Decay?

Early builds had infinite tempo scaling. A burst bonus at 10 tempo was insane—you'd one-shot bosses.

**Solution**: Tempo naturally decays unless reinforced by successful matches. This creates rhythm:
- You spike damage on a successful sequence
- Tempo decays back down
- You need to manage the timing of your sequences around the grace period

It's like a *drummer fighting against entropy*, trying to maintain rhythm against the chaos of Static pressure.

### Decision 2: Why Static Pressure?

Without a risk system, the "Tempo Spam" strategy would dominate: play infinite cheap cards, build infinite tempo, win.

Static adds **deck variety pressure**. If you only run Pulse cards, playing multiples corrupts your signal. You're incentivized to include cards of different types even if they're suboptimal.

This prevents degenerate strategies while rewarding creative deckbuilding.

### Decision 3: Why Not Direct Card Selection?

In Slay the Spire, you always get offered choices when leveling up or in shops. We tried this in early Signal Forge.

**Problem**: With 40 cards, the choice paralysis was real (Analysis paralysis). Players would spend minutes debating a single card replacement.

**Solution**: Randomized shop pools with 4-card offerings. You can't optimize perfectly—you have to adapt. This returns agency to *player strategy*, not *menu optimization*.

### Decision 4: Why Seeded Runs?

We noticed that roguelikes create emergent narratives. Your "story" is the run: "I had an insane synergy with Glitch Catalyst and two copies of Pattern Recognition, and I won on floor 2 against odds."

Seeded runs (same seed = same enemy/shop sequence) enable:
- **Replayability**: "What if I'd made a different choice there?"
- **Community**: Players can share seed numbers and discuss strategies
- **Fairness**: Leaderboards can be verified (same seed = same game)

## Balance Philosophy

Signal Forge uses **asymmetric balance**. We don't want all decks equally powerful. Instead:

- **Beginner Decks** (~63% win rate floor 1): Shield Wall is stable, forgiving, straightforward
- **Expert Decks** (~58% win rate floor 1): Glitch Abuse requires perfect relics, risky early

The leaderboard will show which strategies *skilled players* can push furthest. But casual players can absolutely beat floor 1 with Shield Wall. The game scales from accessible to expert.

## The Enemy Design Philosophy

Each enemy archetype teaches players something:

| Enemy Type | Lesson | Counters |
|--|--|--|
| Disruptor Drone | Static is a real threat; you can't ignore it | Shield Wall or Static removal |
| Static Leech | Some threats scale *with* your corruption | Tempo or burst strategies |
| Rhythm Overlord | Simple patterns > complexity sometimes | Glitch Abuse (predictable) |
| The Void | Adaptation is essential; nothing works forever | Diversified decks |

Bosses on floor 3 change patterns mid-combat, forcing players to reorganize their entire strategy. It's hard. But that's the point.

## Why Canvas, Not WebGL?

Signal Forge is built on HTML5 Canvas, rendering everything in 2D. This was a deliberate choice:

- **Accessibility**: Works on 10-year-old laptops
- **Performance**: No GPU required
- **Portability**: Browser-native, no plugins
- **Simplicity**: Focused development on mechanics, not visuals

We learned this from Laundry Sort's early WebGL builds—3D on browser is amazing, but when it crashes, it crashes hard.

## What's Next?

We're collecting player data on:

1. **Win Rate Psychology**: Does 60% feel too easy? 35% feel fair? Should we adjust?
2. **Card Popularity**: Which cards do players always ban from their deck? That's a signal to rebalance
3. **Relic Discovery**: Are players finding hidden synergies we didn't anticipate? (We hope so!)
4. **Feedback Loops**: Is the sequence matching satisfying or gimmicky in practice?

The leaderboard and telemetry will guide post-launch balance patches.

## The Roguelike Renaissance

We're in an amazing era for roguelikes:
- Slay the Spire (2017): Deckbuilder roguelike becomes phenomenon
- Hades (2020): Action roguelike with story
- Balatro (2024): Poker roguelike explodes on Steam

Each proves the same principle: **novelty + depth + iteration = magic**. Signal Forge adds sequence matching. It might not stick. But we're excited to find out.

If you play Signal Forge, thank you. Your runs generate our next design decisions.
