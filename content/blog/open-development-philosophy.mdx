## The Black Box Problem

The traditional model of game development is a black box. A studio announces a game, goes silent for two to four years, occasionally drops a carefully produced trailer, and then ships (or doesn't). Players are expected to sustain interest based on marketing materials alone, with no visibility into the actual state of development.

This model made sense when game development was a linear process with a clear endpoint: gold master, print discs, ship to retail. But in the era of live-service games, early access, and continuous updates, the black box model creates a trust gap that serves no one well.

At RMH Studios, we develop in the open. Our dev blog, our Discord, our public roadmaps — they're not afterthoughts or marketing channels. They're integral to how we build games.

## Studios That Got Open Development Right

We're not the first studio to embrace open development, and we've learned from those who did it well.

### Larian Studios and Baldur's Gate 3

**Larian Studios** launched Baldur's Gate 3 into early access on October 6, 2020, nearly three years before its full release in August 2023. During that period, Larian shipped major content updates called "Panels from Hell," hosted community discussions, iterated on mechanics based on player feedback, and fundamentally reshaped parts of the game in response to how players actually used the systems.

The result was one of the most critically acclaimed RPGs of all time. BG3 won Game of the Year at The Game Awards 2023 and sold over 10 million copies in its first months. But what's often overlooked is how much the open development period contributed to that quality. Larian didn't just use early access for bug reports — they used it to validate design decisions, identify pain points, and build a community of invested players who became the game's most powerful advocates at launch.

### ConcernedApe and Stardew Valley

**Eric "ConcernedApe" Barone** took a different approach to open development with Stardew Valley. Rather than early access, he maintained a transparent development blog and active community engagement throughout the game's solo development. After launch in February 2016, he continued this pattern with detailed patch notes, honest timelines for the multiplayer update, and open communication about the 1.5 content update.

When Barone announced he was working on Haunted Chocolatier, he shared early footage and design thinking long before the game was anywhere near release. The trust he'd built through years of transparent communication meant that the announcement generated genuine excitement rather than skepticism.

### The Contrast: Studios That Go Silent

The value of open development becomes clearest when you look at studios that took the opposite approach. **Bethesda's** near-total silence between Starfield's announcement in 2018 and its gameplay reveal in 2022 generated years of speculation, unrealistic expectations, and eventual disappointment when the game launched to mixed reception. **BioWare's** opaque development of Anthem left players and even internal developers unsure of what the game was supposed to be, contributing to one of the most high-profile failures of 2019.

Silence doesn't create mystery anymore. It creates suspicion.

## What Open Development Looks Like at RMH

Our open development process has several concrete components:

### Public Roadmaps

For each active game, we maintain a roadmap that shows what we're working on, what's next, and what's on the backburner. These roadmaps are honest — items get moved, reprioritized, or cut, and we explain why when that happens. A roadmap that only ever adds features and never removes them isn't a roadmap; it's a wish list.

### Dev Blog and Technical Writing

This blog is part of our open development practice. We write about our technical decisions, our design philosophy, and the challenges we face. Not every post will be exciting, and that's the point — open development means sharing the mundane alongside the milestones.

### Discord as a Development Channel

Our Discord server isn't just a community hang-out space — it's where we share work-in-progress screenshots, solicit feedback on mechanics, and sometimes debug issues in real time with players who are experiencing them. When we were iterating on RMHbox's minigame selection system, community feedback directly influenced the rotation mechanics we shipped.

### Honest Timelines

When someone asks "when is X feature shipping?" we give the most honest answer we can, even when that answer is "we don't know yet" or "it's been delayed because we underestimated the complexity." We've found that players respect uncertainty far more than they respect confident timelines that turn out to be wrong.

## The Risks of Open Development

We'd be dishonest if we didn't acknowledge that open development carries real risks.

**Feature expectations can calcify.** When you share a work-in-progress feature, some players treat it as a promise. Cutting or significantly changing a publicly discussed feature can generate backlash, even when the change improves the game.

**Competitors can see your roadmap.** In theory, a competitor could watch our public development and clone features. In practice, ideas are abundant and execution is everything — we're more worried about building the best possible games than about someone copying our roadmap items.

**The noise-to-signal ratio increases.** Not all feedback is useful, and not all community discussion is constructive. Open development requires a thick skin and the judgment to distinguish between actionable feedback and loud opinions.

We accept these risks because the benefits — better games, stronger community relationships, and a development process that's accountable to the people who actually play our games — far outweigh them.

## Why This Matters for Browser Games

Open development is especially powerful for browser games because the feedback loop is so tight. When we push an update, players can experience it immediately — no patch download, no Steam update queue. That means we can share a change, get feedback within hours, and iterate the same day.

This rapid cycle turns open development from a communication strategy into a genuine development methodology. Our players aren't just an audience — they're part of the process.

**We build in the open because it makes our games better. Full stop.** The trust, the feedback, and the accountability that come from transparency aren't nice-to-haves. For a studio committed to honesty, they're the whole point.
