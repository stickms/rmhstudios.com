## The Design Philosophy

When I started designing towers for Project Vega, I set myself a constraint: **four towers, maximum**. Not six, not twelve, not a sprawling tech tree. Four. Each one had to fill a distinct strategic role, and more importantly, each had to interact meaningfully with the Ghost Protocol system across time loops.

The reasoning is simple. In a game where your towers compound across three loops, complexity multiplies fast. Four tower types across three loops means up to twelve layers of overlapping strategy. That's already deep enough to drown in. Adding more types would create noise, not depth.

I looked at games like *Into the Breach* — three mechs, infinite strategic depth — and knew that constraint would be my friend.

## Synapse: The Workhorse

**Role:** Rapid-fire DPS
**Inspiration:** Neural synapses firing in rapid succession

The Synapse tower is your bread and butter. It fires fast, deals modest damage per shot, and excels at shredding groups of low-HP enemies. It's the first tower most players will place, and it scales well as a Ghost Protocol because its sustained DPS remains valuable in later loops.

### Stats at a Glance

| Level | Fire Rate | Damage | Range |
|-------|-----------|--------|-------|
| 1     | 4/sec     | 8      | 3 tiles |
| 2     | 6/sec     | 12     | 3.5 tiles |
| 3     | 8/sec     | 18     | 4 tiles |

The Synapse was the easiest tower to design but the hardest to balance. Because it's the "default" tower, it needs to be strong enough to feel good but not so strong that it obsoletes the other three. I went through about a dozen iterations on the fire rate before settling on the current values.

**Ghost Protocol Interaction:** Synapse ghosts are excellent area denial. A cluster of Loop 1 Synapses continues providing sustained damage in later loops, freeing the player to invest Focus in more specialized towers.

## Suppressor: The Controller

**Role:** Slow, crowd control, area denial
**Inspiration:** Suppressant medication, neural inhibitors

The Suppressor doesn't deal much damage — instead, it applies a stacking slow effect to all enemies in its radius. One Suppressor is manageable. Three Suppressors overlapping turns a corridor into quicksand.

```typescript
interface SuppressorEffect {
  slowPercent: number;     // 20% per stack
  maxStacks: number;       // 5 stacks = 100% slow (frozen)
  stackDuration: number;   // 2 seconds per stack
  auraRadius: number;      // 2.5 tiles
}
```

The stacking mechanic is crucial. A single Suppressor slows by 20%. But if you layer Suppressors across loops — one from Loop 1, one from Loop 2, one placed in Loop 3 — enemies in that zone are virtually frozen. This makes the Suppressor the **best tower for cross-loop synergy**.

**Design Challenge:** Early playtests revealed that Suppressors were either useless (one wasn't enough) or broken (three turned the game into a slideshow). The stack cap at 5 and the 2-second decay timer were the fix — you can achieve maximum slow, but only briefly and with significant investment.

**Ghost Protocol Interaction:** Suppressor ghosts are the backbone of loop-aware strategy. Placing Suppressors in chokepoints during Loop 1 means those chokepoints are pre-slowed for Loops 2 and 3, allowing you to focus firepower there.

## Lobotomizer: The Assassin

**Role:** High single-target damage, long range, slow fire rate
**Inspiration:** Precision surgical instruments, targeted intervention

The Lobotomizer is Vega's sniper. It fires once every 3 seconds but hits like a truck. Its specialty is eliminating high-priority targets — Depression Hulks, boss enemies, anything that your Synapses can't chew through fast enough.

### The Priority Targeting System

Unlike other towers that target the nearest enemy, the Lobotomizer uses a **threat-priority targeting system**:

```typescript
const selectTarget = (enemies: Enemy[], range: number): Enemy | null => {
  const inRange = enemies.filter(e => distance(tower.pos, e.pos) <= range);
  return inRange.sort((a, b) => {
    // Priority: highest HP first, then closest to core
    const hpDiff = b.currentHP - a.currentHP;
    if (Math.abs(hpDiff) > 50) return hpDiff;
    return a.distanceToCore - b.distanceToCore;
  })[0] ?? null;
};
```

It preferentially targets the beefiest enemy in range, then falls back to whoever is closest to the Memory Core. This makes it feel *smart* — players quickly learn to trust the Lobotomizer's judgment.

**Ghost Protocol Interaction:** Lobotomizer ghosts are insurance policies. A strategically placed Loop 1 Lobotomizer will continue sniping high-value targets in later loops, providing a safety net against enemies that slip through your newer defenses.

## Echo: The Force Multiplier

**Role:** Buffer, aura support, synergy enabler
**Inspiration:** Neural echo patterns, resonance feedback

The Echo tower doesn't attack at all. Instead, it emits an **aura that enhances all nearby towers** — including Ghost Protocol towers from previous loops. This makes the Echo the most strategically interesting tower in the game and the one that rewards forward-thinking players the most.

### Aura Effects by Level

- **Level 1:** +15% fire rate to towers within 2 tiles
- **Level 2:** +25% fire rate, +10% damage within 2.5 tiles
- **Level 3:** +35% fire rate, +20% damage, +0.5 tile range within 3 tiles

The Echo's power scales with the density of towers around it. In Loop 1, you might place it near two Synapses for a modest boost. But by Loop 3, that same Echo (now a ghost) is potentially buffing six or seven towers from across all three loops. **The Echo gets stronger as the game progresses, inversely to when it was placed.**

This creates a beautiful design tension: placing an Echo early (Loop 1) maximizes its long-term value because it buffs ghost towers in subsequent loops. But in Loop 1, you have fewer towers to buff, so the immediate payoff is weak. Do you invest in future power or survive the present?

**Ghost Protocol Interaction:** Echo ghosts are the glue that holds multi-loop strategies together. A well-placed Loop 1 Echo can turn a mediocre Loop 3 defense grid into an impenetrable fortress.

## Balancing Across Loops

The hardest part of tower design wasn't any individual tower — it was balancing them **across the loop system**. Each tower needed to be viable as a fresh placement *and* as a Ghost Protocol. If any tower was strictly better as a ghost (place it early, forget it), players would solve the game by front-loading those towers in Loop 1.

My solution was to make each tower scale differently across loops:

- **Synapse:** Consistent value. Good in any loop, good as a ghost.
- **Suppressor:** Increasing value. Better with multiple stacking across loops.
- **Lobotomizer:** Spike value. Best against specific threats that appear in later waves.
- **Echo:** Compounding value. Gets exponentially better with more surrounding towers.

This ensures that the optimal strategy isn't "spam one tower type in Loop 1." You need a mix across all three loops, and the *composition* of that mix is where the deep strategy lives.

## What I Learned

Designing four towers taught me more about game balance than any GDC talk ever could. The key insight: **constraints create depth**. With only four options, every choice carries enormous weight. Players can't hide behind a bloated roster — they have to understand each tower deeply and deploy them with intention.
