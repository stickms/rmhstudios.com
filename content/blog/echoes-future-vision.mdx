## Looking Forward

Ten months of development. Thousands of lines of code. Hundreds of hours of playtesting. Echoes of the Spire has grown from a prototype made of white squares into something I'm genuinely proud of. But the truth is, we're just getting started. The Spire has so many more floors to climb, and I want to share where we're heading.

This post is a transparent look at the roadmap — what's planned, what's dreamed, and what I hope Echoes becomes over the next year and beyond. Some of these features are in active development. Others are aspirational goals that depend on community growth and resources. I'm sharing all of it because I believe in building games in the open.

## New Classes: Expanding the Roster

The current three classes — Vanguard, Spectre, and Conduit — represent core archetypes: tank, assassin, and mage. But the class system was designed for expansion, and I have two new classes in various stages of development.

### The Warden

The Warden is a **summoner/support hybrid**. Instead of dealing damage directly, the Warden deploys autonomous constructs — turrets, barriers, healing drones — that fight and support on their behalf. The core verb set is *Deploy, Command, Salvage*. Playing Warden should feel like conducting an orchestra of machines while you position yourself safely behind your creations.

This class introduces a fundamentally new resource: **construct slots**. You can have a limited number of constructs active at once, and deploying a new one when you're at capacity requires salvaging an existing one. The moment-to-moment gameplay becomes a spatial puzzle — where do I place my turret for maximum coverage? Should I salvage my barrier to make room for a healing drone?

### The Echo

The Echo is the most experimental class concept. It's a **time-manipulation class** whose abilities interact with past actions. The core verb set is *Record, Replay, Rewind*. The Echo can record a 3-second window of their actions, then replay that recording as a phantom that repeats those exact movements and attacks. They can also rewind their own position to where they were moments ago — a creative dodge that rewards planning.

This class is technically challenging because it requires the game to buffer recent player actions and replay them with correct timing and collision. The implementation involves recording input states, positions, and ability activations into a circular buffer, then spawning a ghost entity that reads from that buffer.

```typescript
interface EchoRecording {
  frames: RecordedFrame[];
  duration: number;
  startPosition: Vec2;
}

interface RecordedFrame {
  timestamp: number;
  position: Vec2;
  facing: Direction;
  actions: PlayerAction[];
}

function replayRecording(recording: EchoRecording, currentTime: number): GhostState {
  const elapsed = currentTime - recording.startTime;
  const frameIndex = Math.floor((elapsed / recording.duration) * recording.frames.length);
  return recording.frames[Math.min(frameIndex, recording.frames.length - 1)];
}
```

I'm actively prototyping the Echo, and the early results are fascinating. Watching your ghost double execute a perfect attack pattern that you just performed creates a uniquely satisfying loop. But the complexity is real — balancing a class that effectively doubles the player's output, debugging phantom collision edge cases, and communicating the recording/replay state clearly to the player are all ongoing challenges.

## New Dungeon Biomes

Currently, the Spire's aesthetic is consistent throughout: dark purple stone, sci-fi energy streams, crystalline outcroppings. It works, but visual variety across a multi-floor run would dramatically improve the sense of journey and progression.

I'm designing **three new biome themes** that will replace the monotone dungeon with distinct visual and mechanical zones:

### The Datastream (Floors 1-5)

A digital space with grid-line aesthetics, floating data fragments, and holographic enemies. This biome introduces the game's world and mechanics with a clean, readable visual language. Environmental hazards include data corruption zones that scramble your controls temporarily and firewall barriers that must be hacked (solved via brief pattern-matching minigames) to pass.

### The Overgrowth (Floors 6-10)

Organic matter has reclaimed this section of the Spire. Bioluminescent plants cast eerie green-blue light. Vine walls are destructible, opening shortcuts. Spore clouds create vision-obscuring zones. The aesthetic shift from digital to organic feels like descending into something alive and hostile.

### The Core (Floors 11-15)

The deepest section exposes the Spire's true nature — raw energy, unstable geometry, and reality-warping effects. Rooms occasionally rotate or reconfigure while you're in them. Gravity shifts create sections where the player walks on walls or ceilings. The visual language goes full sci-fi: plasma corridors, energy cascades, impossible architecture.

Each biome isn't just a reskin — it introduces **unique enemy types, environmental mechanics, and room templates** that create distinct gameplay identities for each section of a run.

## The Multiplayer Dream

This is the big one. The feature that keeps me up at night sketching designs and architectures. **Co-op multiplayer** for Echoes of the Spire.

Imagine exploring the Spire with a friend. A Vanguard holding the frontline while a Conduit builds charge safely behind them. A Spectre flanking enemies that are distracted by a Warden's turrets. The class system was practically designed for cooperative play — each class covers different roles, and their abilities synergize naturally.

### Technical Reality

Browser-based multiplayer is non-trivial. I've been researching WebSocket and WebRTC approaches, and I currently have a **Socket.IO-based real-time server** running for another project on this site. Extending that infrastructure to handle Echoes' game state synchronization is the foundation of the multiplayer plan.

The biggest challenges are:

**State synchronization.** Two players need to see the same dungeon, the same enemies, and each other's actions with minimal latency. I'm leaning toward an **authoritative server** model where the server runs the game simulation and clients send inputs and receive state updates. This prevents cheating and ensures consistency at the cost of input latency.

**Scaling the dungeon.** Rooms designed for one player need to scale for two. More enemies, more health, more drops. The procedural generator needs a "multiplayer modifier" that adjusts encounter templates for the increased player firepower.

**Shared progression.** When the upgrade screen appears, both players pick simultaneously from independent pools. This prevents one player from "stealing" upgrades and ensures both players have agency in their build direction.

### Timeline

Multiplayer is a significant engineering effort. I'm targeting an initial two-player co-op prototype within six months, with a polished release following several months of testing and iteration. It might ship as an opt-in beta before becoming a standard feature.

## Quality of Life Improvements

Not everything on the roadmap is a flashy feature. Some of the most impactful work is in quality of life:

- **Customizable keybinds** — Already partially implemented, but expanding to full remapping for all actions including UI navigation.
- **Run history** — A log of past runs showing floor reached, upgrades chosen, cause of death, and run statistics. This data helps players analyze their play patterns and improve.
- **Accessibility options** — Colorblind modes for the particle and UI color systems, screen reader support for menus, adjustable game speed for players who need more reaction time.
- **Daily challenge runs** — Seeded runs that refresh every 24 hours, with a separate leaderboard for competitive scoring.

## The Long-Term Vision

If I'm being honest about what I hope Echoes of the Spire becomes, it's this: **the definitive browser-based roguelike**. A game that proves the web platform can host experiences just as deep, just as polished, and just as replayable as anything on Steam or console.

I want someone to discover Echoes through a shared link, play it in their browser during a lunch break, and find themselves still playing it three months later because they haven't seen everything. I want the class system to have enough depth that players main a class like they main a character in a fighting game. I want the procedural generation to create stories that players share: "you won't believe the run I just had."

The web is the most accessible platform in the world. No installation, no store approval, no $60 price tag. Just a URL. I want to prove that accessible doesn't have to mean shallow.

## Community Is Everything

None of this vision works without a community. Players who give feedback, report bugs, share runs, compete on leaderboards, and tell their friends. The greatest games aren't just built by developers — they're shaped by players who care enough to engage.

If you've played Echoes, if you've read these devlogs, if you've told someone about this weird browser dungeon crawler — thank you. Every player who shows up and spends time in the Spire is fuel for this dream.

The future of Echoes of the Spire is bright. The roadmap is ambitious. The Spire is deep, and we've only scratched the surface.

Let's keep climbing.
