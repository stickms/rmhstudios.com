## The Problem with RNG in Games

Random number generators have a bad reputation in game design, and honestly? They've earned it. Loot boxes, gacha mechanics, random critical hits that decide competitive matches — RNG in games is often a tool for extracting money or masking shallow design. Players rightly distrust it.

So when I decided that **House Always Wins** — a game set in a casino — would have gambling as a core mechanic, I knew I had to do it differently. The question wasn't *whether* to include RNG. A casino without gambling isn't a casino. The question was: **what should RNG be allowed to control?**

My answer: atmosphere. Never progression.

## The Golden Rule

Here it is, the single design principle that governs every gambling mechanic in House Always Wins:

> **RNG can modify how the world feels. It cannot modify whether you can move forward.**

No gamble in the game will ever lock a door, block a path, hide a required item, or gate story content. You can walk through the entire casino without gambling once and reach the end. Gambling is optional.

But gambling changes *everything else*.

Win consistently, and the casino brightens — subtly, like the lights are warming up for you. NPCs relax. The music gains a confident swagger. The world feels like it's on your side.

Lose heavily, and Debt accumulates. The casino darkens. NPCs grow distant. The music warps. The world feels like it's closing in.

Both experiences are valid. Both are completable. But they feel *profoundly* different. That difference — that atmospheric transformation driven by optional RNG — is the heart of the gambling system.

## Designing Fair Casino Games

There are several gambling minigames scattered throughout the casino, and each one is designed with fairness as a non-negotiable constraint.

### Coin Flip

The simplest gamble. The ritual opening of the game is a coin flip, and coin flip tables appear throughout the casino. The odds are exactly 50/50 — no hidden house edge. The player chooses heads or tails, the coin spins, fate decides. 

What makes the coin flip interesting is the **wager system**. You're not betting Casino Tokens against a payout. You're betting Debt modification. Win, and your Debt decreases slightly. Lose, and it increases. The amounts are small — this is a gentle, atmospheric gamble, not a high-stakes table. It's designed to be something you do casually as you explore, like flipping a coin in your pocket for luck.

### Card Draw

A single-card draw against the House. You draw a card, the House draws a card. Higher card wins. Ties go to the House (the only place the House has a mathematical edge, and it's stated upfront). The simplicity is intentional — this isn't poker. There's no bluffing, no strategy, no skill expression. It's pure luck, and the game is honest about that.

Card Draw wagers are medium-stakes. Wins reduce Debt noticeably; losses increase it by the same amount. The symmetry is important. Players should never feel like the game is mathematically rigged against them, because it isn't. The house edge on ties is tiny and transparent.

### The Wheel

A spinning wheel with segments of varying value. Some segments reduce Debt, some increase it, some trigger special events — a free Event Room attempt, an NPC encounter, a temporary lighting change. The Wheel is the highest-variance gamble, and it's positioned in the center of the casino as a destination, not something you stumble into.

The Wheel's odds are displayed on-screen. Every segment's probability is visible. There's no hidden math. If the wheel says "10% chance to reduce Debt by 20," that's exactly what it is. Transparency isn't just ethical — it's thematic. The casino plays fair. It just happens to be a game where the House always wins *in the long run*, because the mere act of gambling drives engagement with the Debt system.

```typescript
// Wheel segment definition
interface WheelSegment {
  label: string;
  probability: number; // Displayed to player
  effect: {
    type: 'debt_modify' | 'event_trigger' | 'npc_encounter' | 'atmosphere_shift';
    value: number;
    description: string;
  };
}

// Ensure probabilities sum to 1.0
function validateWheel(segments: WheelSegment[]): boolean {
  const total = segments.reduce((sum, s) => sum + s.probability, 0);
  return Math.abs(total - 1.0) < 0.001;
}
```

## Why Transparency Matters

I've spent a lot of time thinking about the ethics of putting gambling mechanics in a game, even a single-player narrative one. Real casinos are designed to obscure odds, manipulate psychology, and extract money. House Always Wins is *about* that exploitation — but I don't want to *perpetuate* it.

So every gamble in the game shows its math. Every probability is stated. Every outcome is fair. The game is *thematically* about a rigged system, but *mechanically* about transparent odds.

This creates an interesting tension. The casino *looks* sinister. The NPCs warn you about gambling. The atmosphere screams "this is a trap." But if you look at the actual numbers, the games are fair. The real trap isn't the math — it's the **Debt system** that compounds the atmospheric consequences of losses over time. You're not losing progress. You're losing comfort.

### Player Agency in Randomness

One of my favorite design details: before every gamble, the game offers you a choice. Not the gamble itself — the choice of *whether to gamble at all*. There's always a "walk away" option. No NPC pressures you. No locked door dangles on the other side of a bet. The gamble is always, always your choice.

This matters because it reframes RNG as something the player *opts into*. If the world darkens because your Debt increased, it's not because the game forced random punishment on you. It's because you chose to sit down at the table. You chose to flip the coin. You chose to spin the wheel.

That distinction — between imposed randomness and chosen randomness — is the difference between frustrating and atmospheric.

## The Long Game

The gambling system is designed to create a **long-tail narrative arc** across a playthrough. Early in the game, most players will gamble casually. Debt is low, stakes feel meaningless, wins and losses blur together. The casino feels like a playground.

As Debt accumulates — gradually, almost imperceptibly — the gambling takes on new weight. The same coin flip that felt carefree an hour ago now carries tension. Not because the mechanics changed. The odds are the same 50/50 they've always been. But the *context* changed. You're deeper in Debt. The lights are dimmer. The bartender won't look at you. And you're thinking: *one more win could turn this around.*

That thought — "one more win" — is the most dangerous idea in any casino, real or virtual. And House Always Wins doesn't punish it. It just *mirrors* it back to you through the world's atmosphere. The game doesn't judge you for gambling. It shows you what gambling does to the world around you.

## Lessons from Design

Building a fair gambling system that *feels* atmospheric has taught me a few things:

**Simplicity wins.** Complex gambling games with elaborate rules and strategies pull players out of the narrative and into optimization mode. Simple games — coin flips, card draws, wheel spins — keep the focus on the *feeling* of gambling, not the math.

**Show the odds.** Hiding probabilities feels clever until you realize it breeds distrust. Players who trust the system engage with it emotionally. Players who distrust it engage with it analytically, looking for exploits. I want emotional engagement.

**Make it optional.** The moment gambling feels required, it becomes frustrating. Optional gambling is atmospheric. Required gambling is a gate. And gates don't belong in this casino.

**Consequences should transform, not terminate.** A loss at the table should change how you experience the next room, not whether you can enter it. Transformation is compelling. Termination is hostile.

The casino's games are fair. The odds are transparent. The choice is always yours. But the House still always wins, because the act of gambling — win or lose — feeds the system that shapes your world. That's the real game.

And the House has been playing it much longer than you.
