## The Invisible Art

In his seminal 2008 book **"Game Feel: A Game Designer's Guide to Virtual Sensation,"** Steve Swink argues that the feeling of interacting with a game — what he calls "game feel" — is built on three pillars: real-time control, simulated space, and **polish**. Audio sits at the heart of that third pillar.

A button click without a sound feels broken. A jump without a whoosh feels weightless. An explosion without a boom feels fake. Players don't consciously think about these sounds — they process them instinctively, and the absence registers as wrongness before the mind can articulate why.

We think about audio constantly at RMH Studios. We build browser games, which means we build on the **Web Audio API**, which means we operate in a unique and sometimes frustrating sonic landscape. This post is about our philosophy, our tools, and the hard-won lessons we've learned about making games that sound as good as they play.

## The Web Audio API: Power and Constraints

The **Web Audio API** is one of the web platform's most underappreciated capabilities. Introduced in Chrome in 2011, standardized by the W3C, and now supported across all major browsers, it provides a node-based audio processing graph that can handle:

- Sample playback with precise timing
- Real-time audio synthesis (oscillators, noise generators)
- Spatial audio (panning, distance attenuation)
- Audio effects (reverb, delay, compression, filtering)
- Real-time analysis (FFT, waveform visualization)
- Custom audio processing via AudioWorklets

For a browser API, this is remarkably powerful. Professional audio middleware like **FMOD** and **Wwise** — the industry standard tools used in AAA game development — offer more features (adaptive music systems, complex event hierarchies, integrated authoring tools), but the Web Audio API provides a solid foundation for building similar systems from scratch.

Where the Web Audio API falls short compared to FMOD/Wwise:

- **No built-in authoring tool.** FMOD and Wwise provide visual editors where sound designers can build complex audio events without writing code. The Web Audio API is pure code.
- **No middleware-level abstraction.** In FMOD, you can define an event like "footstep_on_grass" that automatically selects from a pool of variations, adjusts pitch randomly, and attenuates based on distance. In the Web Audio API, you build all of that logic yourself.
- **Limited codec support.** Browsers support MP3, AAC, OGG Vorbis, WAV, and Opus, but not the proprietary compressed formats that middleware uses for efficient streaming.

Despite these limitations, we've built audio systems for our games that handle dynamic music, responsive sound effects, spatial audio, and real-time synthesis — all within the Web Audio API.

## The Autoplay Problem

If you've built anything with audio on the web in the last several years, you've hit this wall.

In 2018, **Chrome implemented an autoplay policy** that blocks AudioContext creation until a user interacts with the page (a click, tap, or keypress). Other browsers followed with similar policies. The intent was good — nobody likes a website that blasts audio unprompted — but for game developers, it created a design challenge.

Our solution is straightforward but required thought:

- **We resume the AudioContext on the first user interaction**, typically the "Play" or "Start" button that every game naturally has.
- **We design our games so that no critical audio plays before user interaction.** Title screens use visual cues; the audio experience begins when the gameplay begins.
- **We provide clear volume controls and mute options** in every game, because respecting the player's audio preferences is as important as having good audio in the first place.

## Audio Design Across the RMH Catalog

Different games demand different audio approaches. Here's how we think about sound across our catalog:

### RMHbox (Party Games)

Audio in party games serves two purposes: **feedback and energy**. Every action a player takes — selecting an answer, bumping into another player, scoring a point — needs an immediate, satisfying sound response. The audio feedback loop is tight: action → sound → emotional response → next action.

We use layered music systems that increase in intensity as rounds progress. Percussion layers add on, harmonic elements become more complex, the tempo subtly increases. Players don't consciously notice, but they feel the escalation.

### Slice It! (Rhythm Game)

A rhythm game where the audio feels wrong is a broken rhythm game. For Slice It!, audio timing precision is critical. We use the Web Audio API's high-resolution scheduling (`AudioContext.currentTime`, which runs on a separate high-priority thread) rather than `setTimeout` or `requestAnimationFrame`, which are subject to main-thread jank.

The input-to-audio latency budget for a rhythm game is around **20 milliseconds** — anything more and players perceive a desync between their actions and the audio response. The Web Audio API can meet this budget, but only if you schedule sounds ahead of time rather than triggering them reactively.

### Echoes of the Spire (Narrative Extraction Thriller)

Echoes uses audio for **atmosphere and tension**. Ambient soundscapes — dripping water, distant machinery, resonant hums — create the sense of being deep inside a structure. We use convolutional reverb (the Web Audio API's `ConvolverNode`) to simulate acoustic spaces, and we crossfade between ambient layers as the player moves between areas.

Music in Echoes is sparse and deliberate. Long stretches of gameplay have no music at all — just ambiance. When music does appear, it signals something important: a story beat, a danger, a revelation. This makes the music more impactful because it's not competing with a constant soundtrack.

### Temple of Joy (Philosophical Idle Clicker)

Idle games present an interesting audio challenge: the player might have the game running for hours. Audio that's engaging in the first minute becomes torture by the thirtieth. We use generative ambient music — evolving, non-repeating soundscapes built from randomized musical fragments — so that the audio environment stays fresh without requiring an hours-long composed soundtrack.

### Neon Driftway (Endless Racer)

Speed needs sound. In Neon Driftway, the audio design reinforces the sensation of velocity: engine frequencies pitch up with speed, the wind layer intensifies, the music becomes more driving. We use the Web Audio API's `BiquadFilterNode` to apply high-pass filtering that increases with speed, simulating the way wind noise masks lower frequencies at high velocity.

## Principles We Follow

Across all our games, we hold to a few core principles:

- **Audio is not optional.** We design and budget for sound from the beginning of development, not as a polish pass at the end. Sound is not decoration — it's structure.
- **Silence is a tool.** The absence of sound is as powerful as its presence. We deliberately create quiet moments to make loud moments hit harder.
- **Variation prevents fatigue.** Any sound that plays frequently (footsteps, UI clicks, coin pickups) has multiple variations with randomized pitch and volume. The human ear is incredibly sensitive to repetition, and even subtle variation makes sounds feel alive.
- **Respect the player's environment.** Browser games are played in all kinds of contexts — on a bus, in a library, at 2am with a sleeping partner nearby. We always provide easy-to-find volume controls and mute toggles, and we never punish players for playing with audio off.

## The Road Ahead

We're actively experimenting with **AudioWorklets** for custom DSP processing — think real-time audio effects that respond to game state, procedural sound synthesis that generates sounds from parameters rather than samples, and low-latency audio processing for our rhythm game pipeline.

We're also watching the development of the **WebCodecs API**, which promises more efficient audio decoding and could help with the codec limitations we currently work around.

Sound design in browser games is an evolving craft. The tools are getting better, the platform is getting more capable, and the creative possibilities are expanding. We're committed to making games that sound as thoughtfully designed as they look and play — because game feel isn't just visual. It's sonic.
