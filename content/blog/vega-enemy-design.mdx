## Enemies as Mechanical Storytelling

In most tower defense games, enemies are target practice. They walk down a path, you shoot them, they die or they don't. The visual design is an afterthought — a goblin, a knight, a dragon, whatever fits the theme.

In Project Vega, enemies are **the theme**. Each enemy type exists at the intersection of a mechanical need and a thematic truth. The Anxiety Sprite isn't just a fast enemy — it *behaves* like anxiety. The Depression Hulk isn't just a tank — it *feels* like depression. This dual-purpose design is what makes Vega's enemies memorable, and getting it right has been one of the most rewarding parts of development.

## Designing from the Inside Out

My process for each enemy type starts with the emotional experience, not the stats sheet. I ask: **what does this feel like to deal with in real life?** Then I translate that feeling into mechanical behavior.

Anxiety feels fast, scattered, overwhelming in numbers, and hard to pin down. Depression feels slow, heavy, immovable, and draining. Mania feels unpredictable, surging, alternating between too-much and too-little. Each emotional profile maps to a distinct gameplay archetype.

Let me break down each enemy type in detail.

## Anxiety Sprite

**Visual Design:** Semi-transparent, flickering blobs with jittery movement. They're small — about half a grid cell — and their outlines constantly shift and distort.

**Mechanical Profile:**

```typescript
interface AnxietySprite {
  hp: 15;
  speed: 3.5;          // fast
  sanityDamage: 2;
  focusReward: 5;
  spawnPattern: 'swarm';  // always spawns in groups of 5-8
  specialBehavior: 'erratic_pathing';
}
```

### The Erratic Pathing System

Anxiety Sprites don't follow the optimal A* path perfectly. Every few seconds, they make a **random deviation** — stepping one cell off their calculated path before correcting back. This makes them harder to hit and creates the visual impression of nervous, twitchy movement.

```typescript
const updateSpriteMovement = (sprite: AnxietySprite, path: GridCell[]) => {
  if (Math.random() < 0.15) {  // 15% chance per tick
    const deviation = getRandomAdjacentWalkable(sprite.position, grid);
    if (deviation) {
      sprite.position = deviation;
      sprite.pathDirty = true;  // recalculate path next tick
    }
  }
};
```

This deviation chance means that even with perfect tower placement, some Sprites will juke your defenses. You can't reliably snipe them with a Lobotomizer — the unpredictability makes it miss too often. Instead, you need area denial: Synapse clusters and Suppressor zones that cover enough space to catch the deviations.

**Thematic resonance:** Anxiety doesn't respond to precise, targeted intervention as well as it responds to broad, consistent coping frameworks. The mechanic teaches this through gameplay without ever saying it explicitly.

### Swarm Behavior

Anxiety Sprites always spawn in groups. A wave might contain 6 Sprites that all enter within a 2-second window. Individually, each Sprite is trivial. A single Synapse tower can handle one or two. But six at once, each deviating randomly? That's overwhelm — and it's exactly what anxiety feels like.

## Depression Hulk

**Visual Design:** Large, dark masses with slow, oozing movement. They absorb light — areas around them dim slightly. Their visual footprint is oppressive by design.

**Mechanical Profile:**

```typescript
interface DepressionHulk {
  hp: 300;
  speed: 1.0;           // very slow
  sanityDamage: 12;
  focusReward: 25;
  armor: 3;              // flat damage reduction per hit
  specialBehavior: 'cc_resistance';
  ccImmunityDuration: 2000;  // immune to slow for first 2 seconds
}
```

### Armor and CC Resistance

The Hulk's defining traits are its massive HP pool and damage resistance. The flat armor of 3 means that Synapse towers (8 damage per shot) only deal 5 effective damage. With 300 HP, that's 60 Synapse shots to kill one Hulk. At 4 shots per second, one Synapse needs 15 seconds — nearly the Hulk's entire transit time across the map.

The CC resistance adds another layer. Suppressors' slow effect doesn't work for the first 2 seconds of application. This means Hulks power through initial Suppressor zones before the slow kicks in. Players need to stack Suppressors in depth, not just at a single chokepoint, ensuring sustained slow application.

**The Counter:** Lobotomizers. With 80 damage per shot (minus 3 armor = 77 effective), a Lobotomizer drops a Hulk in 4 shots. Place two Lobotomizers covering the same corridor, and Hulks melt. The game teaches this through failure — the first time a Hulk tanks your entire Synapse row and reaches the core for 12% Sanity damage, you *immediately* understand why Lobotomizers exist.

**Thematic resonance:** Depression can't be managed with quick fixes or scattered efforts. It requires focused, sustained, specialized intervention. The Hulk mechanic mirrors this truth.

## Manic Spike

**Visual Design:** Bright, crackling energy with irregular geometry. They pulse with light — bright during fast phases, dim during pauses. Visually, they look like static electricity given form.

**Mechanical Profile:**

```typescript
interface ManicSpike {
  hp: 60;
  speed: varies;         // oscillates between 5.0 and 0.5
  sanityDamage: 6;
  focusReward: 15;
  specialBehavior: 'oscillating_speed';
  burstDuration: 1500;
  pauseDuration: 800;
}
```

### The Oscillation Pattern

Manic Spikes alternate between **burst phases** (1.5 seconds at speed 5.0 — faster than anything else in the game) and **pause phases** (0.8 seconds at speed 0.5 — nearly stationary). During bursts, they outrun most tower targeting systems. During pauses, they're sitting ducks.

```typescript
const updateManicSpike = (spike: ManicSpike, elapsed: number) => {
  const cycleTime = spike.burstDuration + spike.pauseDuration;
  const cyclePosition = elapsed % cycleTime;

  if (cyclePosition < spike.burstDuration) {
    spike.currentSpeed = 5.0;
    spike.visualIntensity = 1.0;  // bright glow during burst
  } else {
    spike.currentSpeed = 0.5;
    spike.visualIntensity = 0.3;  // dim during pause
  }
};
```

**The Counter:** Timing and positioning. Place Suppressors where Spikes tend to enter their pause phase. Their pause points are deterministic — given the same entry point and speed pattern, a Spike always pauses at the same locations. In Loop 1, you learn where the pauses happen. In Loop 2, you build defenses there. This is the loop system at its best.

**Thematic resonance:** Mania is characterized by unpredictable surges of energy followed by crashes. The Spike captures the whiplash experience — the sense that you're alternately overwhelmed by intensity and caught off-guard by sudden stillness.

## Wave Composition

Individual enemies are ingredients. **Waves are recipes.** The composition of each wave is designed to test specific skill sets and create narrative pacing:

### Early Waves (Timeline 0:00 - 1:30)
Pure Anxiety Sprite swarms. Establishes the baseline threat and teaches players about swarming. Builds Focus income for later builds.

### Mid Waves (Timeline 1:30 - 3:30)
Mixed compositions. Sprites mixed with occasional Hulks. Forces the player to address two threat types simultaneously. The Hulk usually arrives right as a Sprite swarm demands attention — creating a prioritization dilemma.

### Late Waves (Timeline 3:30 - 5:00)
All three types simultaneously, with increased numbers and elite variants (higher stats, visual indicators). This is where under-prepared defenses collapse and the loop system's value becomes clear. You *need* Ghost Protocols from previous loops to survive the late game.

### Boss Waves

Special wave events that occur at the 2:30 and 4:30 marks. These feature **unique enemy variants** — oversized Hulks with regeneration, Sprite swarms that split on death, Manic Spikes with randomized oscillation timing. Boss waves are the skill checks that separate a decent defense from a great one.

## The Emotional Arc

Wave composition creates a deliberate emotional arc within each five-minute loop:

1. **Confidence** (0:00-1:30): You're handling Sprites. Your towers are working. Things feel manageable.
2. **Tension** (1:30-3:00): Hulks arrive. Your defenses are tested. Cracks start to show.
3. **Crisis** (3:00-4:00): Everything at once. Sanity dropping. Glitches intensifying. You're firefighting.
4. **Resolution** (4:00-5:00): Either your defenses hold (triumph) or they buckle (motivation for the next loop).

This arc repeats across three loops, with each loop's Ghost Protocols shifting the inflection points. By Loop 3, the "confidence" phase might extend to 2:00 because your ghost network is already established, but the "crisis" phase hits harder because enemies scale too.

Good enemy design isn't about making tough enemies. It's about making enemies that tell a story, create meaningful player choices, and produce emotional experiences through mechanics alone. In Vega, every Sprite, Hulk, and Spike is a sentence in a story about the mind fighting itself — and winning through persistence, strategy, and growth.
