## Why Procedural Generation Matters

Roguelikes live and die by replayability. If a player can memorize the layout, optimize a single path, and repeat it endlessly, the magic evaporates. **Procedural generation is the engine of surprise** — it's what makes your fifteenth run feel as fresh as your first.

But here's the thing about procedural generation that nobody tells you when you start: random doesn't mean interesting. I could scatter rooms randomly across a grid and connect them with hallways, and technically every dungeon would be "unique." But unique and *good* are very different things. The real challenge isn't generating variety — it's generating variety that consistently produces fun, playable, well-paced dungeon floors.

## The Algorithm: Room-First Generation

Echoes uses what I call a **room-first** generation approach. Instead of carving tunnels through solid rock (like many traditional roguelikes), I start with rooms and then figure out how to connect them. Here's the basic flow:

1. **Room Placement** — Drop a set number of rooms onto the floor grid using a modified Poisson disc sampling algorithm. This ensures rooms are spread out without clustering too tightly or leaving huge empty gaps.

2. **Room Typing** — Assign each room a type: combat, treasure, shop, rest, challenge, or boss. The typing follows rules — boss rooms are always at the end, rest rooms appear at roughly the midpoint, and shops are never adjacent to each other.

3. **Corridor Generation** — Connect rooms using a minimum spanning tree to guarantee reachability, then add extra connections based on a "connectivity factor" that increases on deeper floors.

4. **Population** — Fill combat rooms with enemy encounters scaled to the floor depth and the player's current power level. Place loot, environmental hazards, and interactive objects.

5. **Validation** — Run a pathfinding check from the entrance to every room. If any room is unreachable (which shouldn't happen, but defensive programming saves lives), regenerate that section.

```typescript
function generateFloor(depth: number, seed: number): DungeonFloor {
  const rng = createSeededRNG(seed);
  const rooms = placeRooms(rng, depth);
  const typed = assignRoomTypes(rooms, depth);
  const connected = generateCorridors(typed, rng);
  const populated = populateRooms(connected, depth, rng);
  
  if (!validateReachability(populated)) {
    return generateFloor(depth, seed + 1); // retry with new seed
  }
  
  return populated;
}
```

### Seeded Randomness

Every dungeon is generated from a **seed value**. This is crucial for two reasons. First, it means I can reproduce and debug specific dungeon layouts when a bug report comes in. "The game crashed on floor 7" becomes actionable when I can regenerate exactly the floor they were on. Second, it opens the door to daily challenge runs — everyone plays the same seed, and the leaderboard ranks completion time and score.

## Room Types: The Building Blocks of Pacing

The room type system is where procedural generation transitions from a technical problem to a **design problem**. The sequence of room types defines the pacing of a run, and bad pacing kills engagement faster than bad combat.

### Combat Rooms

These are the meat of the experience. Each combat room has an **encounter template** that defines enemy composition, spawn timing, and optional modifiers (darkness, hazardous terrain, timed survival). I maintain a library of encounter templates that grows with floor depth, ensuring players face new combinations as they go deeper.

### Treasure Rooms

Treasure rooms are breathers. The player walks in, finds a chest, and gets a reward. Simple, but the placement matters enormously. A treasure room after a grueling combat gauntlet feels like a gift from the gods. A treasure room right at the start feels meaningless because there's nothing to recover from yet.

### Rest Rooms

Rest rooms let the player heal and optionally spend resources on temporary buffs. I place these at roughly the 40-60% mark of each floor to create a natural midpoint. The player pushes through the first half, reaches a safe harbor, then faces the more challenging second half refreshed.

### Challenge Rooms

These are optional, high-risk rooms marked on the minimap with a distinct icon. They contain harder encounters or puzzle mechanics but offer premium rewards. The decision to enter or skip a challenge room is one of my favorite player choices in the game — it's pure risk-reward with no right answer.

## Connectivity: Not Too Open, Not Too Linear

The topology of a dungeon floor — how rooms connect to each other — has a massive impact on how it plays. Too linear, and exploration feels like walking down a hallway. Too open, and there's no sense of progression or discovery.

My approach uses the minimum spanning tree as a skeleton, then probabilistically adds extra edges. On early floors, I keep the connectivity factor low, creating more linear paths that guide new players through the experience. On deeper floors, I increase connectivity, creating webs of interconnected rooms that reward exploration and give experienced players meaningful routing choices.

I also implemented **one-way connections** on some edges — doors you can drop through but not climb back up, teleporters that go only one direction. These create strategic decisions: do you take the shortcut knowing you can't backtrack?

## Ensuring Every Run Feels Fresh

Even with all of this infrastructure, there's a risk of generated dungeons feeling *samey* after enough runs. I combat this with several layers of variation:

**Environmental themes** rotate between floors. One floor might have a corrupted crystal cavern with reflective surfaces that bounce projectiles. The next might have a flooded chamber where movement is slowed but electrical attacks are amplified. These themes aren't just visual — they change the tactical landscape.

**Modifiers** are applied globally to some floors. "Lights Out" reduces visibility to a small radius around the player. "Swarm Protocol" doubles enemy count but halves their health. "Volatile" makes defeated enemies explode. Each modifier forces the player to adapt their strategy, even if they've seen the room layout pattern before.

**Secret rooms** are generated with low probability and hidden behind destructible walls or puzzle triggers. Finding one feels like a genuine discovery, and their contents are always worth the detour.

## The Hard Lessons

Procedural generation taught me humility. My first implementations produced dungeons that were technically correct but emotionally flat. Rooms were evenly spaced, encounters were balanced, everything was *fine* — and fine is the enemy of engaging.

The breakthrough came when I started thinking about **contrast**. A difficult room becomes memorable when it follows an easy one. A treasure feels valuable when you earn it. The generator needed to create *rhythm*, not just content. Tension and release, push and pull, challenge and reward.

I also learned that **handcrafted elements within procedural systems** are incredibly powerful. Some of my best room layouts are hand-designed templates that the generator places and rotates. The generator handles macro-level design — floor structure, pacing, difficulty curve — while hand-crafted templates ensure micro-level quality in individual rooms.

Procedural generation is an ongoing process. Every week I add new room templates, tweak the connectivity algorithms, and adjust the encounter library. The dungeon is alive, always evolving, and that's exactly how it should be. The Spire never shows you the same face twice.
