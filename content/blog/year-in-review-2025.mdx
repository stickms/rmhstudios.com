## Looking Back at 2025

This has been the most consequential year in RMH Studios' history. We shipped more games, grew our community faster, and pushed our technical foundations further than ever before. We also made mistakes, hit walls, and learned lessons that will shape everything we do going forward.

This is our honest accounting of 2025 — the wins, the struggles, and the context around both.

## What We Shipped

### RMHbox Expansion

RMHbox started the year with a solid foundation of party minigames, and we spent 2025 expanding that catalog to **16+ minigames** spanning trivia, reflexes, creativity, and strategy. Each new minigame taught us something about real-time multiplayer game design — what works with latency, what breaks with large groups, and what makes people laugh so hard they forget they're playing in a browser.

Key additions included physics-based games powered by our improved collision systems, drawing games with real-time canvas synchronization, and word games that leverage our growing dictionary and NLP capabilities.

### Signal Forge

Our roguelike deckbuilder moved from prototype to playable reality in 2025. Signal Forge represents our most ambitious single-player experience — a game with persistent progression, procedurally generated encounters, and deep strategic mechanics, all running in a browser tab.

Building a deckbuilder taught us the value of systems design. Every card, every encounter, every upgrade path needs to be balanced not in isolation, but in combination with everything else. It's a design challenge that gets exponentially harder as the card pool grows.

### New Titles

We launched several new experiences this year:

- **Synapse Storm**, a micro-challenge juggler that tests your ability to manage multiple tasks simultaneously — inspired by the WarioWare school of game design.
- **Temple of Joy**, a philosophical idle clicker that explores what it means to seek meaning through incremental progress. It's weird, it's contemplative, and it's generated surprisingly passionate discussions in our community.
- **Echoes of the Spire**, a narrative extraction thriller where players piece together a story from fragments. It's our first attempt at serious storytelling in a browser game, and the reception gave us confidence to invest more in narrative.
- **Slice It!**, a rhythm game that uses the Web Audio API for precise beat synchronization.
- **Laundry Sort**, a physics-based sorting game with satisfying tactile feedback.
- **Neon Driftway**, an endless racer with procedurally generated tracks and a synthwave aesthetic.

### Platform and Tools

Beyond games, we expanded our platform offerings:

- **RMHTube** grew into a legitimate watch party platform with synchronized playback, text and voice chat, and room persistence.
- **RMH Type** added competitive multiplayer typing races, custom text passages, and detailed WPM analytics.
- **RMH Study** launched with Pomodoro timer rooms, ambient soundscapes, and co-working sessions.
- **RMHdle and RMHConnections** became daily rituals for our Discord community, with hundreds of puzzles generated and thousands of daily plays.

## The Industry Around Us

2025 was a turbulent year for the gaming industry. The wave of layoffs that began in late 2023 continued into 2025, with major studios including Microsoft Gaming, EA, Sony Interactive, and others reducing headcount significantly. An estimated 14,000+ gaming industry jobs were cut in 2024 alone, and the trend didn't stop.

At the same time, the games that did launch ranged widely. Major releases like GTA VI's anticipation dominated headlines throughout the year. The indie scene continued to prove that small teams can create culturally significant games — titles from tiny studios regularly appeared on Game Awards shortlists and dominated discourse on social media.

For browser gaming specifically, 2025 saw continued growth. CrazyGames and Poki reported increasing traffic to browser game portals. Netflix continued expanding its gaming division, exploring browser-based delivery for its subscriber base. The technical foundations — WebGPU adoption, WASM performance improvements, and modern JavaScript engine optimizations — all advanced meaningfully.

We're a tiny studio in a massive industry, but the trends are moving in our direction.

## Technical Milestones

### WebSocket Infrastructure

We rebuilt our WebSocket infrastructure this year, moving to a more robust architecture that handles connection drops, reconnection, and state synchronization more gracefully. For real-time multiplayer games, reliable WebSocket connections are the foundation everything else rests on. Getting this right eliminated entire categories of bugs and player complaints.

### Web Audio API Mastery

Slice It! forced us to deeply understand the Web Audio API — particularly precise timing, low-latency playback, and synchronization between audio events and visual feedback. The knowledge we built now informs audio across all our games.

### Three.js Performance

Neon Driftway pushed our Three.js rendering pipeline to its limits. We learned hard lessons about draw call batching, geometry instancing, texture atlasing, and shader optimization. These optimizations propagated back to every game that uses 3D rendering.

### Build Pipeline

We invested heavily in our development infrastructure: faster builds, better testing, more automated deployment. These aren't player-facing features, but they directly impact how quickly we can ship and iterate.

## Community Growth

Our Discord community grew substantially in 2025. More importantly, the quality of engagement deepened. We saw:

- Regular RMHbox game nights organized by community members, not by us.
- Player-created guides, tier lists, and strategy discussions for Signal Forge.
- A thriving daily game scene around RMHdle and RMHConnections, with players maintaining months-long streaks.
- Feature requests and bug reports that demonstrate genuine investment in our games' quality.

The community isn't just playing our games — they're co-creating the culture around them. That's the most valuable outcome of 2025, and it's not measurable in a dashboard.

## Lessons Learned

### Ship Imperfect, Iterate Fast

Every game we launched this year launched with rough edges. Temple of Joy's initial balancing was off. Synapse Storm's difficulty curve was too steep. Neon Driftway had performance issues on lower-end devices. In every case, shipping imperfect and iterating based on player feedback produced a better result than trying to polish in isolation.

The browser platform makes this possible in a way that traditional game distribution doesn't. We push fixes in minutes, not weeks. Players see improvements in real time. The feedback loop is tight enough that development feels collaborative.

### Multiplayer Is Hard

Real-time multiplayer over WebSocket is technically challenging in ways that surprise you. Latency compensation, authoritative server state, cheat prevention, connection handling for players on unstable mobile networks — each of these is a deep engineering problem. We got better at all of them in 2025, but humility remains appropriate.

### Scope Is the Enemy

We started more projects than we should have. Some game concepts that seemed exciting in prototype didn't survive contact with the realities of development time and technical constraints. Learning to kill ideas early is a skill we're still developing.

### Community Trust Is Fragile and Precious

When we shipped a bug that caused players to lose RMHdle streaks, the reaction was intense. Streaks matter to people. Daily rituals matter. We learned that maintaining community trust requires not just fixing bugs quickly, but communicating transparently about what happened and why.

## Looking Forward

2025 proved that browser games can be rich, social, and compelling. It proved that a small studio can ship ambitious projects by leveraging the web platform's unique strengths. And it proved that honest, transparent development builds a community that sticks around.

We have big plans for 2026. But for now, we're taking a moment to appreciate what this year was: the year RMH Studios became real.

Thank you to every player who clicked a link, joined a lobby, solved a puzzle, or told a friend. This year happened because of you.
