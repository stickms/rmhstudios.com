## Making Corruption Desirable

Most deckbuilders treat deck pollution as purely negative. Curses in Slay the Spire, Junk cards in other games — they clog your deck, waste your draws, and generally make your life worse. When I started designing Signal Forge's corruption system, I wanted to subvert that expectation entirely.

**What if corruption wasn't just a penalty — what if it was a strategy?**

That question led to the Glitch mechanic, which has become one of Signal Forge's most distinctive and polarizing features. Some players love it. Some players fear it. The best players *weaponize* it.

## What Is a Glitch Card?

A Glitch card is a corrupted version of a normal card. When a card becomes "glitched," its stats are randomized within a range, its artwork visually distorts, and it gains unpredictable side effects. A Glitch card might be stronger than the original — or it might be catastrophically worse. You won't know until you play it.

Here's what corruption looks like mechanically:

```typescript
function corruptCard(card: Card): GlitchedCard {
  return {
    ...card,
    isCorrupted: true,
    damage: randomizeInRange(card.damage, 0.5, 1.5),
    shield: randomizeInRange(card.shield, 0.5, 1.5),
    cost: Math.max(0, card.cost + randomInt(-1, 1)),
    glitchEffect: rollGlitchEffect(), // Random side effect
    visualCorruption: generateCorruptionLevel(),
  };
}
```

The `glitchEffect` is where things get interesting. Each Glitch card rolls a random side effect from a curated pool:

- **Data Leak** — Draw an extra card after playing, but the enemy also heals 3 HP.
- **Signal Overflow** — Deal +50% damage, but take 2 damage yourself.
- **Frequency Drift** — Card cost fluctuates by ±1 each turn it's in your hand.
- **Bit Rot** — Card loses 1 damage permanently each time it's played, but gains +2 shield.
- **Phantom Echo** — Creates a temporary copy in your hand, but it Exhausts.
- **Noise Injection** — Adds a random keyword to the card for this combat only.

Some of these effects are net positive. Some are net negative. Most are *situationally* valuable — which is exactly the design intent.

## The Static Resource Connection

Glitch mechanics are deeply tied to Signal Forge's **Static** resource system. Static is your energy each turn — you spend it to play cards. But Static also has a secondary role: it represents the "cleanliness" of your signal.

When you accumulate Glitch cards in your deck, your Static becomes unreliable. At the start of each turn, each Glitch card in your deck has a small chance of "interfering" with your Static generation, potentially giving you one fewer or one extra Static than expected. This creates a fascinating meta-game:

- A deck with 0-2 Glitch cards? Stable. Predictable. Safe.
- A deck with 3-5 Glitch cards? Occasionally volatile. Manageable with planning.
- A deck with 6+ Glitch cards? Chaotic. Your Static count fluctuates wildly, and every turn requires improvisation.

High-Glitch runs are *exhilarating*. You can't plan ahead because your resources are uncertain. You have to evaluate your hand each turn based on what you actually received, not what you expected. It's a fundamentally different way to play the game.

## The Tempo System and Corruption

**Tempo** is Signal Forge's secondary resource — a persistent counter that builds over the course of a fight and can be spent on powerful burst effects. Glitch cards interact with Tempo in a unique way: playing a Glitch card always generates +1 Tempo, regardless of its other effects.

This means Glitch-heavy decks have access to **more Tempo than clean decks**. That's a real, tangible advantage. Tempo abilities — supercharged attacks, emergency heals, deck manipulation — are some of the most powerful effects in the game. A Glitch player who manages their corruption intelligently can access these abilities faster and more frequently than a "pure" player.

This is the core of the risk/reward design: **Glitch cards make your deck less predictable, but they also give you access to resources that clean decks can't match.** The question isn't whether corruption is good or bad — it's whether you can handle the chaos in exchange for the power.

## Voluntary Corruption Events

During a run, players encounter events that offer voluntary corruption. These are some of the most interesting decisions in the game:

### The Signal Distortion Shrine

> *"The shrine pulses with corrupted energy. You can feel it reaching for your cards. Corrupt your strongest card in exchange for 50 gold and a Rare card reward."*

Do you sacrifice the predictability of your best card for guaranteed Rare value? The answer depends on your deck, your strategy, and your risk tolerance. A Noise player might jump at this — they're already embracing chaos. A Sine player who depends on consistent sustain might refuse.

### The Glitch Amplifier

> *"A device that amplifies the corruption in your deck. All currently Glitched cards gain +3 damage, but you gain 2 additional Glitch cards."*

This event is a trap for timid players and a goldmine for aggressive ones. If you already have several Glitch cards, amplifying them can create a devastating hand. If you're clean, taking on 2 Glitch cards just for a damage boost isn't worth the instability.

### The Purification Station

> *"Remove all corruption from one card, restoring it to its original form."*

Sometimes you need an escape valve. A Glitch card that rolled a terrible side effect can be cleansed — but the Purification Station is rare, and using it means you can't use it on a different corrupted card. Scarcity creates value.

## Designing for Danger

The hardest part of designing the Glitch mechanic was making corruption feel **genuinely dangerous** without making it feel **unfair**. Players need to feel genuine tension when they decide to corrupt a card — but they should never feel like the game cheated them.

My rules for fair corruption:

1. **No instant kills.** A Glitch card should never directly kill the player through its side effect. Indirect damage (like Signal Overflow's self-damage) is fine, but it should never be enough to one-shot you.
2. **Always visible.** Glitch cards are always visually distinct. You always know which cards in your hand are corrupted and what their side effects are. No hidden information.
3. **Always voluntary.** The game never forces Glitch cards on you. Every corruption is the result of a player choice (even if that choice is accepting a reward that comes with corruption strings attached).
4. **Always manageable.** Even in worst-case scenarios, a Glitch card should be *playable*. It might be bad, but it's never a complete dead card.

## The Noise Waveform Synergy

The Noise waveform archetype has a special relationship with Glitch cards. Several Noise cards directly benefit from corruption:

- **Static Harvest**: Deal 2 damage for each Glitch card in your deck.
- **Error Correction**: Remove a negative Glitch effect from one card and apply it as a debuff to an enemy.
- **Corrupt Signal**: Deliberately Glitch a card in your hand to gain +3 Static this turn.

These cards create a viable "Glitch archetype" where corruption isn't a side effect — it's the entire strategy. A player who goes all-in on Noise + Glitch plays a completely different game than someone building a clean Pulse deck. That strategic diversity is what keeps Signal Forge interesting across dozens of runs.

## The Visual Language of Corruption

When a card is Glitched, it doesn't just gain a "corrupted" tag — it *looks* corrupted. The card art distorts with scanline artifacts, the border flickers, the text occasionally scrambles. The more corrupted your deck, the more the entire game UI begins to show interference patterns.

This visual feedback does double duty: it communicates game state instantly (how corrupted am I?), and it reinforces the *feeling* of playing with dangerous power. When your screen is dancing with glitch artifacts and you're still winning? That's the power fantasy the Glitch mechanic is designed to deliver.

Corruption isn't punishment in Signal Forge — it's *transformation*. And the players who embrace it discover entirely new dimensions of the game that clean players never see.
