## The Game Feel Problem

There's a word that game designers use constantly: **juice**. It's the screen shake when you land a hit. The particle burst when an enemy dies. The bass thump in your speakers when your ultimate ability fires. Juice is the gap between a game that *works* and a game that *feels incredible*.

Browser games have historically been terrible at juice. There are technical reasons for this — audio latency, garbage collection pauses, inconsistent framerates across devices. But there are also cultural reasons: browser game developers often prioritize function over feel, treating polish as optional rather than essential.

With Echoes of the Spire, I made a commitment early on: **combat would feel as good as any native indie action game**. Not "good for a browser game." Just *good*. Full stop. That commitment shaped almost every technical decision I made, and I want to walk you through the layers that create Echoes' combat feel.

## Layer 1: Screen Shake

Screen shake is the single highest-impact game feel technique relative to implementation effort. When the player lands a hit, the camera displaces by a few pixels in a random direction and quickly returns to center. When the player takes damage, the shake is larger and longer. When an ultimate ability fires, the entire screen shudders.

The key to good screen shake is **variation and decay**. A constant-magnitude shake looks mechanical and wrong. I use an exponentially decaying shake with randomized direction each frame:

```typescript
function updateScreenShake(shake: ShakeState, dt: number): ShakeState {
  const decay = Math.pow(0.9, dt * 60);
  const magnitude = shake.intensity * decay;
  const angle = Math.random() * Math.PI * 2;
  
  return {
    intensity: magnitude < 0.5 ? 0 : shake.intensity * decay,
    offsetX: Math.cos(angle) * magnitude,
    offsetY: Math.sin(angle) * magnitude,
  };
}
```

Different events trigger different shake intensities. A basic melee hit might be intensity 3. A critical hit might be 6. An explosion might be 12. The player can adjust shake intensity in settings — some people love it, some find it disorienting, and respecting that preference matters.

## Layer 2: Hitstop (Freeze Frames)

This one is subtle but *transformative*. When the player's attack connects with an enemy, the game pauses for 2-3 frames. Not the whole game — just the attacker and the target. Everything else continues moving. This micro-pause communicates impact in a way that nothing else can. It's the technique that makes combat in Hades and Dead Cells feel so weighty, and it translates beautifully to browser games.

Implementation-wise, I track a `hitstopRemaining` timer on affected entities. During hitstop frames, the entity's position, animation, and physics updates are skipped, but their visual rendering still applies a small vibration effect. The result is a percussive "thunk" that you feel before you hear it.

### The Browser Audio Challenge

Speaking of hearing it — audio in browsers is a minefield. The Web Audio API is powerful but has latency characteristics that vary wildly across browsers and devices. A 50ms delay between a hit landing visually and the sound playing is enough to break the feeling of impact entirely.

My solution was an **audio pre-loading and pooling system**. Every sound effect that needs to feel responsive — hit sounds, ability sounds, movement sounds — is pre-decoded and stored as AudioBuffer instances. When a sound needs to play, I grab a pre-created AudioBufferSourceNode from a pool and start it immediately. No decode delay, no allocation delay.

I also implemented a **priority system** for overlapping sounds. In a room full of enemies, dozens of hit sounds might trigger simultaneously. Playing all of them creates audio mud. Instead, I limit concurrent sounds per category and prioritize the most recent or closest events. The player's own attacks always get audio priority.

## Layer 3: Particle Effects

Particles are where combat goes from feeling solid to feeling *spectacular*. Every hit spawns directional sparks. Every ability leaves a trail. Every enemy death produces an explosion of fragments that scatter, bounce, and fade.

My particle system is custom-built for Canvas performance. Each particle is a simple object with position, velocity, acceleration, color, size, lifetime, and an optional behavior function. The system uses object pooling to avoid garbage collection — dead particles are recycled, not deleted and recreated.

For 3D mode, I use point sprites rendered in a separate pass with additive blending. This gives them a glowing, ethereal quality that fits the sci-fi dungeon aesthetic. The particle budget is tighter in 3D (each particle requires a GPU quad), so I rely more on larger, fewer particles with longer lifetimes.

### Color as Communication

Particle color isn't just aesthetic — it communicates game information. Player damage particles are **cyan**. Enemy damage particles are **red-orange**. Healing effects are **green**. Ability-specific effects use the class color (Vanguard gold, Spectre violet, Conduit electric blue). Players learn to read the particle language subconsciously, gaining tactical information from visual noise.

## Layer 4: Animation Timing

The timing of attack animations is critical to combat feel. I follow a principle from fighting game design: **fast startup, impactful contact, slow recovery**. The wind-up to an attack is quick, keeping the game feeling responsive. The contact frame has hitstop and particles. The follow-through is slightly slower, creating openings for enemies to punish over-commitment.

This timing structure also creates a natural rhythm to combat. Attack, pause, attack, pause. Players who internalize this rhythm can optimize their damage output by attacking at the exact moment the recovery ends, creating a satisfying mastery curve.

## Layer 5: Damage Numbers

Controversial choice here — I show floating damage numbers. Some designers hate them because they break immersion. I love them because they provide **immediate, unambiguous feedback** about what just happened. Did that hit crit? You'll know instantly because you see a bigger, differently-colored number fly up. Is this upgrade actually increasing your damage? The numbers don't lie.

My damage numbers use a simple physics simulation: they launch upward with some random horizontal spread, decelerate, and fade. Critical hits get larger font size and a brief scale-up animation. Multi-hits overlap and stagger slightly so you can see each individual number in a burst.

## Layer 6: The Death Explosion

When an enemy dies, I pull out all the stops. Time slows by about 20% for a quarter second. The enemy's sprite shatters into fragments that fly outward. A burst of class-colored particles erupts from the death point. A bass impact sound plays. If it was the last enemy in the room, the door-unlock sound follows immediately, creating an auditory *period* at the end of the combat *sentence*.

This death sequence took a surprising amount of iteration. The first version was too subtle — enemies just disappeared. The second version was too chaotic — too many particles, too much shake. The current version hits a balance where each death feels satisfying without becoming exhausting over a full run.

## The Cumulative Effect

None of these layers is remarkable on its own. Screen shake is basic. Particles are ubiquitous. Damage numbers are standard. But **layered together and carefully tuned**, they create a combat experience that players consistently describe as feeling better than they expected from a browser game.

That's the real lesson: game feel isn't about any single technique. It's about the relentless accumulation of small things done well. Every frame, every sound, every pixel in service of making the player feel powerful, responsive, and connected to the action.

The browser doesn't have to be a second-class platform. You just have to fight for every frame of feel.
