## A Tomato-Shaped Timer Changed Everything

In the late 1980s, a university student named Francesco Cirillo grabbed a tomato-shaped kitchen timer and changed the way millions of people think about focus. His **Pomodoro Technique** — work for 25 minutes, break for 5, repeat — became one of the most widely adopted productivity systems in the world. Cirillo published his foundational paper in 1992 and later expanded it into a full methodology, but the core idea has always been disarmingly simple: time-box your attention, and your attention improves.

We've used the Pomodoro Technique ourselves for years. As indie developers juggling game design, networking code, audio engineering, and marketing, we live and die by our ability to focus. But we noticed something: the technique works better when you're not alone.

## "Study With Me" and the Rise of Co-Working Streams

If you've spent any time on YouTube in the past five years, you've seen them — "study with me" livestreams where someone sits at a desk, studies silently for hours, and hundreds or thousands of viewers study along. Channels like TheStrive Studies, MDprospect, and James Scholz have collectively racked up hundreds of millions of views. The format is almost absurdly minimal. A person. A desk. A timer. Yet it works.

Why? The answer lies in a concept psychologists call **body-doubling**: the practice of working alongside another person to increase focus and accountability. It's not tutoring. It's not collaboration. It's simply *presence*.

Body-doubling has gained particular attention in ADHD research. A 2020 study published in the *Journal of Attention Disorders* found that external accountability structures significantly improved task initiation and sustained attention in adults with ADHD. Dr. Russell Barkley, one of the leading researchers in ADHD, has long argued that the disorder is fundamentally about self-regulation — and that external scaffolding (including the presence of others) can compensate for executive function deficits. Body-doubling provides exactly that scaffolding.

But you don't need an ADHD diagnosis to benefit. The phenomenon taps into something universal: **social facilitation**, first described by psychologist Norman Triplett in 1898. We perform better on familiar tasks when others are present. A study room full of focused people makes you more focused. A livestream of someone studying does the same thing, even through a screen.

## From Passive Viewing to Active Participation

The "study with me" model has a limitation, though. You're watching someone else's timer. You're on someone else's schedule. There's no feedback loop — no one knows if you actually studied or scrolled Twitter the whole time. The accountability is one-directional.

We wanted to fix that. **RMH Study** takes the core insight of body-doubling and the Pomodoro Technique and turns it into something interactive, social, and genuinely accountable.

Here's how it works:

- **Synced Pomodoro timers.** When you join a study room, everyone is on the same clock. Work periods and break periods are synchronized, creating a shared rhythm. You can see who's in their work phase and who's on break.
- **Persistent rooms.** You can create or join rooms organized by topic, subject, or just vibe. Want a room for "CS finals prep"? Make one. Want a room that's just "quiet coworking"? It's there.
- **Session leaderboards.** We track total focused minutes per session and per week. This isn't about competition for its own sake — it's about making your effort *visible*. When you can see that you logged 3 hours of focused work today, it reinforces the habit. When you see others logging similar hours, it normalizes the effort.
- **Break activities.** During break periods, we surface light interactive content — a quick typing challenge from RMH Type, a trivia question, a stretch reminder. The goal is to make breaks restorative without letting them spiral into 45-minute YouTube detours.

## Why a Game Studio Built a Productivity App

This is a fair question. We make games — party games, rhythm games, idle clickers, roguelikes. Why build a study timer?

The honest answer: **because our community asked for it.**

We noticed that a significant portion of the people using RMHbox and our Discord daily games were students. They'd play a round of RMHdle in the morning, grind some Temple of Joy during study breaks, and hang out in our Discord voice channels while doing homework. They were already using our ecosystem as a social backdrop for productivity.

RMH Study was a natural extension. We already had the infrastructure — real-time WebSocket connections, user accounts, room management, leaderboard systems. The technical lift was modest. The impact was not.

Since launching the beta, we've seen users log tens of thousands of focused minutes. More importantly, we've seen **retention patterns that look nothing like a game**. People come back daily, not because of dopamine loops or loot boxes, but because they have a calculus exam on Friday and their study group is meeting in Room 4 at 7pm.

## The Design Philosophy

We made deliberate choices to keep RMH Study honest:

- **No gamification traps.** There are no streaks that punish you for missing a day. No premium tiers that gate basic functionality. No dark patterns that guilt you into opening the app. We've seen what happens when productivity tools adopt the worst habits of mobile gaming, and we want no part of it.
- **Transparency about data.** We show you your own stats and we show aggregate, anonymized room stats. We don't sell your study habits to anyone. We don't build advertising profiles. This is a tool, not a surveillance product.
- **Respect for the science.** The default timer is 25/5 because that's what Cirillo's research supports. You can customize it — some people prefer 50/10, others like 15/3 — but we don't let you set a 4-hour work period because the research is clear that ultra-long focus sessions produce diminishing returns and increased burnout.

## What We've Learned

Building RMH Study taught us something we've carried back into our game development: **the most powerful multiplayer experiences aren't always competitive**. Sometimes they're cooperative. Sometimes they're just *parallel* — people doing their own thing, together.

Body-doubling works because it acknowledges a basic truth about human psychology: we are social animals, and our capacity for focus is shaped by our environment. A "study with me" stream provides a weak version of that environment. A synchronized, interactive study room provides a strong one.

The Pomodoro Technique is nearly 40 years old. "Study with me" streams are a decade-old phenomenon. Body-doubling has decades of clinical support behind it. None of this is new. What's new is putting it all together in a real-time, browser-based experience that requires no downloads, no installations, and no subscriptions.

We built RMH Study because we believe the web is the right platform for tools like this — **instant, accessible, social by default**. And because, frankly, we needed it ourselves. Every blog post you read on this site, every game we ship, every feature we debug — there's a good chance it happened during a Pomodoro session in one of our own rooms.

If you want to try it, just open a browser. That's the whole setup process. We think Francesco Cirillo would approve.
