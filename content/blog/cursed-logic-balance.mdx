## The Fairness Paradox

Here's the impossible thing about designing AI opponents: **a fair AI can feel unfair, and an unfair AI can feel fair.** This paradox has consumed more of my development time than any other aspect of Cursed Logic.

Consider this scenario. The Protocol makes a perfectly rational decision based on its behavioral model. It happens to counter your move three rounds in a row. From the Protocol's perspective, it's playing well. From your perspective, it feels like it's reading your inputs — like the game is cheating. The AI is being completely fair. But it doesn't *feel* fair.

Now consider the opposite. We secretly give the Protocol a 5% chance to make a suboptimal move each round. This is technically unfair — we're handicapping the AI. But when the Protocol occasionally plays a move that doesn't quite make sense, it feels more *human*. And human opponents feel fair because they make mistakes. The unfair AI feels fairer than the fair one.

Navigating this paradox is the central challenge of balancing Cursed Logic.

## What "Fair" Actually Means

After months of iteration, I've arrived at a working definition of fairness for Cursed Logic: **an AI feels fair when the player can identify why they lost.** Not immediately — not in the heat of the moment. But after reflecting on a lost duel, a player should be able to point to specific decisions and say: "That's where I went wrong. I should have read the mode shift. I shouldn't have Probed there. I should have Blocked instead of Striking."

When losses feel like *lessons*, the AI feels fair. When losses feel like *punishments*, the AI feels unfair. The difference isn't in the AI's behavior — it's in the player's ability to understand cause and effect.

This insight guided our entire balancing approach. Every AI behavior needs to be **readable** — not predictable, but learnable. Players need to be able to develop mental models of the Protocol that are approximately correct, even if they're never perfectly accurate.

## The Readability Framework

We balance the Protocol around three levels of readability:

### Level 1: Mode Transparency

The Protocol's current mode is always visible to the player (you don't even need to Probe for it). This is the most basic layer of readability — you always know whether the Protocol is pressuring, defensive, or recovering. This information alone doesn't tell you what it will do, but it narrows the likely action space.

Keeping mode visible was controversial during development. Some team members felt that hiding the mode would add depth. I argued the opposite: visible modes give players a framework for understanding Protocol behavior. Without that framework, behavior feels random. With it, behavior feels like a puzzle.

### Level 2: Behavioral Patterns

Within each mode, the Protocol has tendencies. In pressuring mode, it Strikes approximately 50% of the time. In defensive mode, it Blocks about 45% of the time. These aren't rigid rules — there's randomness involved — but over multiple rounds, the patterns emerge.

These percentages are the core balance levers. Adjusting how strongly the Protocol leans toward specific actions in each mode is how we tune difficulty and feel. Early versions had much more extreme tendencies (70-80% favoring the mode-appropriate action), which made the Protocol predictable. Current values create tendencies that are detectable but not exploitable — you can say "it's probably going to Strike" but you can't say "it's definitely going to Strike."

### Level 3: Variant Signatures

Each Protocol variant has distinct behavioral signatures that experienced players learn to recognize. The Hammer's relentless aggression. The Watcher's patient setup. The Mirror's eerie mimicry. The Glitch's controlled chaos.

These signatures create a **progressive skill curve**. New players learn to read modes. Intermediate players learn to adapt to variants. Advanced players learn to exploit variant-specific weaknesses. This layered skill curve keeps the game challenging at every level without overwhelming beginners.

## The Anti-Streak System

The most important balance mechanism in Cursed Logic is invisible to players: the **anti-streak system.** If the Protocol successfully counters the player's action more than three consecutive rounds, the system intervenes. Not by making the Protocol play badly — that would feel patronizing. Instead, it slightly adjusts the Protocol's action weights to spread its choices more evenly, reducing the chance of another perfect counter.

This system exists because of the perception problem I described earlier. Even when streaks are statistically normal (a 3-round counter streak has roughly a 1-in-27 chance per set of three rounds, which happens more often than you'd think), they feel *terrible*. The anti-streak system sacrifices a tiny bit of AI optimality to preserve the player's sense of agency.

```typescript
function adjustForStreak(weights: ActionWeights, counterStreak: number): ActionWeights {
  if (counterStreak < 3) return weights;

  const spreadFactor = 0.15 * (counterStreak - 2);
  const average = Object.values(weights).reduce((a, b) => a + b) / 4;

  return Object.fromEntries(
    Object.entries(weights).map(([action, weight]) => [
      action,
      weight + (average - weight) * spreadFactor
    ])
  ) as ActionWeights;
}
```

The spread factor increases with streak length, so longer streaks produce stronger correction. A 3-round streak gets a gentle nudge. A 5-round streak (if it somehow happens) gets a significant adjustment. This ensures that extraordinarily unlucky runs are virtually impossible.

## Skill Expression in Simplicity

One of the biggest challenges in balancing Cursed Logic was ensuring **skill expression** — the ability for better players to consistently outperform worse players. In a complex game, skill expression comes from mechanical mastery, build optimization, resource management, and dozens of other dimensions. In a four-action game, where's the skill?

It turns out there's plenty, but it's all *decision-making* skill:

**Read accuracy.** Better players develop more accurate mental models of Protocol behavior. They predict mode shifts earlier. They recognize variant signatures faster. They use Probe data more effectively. This is genuine skill, and it creates a measurable performance gap between experienced and inexperienced players.

**Adaptation speed.** When a mutation changes the rules, better players adapt faster. They recalculate action values, adjust their strategy, and commit to a new approach within a round or two. Weaker players take longer to adapt, losing rounds to confusion while they figure out the new situation.

**Risk calibration.** Better players understand when to gamble and when to play safe. They know that Probing from behind is usually a mistake. They know that Preparing against The Hammer is suicidal. They know that three consecutive Strikes condition the Protocol to Block — and they know exactly when to break that pattern with a Prepare. This calibration is learned through experience and refined through analysis.

**Emotional regulation.** This is the hardest skill and the most important. Cursed Logic is designed to create frustration and pressure. Better players manage those emotions. They don't tilt after a bad round. They don't panic when the Protocol shifts to pressuring mode. They stay analytical when the game is screaming at them to be emotional.

## Win Rate Targets

We balance the Protocol around specific win rate targets for different player skill levels:

- **New players** (first 5 duels): 30-40% win rate
- **Intermediate players** (10-20 duels): 45-55% win rate
- **Experienced players** (50+ duels): 55-65% win rate
- **Expert players** (100+ duels): 60-70% win rate

Notice that even expert players don't win every duel. This is intentional. If the best players always won, the Protocol would feel trivial. A 60-70% win rate means that experts win most of the time but still face genuine threat. Every duel carries real stakes, even for the most skilled duelists.

These win rate targets were achieved through extensive playtesting and iterative adjustment of the Protocol's behavioral parameters. It's not a science — it's a craft. You tune a number, playtest for twenty duels, analyze the data, tune again. Dozens of cycles of this produced the current balance, and it's still evolving.

## The Difficulty Curve

Cursed Logic doesn't have a difficulty setting. Instead, difficulty emerges naturally from the variant system. The Hammer is harder than The Watcher for aggressive players (because it out-aggresses them) but easier for defensive players (because it's predictable). The Mirror is hard for habitual players but easy for adaptable ones.

This organic difficulty system means that "harder" and "easier" are relative to individual playstyles rather than universal. Every player has a variant they struggle with and a variant they dominate. This personalized difficulty creates engagement because the challenge always feels *personal* — it's not a difficulty setting that someone else chose, it's a specific opponent that exploits your specific weaknesses.

## Ongoing Balance Work

Balance is never done. Every new mutation, shop upgrade, or Protocol variant shifts the equilibrium. A new mutation might inadvertently favor defensive play. A new shop item might make Probe too powerful. A new variant might have a degenerate strategy that trivializes it.

I've learned to embrace this ongoing process rather than fight it. Cursed Logic is a living game, and its balance is alive too. Each adjustment teaches me something new about the interaction between systems, and that growing understanding makes every future adjustment more confident and precise.

The Protocol is never perfectly balanced. But it's getting closer, one duel at a time.

*Fair fights aren't easy fights. Step into the arena.*
