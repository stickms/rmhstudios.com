## The Moment It Clicked

I was standing in front of a laundry basket at 11 PM on a Tuesday, mindlessly sorting clothes into piles by color. Red here, blue there, that yellow shirt goes in its own pile. My brain was on autopilot — and then it hit me. *This is a game.*

Not a complicated, lore-heavy epic. Not a roguelike with procedural generation. Just a clean, satisfying loop: items fall, you sort them, you try to keep up. It was the kind of idea that feels so obvious you're sure someone else has already done it. But when I started sketching it out, I realized there was real depth hiding in the simplicity.

## Why Laundry?

At RMH Studios, we've always been drawn to games that take the mundane and make it magical. There's something deeply satisfying about turning a real-world task into an arcade experience. Think about it — *Tetris* is essentially packing a suitcase. *Fruit Ninja* is chopping vegetables. The best casual games find the hidden joy in everyday actions.

Laundry sorting has a natural gameplay loop baked right in:

- **Recognition** — identify the color of each item
- **Decision** — choose the right bin
- **Execution** — physically move the item before the next one arrives
- **Pressure** — everything speeds up as the pile grows

That's a complete game design framework disguised as a chore. The key insight was that sorting laundry already *feels* like a game when you're rushing to finish before the dryer buzzes. We just needed to lean into that feeling.

## Early Vision

From the very first sketch, I knew a few things had to be true about Laundry Sort:

### 1. Physics Must Feel Real

Clothes don't behave like rigid blocks. They tumble, they rotate, they bounce off each other in unpredictable ways. I wanted items to fall with real gravity, spin with angular velocity, and collide with satisfying weight. No static sprites snapping to a grid — everything needed to feel *alive*.

### 2. The Aesthetic Had to Be Dark and Arcade-Like

I was tired of casual games that look like they were designed for toddlers. I wanted Laundry Sort to feel like something you'd find in a dimly lit arcade in the '80s — black background, neon accents, glowing UI elements. Something that felt cool to play, not embarrassing.

### 3. Sixty Seconds, No More

The session length had to be short. I wanted the kind of game you play while waiting for coffee, while riding the bus, while procrastinating on actual laundry. Sixty seconds felt perfect — long enough to develop a rhythm, short enough to always want "one more round."

### 4. Four Colors, Four Bins

Simplicity was non-negotiable. Red, blue, yellow, green. That's it. No gradients, no ambiguous shades, no "is that teal or cyan?" arguments. Four distinct colors that anyone can identify instantly. The challenge comes from speed and physics chaos, not from color theory.

## Making the Mundane Exciting

The real design challenge wasn't the mechanics — it was the *feeling*. How do you make sorting laundry feel exciting? The answer, I discovered, lives in three places:

**Juice.** Every successful sort needed to feel like a tiny victory. A flash of color, a satisfying sound, a subtle screen shake. We drew inspiration from games like *Peggle* and *Bejeweled* — games that make simple actions feel monumental through audiovisual feedback.

**Chaos.** The physics engine is the secret weapon. When five shirts are tumbling through the air, bouncing off bin walls, spinning wildly — that's not frustrating, that's *entertaining*. The unpredictability of the physics creates emergent moments that a scripted system never could.

**Urgency.** The 60-second timer transforms casual sorting into a frantic race. When you've got 15 seconds left and clothes are piling up faster than you can sort them, your heart rate genuinely increases. That's the magic zone where a simple concept becomes a compelling game.

## From Napkin Sketch to Prototype

I spent the first week just prototyping the core loop in HTML5 Canvas. No fancy framework, no game engine — just raw canvas drawing calls and a basic physics simulation. The very first version had rectangles falling and a mouse cursor that could drag them. It was ugly. It was buggy. And it was *immediately fun*.

That's when I knew we had something. When a prototype made of colored rectangles and placeholder physics is already satisfying to play, you know the core loop is solid. Everything else — the polish, the art, the sound design — is just amplification.
