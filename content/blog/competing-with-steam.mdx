## The Giant in the Room

In January 2025, Steam hit a new record: **34.6 million peak concurrent users.** Valve's platform hosts over 70,000 games, processes billions of dollars in transactions annually, and has become so dominant that "launching on Steam" is synonymous with "launching on PC" for most developers.

So when we say we're building games for the browser instead of Steam, people look at us like we've lost our minds.

We haven't. But we also aren't going to pretend the answer to "Can browser games compete with Steam?" is a simple yes. It's more nuanced than that, and we believe in giving you the honest version.

## What Steam Does Well

Credit where it's due. Steam is dominant for real reasons:

**Discovery and storefronts.** Steam's recommendation algorithm, curated lists, and seasonal sales create a discovery engine that no browser game platform can match today. Getting featured on the Steam front page can generate millions of dollars in revenue overnight.

**Community infrastructure.** Steam reviews, forums, Workshop mod support, and the Steam Community provide a built-in social layer that developers get for free. This infrastructure took Valve decades and enormous resources to build.

**Trust and payments.** Hundreds of millions of people have their credit cards saved in Steam. The purchase friction is near zero for returning customers. Refund policies provide consumer protection. Players trust Steam with their money.

**Achievements, cloud saves, and profiles.** The meta-game of Steam — your profile, your game library, your achievement showcase — creates switching costs that keep players on the platform.

We respect all of this. We'd be foolish not to.

## Where Steam Falls Short

But Steam's dominance comes with real costs, and they're costs that indie developers feel acutely.

### The 30% Cut

Steam takes 30% of every sale for games earning under $10 million. That's not a fee — it's nearly a third of your revenue. For an indie studio operating on thin margins, that 30% can be the difference between sustainability and shutdown.

Epic Games Store launched in 2018 explicitly to challenge this, offering an 88/12 split. Despite spending hundreds of millions on exclusives and free game giveaways, Epic has struggled to dent Steam's market share meaningfully — a testament to how entrenched Steam's network effects are. But the debate Epic started was important: **the 30% platform tax is not an immutable law of nature.**

Browser games pay 0% to a platform holder. Our revenue share with payment processors (Stripe charges ~2.9% + 30¢ per transaction) is a fraction of what Steam takes.

### The Discovery Problem

Steam's discovery algorithm is a double-edged sword. Yes, it can surface your game to millions. But it can also bury it. With thousands of games launching every month, the median Steam game earns very little. A 2023 analysis by game industry consultant Simon Carless estimated that the median indie game on Steam earns around $1,500 in its first month.

The platform giveth, and the platform taketh away. You're building on someone else's land.

### The Download Barrier

Every Steam game requires downloading a client, then downloading the game itself. For a AAA title, this is expected. For a casual party game or a quick puzzle experience, it's a significant barrier. No one downloads a Steam client to play a five-minute typing race.

Browser games load in seconds. The entire concept of "installation" doesn't exist. You click a link, you're playing. This zero-friction access is our single greatest competitive advantage.

## The Itch.io Alternative

It's worth mentioning itch.io, which has carved out a genuine niche as the indie-friendliest game platform. Itch.io lets developers set their own revenue split (including 0%), supports browser-based games directly, and has fostered an incredible community of experimental and jam games.

But itch.io's audience is relatively small and skews heavily toward other developers and game jam enthusiasts. It's an amazing community platform, but it's not a commercial distribution channel for most studios.

We admire what itch.io represents — open, developer-first, low-friction — and we're essentially building on those same principles, but with our own domain, our own community, and our own infrastructure.

## The Browser's Unique Advantages

Here's where we get specific about why browser distribution isn't just "Steam but worse":

### Zero Friction, Truly

We keep saying this because it's the foundation of everything. When someone shares an RMHbox link in a group chat, every recipient is one click from playing. Not "one click from a store page where they might create an account, enter payment info, download a client, download the game, and then play." One click from **playing**.

This matters enormously for multiplayer games. The host doesn't need to check whether everyone has the game installed. They send a link. Everyone joins. The game starts.

### Cross-Platform by Default

A browser game runs on Windows, Mac, Linux, ChromeOS, and mobile devices (with responsive design) from a single codebase. Steam supports Windows, Mac, and Linux, but each requires separate builds and testing. Console ports require entirely separate development efforts.

We write our games once. They run everywhere a modern browser runs.

### Instant Updates

When we fix a bug or ship a feature, every player gets it on their next page load. No download queues, no update prompts, no "restarting to apply update." The deploy-to-player pipeline is measured in minutes, not days.

### Linkability

Every game, every room, every session can have a unique URL. This makes sharing, embedding, streaming, and integrating with other platforms trivially easy. You can't deep-link into a Steam game from a Discord message and have it launch instantly.

## The Honest Tradeoffs

We promised an honest assessment, so here's what we give up by choosing the browser:

**Performance ceiling.** Browser games can't match the raw performance of native applications. WebGL and WebGPU are impressive, but they're running inside a browser sandbox. For our games — party games, card games, puzzle games, 2D and stylized 3D — this doesn't matter. For a photorealistic open-world RPG, it would.

**No built-in storefront discovery.** We don't have Steam's algorithm sending players our way. Our growth depends on organic sharing, community building, and eventually our own marketing. This is harder, but it means our relationship with players is direct, not mediated by a platform.

**Offline play is limited.** Browser games generally require an internet connection. Service workers and progressive web app techniques can enable some offline functionality, but it's not the same as a fully offline native game.

**Perception.** There's still a stigma that browser games are "lesser" — that real games are downloaded executables. This perception is changing, but it hasn't fully changed yet. Every game we ship that surprises people with its quality helps erode that stigma.

## Not Competing — Expanding

Here's our real thesis: **we're not trying to compete with Steam. We're trying to reach the players Steam can't.**

Steam serves an audience that's willing to install a client, browse a store, make purchases, and manage a library. That's a huge audience, but it's not everyone.

We're serving the audience that clicks a link in a group chat. The audience that wants to play something *right now* without committing to anything. The audience that's on a Chromebook at school, or a work laptop without admin privileges, or a phone without storage space.

These audiences have been underserved by the gaming industry for years. Flash games served them once, and when Flash died in December 2020, a gap opened. io games like Agar.io and Slither.io partially filled it, proving that browser games could attract millions of concurrent players.

We're building the next generation to fill that gap — with richer experiences, better technology, and a studio behind them that's in it for the long run.

## The Bottom Line

Can browser games compete with Steam? Not on Steam's terms. But on our terms — instant access, zero friction, cross-platform, community-driven, developer-owned — the browser offers something Steam fundamentally cannot.

We're not trying to be the next Steam. We're trying to make Steam optional.
