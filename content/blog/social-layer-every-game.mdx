## The Loneliness Problem

In May 2023, U.S. Surgeon General Dr. Vivek Murthy issued an advisory titled *Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation*, calling social disconnection a public health crisis on par with smoking and obesity. The advisory cited research showing that lacking social connection increases the risk of premature death by 26% and is associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, and dementia.

This isn't abstract public health data. It describes the lived experience of millions of people — particularly young adults — who spend hours each day in digital environments but still feel fundamentally alone.

Games can be part of the solution. Not as therapy, not as a replacement for real relationships, but as **spaces where genuine human connection happens naturally**, as a byproduct of shared experience. This is why we believe every game needs a social layer — not because social features drive engagement metrics, but because connection is what makes games matter.

## Games as Social Infrastructure

The most culturally significant gaming moments of recent years haven't been about gameplay — they've been about togetherness.

**Fortnite's in-game concerts** — Travis Scott's Astronomical event in April 2020 drew 12.3 million concurrent players. Ariana Grande's Rift Tour in August 2021 similarly attracted millions. These weren't just spectacles; they were shared experiences that people remember and talk about years later.

**Roblox as a hangout space.** Roblox's 71.5 million daily active users (as of Q3 2024) don't just play games — they socialize. For millions of kids and teenagers, Roblox is where they meet friends after school, the way previous generations met at malls or playgrounds.

**Animal Crossing during lockdown.** When COVID-19 lockdowns began in 2020, Animal Crossing: New Horizons became a lifeline. People held weddings, graduation ceremonies, and birthday parties on their islands. The game's value wasn't its mechanics — it was the social space it provided.

**Among Us as social glue.** Among Us exploded in 2020 not because of its game design (which had existed since 2018 with minimal traction) but because it gave friend groups a reason to get on voice chat and laugh together.

The pattern is clear: **the games that matter most are the ones that create shared social context.** The gameplay is the scaffold. The connection is the building.

## How We Build Social Into Everything

At RMH Studios, social features aren't an afterthought bolted onto finished games. They're part of the foundational architecture.

### RMHbox: Social by Nature

RMHbox is a party game platform, so it's inherently social — but the design decisions that make it *effectively* social are intentional:

**Lobbies are gathering spaces.** When you create or join an RMHbox room, you're not immediately thrown into a game. You're in a lobby where you can chat, see who's present, and build anticipation. The lobby is the porch before the party.

**Games are designed for reactions.** Our minigames are tuned for moments that provoke laughter, surprise, and friendly trash talk. A well-designed party game creates stories that players retell — "Remember when you fell off the platform three times in a row?" These micro-stories are the raw material of friendship.

**Spectating is a feature.** Players waiting between rounds or arriving mid-game can watch the action. Spectating transforms waiting from dead time into entertainment, and it lets newcomers observe before participating — lowering the social anxiety of joining a group.

### RMHTube: Watching Together

RMHTube — our watch party platform — is built entirely on the premise that watching alone is worse than watching together. Synchronized video playback, real-time chat, and shared playlists turn passive media consumption into active social experience.

The technical challenge is interesting: keeping video playback synchronized across multiple clients with different network conditions and buffer states requires constant correction. But the social outcome is worth the engineering complexity. People use RMHTube for movie nights, music video sessions, and watching gaming tournaments together. The content is the excuse. The company is the point.

### RMH Study: Productive Together

RMH Study applies the same social logic to productivity. Pomodoro rooms let people work alongside each other in timed focus sessions, with break periods for casual conversation.

This mirrors the real-world phenomenon of "body doubling" — the productivity boost people get from simply being in the presence of others who are also working. Coworking spaces, library study halls, and coffee shop work sessions all leverage this effect. RMH Study digitizes it.

The ambient social presence — knowing someone else is in the room, focused on their own work — creates gentle accountability without pressure. It's social without being distracting.

### RMH Type: Competing Together

Typing tests are inherently solo activities. RMH Type makes them social by adding real-time multiplayer races. You're not just measuring your WPM against a historical average — you're racing against someone right now, watching their cursor advance alongside yours.

Competition is a social act. It requires an opponent, which means it requires another person. Even the simplest competitive mechanic — "who can type this passage faster?" — transforms a solitary exercise into a shared experience with stakes, tension, and resolution.

### Daily Games: Rituals Together

RMHdle and RMHConnections, our Discord daily games, create social touchpoints that recur every single day. When everyone in a community solves the same puzzle, the puzzle becomes a conversation starter. "How did you get today's RMHdle?" is a greeting, a bonding ritual, and a low-stakes competition wrapped in five words.

## The Design Principles

Across all our products, a few social design principles guide our decisions:

### Lower the Barrier to Social Interaction

Every layer of friction between a player and a social experience is a layer where they might give up. No account required for first play. No friend codes to exchange. No complex room setup. Send a link, click a link, you're in a room with people.

### Make Being Together the Default

Solo modes exist where they make sense (Signal Forge is a single-player game), but wherever possible, the default experience includes other people. The multiplayer lobby is the home screen, not a menu option buried three clicks deep.

### Support Asynchronous Sociality

Not all social experiences need to be real-time. Daily games let players participate on their own schedule while still being part of a shared experience. Leaderboards let players compete without being online simultaneously. Shared profiles let players express identity persistently.

### Create Stories, Not Just Scores

The most social games are the ones that generate memorable moments — unexpected outcomes, dramatic reversals, absurd situations. We design for stories. A minigame where someone wins by one point after a comeback is more social than one where the best player wins every time, because the comeback creates a story worth telling.

### Respect Boundaries

Social features should invite, not coerce. We don't send push notifications saying "your friends are playing without you." We don't create FOMO mechanics that punish players for not being constantly available. Social features work best when they make connection easy and absence painless.

## The Broader Responsibility

We don't think games alone can solve the loneliness epidemic. That's a societal challenge that requires systemic solutions — urban design, healthcare, education, labor policy. Games are one small piece.

But games are a piece that reaches billions of people, many of them in the demographics most affected by social isolation. If we can make our games places where people feel genuinely connected — even briefly, even casually — that's a meaningful contribution.

The Surgeon General's advisory noted that digital tools can either exacerbate or alleviate isolation, depending on how they're designed. Passive social media consumption tends to increase loneliness. Active, reciprocal social experiences tend to decrease it. Games — when designed for genuine interaction rather than solitary engagement loops — fall into the second category.

## Social Is Not a Feature

Here's the core conviction: **social is not a feature you add to a game. It's the reason games exist.**

Humans have been playing games together for thousands of years — not because the games were sophisticated, but because playing together is one of the most natural forms of human connection. A ball, a set of rules, and some friends. That's the oldest social technology on Earth.

Every game we build at RMH Studios starts with a question: "How will this bring people together?" If we don't have a compelling answer, we rethink the design.

Because a game you play alone is entertainment. A game you play with others is a relationship. And relationships are what we're really building.
