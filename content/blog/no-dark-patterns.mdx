## The Cost of Manipulation

The gaming industry has a monetization problem. Not a revenue problem — the global games market generates over $180 billion annually. The problem is *how* much of that revenue is extracted through design patterns that exploit psychological vulnerabilities rather than deliver genuine value.

Loot boxes, gacha mechanics, artificial scarcity timers, pay-to-win progression, FOMO-driven battle passes, misleading currency conversions — these aren't features. They're **dark patterns**, and they've become so normalized that many players (and developers) have stopped questioning them.

We question them. And we've made a deliberate decision at RMH Studios: **none of these patterns will ever appear in our games.**

## The Regulatory Reckoning

The industry's reliance on manipulative monetization has attracted serious regulatory scrutiny, and the consequences are already material.

In **December 2022**, the Federal Trade Commission reached a landmark **$520 million settlement with Epic Games** over Fortnite's practices. The FTC alleged that Epic used dark patterns to trick players into making unwanted purchases, charged parents for children's in-game purchases without proper consent, and locked accounts of players who disputed charges. The settlement included $245 million in refunds to consumers — one of the largest FTC gaming enforcement actions in history.

**Belgium banned loot boxes outright in 2018**, classifying them as a form of gambling under its Gaming Commission. EA pulled FIFA Ultimate Team packs from the Belgian market rather than comply with transparency requirements. The Netherlands followed with similar enforcement, though EA successfully appealed the Dutch ruling in 2020 on narrow legal grounds.

In the UK, the **Digital, Culture, Media and Sport Committee published a report on immersive and addictive technologies in September 2019** that explicitly called loot boxes "a form of gambling" and recommended they be regulated under the Gambling Act. While the UK government's response has been slow, the parliamentary report signaled a clear direction: regulators are watching, and the industry's self-regulation has failed.

These aren't fringe positions. The consensus among researchers, regulators, and increasingly among players themselves is that many common monetization practices cross ethical lines.

## What We Mean by "No Dark Patterns"

It's easy to say "we don't do dark patterns." It's harder to define exactly what that means and hold yourself to it. Here's our framework:

### No Randomized Paid Rewards

If a player spends money in one of our games, they know exactly what they're getting. No loot boxes, no gacha pulls, no mystery bundles. Randomized rewards can be fun as gameplay mechanics (roguelike games are built on randomness), but **the moment you attach real money to a random outcome, you've built a slot machine.**

### No Artificial Urgency

Limited-time offers, countdown timers on purchases, "only 3 left!" scarcity messaging — these are techniques borrowed from e-commerce dark patterns, and they work by triggering anxiety rather than desire. We don't use them.

If something is available in our games, it's available. If we retire content, we communicate it clearly and with notice. We don't manufacture pressure to spend.

### No Pay-to-Win

In competitive games like RMH Type or the minigames in RMHbox, competitive integrity is sacred. No amount of money should give a player an in-game advantage over someone who hasn't paid. Cosmetics, personalizations, quality-of-life features — these are all fair game. Gameplay advantages for money are not.

### No Manipulative UX

This one is subtle but important. Dark patterns aren't just about monetization — they also show up in UX design:

- Making the "buy" button larger and more colorful than the "skip" button
- Burying cancellation flows behind multiple confirmation screens
- Using confusing premium currency conversions to obscure real costs
- Auto-selecting the most expensive option in purchase flows

We design our interfaces to be clear, honest, and respectful of the player's decision-making autonomy. If a player chooses not to spend money, the UX should make that as easy as choosing to spend.

### No Exploitative Engagement Loops

Idle games and live-service games often use notification pressure, daily login streaks, and loss-aversion mechanics to drive compulsive engagement. Our game **Temple of Joy** is literally an idle clicker that explores the psychology of the hedonic treadmill — we're acutely aware of how these mechanics can be weaponized.

We design for engagement that comes from genuine interest, not from fear of missing out. If a player stops playing one of our games for a week, they shouldn't feel punished when they come back.

## The Business Case for Ethics

Let's be practical: does ethical design hurt revenue? In the short term, it almost certainly leaves money on the table. Loot boxes and FOMO mechanics are effective precisely because they exploit deep psychological biases. A studio that refuses to use them is voluntarily capping certain revenue streams.

But we believe the long-term math favors ethical design, for several reasons:

- **Regulatory risk is real.** Studios that build their revenue models on loot boxes are one legislative session away from a business model crisis. Belgium already happened. The EU's Digital Services Act is expanding. Building on exploitative mechanics is building on unstable ground.
- **Player trust compounds.** A player who trusts your monetization earns you more lifetime value than a player who feels tricked into a single purchase and never comes back.
- **The market is shifting.** Games like *Balatro*, *Hades*, and *Stardew Valley* have proven that massive commercial success is possible with fair, transparent business models. Players are actively seeking alternatives to exploitative free-to-play.

## Our Promise

We're not saints, and we're not building a charity. RMH Studios is a business, and we need revenue to keep making games. But there are lines we will not cross.

**Every transaction in an RMH game will be transparent, optional, and honestly presented.** No hidden costs, no manipulative nudges, no exploitative targeting of vulnerable players.

We believe you can build a sustainable game studio without dark patterns. We believe the games industry will be better off when more studios make that choice. And we believe our players deserve to be treated as people to delight, not wallets to extract.

That's the standard we hold ourselves to. Hold us to it.
