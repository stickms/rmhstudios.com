## Looking Forward

Nine months into Signal Forge's development, the game has grown from a whiteboard sketch into a fully playable roguelike deckbuilder with 60+ cards, intelligent enemies, and a keyword system I'm genuinely proud of. But we're far from done. In many ways, the foundation is just now solid enough to build the *really* exciting stuff on top of it.

This post is a transparent look at where Signal Forge is going — the features I'm actively developing, the mechanics I'm prototyping, and the long-term vision for the game. Think of this as a living document. Some of these ideas will ship as described. Some will morph into something unrecognizable. Some might get cut entirely. That's the nature of game development, and I'd rather share the messy, honest roadmap than a polished marketing timeline.

## Near-Term: The Next Three Months

### New Waveform: Square

The four current waveform types (Pulse, Sine, Saw, Noise) are getting a sibling: **Square**. Where Pulse is a sharp, single-cycle burst, Square waves have a symmetrical duty cycle — equal time at high and low states. In gameplay terms, Square cards will specialize in **duality effects**: abilities that trigger differently based on whether you're at "high" or "low" state.

The Square archetype introduces a toggle mechanic — each Square card flips your state between High and Low when played. High-state Square cards deal bonus damage. Low-state Square cards generate bonus shields. The strategic challenge is sequencing your Square cards to be in the right state at the right time.

```
// Square card example
Square Oscillate: Cost 1 | Toggles state
  High State: Deal 6 damage, apply 2 Vulnerable
  Low State: Gain 8 Shield, draw 1 card
"Alternation is power. Symmetry is strength."
```

I'm excited about Square because it adds a completely new axis of decision-making without adding complexity to existing archetypes. If you don't want to play Square cards, you can ignore them entirely. But if you lean in, you get access to a rich, state-based playstyle that rewards careful sequencing.

### Enemy Faction: The Harmonics

Currently, Signal Forge's enemies are individual encounters without faction identity. The Harmonics are the first enemy **faction** — a group of thematically connected enemies that appear together and synergize with each other.

Harmonic enemies are based on harmonic overtone series. Each individual enemy in a Harmonic group represents a different overtone, and they buff each other when in proximity:

- **Fundamental** — The anchor. Tanky, moderate damage, provides shields to other Harmonics.
- **Second Harmonic** — Aggressive. Deals double damage when Fundamental is alive.
- **Third Harmonic** — Supporter. Heals and buffs other Harmonics each turn.
- **Overtone Swarm** — Weak individually, but appears in groups of 3-4. If any survive, they regenerate the others.

The Harmonics force players to think about **target priority** — a strategic dimension that barely exists in current Signal Forge. Do you kill the Fundamental to weaken the group? Rush the Third Harmonic to stop healing? AOE the Overtone Swarm before they regenerate? Different deck compositions will prefer different kill orders.

### Expanded Event System

The between-combat event system is getting a major expansion. Currently, events are mostly transactional: gain gold, upgrade a card, trade health for resources. The new events will be more narratively rich and mechanically complex:

- **Signal Labs** — Choose a card from your deck to "experiment" on. It gains a random additional keyword, but there's a 25% chance it becomes Glitched instead.
- **Frequency Auction** — Bid Static resources against an AI bidder for powerful cards. Overbid and you start the next fight with reduced Static.
- **Echo Chamber** — Duplicate any card in your deck, but both copies become slightly weaker (-10% stats). Do you value consistency over raw power?
- **The Dead Channel** — Remove up to 3 cards from your deck for free. Deck thinning is one of the most powerful strategies in deckbuilders, so this event is intentionally rare.

## Mid-Term: Three to Six Months

### The Campaign Structure

Right now, Signal Forge runs are standalone — you start fresh each time with no meta-progression. While some players prefer pure roguelike runs, most players want *some* sense of progression between runs. The campaign structure will provide that.

**The Signal Archive** is a persistent meta-progression system:

- **Unlockable cards** — Start with a base card pool of ~30 cards. Win runs to unlock new cards that enter the available pool. Eventually all 100+ cards are available, but new players aren't overwhelmed.
- **Transmitter upgrades** — Permanent upgrade paths that slightly modify run parameters. "+1 starting Static" or "Start with a Rare card" — small but meaningful advantages that reward dedication.
- **Lore fragments** — Each run drops fragments of Signal Forge's backstory. Collect enough and you piece together the world's narrative: what are these signals? Who's transmitting them? Why are you forging them? The story unfolds through play.

### Boss Encounters

The current enemy roster is missing a critical element: **bosses**. Each zone of a run will culminate in a boss fight — a multi-phase encounter against a powerful enemy with unique mechanics.

The three planned bosses for the initial release:

#### The Carrier Wave
A massive, slow enemy that periodically emits a devastating carrier signal. Players must "modulate" their attacks to match the carrier's frequency (indicated by a visual rhythm mechanic) to deal double damage. Attacks at the wrong frequency deal half damage. It's a test of timing and rhythm.

#### The Feedback Loop
An enemy that copies and amplifies the player's strategy. If you deal heavy damage, it retaliates with amplified counterattacks. If you stack shields, it stacks bigger shields. The only way to win is to constantly shift strategies mid-fight, never letting the Feedback Loop lock onto your pattern.

#### The Dead Signal
A corrupted entity that exists in a state of permanent Glitch. All of the player's cards become temporarily Glitched during this fight. The battle is about *managing corruption* — using Glitch effects to your advantage rather than fighting against them. It's the ultimate test of the Glitch mechanic, and it's designed to make Glitch-avoidant players reconsider their relationship with corruption.

### Difficulty Scaling: Signal Strength

I'm implementing a "Signal Strength" system (similar to Slay the Spire's Ascension levels) that adds incremental difficulty modifiers:

- **Signal Strength 1**: Enemies have +10% HP.
- **Signal Strength 5**: Enemies start with 1 random buff. Events are more dangerous.
- **Signal Strength 10**: Boss encounters have an additional phase. Start with 1 Glitch card.
- **Signal Strength 15**: Enemies act on the first turn before the player. Healing is reduced by 25%.
- **Signal Strength 20**: Maximum difficulty. Only for the truly dedicated.

Each level is unlocked by completing a run at the previous level, creating a long-term challenge that keeps experienced players engaged for months.

## Long-Term: Six to Twelve Months

### Multiplayer: Signal Clash

This is the big dream. **Signal Clash** is a competitive multiplayer mode where two players build decks and fight each other through a series of encounters. It's not direct PvP — instead, both players tackle the same enemy encounters simultaneously, and the player who completes them faster (or with more health remaining) wins the round.

Signal Clash adds draft mechanics: both players draw from the same card pool, and picking a card denies it from your opponent. This introduces a draft metagame where you're not just building *your* best deck, but also considering what you're giving *them*.

The technical infrastructure for this already exists conceptually — Signal Forge's state management through Zustand is designed to support serializable game states, which can be synced between clients. The challenge is more in balance and design: a game balanced for PvE might not be balanced for competitive play, and supporting both means maintaining two balance streams.

### Modding Support: The Signal Workshop

The furthest-horizon feature on the roadmap is modding support. I want players to be able to create their own cards, enemies, and even keywords using a visual editor. The `Card` class's keyword pipeline architecture was designed from the start to be extensible — adding a new keyword is a matter of implementing a resolution function and registering it in the proper pipeline phases.

The Signal Workshop would let creators:

- Design custom cards with any keyword combination
- Create custom enemies with authored AI behaviors
- Build custom events with branching choices
- Share creations through an integrated workshop

This is obviously a massive undertaking, and it's firmly in the "vision" category rather than the "roadmap" category. But the foundation is there, and I believe community-created content is what gives a game true longevity.

## The Philosophy Behind the Roadmap

Everything on this roadmap serves one principle: **deepening the core loop without complicating it.** The core loop of Signal Forge — draw cards, make strategic decisions, manage resources, adapt to enemies — should remain accessible to a new player in ten minutes. Every new feature should add *depth* (more strategic options for engaged players) without adding *complexity* (more rules that everyone must learn).

It's a fine line, and I'll inevitably stumble on it. But that's the guiding star. Signal Forge should always feel like a game where you can sit down, play a run, and have a unique experience — whether it's your first run or your hundredth.
