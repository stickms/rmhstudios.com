## The Invisible Half of Combat

When people think about card game strategy, they usually think about damage numbers and card costs. But in Signal Forge, some of the deepest strategic decisions revolve around something less flashy: **status effects**. These persistent conditions that linger on enemies (and sometimes on yourself) create the layered, multi-turn planning that separates a good deckbuilder from a great one.

I think of status effects as the "invisible half" of combat. Your cards are what you *do*. Status effects are what you *set up*. The best plays in Signal Forge usually involve spending a turn applying status effects so that your next three turns hit exponentially harder. That delayed gratification loop — invest now, profit later — is deeply satisfying when it pays off.

## The Status Effect Roster

Signal Forge currently has seven core status effects, each serving a distinct strategic purpose.

### Bleed

**Bleed** is a stacking damage-over-time effect. Each stack of Bleed deals 1 damage to the target at the end of their turn, and then the stack count decreases by 1. So 5 stacks of Bleed deal 5 damage, then become 4 stacks, then deal 4 damage, and so on.

The total damage from Bleed stacks follows the triangular number formula:

{'$$'}
{String.raw`\text{Total Bleed Damage} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} i = \frac{n(n+1)}{2}`}
{'$$'}

This means Bleed scales *quadratically*. 5 stacks deal 15 total damage. 10 stacks deal 55. 20 stacks deal 210. This quadratic scaling is intentional — it rewards players who invest heavily in Bleed application but makes casual Bleed application less impressive.

Bleed synergizes beautifully with several keywords:

- **Multihit** cards apply Bleed per hit, rapidly stacking it.
- **Chain** cards can spread Bleed across multiple enemies in AOE scenarios.
- **Growing** Bleed cards become increasingly efficient stack builders over a fight.

The counterplay: enemies can cleanse Bleed when they enter Recovering mode, and some enemies are naturally resistant. This prevents Bleed from being a universal solution.

### Freeze

**Freeze** is a tempo control effect. Each stack of Freeze reduces the enemy's action intensity by one level. In practice:

- 1 stack: Enemy's damage is reduced by 25% this turn.
- 2 stacks: Enemy's damage is reduced by 50% this turn.
- 3+ stacks: Enemy skips their action entirely.

Freeze stacks don't persist — they're consumed at the end of the enemy's turn. This makes Freeze a *reactive* tool rather than a *proactive* one. You apply Freeze on the turn you need protection, not turns in advance. The ephemeral nature keeps Freeze from being oppressive while still making it a powerful emergency button.

Freeze's design was inspired by the concept of cryogenic signal preservation — "freezing" a signal in time. I wanted the effect to feel like you're literally stopping the enemy's waveform mid-oscillation.

### Vulnerable

**Vulnerable** is the universal damage amplifier. A Vulnerable target takes **50% more damage** from all sources for the duration. Vulnerable lasts for a set number of turns (usually 2-3) and doesn't stack — reapplying Vulnerable refreshes the duration but doesn't increase the multiplier.

The non-stacking design was a deliberate choice. If Vulnerable stacked multiplicatively, it would quickly spiral into absurd damage numbers. By capping at 50%, I keep Vulnerable as a strong setup tool without letting it overshadow the actual damage cards.

Vulnerable is the "glue" keyword — it makes everything else better. A Bleed deck with Vulnerable access deals 50% more DoT damage. A Pulse deck with Vulnerable can burst through enemies that would otherwise survive. It's intentionally universal because it rewards players for *combining* strategies rather than hyper-specializing.

### Weak

**Weak** is Vulnerable's defensive counterpart. A Weak enemy deals **25% less damage** for the duration. Like Vulnerable, Weak has a set duration and doesn't stack.

The asymmetry is intentional: Vulnerable amplifies by 50% but Weak only reduces by 25%. This biases the game toward offense — Signal Forge is designed to reward proactive play over turtling. Making Weak less impactful than Vulnerable ensures that purely defensive strategies are *viable* but not *optimal*.

### Siphon

**Siphon** is my favorite status effect because it's both offensive and defensive simultaneously. A Siphoned enemy loses a portion of their shield each turn, and that shield is transferred to the player.

```typescript
function resolveSiphon(enemy: Enemy, player: Player, stacks: number): void {
  const stolen = Math.min(enemy.shield, stacks * 2);
  enemy.shield -= stolen;
  player.shield += stolen;
  // Siphon doesn't decrease in stacks — it persists
  // until the enemy has no shield left
}
```

Siphon creates a fascinating dynamic: enemies that shield heavily become *fuel*. A Defensive-mode enemy stacking 20 shield per turn is a goldmine for a Siphon deck. This elegantly counters the "enemy turtling" problem that plagues many deckbuilders — instead of being frustrated by a shielded enemy, Siphon players actively *want* enemies to shield.

### Overdrive

**Overdrive** is a self-buff status effect. When active, your cards deal +2 damage per Overdrive stack, but you take +1 damage from all sources. It's the "glass cannon" status — incredible when you can close out a fight quickly, devastating if the fight drags on.

Overdrive pairs beautifully with Pulse decks that want to end fights fast. Three stacks of Overdrive on a Pulse turn can add 6+ damage across your played cards, but you're taking 3 extra damage from the enemy's attack. The math either works in your favor or it doesn't, and knowing which fights to Overdrive and which to play safe is a skill that separates good Signal Forge players from great ones.

## Status Effect Combos

The real depth of the status system emerges from combinations. Here are some of the combos players have discovered:

### The Shredder Combo (Bleed + Vulnerable)

Apply Vulnerable first, then stack Bleed. Since Vulnerable increases all damage by 50%, and Bleed deals damage each turn, the Bleed damage is also amplified. 10 stacks of Bleed on a Vulnerable target deal effectively 82 total damage instead of 55. The setup cost is two turns of not dealing direct damage, but the payoff is enormous.

### The Lockdown Combo (Freeze + Bleed)

Apply heavy Bleed, then Freeze the enemy. While Frozen, the enemy can't attack — but Bleed keeps ticking. You get free damage turns without any risk. The downside: you need cards for both Freeze and Bleed, which means a more diluted deck.

### The Vampire Combo (Siphon + Leech + Weak)

Weaken the enemy to reduce incoming damage, Siphon their shields for your own defense, and Leech health through your attacks. This creates a nearly self-sustaining combat loop where you're gaining resources on every axis while the enemy slowly withers. It's slow but incredibly resilient.

### The Glass Cannon Combo (Overdrive + Piercing + Exhaust)

Stack Overdrive high, play multiple Piercing Exhaust cards for massive burst damage, and hope to kill the enemy before the Overdrive self-damage catches up. It's the most thrilling way to play Signal Forge and the most likely to end your run in spectacular failure.

## Lessons from Slay the Spire (and Beyond)

I'd be dishonest if I didn't acknowledge Slay the Spire's massive influence on Signal Forge's status system. Poison in StS was the template for Bleed. Vulnerable and Weak are direct parallels. But I've tried to push beyond StS in several ways:

1. **Bidirectional effects.** Siphon and Overdrive affect both player and enemy, creating more nuanced cost-benefit calculations.
2. **Interaction density.** Every status effect interacts meaningfully with at least two others, creating combo depth that rewards experimentation.
3. **Thematic grounding.** Each status effect maps to a signal processing concept — Bleed is signal degradation, Freeze is signal pause, Vulnerable is impedance mismatch. The theme teaches the mechanic.

Status effects are the strategic skeleton of Signal Forge. Cards are the muscles, keywords are the tendons, but status effects are the bones that give the whole system structure. Building this system has been one of the most intellectually satisfying challenges of the project, and I'm excited to keep expanding it as the game evolves.
