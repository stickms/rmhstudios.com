## The Problem With Perfect Symmetry

Early versions of Cursed Logic were completely symmetrical. Both sides had three actions: Strike, Block, Prepare. The Protocol chose its action, you chose yours, outcomes resolved. Simple, clean, elegant.

And kind of... hollow.

The problem was that symmetry made the game feel like coin-flipping with attitude. Without any information advantage, every round was a pure guess. You could develop instincts about the Protocol's tendencies over time, but individual rounds felt arbitrary. Wins didn't feel earned because you couldn't point to a specific *reason* your decision was correct. It was just... a guess that happened to land.

I needed to break the symmetry. I needed to give the player something the Protocol didn't have — a tool that created genuine strategic depth. Something that transformed guessing into *deduction*.

That's when Probe was born.

## What Probe Does

When you choose Probe as your action for a round, you sacrifice your ability to Strike, Block, or Prepare. You do nothing tactically. The round resolves with you passive — vulnerable to Strikes, gaining no stance from Prepare, providing no defense via Block.

In exchange, you receive **a window into the Protocol's mind.**

Probe triggers the Protocol's predictive state modeling system, which builds projected future round states based on the AI's current analysis. You see:

- The Protocol's **current mode** (pressuring, defensive, or recovering)
- Its **likely next action** with a confidence percentage
- A short-term **trajectory projection** showing probable mode shifts over the next few rounds
- The Protocol's **read on you** — how it categorizes your current playstyle

This is devastating intelligence. Knowing that the Protocol is in defensive mode and likely to Block next round means you can safely Prepare. Knowing it's about to shift to pressuring mode means you can pre-emptively Block. Knowing it reads you as aggressive means you can exploit that expectation by doing the opposite.

But you paid for this information with a round of inaction. The Protocol acted while you watched. Was it worth it?

## The Economy of Information

This is the central tension of Probe, and it's what elevates Cursed Logic's strategic depth beyond what I initially imagined. Every Probe is a **trade**: you exchange present-round agency for future-round advantage.

The value of that trade depends entirely on context:

### When Probe is Strong

Probing is most valuable when you're **uncertain and can afford the cost**. If you have a healthy lead and the Protocol's behavior has become unpredictable (maybe it recently switched variants mid-duel, or a mutation disrupted normal patterns), Probing from a position of strength gives you intelligence without risking the match.

Probing is also strong when you suspect the Protocol is about to **mode switch**. Mode transitions are moments of vulnerability for the AI — its behavior becomes temporarily less consistent as it shifts strategies. Catching a mode switch via Probe lets you exploit the transition window.

### When Probe is Dangerous

Probing when you're behind on health is almost always a mistake. You're already losing, and spending a round not fighting means falling further behind. The information you gain has to lead to multiple successful reads to make up for the round you spent not dealing damage or defending. The math rarely works out.

Probing against an aggressive Protocol variant (like The Hammer) is also risky. The Hammer Strikes frequently, which means Probing will often result in taking undefended damage. You're not just spending a round — you're *spending health*.

### The Counter-Intelligence Layer

Here's where it gets really interesting: the Protocol tracks your Probe frequency. If you Probe too often, the Protocol incorporates that into its model. It knows you value information. It knows you're likely to play conservatively after a Probe (because you'll try to use what you learned). It can begin to predict your post-Probe behavior.

This creates a beautiful recursive loop:

1. You Probe to learn the Protocol's intentions
2. The Protocol learns that you Probe frequently
3. The Protocol adjusts its behavior post-Probe, knowing you'll act on the intelligence
4. You notice the Protocol adjusts post-Probe and begin to counter-adjust
5. The Protocol notices your counter-adjustment...

This is the **yomi layer** concept from fighting games, translated into an information warfare context. Every layer of "I know that they know that I know" adds depth to the Probe decision.

## Designing Information Reveals

One of the hardest design challenges was deciding *how much* information Probe should reveal. Too much, and a single Probe solves the entire duel. Too little, and Probe feels pointless.

We went through several iterations:

**Version 1: Binary reveals.** Probe showed the Protocol's exact next action. This was too powerful. Knowing the exact next action made that round essentially free — you'd always choose the counter-action and win. It trivialized the next round's decision entirely.

**Version 2: Probability distributions.** Probe showed percentage chances for each action. This was interesting but too abstract. Players struggled to translate "45% Strike, 30% Block, 25% Prepare" into a clear decision. It felt like reading a spreadsheet during a duel.

**Version 3: Mode and trajectory (current version).** Probe shows the Protocol's current mode and projected behavior over the next few rounds. This gives you *strategic* information without tactical certainty. You know the Protocol is in pressuring mode and trending aggressive — but you don't know if it will Strike *this particular round*. The information informs your strategy without automating your decisions.

This version works because it rewards **interpretation**. Two players can receive the same Probe data and draw different conclusions based on their understanding of the Protocol's behavior. It's a skill — reading the Probe — that develops over time.

## Probe and the Stance System

Probe interacts with the stance system in a subtle but important way. Because Probing doesn't build stance (you're not Preparing) and doesn't spend stance (you're not Striking or Blocking), it creates a **stance freeze** for one round. If you've been building stance through Prepares, Probing pauses that progression.

This means there's a hidden cost to Probing beyond the obvious one: it disrupts your stance's momentum. Players who rely on building up stance for powered Strikes need to weigh whether the Probe's intelligence is worth delaying their power spike.

This interaction wasn't designed — it emerged from the systems interacting naturally. And those emergent properties are my favorite kind of game design. When systems create depth you didn't plan, you know you've built something real.

## The Philosophy Behind Probe

At a deeper level, Probe represents something I believe about competition: **information should be earned, not given.** Many games provide complete information freely (chess, checkers) or hide it behind randomness (poker, card games). Cursed Logic occupies a middle ground where information is available but costly.

This creates a metagame around information itself. How much is knowledge worth? When does gaining intel shift from smart play to a crutch? How do you balance intelligence-gathering against raw aggression?

These aren't just game design questions — they're questions about decision-making under uncertainty. And I think that's why Probe resonates with players. It takes an abstract concept (the value of information) and makes it *tangible*. You can feel Probe's cost and benefit in every duel. You develop an intuition for when to gather intel and when to trust your gut.

That intuition — the feeling of *knowing* when to Probe — is one of the deepest skills in Cursed Logic. And it's entirely about understanding information as a resource that has to be managed alongside health, stance, and tactical position.

*Knowledge has a price. Are you willing to pay it?*
