## Games Don't Exist in a Vacuum

There's a persistent notion that because games are entertainment — often fantastical entertainment — accuracy doesn't matter. It's just a game. It's not a documentary. Why fact-check a dragon?

We disagree. Not because games should be documentaries, but because **everything a studio communicates — from in-game lore to marketing copy to technical blog posts — carries an implicit claim of credibility.** When that credibility is misplaced, it damages trust with players, perpetuates misinformation, and ultimately undermines the medium's potential to be taken seriously.

At RMH Studios, fact-checking is part of our development process. Here's why, and how.

## When Games Get Facts Wrong

Games regularly incorporate historical, scientific, and cultural elements, and when those elements are wrong, the consequences range from embarrassing to harmful.

### The Assassin's Creed Conversations

Ubisoft's **Assassin's Creed** franchise has been a recurring flashpoint for debates about historical accuracy in games. The series markets itself on historical settings — Renaissance Italy, Revolutionary France, Ptolemaic Egypt, Viking-age England — and invests heavily in recreating architecture, clothing, and cultural details.

But the franchise has also drawn criticism for historical inaccuracies that cross the line from creative license into misleading representation. The inclusion and exclusion of certain historical figures, the compression of timelines, and the portrayal of social dynamics have all generated controversy. Ubisoft has at various times positioned the games as "inspired by real history" while also marketing their historical authenticity.

The debate is nuanced. Creative license is essential in historical fiction — no game can or should be a perfect simulation of the past. But there's a difference between **intentional creative choices that enhance storytelling** and **careless errors that misrepresent reality.** The former should be defended; the latter should be caught in development.

### No Man's Sky: The Redemption Arc

**No Man's Sky** by Hello Games is perhaps the most instructive case study in what happens when marketing outpaces reality. Sean Murray's pre-launch interviews in 2015-2016 described features — multiplayer encounters, varied planetary ecosystems, sophisticated AI — that the August 2016 launch simply didn't deliver. The gap between what was promised and what was shipped was so vast that the UK's Advertising Standards Authority investigated the game's Steam store page, and the game received one of the most hostile player receptions in gaming history.

What makes No Man's Sky remarkable is what happened next. Hello Games went silent, then spent years systematically building every promised feature and more. By 2024, No Man's Sky is a genuinely excellent game with full multiplayer, base building, living ships, and VR support. The redemption arc is real and earned.

But the lesson isn't "it's okay to overpromise because you can fix it later." The lesson is that **the overpromise cost Hello Games years of goodwill, enormous personal stress on the development team, and a reputation that required heroic effort to rebuild.** The simpler path would have been honest marketing from the start.

## Our Approach to Fact-Checking

At RMH Studios, we apply fact-checking rigor to three distinct areas:

### 1. In-Game Content

When our games reference real-world concepts, we verify them. Temple of Joy's philosophical themes — the hedonic treadmill, Samsara, the psychology of desire — are grounded in actual philosophical and psychological literature. Signal Forge's waveform mechanics are based on real signal processing principles. When Echoes of the Spire references architectural or historical concepts, we check our sources.

This doesn't mean our games are textbooks. We take creative liberties constantly — that's what makes them games rather than lectures. But when we present something as factual, it needs to actually be factual.

### 2. Technical Claims

When we write about our technology — in this blog, in documentation, in marketing materials — we verify our claims. If we say "our WebSocket architecture supports X concurrent connections," that number comes from load testing, not estimation. If we compare the Web Audio API to native audio middleware, we represent both fairly rather than exaggerating our platform's capabilities.

This matters more than it might seem. Developer-focused content is often taken at face value by readers who trust the author's expertise. Publishing incorrect technical information erodes that trust and can lead other developers to make poor decisions based on wrong data.

### 3. Marketing and Communication

This is where the No Man's Sky lesson is most directly applicable. **We don't show features that don't exist. We don't promise timelines we can't meet. We don't describe our games in terms that the actual experience can't support.**

When we say a game is "playable," it's playable right now. When we describe a feature, it's implemented and working. When we share a screenshot, it's from the actual game, not a mockup or a "target render."

This sounds like a low bar. It should be a low bar. But the gaming industry has normalized aspirational marketing to such a degree that simply showing what actually exists feels radical.

## The Process

Concretely, our fact-checking process looks like this:

- **Source verification.** When a blog post or in-game text references a real-world fact, event, or concept, we trace it back to a primary or authoritative secondary source. Wikipedia is a starting point, not an endpoint.
- **Technical validation.** Performance claims, compatibility statements, and technical comparisons are validated through testing, not assumption.
- **Marketing review.** Before any public-facing description of our games goes live, we ask: "Does the current build deliver what this description promises?" If the answer is no, we rewrite the description, not ship and hope.
- **Peer review.** Blog posts, documentation, and significant marketing copy are reviewed by someone other than the author. Fresh eyes catch errors that familiarity blinds you to.
- **Correction culture.** When we get something wrong — and we will, because everyone does — we correct it publicly, promptly, and without making excuses. Errata are not embarrassing; pretending you didn't make an error is.

## Why This Matters for the Industry

The gaming industry's relationship with truth is, charitably, complicated. Misleading trailers are routine. "Gameplay footage" labels are applied to cinematics. Feature lists describe aspirations rather than realities. Review embargo strategies are designed to delay negative coverage past the peak sales window.

This environment of normalized dishonesty has real costs:

- **Players become cynical.** When every trailer is assumed to be misleading, genuine innovation struggles to be believed.
- **Developers burn out.** The pressure to deliver on overpromised marketing is a significant contributor to crunch culture.
- **The medium loses credibility.** Every "but it's just a game" dismissal is reinforced when the industry treats accuracy as optional.

We can't fix the industry. We can hold ourselves to a standard that we believe the industry should aspire to. **Fact-checking isn't glamorous. It doesn't generate hype. It's invisible when done well and only noticed when it's absent. But it's a fundamental expression of respect — for our players, for truth, and for the medium we've chosen to work in.**

At RMH Studios, we check our facts because that's what honest people do. It's that simple.
