## The Idle Game Paradox

Idle games are, on their surface, absurd. You click a thing. The number goes up. You buy an upgrade so the number goes up faster. Eventually, the number goes up on its own, and your role shifts from active participant to passive observer of exponential growth. The genre asks almost nothing of the player, yet millions of people play idle games for hundreds of hours.

**Cookie Clicker**, created by Julien "Orteil" Thiennot and released in August 2013, is the game that crystallized this paradox into a cultural phenomenon. Starting as a browser experiment, Cookie Clicker attracted millions of players and spawned an entire genre: Clicker Heroes, Adventure Capitalist, Idle Miner, and countless variations. Over a decade later, Cookie Clicker is still actively developed and played. Its Steam release in September 2021 sold hundreds of thousands of copies — for a game about clicking a cookie.

What Cookie Clicker proved, beyond the commercial viability of the format, is that the idle game loop taps into something deep in human psychology. And that's where Temple of Joy begins — not as a celebration of that loop, but as an examination of it.

## The Psychology of the Number Going Up

The psychological foundation of idle games is well-documented, and it maps directly onto research in behavioral psychology.

**B.F. Skinner's operant conditioning experiments** in the 1930s established the power of variable-ratio reinforcement schedules — the same mechanism that makes slot machines compulsive. Skinner's rats pressed levers more persistently when rewards were delivered on unpredictable schedules than when rewards were consistent. Idle games use a variation of this: the rewards (upgrades, unlocks, prestige tiers) come at increasing intervals, creating a pattern where the *anticipation* of the next reward drives continued engagement more than the reward itself.

This is also the core mechanism of the **hedonic treadmill** (hedonic adaptation), a concept formalized by psychologists Philip Brickman and Donald Campbell in their 1971 essay "Hedonic Relativism and Planning the Good Society." The hedonic treadmill describes how humans return to a baseline level of happiness regardless of positive or negative events. You get the upgrade, you feel a brief spike of satisfaction, and then you recalibrate — the new normal feels just like the old normal, and you need the next upgrade to feel that spike again.

Most idle games exploit this cycle without acknowledging it. Temple of Joy makes it the subject.

## Samsara as Game Mechanic

**Samsara**, the Buddhist and Hindu concept of the cycle of death and rebirth, is the philosophical backbone of Temple of Joy. In Buddhist thought, Samsara is driven by **tanha** — craving, desire, the endless wanting that keeps beings cycling through existence. Enlightenment (nirvana) is achieved not through satisfying desire but through releasing attachment to it.

In Temple of Joy, the idle game loop *is* Samsara. The player clicks, accumulates, grows — and then resets. The prestige mechanic, which is standard in idle games (Cookie Clicker calls it "ascending," many games call it "rebirth"), becomes a literal cycle of death and renewal. Each cycle carries forward some progress (karma, in our framing) but resets the material accumulation.

The philosophical question embedded in the mechanics is: **when does the player stop chasing the bigger number and start questioning why they're chasing it?**

We don't preach this. We don't interrupt gameplay with philosophy lectures. The mechanics themselves embody the concepts. Players who engage deeply will find layers of meaning; players who just want the number to go up can enjoy that too. But the experience is designed so that, eventually, the repetition itself becomes the message.

## Designing for Meaning, Not Addiction

This is where Temple of Joy diverges from the conventional idle game design philosophy, and it connects to our broader commitment to ethical design.

Most idle games are optimized for **retention** — keeping players coming back day after day, ideally through notification pressure, daily login bonuses, and FOMO mechanics. The longer a player stays engaged, the more ad revenue or in-app purchase revenue the game generates.

We designed Temple of Joy with a different goal: **we want players to reach a point where they choose to stop.** Not out of frustration, not because they hit a paywall, but because they've internalized the game's message about the nature of desire and decided, on their own terms, that they're done.

This is, frankly, a commercially risky design choice. A game that succeeds by convincing players to stop playing has an unusual relationship with traditional engagement metrics. But it's honest to the philosophical themes we're exploring, and honesty is the foundation we've built this studio on.

### Mechanical Decisions That Support Meaning

Several specific design decisions in Temple of Joy serve the philosophical themes:

- **No daily login rewards.** These mechanics create obligation, not enjoyment. Temple of Joy doesn't punish absence or reward presence — it's there when you want it and doesn't guilt you when you're not.
- **Diminishing returns are the point.** Most idle games fight diminishing returns with new systems, prestige layers, and complexity. Temple of Joy leans into diminishing returns, making the hedonic treadmill visible and felt.
- **The "Release" mechanic.** At certain points, players can choose to release accumulated progress permanently — not as a prestige reset that gives bonuses, but as a genuine letting go. It's a mechanical expression of non-attachment, and it's one of the most controversial design decisions we've made. Some players find it deeply satisfying. Others find it frustrating. Both reactions are valid and, we'd argue, philosophically meaningful.
- **Ambient, non-intrusive presentation.** Temple of Joy's visual and audio design is meditative rather than stimulating. Where most idle games use bright colors, exciting animations, and dopamine-triggering sound effects, Temple of Joy uses muted tones, gradual transitions, and ambient soundscapes. The aesthetic discourages compulsive checking and encourages contemplation.

## The Legacy of Cookie Clicker

We have enormous respect for Cookie Clicker and the genre it created. Orteil's game is often dismissed as shallow, but it contains surprising depth — the "grandmapocalypse" storyline, the tongue-in-cheek commentary on capitalism and automation, the way the game's own upgrade descriptions become increasingly existential. Cookie Clicker was always smarter than it appeared, and it gave us permission to take the idle game format seriously as a vehicle for ideas.

Temple of Joy wouldn't exist without Cookie Clicker. We stand on its shoulders, and we're trying to ask the next question: if Cookie Clicker showed that idle games can be meaningful despite their simplicity, can we build an idle game where the meaning is the mechanic?

## What Players Have Taught Us

Since launching Temple of Joy, we've had conversations with players that surprised us. Some play it as a straightforward idle game and enjoy the progression loop on its own merits. Some engage with the philosophical themes and tell us it's changed how they think about their relationship with games. A few have told us it helped them think about their relationship with *wanting* in general — desire for achievement, for accumulation, for the number going up.

We don't claim Temple of Joy is therapy or philosophy class. It's a game. But we believe games can carry ideas, and the best way to carry ideas is not to lecture about them but to build mechanics that embody them.

**Temple of Joy is our most unusual game, and it's the one that most fully embodies what RMH Studios is about.** Not just building games that are fun, but building games that are honest — even when honesty means questioning the very impulse that brings players to our games in the first place.
