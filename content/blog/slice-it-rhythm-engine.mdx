## The Beat Must Be Exact

Rhythm games are unforgiving. A platformer can have 50 milliseconds of input lag and still feel responsive. A rhythm game with 50 milliseconds of desync is broken. Players don't consciously measure latency — they **feel** it. When the visual cue says "now" and the audio says "not yet," the entire experience collapses.

Building Slice It! — our browser-based rhythm game — meant solving this precision problem in the most challenging runtime environment possible: a web browser. This is the story of how we built the rhythm engine, what Beat Saber taught us about the genre, and how community beatmaps turned a single-player game into a living platform.

## The Beat Saber Effect

**Beat Saber** didn't invent the rhythm game, but it redefined the genre for a generation. Released in 2018 by Beat Games (later acquired by Meta), it proved that rhythm gameplay could be visceral, physical, and endlessly replayable. By 2024, Beat Saber had sold over 6 million copies and become one of VR's defining titles.

Beat Saber's genius is the mapping between music and motion. Notes don't just arrive on beat — they arrive in **spatial patterns** that turn your body into an instrument. The best Beat Saber maps feel choreographed, like dancing with a lightsaber.

Slice It! takes direct inspiration from this philosophy. We can't replicate the physicality of VR in a browser, but we can replicate the core design principle: **every input should feel musical**. When you tap, swipe, or hold in Slice It!, the action should feel like it belongs in the song. Not just on beat, but on the right beat, with the right weight, creating the right emphasis.

This means our rhythm engine can't just track timing — it has to understand musical structure.

## The Web Audio API: Power and Pain

The foundation of our rhythm engine is the **Web Audio API**, the browser's built-in audio processing system. The Web Audio API is remarkably powerful — it provides sample-accurate timing, real-time audio processing, and a node-based architecture that can handle complex audio routing.

But it also has quirks that make rhythm game development challenging:

### The Autoplay Problem

Modern browsers block audio playback until the user interacts with the page. This is a sensible policy for preventing obnoxious auto-playing ads, but it means a rhythm game can't start audio on its own. We solve this with a deliberate "Start" interaction — when the player taps to begin a song, we initialize the AudioContext and begin playback in the same user gesture handler.

### The Clock Problem

JavaScript's `Date.now()` and `performance.now()` provide millisecond-resolution timing, but they're not synchronized with audio playback. The Web Audio API has its own clock — `AudioContext.currentTime` — which runs on the audio thread and provides high-resolution, drift-free timing.

Our rhythm engine uses `AudioContext.currentTime` as the **single source of truth** for all timing decisions. Visual elements, input windows, and scoring all reference this clock. This eliminates the class of bugs where visual and audio timing drift apart over the course of a song.

### The Latency Problem

Audio output latency varies dramatically across devices and browsers. A desktop running Chrome might have 10ms of output latency. A mobile device running Safari might have 100ms. This latency is the delay between when the browser sends audio data to the sound card and when the player actually hears it.

We handle this with a **calibration system**. Before their first session, players tap along to a metronome, and we measure the average offset between their taps and the audio beats. This offset is stored and applied to all timing calculations, effectively personalizing the engine to each player's device.

## The Beatmap Format

A beatmap is the heart of a rhythm game level. It defines when notes appear, what type they are, and where they're positioned. Our beatmap format is a JSON structure with the following key components:

- **Song metadata** — title, artist, BPM, duration, difficulty rating
- **Timing points** — BPM changes, time signature changes, and offset adjustments
- **Note events** — timestamp, type (tap, hold, swipe, multi-tap), position, and duration
- **Effect events** — visual effects, camera movements, and background changes synchronized to the music

Each note event is defined relative to the song's beat structure, not absolute time. A note at beat 4.5 of measure 12 stays at beat 4.5 of measure 12 regardless of BPM. This makes beatmaps resilient to timing adjustments and allows us to support songs with tempo changes.

### Scoring and Judgment

When a player inputs at a given moment, the engine compares the input timestamp against the nearest expected note. The timing window is divided into judgment tiers:

- **Perfect** — within 25ms of the target
- **Great** — within 50ms
- **Good** — within 100ms
- **Miss** — beyond 100ms or no input

These windows are tighter than most casual rhythm games but looser than hardcore titles like Sound Voltex or IIDX. We tuned them through extensive playtesting to find the sweet spot where skilled players feel rewarded but casual players aren't constantly failing.

Scoring uses a weighted system where judgment quality, combo length, and difficulty multipliers all contribute. The combo system — where consecutive non-miss hits build a multiplier — creates the risk-reward tension that makes rhythm games compelling. Breaking a 200-hit combo on a Perfect run is devastating, and that emotional weight is what keeps players coming back.

## Custom Beatmap Generation

Creating beatmaps by hand is an art form. The Beat Saber modding community has produced thousands of custom maps, and the best mappers are celebrated artists in their own right. But hand-crafted maps don't scale — especially for a platform that wants to support community song uploads.

We built a **procedural beatmap generator** that analyzes uploaded audio and produces playable maps. The algorithm works in stages:

1. **Onset detection** — identify when musical events (beats, notes, percussive hits) occur in the audio
2. **Beat tracking** — determine the song's tempo and meter
3. **Energy analysis** — measure the intensity of the audio over time to create difficulty curves
4. **Pattern generation** — place notes based on detected onsets, filtered by difficulty level and constrained by ergonomic rules (no physically impossible patterns)
5. **Flow optimization** — adjust note placement to create smooth movement patterns that feel natural to play

The generator produces maps at three difficulty levels. Easy maps follow the main beat and melody. Medium maps add syncopation and faster patterns. Hard maps track detailed rhythmic subdivisions and demand precise, rapid inputs.

Generated maps aren't as good as hand-crafted ones — they lack the intentional choreographic quality of a skilled mapper's work. But they're surprisingly playable, and they allow any song in our library to be immediately available at multiple difficulty levels.

## Community Song Uploads

The feature that transformed Slice It! from a rhythm game into a rhythm **platform** is community song uploads. Players can upload their own audio, and our system processes it through the beatmap generator to create playable levels.

The upload pipeline works as follows:

- **Audio upload** — player uploads an MP3 or WAV file
- **Audio processing** — server-side analysis extracts timing data and generates beatmaps
- **Review queue** — generated maps go through a brief automated quality check (minimum note density, playability verification)
- **Publication** — approved maps appear in the community library with the uploader's credit

We also support **community mapping** — players can use our browser-based beatmap editor to create hand-crafted maps for any song. These maps are tagged separately from generated ones, and the best community maps are featured in our curated playlists.

### The Copyright Question

We want to be transparent about copyright. Community song uploads operate under the same legal framework as user-generated content on any platform. We implement content identification and respond to takedown requests in compliance with the DMCA. Players are responsible for uploading content they have the right to share, and our terms of service reflect this.

## Visual Sync

Audio-visual synchronization in a rhythm game isn't just about timing — it's about **anticipation**. Notes need to appear before the player needs to hit them, giving visual warning that maps to the audio cue. The travel time of a note from its spawn point to the hit zone must be consistent and predictable.

We render note tracks using a custom canvas-based renderer that maintains a steady visual frame rate independent of the audio clock. The renderer interpolates note positions between audio timing events, creating smooth visual motion even when the display refresh rate doesn't perfectly align with the audio sample rate.

Visual effects — particle bursts on perfect hits, screen shakes on misses, combo fire on long streaks — are purely cosmetic and rendered independently of the timing system. They never interfere with note visibility or input detection. Eye candy is important, but **clarity is sacred**.

## Lessons From the Rhythm Trenches

Building a rhythm engine in the browser taught us several lessons:

- **Trust the audio clock.** Every timing bug we encountered was caused by using a non-audio clock for timing decisions. `AudioContext.currentTime` is the only truth.
- **Calibration is not optional.** Device latency varies too much to use a fixed offset. Per-device calibration is the difference between "this game feels off" and "this game feels perfect."
- **Generated maps need constraints.** An algorithm that places notes on every detected onset creates unplayable chaos. Ergonomic constraints — maximum note density, minimum spacing, pattern variety — are essential.
- **Community content changes everything.** The moment players could upload their own songs, engagement transformed. People play their music differently than they play curated selections.

Slice It! started as an experiment: can we build a rhythm game that feels precise in a browser? The answer is yes — with careful engineering, creative compromise, and respect for the genre's unforgiving standards. The Web Audio API gives us the tools. The community gives us the music. The browser gives us the reach.

Every beat matters. Every frame matters. And every player who hits a Perfect streak on their favorite song makes the engineering worth it.
