## The Terrifying First Playtest

There's a specific kind of vulnerability involved in handing someone your game and saying "play this, tell me what you think." You've spent months building systems, balancing cards, crafting an experience that makes sense in your head. And then a player sits down and does something you never imagined within the first thirty seconds.

My first external playtest happened about four months into Signal Forge's development. I had invited five friends — three gamers and two non-gamers — to play through a run and provide feedback. I sat behind them, notebook in hand, trying not to hover.

Player one looked at their opening hand, played all their cards in cost order (cheapest first), and got destroyed by the second enemy. Player two found a combo I'd never considered and steamrolled the entire run in record time. Player three couldn't figure out what the Tempo resource did, despite what I thought was a clear tutorial tooltip. Player four had a great time but said the cards "all felt the same." Player five accidentally discovered a game-crashing bug within two turns.

It was the most educational 90 minutes of the entire development process.

## Lesson 1: Players Don't Read (And That's Your Fault)

I had spent days writing clear, concise tooltip text for every keyword. I was proud of those tooltips. During playtesting, exactly zero percent of players hovered over a keyword to read its tooltip before playing the card.

The problem wasn't laziness — it was flow. Players are in a decision-making mindset, not a reading mindset. They want to *act*, not *study*. If understanding a card requires pausing gameplay to read a paragraph, the card has failed at self-communication.

My response was a complete redesign of card presentation:

- **Keyword icons** — Each keyword now has a small, recognizable icon. Players learn to associate the icon with the effect through play, not through reading.
- **Stat preview** — When you hover a card over a target, the UI shows a preview of the expected outcome (damage after all modifiers, status stacks to be applied). This "what will happen" preview eliminated most confusion.
- **First-encounter popups** — The first time a player sees a keyword, a non-intrusive popup explains it. Only the first time. After that, the icon carries the information.

### The "Juice" Truth

One tester mentioned that attacks "didn't feel impactful" despite dealing significant damage. The numbers were right, but the *sensation* was wrong. Adding screen shake, hit flash, damage number pop-ups with scale animation, and a brief pause on hit transformed the feel entirely. The same 8 damage that felt weak now felt *powerful*.

The lesson: **feedback matters as much as mechanics.** A perfectly designed system that lacks satisfying feedback will feel worse than a mediocre system with great "juice."

```typescript
// Hit feedback implementation
function onDamageDealt(amount: number, target: Enemy): void {
  // Screen shake proportional to damage
  screenShake(Math.min(amount * 0.5, 8));
  
  // Hit flash on enemy
  target.flashWhite(100); // milliseconds
  
  // Floating damage number with scale animation
  spawnDamageNumber(target.position, amount, {
    scale: 1.0 + (amount / 20), // bigger hits = bigger numbers
    color: amount > 10 ? '#FF4444' : '#FFFFFF',
    duration: 800,
  });
  
  // Brief pause for weight
  if (amount > 15) {
    pauseGameTime(50); // hit stop for heavy hits
  }
}
```

## Lesson 2: The Combos You Design Aren't the Combos Players Find

I designed Signal Forge's keyword system to create specific synergies. Bleed + Vulnerable was an intended combo. Echo + Growing was an intended combo. Multihit + Leech was an intended combo. These are the combos the spreadsheets predicted and the balance was tuned around.

But players are *creative*. Within the first week of playtesting, someone discovered what they called the **"Infinite Echo Cascade"**:

1. Play a card with **Echo** and **Chain**.
2. The Echo copy triggers.
3. The Chain condition is met (two cards played this turn).
4. Chain plays another card from hand.
5. That card *also* has Echo.
6. Repeat until hand is empty.

The entire hand plays itself in a single turn, dealing massive damage and triggering every on-play effect in the deck. It was absolutely not how I intended Chain to work — I'd imagined Chain as playing *one* additional card, not creating a cascade.

I had two options: kill the combo or embrace it. I chose a middle path: **Chain now has a per-turn limit of 3 triggers.** This still allows exciting Chain turns where 3-4 cards play in rapid succession, but prevents the infinite cascade. The combo exists, it's satisfying to execute, but it has a ceiling.

Other notable player-discovered strategies:

### The Empty Deck Strategy

One tester exclusively picked Exhaust cards, burning through their deck until only 3 cards remained. With a 3-card deck and 5 cards drawn per turn, they saw their entire deck every turn — perfect consistency. I had never considered that Exhaust could be a *deck thinning* strategy rather than a "powerful one-shot" mechanic.

This strategy is now intentionally supported. Some cards gain bonuses based on deck size ("deal 1 damage per card fewer than 15 in your deck"), and a few events let you remove cards entirely.

### The Shield Stacker

Another player ignored damage entirely and built a pure shield deck. Sine cards with Sustain and Retain, stacking enormous shields every turn. They never attacked — they won by waiting for enemies to kill themselves against reflected damage from a card they found mid-run.

The run took 45 minutes for a fight that should have taken 5. But it *worked*. I added a soft enrage timer to enemies (they gain +2 damage every 10 turns) to prevent pure stall strategies from being optimal while still allowing defensive playstyles to be viable.

## Lesson 3: Onboarding Is Everything

The gap between playtest group one (gamers) and playtest group two (non-gamers) was enormous. Gamers intuitively understood concepts like "mana cost" and "status effects" because they'd internalized those patterns from other games. Non-gamers were overwhelmed by the sheer amount of information on screen.

My onboarding improvements:

### Progressive Complexity

The first enemy encounter now uses a restricted card pool — only Common cards with 0-1 keywords. Players learn the basic flow (draw cards, spend Static, play cards, end turn) without any keyword complexity. By the third fight, Uncommon cards with 2 keywords start appearing. Rare cards don't appear until the second "zone" of the run.

### Visual Hierarchy

I reorganized the battle screen to follow natural eye movement:
1. **Top**: Enemy info (what am I fighting?)
2. **Center**: Battle area (what's happening?)
3. **Bottom**: Hand and resources (what can I do?)

Each zone has a clear visual boundary. The player's eyes naturally flow from "threat" to "action" to "options." This sounds obvious in retrospect, but the original layout had resources at the top, enemies on the left, and cards at the bottom right. The eyes had to ping-pong across the screen to process a single turn.

### The "Why Did I Lose" Screen

After a game-over, Signal Forge now shows a detailed breakdown: which enemy killed you, what your health graph looked like over time, which turns were critical, and what your deck composition was. This turns a negative experience (dying) into a learning experience (understanding *why* you died and what to change next run).

## Lesson 4: Data Doesn't Lie, But It Also Doesn't Explain

I implemented analytics tracking early — session length, win rate per card, pick rate per card, most common cause of death, average run length. The data told me *what* was happening but rarely *why*.

For example, the data showed that a card called **Resonant Shield** had a 95% pick rate but only an 48% run win rate for decks containing it. High pick rate + average win rate usually means a card that *feels* good but isn't actually strong. Players were picking it because the big shield number felt safe, not because it was strategically correct.

The fix wasn't nerfing Resonant Shield — it was buffing the alternatives. Players need to *see* that other options are viable before they're willing to experiment. I added preview tooltips that show "This card has a 62% win rate in similar decks" to nudge players toward data-informed decisions without removing the joy of choice.

## The Humbling Art of Listening

Playtesting is humbling. Your baby is ugly and players will tell you so — nicely if you're lucky, bluntly if you're not. But every piece of feedback — every confused look, every misclick, every "this feels weird" — is a gift. It's information you can't get any other way.

Signal Forge is a fundamentally better game because of the players who took the time to break it, question it, and push it beyond what I imagined. If you're making a game and you haven't shown it to real humans yet: do it today. You'll hate some of what you hear. And then you'll make the best improvements of your entire project.
