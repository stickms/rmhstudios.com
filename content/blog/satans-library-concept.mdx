## Where It All Started

Every game begins with a feeling. For Satan's Library, that feeling was the quiet, creeping dread of being alone in a place that should feel safe — surrounded by knowledge, by books, by the accumulated wisdom of centuries — and realizing that none of it can save you. The idea hit me late one night while I was reading about the myth of forbidden libraries, places where knowledge itself was considered dangerous. What if a game captured that? What if *learning* was the threat?

I've been a survival horror fan for as long as I can remember. **Resident Evil** taught me that resource scarcity creates tension. **Silent Hill** showed me that atmosphere and psychological unease can be more effective than any jump scare. **Amnesia: The Dark Descent** proved that vulnerability — true, mechanical vulnerability — is the greatest horror tool a designer has. Satan's Library is my attempt to take the lessons from all three and build something that feels distinctly its own.

## Why a Library?

Horror games love mansions, hospitals, and abandoned towns. Libraries are criminally underused. Think about it — a library is a labyrinth by design. Towering shelves create natural corridors. Reading rooms become arenas. Archives become crypts. The verticality alone offers incredible level design potential: mezzanines, spiral staircases, balconies overlooking vast reading halls where something might be watching you from below.

But beyond the architecture, libraries carry *meaning*. They are places of order. Of silence. Of rules. Horror thrives when order breaks down. When the silence is violated. When the rules no longer apply. Satan's Library takes a space that represents humanity's attempt to organize and understand the world and corrupts it. The books whisper. The shelves rearrange. The Dewey Decimal System becomes a cipher for something much older and much worse.

### The Setting as Character

I wanted the library itself to feel alive — not in a haunted house sense, but in a way that suggests intelligence. Rooms that subtly change when you're not looking. A catalog system that leads you deeper instead of out. Environmental storytelling through marginalia, bookmarks left in specific pages, notes scrawled by previous visitors who didn't make it. The library isn't just where the horror happens; it *is* the horror.

## The Core Horror Vision

Satan's Library isn't about monsters jumping out of closets. It's about **sustained dread**. The kind of horror that builds in your chest and doesn't let go. To achieve this, every system in the game is designed to feed tension:

- **The Aura system** tracks your character's stress and feeds it back into the gameplay and visuals
- **The camera** dynamically shifts based on room geometry and stress levels
- **Movement** is deliberate and weighted, making every corridor feel like a commitment
- **Exploration** is rewarded but always carries risk

The philosophy is simple: *the player should never feel safe, but they should always feel compelled to push forward*. That balance between fear and curiosity is the heart of the entire experience.

### Blending Old and New

One of the biggest design challenges has been honoring the legacy of classic survival horror while making something that feels modern. I don't want tank controls, but I do want the *feeling* of tank controls — that sense of deliberate, committed movement where turning a corner is a decision, not a reflex. I want fixed camera angles, but I also want the intimacy of an over-the-shoulder view. The solution has been a dynamic camera system that transitions between these styles based on context, which I'll dive deep into in a future post.

## Inspirations Beyond Games

Games aren't my only inspiration. **House of Leaves** by Mark Z. Danielewski is a massive influence — the idea of a space that is larger on the inside than the outside, that defies measurement, that drives people mad through architecture. Borges' **Library of Babel** is another touchstone — an infinite library containing every possible book, most of them gibberish, where the search for meaning is itself a kind of madness.

Film influences include the slow-burn tension of **The Shining** (another story about a building with a mind of its own) and the body-horror psychological collapse of **Possession** (1981). I want that sense of a character unraveling, and I want the player to feel it through the mechanics, not just the cutscenes.

## The Road Ahead

Satan's Library is in early prototyping right now. I'm building the foundation — movement, camera, the Aura stress system — before layering on content. It's tempting to jump ahead to the cool stuff (enemy encounters, set pieces, story beats), but I've learned from past projects that **if the core loop doesn't feel right, nothing built on top of it will either**.

This devlog series will document the entire journey. The breakthroughs, the dead ends, the moments where a mechanic clicks into place and suddenly the game *feels* like what I imagined. I'm building this in the open because I believe survival horror deserves more voices, more experimentation, and more games that respect the player's intelligence.

### What's Next

In the next post, I'll be diving into the **Aura system** — the stress mechanic at the core of Satan's Library. It's the system that ties everything together: the camera, the movement, the exploration, the horror. It's also the system that's given me the most trouble in prototyping, which means it's probably the most important one to get right.

If you've ever felt that quiet dread in a library after hours — that sense that the silence is *listening* — that's the feeling I'm chasing. Welcome to Satan's Library. Try to keep your composure.
