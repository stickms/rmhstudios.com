## Where It All Started

Every game begins with a moment of frustration. For me, it was sitting in front of my monitor, tabbing between osu! and Guitar Hero clone after Guitar Hero clone, thinking: *why does every rhythm game make me wait for someone else to create the beatmap?*

I've been a rhythm game fanatic for as long as I can remember. Guitar Hero got me hooked as a kid — the plastic guitar, the colored notes streaming down the highway, the thrill of nailing a solo on Expert. Later, osu! consumed hundreds of hours of my life. The precision, the community, the endless song library. These games shaped how I think about interactive music experiences.

But there was always a gap. A song I loved would have no beatmap. Or the only available map was poorly timed. Or I'd discover an incredible track from an indie artist and realize I'd have to spend hours manually charting it before I could play. That friction between *hearing* a song and *playing* a song felt like a problem worth solving.

## The Core Vision

Slice It! was born from a simple question: **What if the game could listen to any song and generate a playable beatmap automatically?**

Not a gimmick. Not a rough approximation. A genuinely musical, fun-to-play map that responds to the dynamics of the audio in real time. I wanted a system that could take any MP3 or audio file — whether it's a symphonic metal track, a lo-fi beat, or an electronic banger — and produce a note chart that *feels* right.

That meant building an audio analysis engine from scratch. No relying on BPM metadata tags (which are often wrong or missing). No requiring users to manually input tempo information. The game would parse the actual waveform, detect onsets, analyze frequency content, and translate musical events into gameplay moments.

The idea excited me more than anything I'd worked on in years.

## What Makes Slice It! Different

There are a few pillars that I knew from day one would define Slice It! and separate it from the rhythm games I grew up with:

### 1. Automatic Beatmap Generation

This is the headline feature. Drop in any audio file, and the game generates a beatmap. No editor needed (though we may add one later for fine-tuning). The analysis engine handles onset detection, spectral analysis, and dynamic difficulty scaling. It's not perfect — and honestly, achieving "perfect" may be impossible — but it's surprisingly musical, and it gets better with every iteration.

### 2. A Softer Aesthetic

Most rhythm games lean into neon, high-contrast visuals. Flashy particle effects, dark backgrounds, aggressive color palettes. I love that aesthetic, but I wanted Slice It! to feel different. We went with a **neumorphic design system** — soft shadows, rounded corners, muted blues and pinks against white and light gray backgrounds. It's calming. It lets the music be the star. Players have told me it feels like playing a rhythm game inside a cloud, and I think that's the best compliment we've received.

### 3. Community-First Design

From the earliest planning documents, I knew Slice It! needed a public music library. Users upload songs, rate them, leave comments, and compete on per-song leaderboards. The game isn't just a solo experience — it's a living ecosystem of music and competition. Every song someone uploads becomes a new playground for every other player.

### 4. Real-Time Multiplayer

Up to four players can compete simultaneously via WebSocket connections. Lobby codes, shareable links, queued song playlists. The synchronization challenges were enormous (more on that in a future post), but the payoff is incredible. There's nothing quite like going head-to-head with friends on a song none of you have ever played before, with the beatmap generating in real time for everyone.

## Early Days and Doubts

I won't pretend the early days were all excitement. There were moments of serious doubt. Could browser-based audio processing really handle real-time analysis? Would the Web Audio API give me enough control? Was HTML5 Canvas fast enough for a 60fps rhythm game with potentially hundreds of notes on screen?

I spent the first two weeks just prototyping the audio pipeline. Loading files, decoding them, running FFT analysis, and visualizing the results. The early outputs were rough — the onset detection triggered on everything, generating chaotic walls of notes that were impossible to play. But buried in that chaos were moments where the system *got it right*. A snare hit would trigger a note. A bass drop would spawn a cluster. A vocal phrase would create a flowing sequence.

Those moments kept me going.

## The Tech Stack Decision

I chose **Next.js with TypeScript** as the foundation. It might seem unconventional for a game, but the reasoning was practical: Slice It! isn't just a game — it's a platform. User accounts, song uploads, leaderboards, comments, ratings. All of that needs a robust web framework. Next.js gives me server-side rendering for the community pages, API routes for the backend, and a solid React foundation for the UI.

The actual gameplay runs on **HTML5 Canvas**, which sits inside the Next.js app. The canvas handles all the real-time rendering — the note highway, hit animations, score displays — while React manages everything around it: menus, song selection, user profiles, multiplayer lobbies.

TypeScript was non-negotiable. A project this complex, with audio processing, real-time networking, and game state management, would be a debugging nightmare without static types. TypeScript has caught countless bugs before they ever reached a browser.

## Looking Forward

This post is the first in what I hope will be a long series of devlogs. I want to document the journey honestly — the breakthroughs and the setbacks, the clever solutions and the ugly hacks, the moments of joy and the 2 AM debugging sessions.

Slice It! is the most ambitious project I've ever undertaken at RMH Studios. It combines my love of music, my passion for game development, and my belief that the web platform is capable of far more than most people give it credit for.

The road ahead is long, but the first notes are already playing. Let's see where the rhythm takes us.
