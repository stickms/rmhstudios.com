## Beyond the Single Duel

For the first few months of development, Cursed Logic was a standalone duel. You fought the Protocol, you won or lost, and you did it again. Each duel was isolated — a fresh start, no history, no progression. And while individual duels were satisfying, something was missing.

**There was no reason to come back.**

Each duel existed in a vacuum. Your victories didn't accumulate toward anything. Your losses didn't cost you anything lasting. The emotional arc of a single duel was compelling, but there was no *meta-arc* — no larger story of your progression as a Protocol duelist. I needed a system that connected individual duels into a journey.

Enter the fragment currency and the persistent shop.

## What Are Fragments?

Fragments are the currency of Cursed Logic. You earn them by dueling — win or lose, every duel generates fragments based on your performance. Winning earns more. Efficient wins (fewer rounds, less health lost) earn even more. But even a loss generates a small fragment reward, ensuring that no duel feels entirely wasted.

The name "fragments" fits the cyber-noir aesthetic. They're pieces of data, shards of code, remnants of broken protocols. In the fiction of the game, you're harvesting fragments from the Protocol's defenses — extracting value from every interaction with the system you're trying to crack.

The fragment reward calculation considers several factors:

```typescript
function calculateFragments(duelResult: DuelResult): number {
  let base = duelResult.won ? 100 : 25;

  // Efficiency bonus for quick wins
  if (duelResult.won && duelResult.roundsUsed <= 8) {
    base += (8 - duelResult.roundsUsed) * 15;
  }

  // Health bonus for clean victories
  if (duelResult.won) {
    base += Math.floor(duelResult.remainingHealth * 0.5);
  }

  // Variant difficulty multiplier
  base = Math.floor(base * duelResult.variantMultiplier);

  // Mutation survival bonus
  base += duelResult.mutatedRoundsSurvived * 5;

  return base;
}
```

This reward structure creates *multiple goals* within each duel. You're not just trying to win — you're trying to win efficiently, with high health, against difficult variants, while surviving mutations. Fragments give every aspect of your performance tangible value.

## The Shop

The persistent shop is where fragments become power. Between duels, you visit the shop and spend fragments on upgrades that carry across all future duels. These upgrades don't reset when you lose. They're permanent investments in your dueling capacity.

The shop contains several categories of upgrades:

### Passive Augments

These are always-on modifiers that subtly shift the game in your favor:

- **Reinforced Strikes** — Strike damage increased by a small percentage
- **Deep Preparation** — Prepare grants additional stance charge
- **Hardened Block** — Block reflects a tiny amount of damage back at the Protocol
- **Extended Probe** — Probe reveals additional rounds of projected behavior

Each augment has multiple tiers, and costs increase with each tier. A fully upgraded augment provides a meaningful but not overwhelming advantage. The goal is that upgrades make you *slightly better*, not invincible.

### Consumables

Single-use items that you bring into a duel:

- **Override Chip** — Forces the Protocol to reveal its action before you choose yours (once per duel)
- **Emergency Patch** — Restores a small amount of health mid-duel
- **Mutation Nullifier** — Cancels one mutation during the duel
- **Stance Battery** — Instantly charges your stance to maximum

Consumables are expensive relative to their single-use nature, creating an interesting spending decision. Do you invest in permanent augments or keep a supply of powerful single-use tools?

### Cosmetic Unlocks

Fragment spending isn't all about power. Some shop items are purely aesthetic — different color themes for the duel interface, alternative action icons, custom victory animations. These serve an important psychological purpose: they give fragment-rich players something to spend on without creating power imbalances.

## Design Philosophy

The shop system in Cursed Logic is guided by several principles that I feel strongly about:

### Roguelike, Not Roguelite

The distinction matters. In a roguelite, meta-progression can trivialize the core game over time. If you play enough and buy enough upgrades, the game becomes easy regardless of skill. I didn't want that for Cursed Logic.

Our approach is closer to a roguelike sensibility: upgrades provide **marginal advantages**, not transformation. A fully upgraded player is maybe 15-20% more effective than a fresh player. The core challenge remains intact. You still need to read the Protocol, adapt to mutations, and make good decisions. No amount of fragments can substitute for skill.

### Meaningful Choices Over Raw Power

Every upgrade in the shop was designed to change *how you play*, not just make you stronger. Reinforced Strikes don't just increase damage — they make aggressive strategies slightly more viable, encouraging you to Strike more often. Extended Probe doesn't just give more info — it makes information-heavy playstyles more rewarding.

This means that shop choices reflect and reinforce your personal playstyle. An aggressive player gravitates toward Strike augments. A careful player invests in Probe extensions. A survivalist focuses on Block and health upgrades. Your shop history tells a story about the kind of duelist you've become.

### Loss Should Still Teach

By giving fragments even on losses, the shop ensures that every duel contributes to progression. This was a deliberate anti-frustration measure. Losing streaks are already demoralizing — if they also meant zero progress, players would quit. The small fragment reward for losses says: "That didn't go well, but you still learned something. Here's proof."

## Technical Implementation

The shop system is managed through a Zustand store, which made the implementation surprisingly clean. Zustand's simplicity was perfect for this use case — persistent state that needs to survive between game sessions without the overhead of a full state management framework.

```typescript
interface ShopState {
  fragments: number;
  ownedAugments: Record<string, number>; // augment ID -> tier
  consumables: Record<string, number>;   // consumable ID -> quantity
  cosmetics: string[];                   // unlocked cosmetic IDs

  addFragments: (amount: number) => void;
  purchaseAugment: (id: string) => boolean;
  purchaseConsumable: (id: string) => boolean;
  useConsumable: (id: string) => boolean;
}
```

The store persists to localStorage, which means your progression survives browser refreshes and returns. For a web-based game, this is the sweet spot — real persistence without requiring user accounts or server-side storage (though we do sync to the server for authenticated users).

One engineering challenge was handling **store hydration** correctly in Next.js. Since Zustand stores initialize on the client but Next.js renders on the server, we needed to handle the initial server render carefully to avoid hydration mismatches. The solution was lazy client-side initialization with a loading state during hydration — not the most elegant approach, but reliable.

## The Fragment Economy

Balancing an in-game economy is an exercise in patience and iteration. Prices and rewards have been adjusted dozens of times based on playtesting feedback.

Key tensions we had to balance:

- **Earning rate vs. spending rate.** If players accumulate fragments too quickly, upgrades feel trivial. Too slowly, and progression feels impossible. Our target is roughly one tier-1 augment purchase every three to four duels.
- **Augment power vs. core difficulty.** Augments needed to feel impactful without undermining the skill-based nature of the game. Each augment went through extensive testing to ensure it didn't break the action interaction matrix.
- **Consumable pricing vs. value.** Consumables are deliberately expensive because they provide guaranteed value. An Override Chip is essentially a free round win. That power needs a steep price tag.

The fragment economy is a living system that I continue to tune. Every new Protocol variant or mutation we add has implicit effects on fragment generation, which requires rebalancing. It's a never-ending process, but that ongoing attention is what keeps the economy feeling healthy and the progression feeling rewarding.

*Every duel leaves its mark. Spend your fragments wisely.*
