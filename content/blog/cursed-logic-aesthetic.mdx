## Atmosphere Is Gameplay

There's a school of thought that says visuals are separate from gameplay. That a game with programmer art can be just as compelling as one with beautiful design. I understand this argument, and there's truth in it — a great core loop is a great core loop regardless of how it looks.

But Cursed Logic taught me that this separation is false for certain types of games. **In a game about psychological tension, atmosphere IS gameplay.** The way the interface looks, moves, and feels directly influences how players think and decide. The UI isn't a skin over the game — it's part of the duel.

When you sit down to face the Protocol, you should feel like you're jacking into a hostile system. The screen should feel like a terminal. The text should feel like code. The colors should feel like warning signals in a dark server room. Every visual element should reinforce the central question of the game: *can you outthink this thing?*

## The Deep Black Foundation

Cursed Logic's background color is `#0a0a0f` — not pure black, but close. There's a reason for this choice. Pure black (`#000000`) on screens creates harsh contrast that strains eyes over time. Our very-dark blue-black is easier to look at during extended dueling sessions while still feeling oppressively dark.

This choice echoes a philosophy I borrowed from filmmakers: **true darkness is more unsettling than pitch black.** When the background has the faintest hint of color — in our case, a barely perceptible blue undertone — it triggers an instinctive response. Your brain registers that something is *there* in the darkness, even if you can't quite see it. That subliminal unease is the foundation of Cursed Logic's atmosphere.

```css
:root {
  --bg-primary: #0a0a0f;
  --bg-surface: #12121a;
  --bg-elevated: #1a1a25;
}
```

We use three levels of dark: the primary background, a slightly lighter surface color for card-like elements, and an elevated color for interactive components. This three-tier system creates visual depth without introducing lightness. Everything feels submerged in shadow, with elements distinguished by subtle graduation rather than brightness.

## Cyan and Amber: The Dual Accents

Color choices in Cursed Logic are deliberately limited. We use exactly two accent colors: **cyan** (`#00e5ff`) and **amber** (`#f5a623`). Everything else is grayscale. This extreme constraint serves multiple purposes.

### Cyan — The Player's Color

Cyan represents the player's actions, health, and status. It's the color of your interface — the cursor, the action buttons, the probe readouts. Cyan was chosen because it feels *digital* and *cold*. It evokes terminal prompts, circuit boards, and the blue glow of an active screen in a dark room.

Cyan also has excellent visibility on dark backgrounds. It cuts through the blackness with surgical precision, which is functionally important — during intense rounds, you need to instantly identify your options without visual search.

### Amber — The Protocol's Color

Amber represents the Protocol. Its health bar is amber. Its mode indicator is amber. When it acts, amber accents pulse on screen. The choice of amber was inspired by vintage warning systems — those amber caution lights on old industrial panels, the amber glow of a warning notification.

Amber says "danger" in a way that red doesn't. Red is aggressive and immediate — it screams. Amber is steady and persistent — it *warns*. The Protocol isn't a raging beast; it's a patient, calculating system. Amber captures that personality perfectly.

### The Emotional Geography of Color

By limiting ourselves to two accent colors, we created what I call an **emotional geography**. Every screen tells you who's winning just through the balance of cyan and amber. A screen dominated by cyan feels safe — the player is in control. A screen where amber is encroaching feels dangerous — the Protocol is advancing. The color balance becomes an intuitive status indicator that players process unconsciously.

During mutations, we occasionally introduce brief flashes of other colors — red for damage amplification, purple for information corruption, white for system anomalies. These departures from the cyan-amber palette feel *wrong* and *alarming* precisely because they violate the established visual language. The mutation literally disrupts the color space, reinforcing its mechanical disruption of the game's rules.

## Monospace Everything

Every piece of text in Cursed Logic is rendered in a monospace font. This was a non-negotiable aesthetic decision that some people thought was crazy.

"Monospace is for code editors," they said. "Regular people associate it with boring technical documentation."

They're right that monospace carries those associations. But those associations are *exactly what we want*. Cursed Logic presents itself as a system interface, not a game. The monospace font reinforces the fiction that you're interacting with a terminal, reading system outputs, and inputting commands. The font *is* the aesthetic.

### Readability Considerations

Monospace fonts are actually excellent for the kind of short, high-impact text that Cursed Logic displays. Action names (STRIKE, BLOCK, PREPARE, PROBE) are crisp and distinct in monospace. Status readouts feel authentic. Probe data looks like actual system diagnostics rather than game UI.

We use variable font weights within the monospace family to create hierarchy: bold for action names, regular for descriptions, light for secondary information. This provides enough typographic variation for usability while maintaining the monospace character throughout.

```css
.action-label {
  font-family: 'JetBrains Mono', 'Fira Code', 'Consolas', monospace;
  font-weight: 700;
  letter-spacing: 0.15em;
  text-transform: uppercase;
}

.probe-readout {
  font-family: 'JetBrains Mono', 'Fira Code', 'Consolas', monospace;
  font-weight: 400;
  font-size: 0.85rem;
  color: var(--cyan-primary);
  opacity: 0.9;
}
```

The letter-spacing on action labels is another deliberate choice. Widely spaced uppercase monospace text feels *mechanical* and *authoritative*. When you see **S T R I K E** on a button, it doesn't feel like a game option — it feels like a command you're issuing to a system.

## Animation Philosophy

Cursed Logic's animations follow a strict philosophy: **everything should feel like data, not motion graphics.** We avoid smooth, organic bezier curves. Instead, animations use stepped easing, glitch effects, and digital distortion.

When a round resolves, health bars don't smoothly animate — they *tick down* in discrete steps, like a digital readout. When the Protocol switches modes, its indicator doesn't fade between colors — it *flickers* and *snaps*, like a system struggling with a state change. When you Probe, the data doesn't slide in — it *materializes*, character by character, like a terminal printing output.

These animation choices reinforce the feeling that you're operating within a digital system. Every visual transition says: "This is not a game. This is a system. You are inside it."

### Glitch Effects

The glitch aesthetic is used sparingly and purposefully. Random visual glitches occur during mode transitions, mutation activations, and minigame entry/exit. These glitches serve a dual purpose: they're visually striking, and they signal systemic instability. A glitch during a mode transition tells the player "something is changing" before the mechanical change even takes effect.

We implemented glitches through CSS animations that briefly displace text, distort color channels, and introduce horizontal scan lines. The effect is brief — never more than 300 milliseconds — but unmistakable. It breaks the clean interface just enough to create unease without disrupting usability.

## The Sound of Darkness

Though this post focuses on visual design, I can't discuss Cursed Logic's aesthetics without mentioning audio. The sound design follows the same principles: minimal, digital, cold. Clicks rather than thuds. Synthesized tones rather than orchestral swells. The audio reinforces the visual atmosphere at every moment.

Each action has a distinct sound signature — Strike is a sharp digital crack, Block is a low resonant pulse, Prepare is an ascending synthesized charge, and Probe is a rapid data-scan chirp. These sounds are designed to be instantly identifiable even without looking at the screen, providing audio feedback that matches the visual language perfectly.

## Lessons in Restraint

The biggest lesson from designing Cursed Logic's UI was the power of **restraint**. Every time I wanted to add a visual flourish — a particle effect, a gradient, an extra color — I asked: "Does this serve the atmosphere?" If the answer was no, it didn't go in.

The result is an interface that feels *intentional*. Nothing is decorative. Every pixel serves the fiction. And players notice. The most common feedback about Cursed Logic's aesthetics isn't "it looks cool" — it's "it *feels* like something." That feeling — that atmospheric immersion — is the highest compliment a UI designer can receive.

*In the darkness, the Protocol waits. The cursor blinks. Choose your action.*
