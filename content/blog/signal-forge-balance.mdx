## Balance Is a Moving Target

If you've ever tried to balance a card game, you know the feeling: you fix one overpowered combo, and three new ones emerge from the shadows. You nerf a dominant card, and suddenly a previously mediocre card becomes king. Balancing a deckbuilder with 20+ keywords, four waveform types, multiple enemy archetypes, and an upgrade system is less like solving an equation and more like tuning a living ecosystem.

I won't pretend Signal Forge is perfectly balanced — no card game is. But I've developed frameworks and principles that keep the game in a healthy state, and I want to share them because balance methodology is one of the least-discussed topics in indie game development.

## The Cost Curve

Every card in Signal Forge has a cost in the **Static** resource (your energy for the turn). The fundamental question of balance is: *how much should X cost?* My approach starts with a baseline cost curve.

### The Baseline Formula

```
Base Value = (Damage × 1.0) + (Shield × 0.8) + (Keyword Value × varies)
Cost = ceil(Base Value / Cost Efficiency Ratio)
```

Each keyword has an assigned value based on its impact:

| Keyword | Value Modifier | Reasoning |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Piercing | +1.5 | Bypasses shields entirely — extremely powerful |
| AOE | +2.0 | Multiplied by enemy count, huge in multi-fights |
| Echo | +1.0 | Effectively doubles the card at reduced power |
| Exhaust | -1.0 | *Reduces* cost — one-time use is a real downside |
| Growing | +1.5 | Infinite scaling has massive late-fight value |
| Retain | +0.5 | Convenience, not raw power |
| Bleed | +0.8 per stack | DoT value depends on fight length |
| Wildcard | +0.0 | Variance averages out; neither buff nor nerf |

This formula gives me a starting point — a mathematically "fair" cost for every card. But math alone doesn't make good balance.

## Why Math Isn't Enough

The cost curve formula tells me that a 6-damage Piercing card should cost about 2 Static. And mathematically, that's fair. But in practice, that card might be the best card in the game against certain enemies and worthless against others. **Context matters more than numbers.**

This is where playtesting becomes indispensable. I track win rates per card — which cards appear in winning runs and which appear in losing runs. If a card shows up in 80% of winning decks, it's either too strong or too universally useful. Either way, something needs to change.

### The "Always Pick" Problem

The biggest balance red flag is a card that players **always** pick when offered. In a well-balanced game, every card choice should depend on context: your current deck, the enemies ahead, your resources, your strategy. If a card is *always* good regardless of context, it's warping the game around itself.

Early in development, I had a card called **Full Spectrum Blast**: 3 cost, 10 damage, AOE, Piercing. It was comically overpowered. Players would pick it in every single run regardless of their deck strategy. The card was so good that it eliminated meaningful deckbuilding decisions — which is the *opposite* of what a deckbuilder should do.

I split it into two cards: one with AOE at lower damage, one with Piercing at higher cost. Both are good in the right context, but neither is an auto-pick. **Constraint creates choice.**

## Rarity as a Balance Lever

Signal Forge has three card rarities: **Common**, **Uncommon**, and **Rare**. Rarity serves two purposes:

1. **Power gating** — Stronger cards appear less frequently, preventing early snowballing.
2. **Excitement generation** — Rare cards feel special precisely because you see them infrequently.

### Rarity Distribution Guidelines

- **Common** cards: Bread and butter. 1-2 keywords, straightforward effects, cost 0-2 Static. You'll see these constantly.
- **Uncommon** cards: Synergy enablers. 2-3 keywords, conditional power, cost 1-3 Static. These define your deck's direction.
- **Rare** cards: Build-arounds. 3-4 keywords, powerful but demanding effects, cost 2-4 Static. Finding a Rare card should make you reconsider your entire strategy.

The key insight is that rarity isn't just about power level — it's about **complexity**. Common cards should be instantly readable. Rare cards can demand more thought because players encounter them less frequently and are willing to invest more mental energy evaluating them.

## The Upgrade System: +25% Stats

Every card in Signal Forge can be upgraded once, gaining a flat **+25% boost to all numerical stats** (rounded up). A card that deals 8 damage becomes 10. A card that shields 6 becomes 8. Costs remain the same.

The +25% number wasn't arbitrary. I tested values from +10% to +50%:

- **+10%** felt imperceptible. Players didn't feel rewarded for upgrading.
- **+50%** made upgraded cards so much better than base cards that un-upgraded cards felt unplayable.
- **+25%** hits the sweet spot: *noticeable but not mandatory*. An upgraded card is clearly better, but an un-upgraded card with better synergy can still be the right choice.

### The Upgrade Decision

Upgrades in Signal Forge are a limited resource — you can only upgrade at specific events during a run. This creates one of the game's most agonizing decisions: *which card do you upgrade?*

The optimal upgrade is rarely the highest-damage card. Consider:

- Upgrading a **Growing** card is incredibly efficient because the +25% applies to the base *and* compounds with each play.
- Upgrading a **Multihit** card multiplies the +25% across all hits.
- Upgrading a **Sustain** card gives value every single turn for the rest of the fight.

These non-obvious upgrade targets are intentional. I want players to think deeply about where +25% has the most impact, not just slap it on whatever hits hardest.

## Avoiding Dominant Strategies

A "dominant strategy" is a strategy that is always optimal regardless of circumstances. In a deckbuilder, this is a death sentence for replayability. If players discover that "just draft every Bleed card" wins 90% of runs, they'll do that once, feel clever, and then never play again because the puzzle is solved.

My approach to preventing dominant strategies:

### Hard Counters in the Enemy Pool

For every strategy, at least one enemy archetype counters it. Bleed decks are countered by enemies that cleanse debuffs. Shield-stacking decks are countered by enemies with Piercing. Scaling decks are countered by enemies with hard enrage timers. No strategy is universally safe.

### Diminishing Returns on Keyword Stacking

Having two Bleed cards is great. Having four is very good. Having eight is... not much better than six. I implement soft diminishing returns on keyword stacking by designing enemy health pools that make excessive stacking unnecessary. If enemies have 80 HP, you don't need 200 points of Bleed — meaning those extra Bleed cards are dead draws.

### Card Offering Variance

The cards offered to players during runs are weighted but randomized. You can't guarantee you'll see specific cards, so building a hyper-specific strategy is inherently risky. This forces adaptive play — you have to work with what the run gives you, not force a predetermined plan.

## The Spreadsheet of Truth

I maintain a massive spreadsheet (affectionately called "The Spreadsheet of Truth") that tracks every card's stats, keywords, cost, rarity, win rate, pick rate, and upgrade priority. When I make a balance change, I log it with the date, rationale, and expected outcome. This historical record is invaluable for understanding *why* things are the way they are — especially when I return to a card months later and wonder what I was thinking.

Balance is never done. It's a continuous conversation between the designer's intent and the players' creativity. And honestly? That's one of the most rewarding parts of making a card game. Every time a player finds a combo I didn't expect, it's an opportunity to learn something new about my own game.
