## The Daily Game Revolution

When Josh Wardle released Wordle to the public in October 2021, he probably didn't expect it to reshape how millions of people start their mornings. By January 2022, the game had exploded from 90 players to over 300,000 daily users, leading to its acquisition by The New York Times for a reported seven-figure sum. Wordle didn't succeed because it was technically impressive — it succeeded because it gave people a tiny, satisfying ritual they could share.

We've been thinking about daily games since before Wordle went viral, but its success crystallized something we'd been feeling: **the most powerful force in game design isn't graphics or mechanics — it's habit.**

## The Habit Loop: Why Daily Works

Nir Eyal's 2014 book *Hooked: How to Build Habit-Forming Products* lays out a four-step model for habit formation: **trigger, action, variable reward, investment.** Daily games hit every step naturally.

- **Trigger**: The game resets at midnight. Your Discord server pings. Your friends have already posted their scores.
- **Action**: You play — it takes two to five minutes, low friction, low commitment.
- **Variable reward**: Some days you nail it in two guesses. Some days you barely scrape by. The unpredictability keeps you coming back.
- **Investment**: You build a streak. You've played 47 days in a row. Breaking the streak feels like losing something real.

This isn't manipulation. It's understanding that people *want* small rituals in their day. The psychologist B.J. Fogg's research on tiny habits confirms this: behaviors that are easy, triggered reliably, and immediately rewarding become automatic. A daily game is the textbook tiny habit.

## Building RMHdle and RMHConnections

When we set out to build daily games for RMH Studios, we made a deliberate choice: **build them inside Discord, not on a website.**

Why? Because that's where our community already lives. Discord is where players discuss RMHbox rounds, share Signal Forge decks, and coordinate RMHTube watch parties. Adding a daily game to that space meant the trigger was already built in — you open Discord to talk to your friends, and the daily puzzle is right there.

### RMHdle

RMHdle is our take on the daily word-guessing format. Each day presents a new puzzle tied to gaming terminology, RMH game references, or web development concepts. We didn't just clone Wordle — we tailored it to our community's shared vocabulary.

The bot posts the puzzle at midnight UTC. Players submit guesses via slash commands. Results render as emoji grids they can share in chat without spoiling the answer. It's simple, it's fast, and it sparks conversation every single morning.

### RMHConnections

Inspired by the NYT's Connections game (launched June 2023), RMHConnections asks players to group 16 items into four hidden categories. Our twist: the categories often reference our games, web tech, or inside jokes the community has built over months.

This is where things get interesting from a community perspective. RMHConnections is *hard*. The categories are intentionally tricky, with red herrings that punish surface-level thinking. This difficulty creates shared struggle — and shared struggle is the foundation of community bonding.

## What the Data Tells Us

After running both daily games for several months, we've observed patterns that reinforce the habit loop research:

- **Streak psychology is real.** Players with streaks longer than 14 days play at a rate 3x higher than new players. They set alarms. They play while commuting. The streak becomes part of their identity.
- **Sharing is the growth engine.** Every time a player posts their emoji grid in a Discord channel, it's an organic advertisement. Other members see it, get curious, and start playing. This mirrors exactly how Wordle spread — through those green and yellow squares on Twitter.
- **Daily games increase overall engagement.** Players who engage with RMHdle or RMHConnections are significantly more likely to join RMHbox sessions, participate in typing races on RMH Type, or hop into RMHTube watch parties. The daily game is a gateway drug to deeper community participation.

## The Design Constraints That Matter

Building a daily game sounds easy. It's not. The constraints are deceptively strict:

### It Must Be Completable in Under Five Minutes

Daily games compete with checking email, scrolling social media, and drinking coffee. If your game takes 15 minutes, it's not a daily habit — it's a commitment. We target a two-to-four-minute solve time for both RMHdle and RMHConnections.

### It Must Be Fair

Every player gets the same puzzle. No randomization, no difficulty scaling. This is critical for the social layer — when everyone faces the same challenge, scores become comparable and conversations become richer. "How did you get it in two?" only works if we both had the same puzzle.

### It Must Support Asynchronous Play

Unlike RMHbox, which requires real-time multiplayer, daily games must work on each player's schedule. Someone playing at 7 AM and someone playing at 11 PM should have identical experiences. This async nature is what makes daily games compatible with real life.

### It Must Reset Cleanly

The daily reset isn't just a timer — it's a psychological fresh start. Yesterday's failure doesn't matter. Today is a new puzzle, a new chance. This is why daily games maintain engagement better than games with persistent failure states.

## Discord as a Platform

Building directly on Discord gave us advantages we wouldn't have on the web:

**Notifications are built in.** Discord already handles push notifications, mentions, and channel alerts. We don't need to build a notification system or beg for email signups.

**Identity is built in.** Every player has a Discord username, avatar, and presence in the server. We don't need accounts, auth flows, or profile systems. Players are already *people* in the community.

**Social context is built in.** When you post your RMHdle score, it appears in a channel where people know you. It's not a tweet into the void — it's a conversation with friends. This dramatically increases the reward phase of the habit loop.

The tradeoff is reach — we're limited to people in our Discord server. But we've found that this constraint actually *strengthens* the community dynamic. A smaller, engaged group creates better social bonds than a massive, anonymous player base.

## Beyond the Puzzle: Community Rituals

The most surprising outcome of our daily games hasn't been retention metrics — it's been the emergence of community rituals we didn't design.

Players created unofficial leaderboards. They developed shorthand for discussing strategies without spoiling answers. A subset of players started a tradition of writing haiku about particularly frustrating puzzles. None of this was planned. It emerged organically because we gave the community a shared daily touchpoint.

This aligns with what the game designer Raph Koster argues in *A Theory of Fun for Game Design*: the best games create spaces for emergent social behavior. The game itself is just the seed. The community is the garden.

## What's Next

We're exploring ways to deepen the daily game experience without breaking what makes it work. Ideas on our radar include weekly meta-puzzles that combine daily answers, community-created puzzle submissions, and cross-game rewards that connect your daily game streaks to unlockables in RMHbox or Signal Forge.

The key principle remains: **respect the player's time, reward their consistency, and give them something to talk about.** That's the formula that turned Wordle into a phenomenon, and it's the formula we're building on at RMH Studios.

Daily games aren't a genre. They're a relationship with your players. And like any relationship, they're built one day at a time.
