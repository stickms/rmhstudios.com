## A Game Is Only as Good as Its Library

The best rhythm game engine in the world doesn't matter if there's nothing to play. I learned this lesson early in development — the technical side of Slice It! was coming together beautifully, but every time I demoed it to someone, the first question was always: *"Where do I get songs?"*

For Slice It! to thrive, it needed more than a handful of demo tracks. It needed a **living, growing library** powered by the community itself. Players upload music. Other players discover it, play it, rate it, compete on it, and talk about it. The library becomes a destination — a reason to come back every day to see what's new.

Building that ecosystem turned out to be one of the most rewarding parts of the entire project.

## The Upload Flow

Uploading a song to Slice It! is intentionally simple. We wanted the barrier to be as low as possible — if you have an audio file, you should be able to share it with the community in under a minute.

The upload form captures a few key details:

- **Audio file** (MP3, WAV, OGG, FLAC — we support the major formats)
- **Song title and artist name**
- **Genre tags** (selected from a predefined list plus custom tags)
- **Optional album art**

When the upload is submitted, the server processes the audio file in the background. It decodes the audio, runs a quick validation pass to ensure the file isn't corrupted or too short, generates a preview waveform for the library UI, and pre-computes the beatmap so the first player doesn't have to wait.

```typescript
async function processSongUpload(file: Buffer, metadata: SongMetadata) {
  const audioBuffer = await decodeAudio(file);
  
  validateAudio(audioBuffer); // throws if invalid
  
  const waveform = generateWaveformPreview(audioBuffer);
  const beatmap = generateBeatmap(audioBuffer);
  const duration = audioBuffer.duration;
  
  await db.song.create({
    data: {
      title: metadata.title,
      artist: metadata.artist,
      genre: metadata.genre,
      duration,
      waveform,
      beatmapData: beatmap,
      uploadedBy: metadata.userId,
    },
  });
}
```

## The Song Library Interface

The library is where players spend their time when they're not actively playing. It's designed to make discovery effortless and browsing enjoyable.

### Search and Filtering

Players can search by song title, artist, genre, or uploader. Filters narrow results by duration, difficulty, rating, and upload date. The search is fuzzy — typing "beathoven" will still find "Beethoven" — because typos shouldn't stand between a player and a great song.

### Sort Options

The library supports multiple sort modes:

- **Trending** — Songs gaining plays and ratings quickly
- **Top Rated** — Highest community ratings over all time
- **Most Played** — Pure play count ranking
- **Newest** — Most recent uploads first
- **Random Discovery** — A random shuffle for adventurous players

The **Trending** algorithm deserves a brief mention. It's a time-decayed scoring system that weights recent activity more heavily than historical totals. A song uploaded yesterday with 50 plays and a 4.5 rating will rank higher than a song from three months ago with 500 plays and a 4.8 rating. This keeps the library feeling fresh and gives new uploads a fair chance at visibility.

### Song Cards

Each song in the library shows a compact card with the title, artist, duration, difficulty indicator, community rating, play count, and the waveform preview. Hovering over a card plays a short audio preview — just enough to decide if you want to play the full song.

## Ratings and Reviews

After playing a song, players can rate it from 1 to 5 stars. They can also leave a written comment — thoughts on the music, the generated beatmap quality, or just general vibes. These ratings and comments are visible on the song's detail page.

Comments are threaded, allowing conversations between players about specific songs. I've already seen fascinating discussions emerge during playtesting — players sharing strategies for tricky sections, debating whether a particular beatmap captured the song's energy, and recommending similar tracks.

The rating system feeds directly into the library's discovery algorithms. Higher-rated songs surface more prominently, creating a virtuous cycle: good music rises, players find it, they rate it highly, which pushes it higher. It's the flywheel effect that every community platform dreams of.

## Per-Song Leaderboards

Every song in the library has its own **leaderboard**. After completing a song, your score is submitted and ranked against every other player who has played that same track.

Leaderboards show the top 100 scores by default, along with your personal ranking and the rankings of anyone on your friends list. Each entry displays the player's name, score, max combo, accuracy percentage, and the date they set the record.

This creates compelling long-tail engagement. Players don't just play a song once — they come back to improve their ranking. A new high score appearing above yours is a notification, a challenge, a reason to fire up the game and try one more time.

### Score Integrity

For leaderboards to matter, players need to trust them. We implement several measures to prevent cheating:

- **Server-side score validation** that cross-references hit counts and timing against the beatmap
- **Replay data submission** that allows suspicious scores to be reviewed
- **Statistical anomaly detection** that flags scores mathematically unlikely given the song's difficulty

We can't prevent all cheating — no system can — but we can make it difficult enough that the vast majority of leaderboard entries are legitimate.

## Content Moderation

Opening uploads to the community means opening the door to abuse. Copyrighted music, inappropriate content, spam uploads, and low-quality files are all potential problems.

Our moderation approach is multi-layered:

### Automated Checks

- **Audio fingerprinting** to detect known copyrighted tracks (we compare against a database of flagged fingerprints)
- **Duration limits** — songs shorter than 30 seconds or longer than 15 minutes are rejected
- **File size limits** — prevents someone from uploading a 2GB WAV file
- **Metadata validation** — titles and artist fields are checked for profanity and spam patterns

### Community Reporting

Players can report songs for copyright issues, inappropriate content, or quality problems. Reports are reviewed by moderators, and songs that violate community guidelines are removed. Repeat offenders receive upload restrictions.

### Upload Reputation

New accounts have limited upload slots. As players build a track record of quality uploads (reflected by ratings and play counts), their upload quota increases. This naturally throttles spam while rewarding genuine contributors.

## Building a Culture

The technical infrastructure of a community platform is only half the battle. The other half is **culture** — the unwritten norms that determine what the community feels like.

We've been intentional about fostering a positive, encouraging atmosphere. The comment system shows the commenter's play stats for that song (so you know if the person giving feedback has actually played it). Rating is constructive, not punitive. The UI language emphasizes discovery and sharing over competition and gatekeeping.

Early playtesting helped enormously here. The first hundred or so players established the tone — supportive, curious, excited to share music. We've learned to protect that tone, because once it's set, it tends to self-reinforce. New players see existing comments and match the vibe.

## Reflections

Building the community features forced me to think about Slice It! differently. The audio engine and beatmap generator are technical achievements. But the community — the shared library, the ratings, the comments, the leaderboards — that's what turns Slice It! from a *product* into a *place*.

A place where someone uploads a song they love, and a stranger on the other side of the world discovers it, plays it, and leaves a comment saying it made their day. That's the magic I was chasing. And slowly, post by post, song by song, it's happening.
