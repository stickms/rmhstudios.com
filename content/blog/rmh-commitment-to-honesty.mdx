## The Trust Problem in Gaming

The gaming industry has a credibility problem. Players have been burned — repeatedly — by studios that prioritize hype over honesty, and the consequences are eroding the relationship between developers and the communities they serve.

Consider the launch of **Cyberpunk 2077** in December 2020. CD Projekt Red, a studio once celebrated for consumer-friendly practices, shipped a game that was borderline unplayable on the consoles it was marketed for. Review copies were restricted to PC only, hiding the console performance issues from press and players alike. The fallout was severe: Sony pulled the game from the PlayStation Store, CD Projekt's stock price cratered, and a class-action lawsuit followed. The trust that studio had built over a decade with *The Witcher 3* evaporated in a week.

Then came September 2023, when **Unity Technologies announced a runtime fee** that would charge developers every time a game using its engine was installed. The backlash was immediate and volcanic. Indie studios that had built their entire businesses on Unity felt betrayed — some had games already shipped, and the retroactive nature of the fee threatened their livelihoods. Unity eventually walked back most of the changes, but the damage was done. Developers learned a hard lesson: the platforms and tools you depend on can change the rules without warning.

These aren't isolated incidents. They're symptoms of a broader pattern where short-term revenue pressure overrides long-term trust.

## Why We Founded RMH Studios on Transparency

When we started RMH Studios, we made a deliberate choice: **we would rather under-promise and over-deliver than chase hype.** That might sound like a platitude, but we've embedded it into how we operate at every level.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **We don't announce features until they're working.** If you see us talk about a mechanic in Signal Forge or a new minigame in RMHbox, it exists in a playable state. We've watched too many studios announce ambitious roadmaps only to silently shelve half the items.
- **We share our development process openly.** Our Discord community sees work-in-progress builds, hears about technical challenges we're facing, and gets honest timelines. When something is delayed, we say so — and we say why.
- **We don't manipulate our marketing.** No bullshots, no "target renders" passed off as gameplay, no cherry-picked review quotes stripped of context. What you see on our site is what you get in the browser tab.

## Honesty as a Competitive Advantage

There's a cynical view that honesty is a luxury — that you need hype to compete. We disagree. For an indie studio building browser games, trust is our most valuable currency.

Our players don't pay $60 upfront. They don't commit to a multi-gigabyte download. They click a link and they're playing. That low barrier to entry means we have to earn attention in seconds, but it also means **our players have zero sunk-cost bias.** They'll leave the moment something feels dishonest or manipulative. And they should.

This dynamic forces us to be better. Every claim we make about our games has to hold up the instant someone opens a browser tab. There's no install process to build anticipation, no loading screen slideshow to set expectations. The game *is* the pitch.

## What Honesty Looks Like Day-to-Day

Transparency isn't just a marketing stance — it shapes our engineering and design decisions too.

When we build features, we document our reasoning. When we make tradeoffs (and browser game development is *full* of tradeoffs — performance vs. visual fidelity, latency vs. feature richness), we talk about them publicly. We believe players are smart enough to understand that a browser game running on WebSockets has different constraints than a AAA title with dedicated servers, and we'd rather have that conversation openly than pretend the constraints don't exist.

We also fact-check ourselves rigorously. When we write about our technology stack, we cite what's real. When we compare our approach to industry alternatives, we do our homework. We've seen too many dev blogs make vague claims about "revolutionary" this or "unprecedented" that. We'd rather be specific, accurate, and occasionally boring than exciting and wrong.

### The Bar We Hold Ourselves To

Every piece of public communication from RMH Studios — whether it's a blog post, a Discord announcement, or a game description — goes through a simple filter:

1. **Is this true?** Not "technically true" or "true from a certain point of view." Actually true.
2. **Would we be comfortable if a player quoted this back to us in six months?** If the answer is no, we rewrite it.
3. **Are we being transparent about what we don't know?** Uncertainty is honest. False confidence is not.

## Building Trust in an Era of Skepticism

The gaming audience in 2024 is more skeptical than ever, and rightfully so. Years of misleading trailers, broken launch promises, and exploitative monetization have trained players to assume the worst. Rebuilding that trust — or building it from scratch as a new studio — requires consistency over time.

We're not going to earn trust with a single blog post or a single game launch. We're going to earn it by doing what we say, shipping what we show, and being honest when we fall short. That's the foundation we're building RMH Studios on, and every game we release, from RMHbox's party chaos to Temple of Joy's philosophical depths, carries that commitment.

**We believe the next generation of browser games deserves a studio that tells the truth.** That's what we're here to build.
