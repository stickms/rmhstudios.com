## The Spark

Every game starts somewhere. For me, it started with a feeling — that particular unease you get walking through a casino at 3 AM. The lights still flash, the machines still hum, but the crowd has thinned to ghosts. There's something deeply unsettling about a place designed to feel alive when it's emptying out. I wanted to bottle that feeling and pour it into a game.

**House Always Wins** is a narrative 2D pixel exploration-platformer set inside an abandoned casino. Not just any casino — one that remembers you. One that *knows* you've been here before. The moment you step in, the game whispers it: "You've been here before." And maybe you have. Maybe you haven't. But the House is certain.

## The Inspirations

I'd be lying if I said this game came from nowhere. Three games carved deep grooves in my brain, and House Always Wins lives in the intersection of all three.

### Hollow Knight: The Interconnected World

Hollow Knight taught me that exploration doesn't need a mini-map screaming at you. It needs *shortcuts*. It needs that moment where you push through a wall of challenge, find a bench, and realize you're back somewhere familiar — but from a completely different angle. The abandoned casino in House Always Wins is built like this. Every floor, every back hallway, every maintenance corridor connects. You'll find doors that don't open from one side. You'll discover elevator shafts that drop you into areas you walked past hours ago. The casino is a living maze, and learning its layout *is* the progression.

### Celeste: Room-Based Precision

Celeste proved that a platformer can make you cry. But mechanically, what I took from it was the beauty of **room-based challenge design**. Each screen is a puzzle. You enter, you assess, you execute, you move on. House Always Wins borrows this philosophy for its Event Rooms — self-contained challenge spaces that last one to three minutes. They're intense, they're themed, and they're woven into the narrative. You don't just *play* an Event Room. You experience a story beat compressed into pure gameplay.

### Undertale: Consequences and Character

And then there's Undertale. The game that made NPCs feel like people. The game where your choices didn't just branch a dialogue tree — they *haunted* you. In House Always Wins, every NPC in the casino has a history, a motive, and an opinion about your Debt level. Talk to the bartender when you're deep in Debt, and she won't just give you different dialogue. Her entire demeanor shifts. The lighting around her bar dims. The music warps. She's not angry — she's *afraid* of what you're becoming.

## The Casino as Horror

Here's the thing about casinos: they're already horror spaces. They're designed to disorient you. No clocks, no windows, carpet patterns that subtly discourage you from looking down, oxygen pumped through the vents to keep you alert. A casino is a machine built to consume time and money while making you feel like you're having fun.

Now imagine that machine *abandoned*. The lights flicker instead of dazzle. The slot machines spin on their own. The carpets are torn, revealing concrete underneath. But the *system* still runs. The House still deals. And it still expects you to play.

That's the tone I'm chasing. Not jump-scare horror. Not gore. **Atmospheric dread.** The feeling that you're inside something that was designed to manipulate you, and even in its decay, it hasn't stopped trying.

### The Color Palette

Visually, this means deep blacks and grays as the foundation. Muted golds where the casino's former glory peeks through — a gilded frame here, a brass railing there. And dim flicker accents: the occasional neon sign that still buzzes to life, a slot machine's screen casting pale light across a dusty floor. The world should feel like it's holding its breath.

## The Ritual Opening

When you first enter the game — every time, not just the first — you're presented with a coin flip. It's not a mechanic. It's a *ritual*. The coin spins in the dark, and the game speaks to you: **"You've been here before."**

The result of the flip doesn't determine whether you can play. Nothing in House Always Wins blocks your progression through RNG. But the flip sets a tone. It establishes a relationship between you and the House. Heads or tails, you're already playing by its rules.

I spent weeks debating whether to include this. It felt indulgent. But every time I prototyped it, something clicked. That moment of quiet before the game begins — it *matters*. It tells the player: this isn't just a platformer. This is a conversation.

## Why a Web Game?

House Always Wins is built with **Next.js, TypeScript, and Prisma** backed by a Neon Postgres database. It runs in the browser. And yes, I know — "a 2D pixel platformer in the browser?" But hear me out.

The web is the most accessible platform on Earth. No downloads, no installs, no Steam page to find. You click a link, you log in, and you're in the casino. For a game that's fundamentally about **persistent identity** — where the system tracks your choices, your Debt, your relationships with NPCs across sessions and even across *resets* — having auth built into the platform isn't a limitation. It's a feature.

Players log in through **Better Auth** with email/password or Discord OAuth. Their game state lives in the database. The casino remembers them not because of a save file on their hard drive, but because the *server* remembers. You can't escape by clearing your cookies. The House always wins.

## What's Next

This is Milestone 0. Right now, I have the login gate and the game shell. The casino exists as a concept, a spec, and a handful of prototype rooms. But the foundation is solid: auth works, state persists, and the architecture is ready for the world to grow.

In the coming months, I'll be sharing devlogs about each major system — the Debt mechanic, Event Room design, NPC dialogue systems, sound design, and more. This is going to be a long journey, and I want to build it in public.

Welcome to the casino. The House has been expecting you.
