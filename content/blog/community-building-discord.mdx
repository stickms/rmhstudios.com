## Why Discord Is Our Living Room

Every game studio needs a home — not just a website, but a **living space** where players, developers, and fans coexist in real time. For RMH Studios, that home is Discord.

Discord reported over **200 million monthly active users** by mid-2024, up from 150 million in 2022. It's no longer just a voice chat app for gamers. It's the default community platform for indie studios, content creators, open-source projects, and increasingly, mainstream brands. For an indie game studio building browser-based games, Discord is where our players already are.

But having a Discord server and having a **community** are different things. A server is infrastructure. A community is people who choose to spend their time with you and with each other. Building the latter requires intention, consistency, and a willingness to be present.

This is what we've learned.

## Starting From Zero

Our Discord server launched with exactly two members: us. No audience, no mailing list, no viral tweet driving signups. The early days were quiet — and that quietness was valuable.

We used the empty period to establish **tone and structure** before anyone arrived. We set up channels, wrote welcome messages, defined rules, and — critically — decided what kind of community we wanted to be. Not every server needs to be the same. Some are chaotic and memey. Some are professional and focused. Ours aims for something in between: **welcoming, curious, and game-obsessed**.

The first members came from personal networks and early playtesters. These founding members shaped the culture more than any moderation policy could. They set the standard for how people talk to each other, what kinds of conversations happen, and how newcomers are received.

### The Cold Start Problem

The hardest phase of community building is the **cold start** — when there aren't enough members to sustain organic conversation. A new member joins, sees an empty chat, and leaves. Without a critical mass of active participants, the server feels dead.

We solved the cold start problem with three strategies:

1. **Developer presence.** In the early months, we (the developers) were the most active members. We shared development updates, asked for opinions, posted behind-the-scenes content, and responded to every message. Players who joined found active conversation because we made sure there was always something happening.

2. **Structured daily content.** We created recurring events — daily game challenges, weekly feedback threads, development livestreams — that gave members a reason to check in regularly. Even with few members, a daily challenge creates a rhythm that makes the server feel alive.

3. **Bot-driven engagement.** This is where our custom Discord bots became essential.

## RMHdle and RMHConnections: Games as Community Tools

Our most effective community engagement tools aren't announcements or update posts — they're **games**.

**RMHdle** is our daily word puzzle, inspired by the Wordle phenomenon that captivated the internet in 2022. Each day, a new puzzle is posted automatically. Members submit their guesses and share their results (using the now-familiar colored square grid) without spoiling the answer. The daily puzzle creates a shared ritual — something members do every morning, a reason to open Discord.

**RMHConnections** is our take on the NYT Connections format — a daily grouping puzzle where players categorize 16 items into four hidden groups. Like RMHdle, it's posted automatically each day and generates conversation as members discuss strategies and compare results.

These aren't just games — they're **conversation starters**. The daily puzzles generate more discussion than any announcement we've ever posted. Members debate strategies, celebrate streaks, commiserate over tough puzzles, and develop friendly rivalries over who solves them fastest. The puzzles transform passive members into active participants.

### Building the Bots

Both RMHdle and RMHConnections run as custom Discord bots built with the Discord.js library. The bots handle:

- **Puzzle generation** — creating or selecting daily puzzles from a curated database
- **Submission tracking** — recording guesses and maintaining streaks
- **Leaderboards** — weekly and monthly rankings that drive competition
- **Spoiler prevention** — ensuring answers aren't visible until the puzzle period ends
- **Statistics** — tracking community-wide solve rates and difficulty ratings

The bots were surprisingly affordable to build and run. Discord's bot API is well-documented, hosting costs are minimal for a bot serving hundreds of users, and the engagement return is enormous relative to the investment.

## Channel Architecture

How you organize a Discord server matters more than most people think. Channel structure determines what conversations happen, where they happen, and whether new members can find their way around.

Our server uses a deliberate channel architecture:

**Welcome zone:**
- `#welcome` — automated greeting with server rules and role selection
- `#introductions` — new members introduce themselves (prompted by the welcome message)

**Game channels:**
- One channel per game (e.g., `#rmhbox`, `#signal-forge`, `#slice-it`)
- `#daily-games` — RMHdle and RMHConnections live here
- `#game-feedback` — structured feedback for any game

**Community channels:**
- `#general` — open conversation
- `#off-topic` — non-gaming discussion
- `#share-your-stuff` — members share their own creative work

**Development channels:**
- `#dev-updates` — official development news (announcements-style, limited posting)
- `#dev-log` — informal development progress, screenshots, questions
- `#bug-reports` — structured bug reporting with templates

The key principle is **channel separation by intent**. A player looking for game tips shouldn't have to scroll through bug reports. A developer posting an update shouldn't compete with general chat. Each channel has a clear purpose, and we gently redirect off-topic messages to the right place.

## Moderation Lessons

We've learned several moderation lessons the hard way:

**Rules must be specific.** "Be respectful" is a good principle but a bad rule. It's unenforceable because everyone's definition of "respectful" differs. Our rules specify concrete behaviors: no personal attacks, no spam, no NSFW content, no unsolicited promotion. Specific rules create clear expectations.

**Moderation must be consistent.** Inconsistent enforcement breeds resentment. If one member is warned for something another member did without consequence, trust erodes. We maintain a moderation log and review it regularly to ensure consistency.

**Automoderation handles volume; humans handle nuance.** We use Discord's AutoMod for keyword filtering, spam detection, and link restrictions. But context-dependent moderation — is this joke offensive or playful? Is this criticism constructive or hostile? — requires human judgment. Our moderators are empowered to use judgment, and we back their decisions.

**Escalation paths matter.** Members need a way to report issues privately. Our server has a `#mod-mail` channel where members can message the moderation team confidentially. This prevents public confrontations and gives moderators time to investigate before acting.

## What Doesn't Work

Transparency means sharing failures, not just successes. Here's what didn't work for us:

**Excessive channel creation.** We initially created channels for every possible topic. Most sat empty, making the server feel abandoned. We consolidated aggressively, and activity concentrated in fewer, busier channels. Empty channels are worse than missing channels.

**Forced engagement.** Early on, we tried assigning "daily discussion questions" to spark conversation. It felt artificial, and members didn't engage. Organic conversation sparked by game updates and daily puzzles was far more effective than manufactured prompts.

**Giveaways for growth.** We experimented with "invite friends for a chance to win" promotions. They drove signups but not retention. Members who join for a giveaway leave after it ends. Organic growth through genuine interest produces more valuable community members than incentivized growth.

## Growing Beyond the Server

As our community grows, we're thinking about how Discord fits into a broader community ecosystem. Discord is excellent for real-time conversation but poor for long-form content, documentation, or searchable knowledge. We're exploring how to extend community interaction through:

- **Our blog** (you're reading it) for long-form development content
- **In-game community features** like shared leaderboards and player profiles
- **Community events** like game jams and tournaments that originate in Discord but play out in our games

The Discord server remains the heartbeat — the place where members connect daily, share ideas, and feel like part of something. But a healthy community eventually outgrows any single platform.

## The Real Community Metric

We don't measure community health by member count. We measure it by **daily active participants** — the number of unique members who post, react, or engage each day. A server with 10,000 members and 20 daily participants is a ghost town. A server with 500 members and 100 daily participants is thriving.

Our daily puzzles, consistent developer presence, and genuine engagement have built something we're proud of: a community where people know each other's names, celebrate each other's achievements, and show up every day not because they have to, but because they want to.

That's what community means. And we're building it one daily puzzle at a time.
