## The Staleness Problem

Here's a confession: about two months into development, Cursed Logic had a problem. The four-action system was elegant. The Protocol AI was smart. But after a dozen duels, patterns started to solidify. Experienced players found "safe" strategies — action sequences that worked reliably against most Protocol behaviors. The game was becoming *solvable*.

This terrified me. A tactical duel game that can be solved is a dead game. If there's a dominant strategy, every duel becomes an exercise in executing that strategy rather than making interesting decisions. The magic vanishes.

I needed something that would break open settled strategies. Something that would make every duel feel different — not just because of the Protocol's variant, but because the *rules themselves* would shift beneath your feet.

That something was the **mutation system**.

## What Are Mutations?

Mutations are modifiers that change how a round works. At the start of certain rounds during a duel, a mutation activates that alters the rules for that round only. Some mutations are mild adjustments. Others are dramatic rule changes that force you to completely reconsider your approach.

Here are some examples of mutations we've implemented:

### Damage Mutations

**Amplified Round** — All Strike damage is doubled. This single change transforms the round's entire decision tree. Striking becomes enormously rewarding but also enormously risky, because if the Protocol Strikes back while you both commit to aggression, the damage is catastrophic. Block becomes significantly more valuable. Prepare becomes terrifying because a double-damage Strike will devastate you.

**Dampened Round** — All damage is halved. Suddenly aggression is less rewarding and defensive play is more viable. This mutation favors Prepare-heavy strategies because the risk of taking a Strike while Preparing is reduced.

### Action Mutations

**Locked Action** — One random action is disabled for the round. Imagine losing access to Block for a round. Suddenly you can't defend traditionally — you have to either match aggression with the Protocol, Prepare and hope they don't Strike, or Probe and accept the damage. Losing an action forces creative adaptation.

**Mirrored Round** — The outcome matrix is reversed. Actions that normally win now lose, and vice versa. Block beats Prepare. Strike loses to Prepare. Everything you've internalized about the action interactions gets flipped. Your muscle memory becomes your enemy.

### Information Mutations

**Fog Round** — Probe returns unreliable information. The data you get might be accurate, might be partially corrupted, or might be completely fabricated. This is devastating for Probe-reliant players and forces them to play on instinct alone.

**Transparent Round** — Both you *and* the Protocol can see each other's action before committing. This transforms the round into a pure game theory problem — with perfect information, the "correct" play depends entirely on what you think the other side will do with their perfect information. It's wonderfully mind-bending.

## Designing Mutations That Force Adaptation

Not all mutation ideas make it into the game. We have a set of criteria that every mutation must meet:

**1. It must change the optimal strategy.** If a mutation doesn't alter which action is "best" in at least some situations, it's not doing its job. Every mutation should make players pause and reconsider.

**2. It must be instantly understandable.** Mutations are announced at the start of a round, and players need to immediately grasp the implications. "Strike damage doubled" is clear. "Action resolution order is modified based on stance differential weighted by previous round outcomes" is not. Clarity is non-negotiable.

**3. It must affect both sides equally.** Mutations change the rules for the round, not the rules for one side. This keeps things fair and prevents the feeling that the game is actively cheating. Both you and the Protocol operate under the same modified rules.

**4. It must create interesting decisions, not random outcomes.** A mutation that says "50% chance all actions fail" would certainly make the round different, but the difference would be random rather than strategic. Good mutations change the *value landscape* of actions rather than introducing dice rolls.

### The Mutation Pipeline

Technically, mutations are implemented as middleware that wraps around the round resolution logic:

```typescript
interface Mutation {
  id: string;
  name: string;
  description: string;
  modifyActions?: (actions: ActionPair) => ActionPair;
  modifyDamage?: (damage: number, source: 'player' | 'protocol') => number;
  modifyProbe?: (probeResult: ProbeData) => ProbeData;
  modifyStance?: (stanceChange: number) => number;
}

function resolveRoundWithMutation(state: GameState, mutation: Mutation | null): GameState {
  let actions = { player: state.playerAction, protocol: state.protocolAction };

  if (mutation?.modifyActions) {
    actions = mutation.modifyActions(actions);
  }

  let result = resolveActions(actions);

  if (mutation?.modifyDamage) {
    result.playerDamage = mutation.modifyDamage(result.playerDamage, 'protocol');
    result.protocolDamage = mutation.modifyDamage(result.protocolDamage, 'player');
  }

  return applyResult(state, result);
}
```

This pipeline approach means mutations are composable. In theory, we could stack multiple mutations on a single round for truly chaotic duels. We haven't shipped that feature yet, but the architecture supports it, and it's something I'm actively considering for a future "Chaos Mode."

## Mutation Frequency and Pacing

Not every round is mutated. Mutation frequency is carefully tuned to create a rhythm: normal rounds establish your baseline strategy, and mutated rounds disrupt it. Currently, about one in every three to four rounds carries a mutation.

This pacing is crucial. If every round were mutated, players would never feel grounded in the core action system. If mutations were too rare, they'd feel like novelty interruptions rather than meaningful strategic elements. The current frequency creates a **heartbeat pattern** — steady, predictable drumbeat of normal rounds punctuated by the irregular, exciting spike of a mutation.

### Mutation Selection

Mutations aren't purely random. The game tracks which mutations have appeared in the current duel and avoids repeats. It also considers the current state of the duel — a close, low-health duel is more likely to generate high-tension mutations like Amplified Round than a lopsided duel where one side is clearly winning.

This adaptive selection creates moments of incredible drama. When both you and the Protocol are at low health, and suddenly Amplified Round activates, the stakes skyrocket. That round becomes a sudden-death showdown where one correct read wins the duel. These moments are emergent — they arise naturally from the intersection of game state and mutation selection — and they're the stories players remember.

## Lessons Learned

Building the mutation system taught me several valuable lessons:

**Disruption is a feature, not a bug.** Players initially resist having their strategies broken. But once they accept that adaptation *is* the strategy, their engagement deepens dramatically. The frustration of losing a settled plan is replaced by the excitement of solving a new puzzle every round.

**Simple rules create complex situations.** "Strike damage is doubled" is a five-word mutation, but its strategic implications cascade through every possible action combination. The best mutations are short sentences with long shadows.

**Fairness is perception.** Even though mutations affect both sides equally, players sometimes feel that a mutation unfairly advantages the Protocol. This usually happens when a mutation invalidates the player's preferred playstyle. The solution isn't removing those mutations — it's ensuring the mutation pool is diverse enough that every playstyle gets disrupted equally over time.

The mutation system transformed Cursed Logic from a clever tactical puzzle into a living, breathing duel that never plays the same way twice. Every duel is a unique combination of Protocol variant, mutation sequence, and player decisions. That combinatorial explosion of possibilities is what keeps players coming back, and it's what keeps me excited to add new mutations to the pool.

*The rules are always changing. Adapt or be consumed.*
