## The Problem With Story in Browser Games

There's a persistent myth that browser games can't tell real stories. The assumption goes something like this: if a game runs in a tab, it must be shallow — a clicker, a puzzle, maybe a tower defense. Narrative depth is reserved for the $60 boxed product or the carefully curated Steam release.

We built Echoes of the Spire to prove that assumption wrong.

Echoes is a narrative extraction thriller. You play as an operative interfacing with a damaged neural archive, pulling fragments of memory from a corrupted data structure. The story doesn't unfold in cutscenes or dialogue trees — it **emerges** from the act of extraction itself. Every fragment you recover reshapes your understanding of what happened inside the Spire, and every choice you make about which threads to pursue changes the story you ultimately piece together.

This post is about how we designed that system, what influenced us, and why we believe the browser is an underrated medium for narrative games.

## Learning From Disco Elysium

You can't talk about modern narrative RPGs without talking about **Disco Elysium**. ZA/UM's 2019 masterpiece didn't just tell a detective story — it made the act of thinking into gameplay. Your skills argued with each other. Your internal monologue was a character. The world reacted not just to what you did, but to what kind of person you were becoming.

Disco Elysium proved that narrative games don't need combat systems or AAA production values to be deeply engaging. What they need is **authorial commitment** — a willingness to write extensively, to branch genuinely, and to trust the player to find meaning in ambiguity.

We took three specific lessons from Disco Elysium into Echoes:

- **Internality as gameplay.** In Echoes, the neural-link interface means the player's "thoughts" are literally game objects. Memory fragments have emotional metadata — fear, regret, hope — and the order in which you process them changes how the operative interprets the Spire's history.
- **Skill-gated narrative.** Disco Elysium gates information behind skill checks. Echoes gates it behind extraction proficiency. The deeper you go into corrupted data sectors, the more unreliable the memories become, and the more the player must decide what to trust.
- **Tonal consistency over spectacle.** Disco Elysium's Revachol feels real because every piece of text serves the tone. We aimed for the same discipline: every fragment in Echoes serves the atmosphere of dread and discovery that defines the Spire.

## Environmental Storytelling Without an Environment Budget

AAA studios spend millions on environment art. A FromSoftware game tells stories through crumbling architecture and item placement. Naughty Dog scatters notes and artifacts across meticulously modeled spaces. These are powerful techniques — and they require budgets we don't have.

So how do you do environmental storytelling when your "environment" is a browser viewport?

Our answer was to **make the interface the environment**. In Echoes, the neural-link UI isn't just a menu system — it's a diegetic space. The corruption spreading across the archive is visible. Memory nodes pulse and decay. Error messages from the failing system tell their own story about what happened to the Spire's AI.

We drew inspiration from games like **Her Story** (Sam Barlow, 2015) and **Return of the Obra Dinn** (Lucas Pope, 2018), both of which proved that a strong interface metaphor can replace traditional environmental storytelling entirely. In Her Story, the "environment" is a police database terminal. In Obra Dinn, it's an insurance ledger and a magic watch. Neither game needs a 3D world to tell a riveting story.

For Echoes, the neural-link archive serves the same function. The player isn't walking through a space — they're navigating a **data structure**. And that data structure is the story.

## Fragmented Memories as Narrative Architecture

The core narrative mechanic in Echoes is **fragmentation**. The Spire's memory archive has been damaged, and the memories you extract are incomplete. Some are corrupted. Some contradict each other. Some are from perspectives you don't initially understand.

This fragmentation isn't a gimmick — it's the architecture of the narrative itself. We designed the story as a **non-linear web** rather than a branching tree. There's no single "correct" order to experience the fragments. Instead, meaning accumulates through juxtaposition. When you place Fragment 47 (a researcher's final log entry) next to Fragment 12 (a security camera recording from three weeks earlier), you understand something neither fragment communicates alone.

This design was influenced by ergodic literature — works like Mark Z. Danielewski's *House of Leaves* and Milorad Pavić's *Dictionary of the Khazars*, where the reader constructs narrative meaning through the act of navigation. In Echoes, the player is both detective and editor, assembling a story from raw material.

### The Technical Side of Fragmented Narrative

Implementing this in a browser required careful engineering. Each memory fragment is a structured data object with:

- **Content** — the actual narrative text, audio, or visual data
- **Metadata** — timestamps, emotional valence, reliability scores
- **Connection nodes** — references to related fragments
- **Corruption level** — determines how much of the content is accessible

The fragment graph is stored in a lightweight state management system built on React. When a player extracts a new fragment, the system recalculates narrative coherence scores and updates the "story so far" summary that the neural-link AI provides. This summary is itself unreliable — another layer of narrative design where the tool the player depends on has its own biases and blind spots.

## The Neural-Link as Unreliable Narrator

One of our favorite design decisions was making the neural-link AI an **unreliable narrator**. The AI that helps you navigate the archive has its own interpretation of events, and that interpretation shifts based on what you've extracted and in what order.

This creates a fascinating dynamic: the player must decide whether to trust the tool they're using to understand the story. It's a meta-narrative layer that works particularly well in a browser game, where the interface between player and content is already thin. The neural-link AI speaks through tooltip text, summary panels, and contextual warnings — all native UI patterns that players instinctively trust. Subverting that trust is one of the most powerful narrative tools we've found.

## Why the Browser Works for Narrative Games

We've come to believe that the browser is actually an **ideal** medium for certain kinds of narrative games. Here's why:

- **Low friction.** There's no install, no launcher, no loading screen. A player can click a link and be inside the story in seconds. For a narrative game that depends on mood and immersion, eliminating friction is enormous.
- **Intimacy of the tab.** A browser tab is a private space. It's where you read articles, check email, do personal things. A game that lives in a tab inherits some of that intimacy. Echoes feels like discovering a secret, not like booting up entertainment.
- **Text is native.** The web was built for text. Typography, layout, responsive design — these are solved problems in the browser. For a narrative game that relies heavily on written content, the browser provides better text rendering than most game engines.
- **Seamless updates.** We can push new story content, fix narrative bugs, and adjust pacing without asking players to download patches. The story evolves in place.

## What's Next for Echoes

We're currently working on the second act of Echoes of the Spire, which introduces a second operative perspective and forces players to reconcile two conflicting accounts of the Spire's collapse. We're also experimenting with **community-driven narrative branches** — letting player choices across the entire player base influence which new fragments become available.

Narrative design in browser games is uncharted territory, and we're building the map as we go. At RMH Studios, we believe that great stories can live anywhere — including in a browser tab. Echoes of the Spire is our proof of concept, and we're just getting started.

The Spire has more secrets to give up. We just need to keep extracting.
