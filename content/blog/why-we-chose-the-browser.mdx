## The Platform Question Every Indie Faces

When you start a game studio, one of the first decisions you make is where your games will live. Steam? Epic Games Store? iOS and Android? Console? Each platform comes with its own ecosystem, audience, revenue split, and set of rules you have to follow.

We chose the browser. Not as a stepping stone to "real" platforms, not as a prototype environment, but as our primary, intentional, long-term home. Here's why.

## The Economics of Traditional Platforms

Let's start with the numbers, because the economics of game distribution are often the elephant in the room.

**Steam takes a 30% cut** of every sale. For a small indie studio, that's enormous. You make a game, you do your own marketing, you build your own community — and Valve takes nearly a third for hosting your store page and processing payments. Steam does reduce its cut to 25% after $10 million in sales and 20% after $50 million, but those thresholds are irrelevant for the vast majority of indie developers.

This revenue split became a major industry flashpoint when **Epic Games sued Apple in August 2020**, challenging the App Store's identical 30% commission. The trial, decided in September 2021, didn't fully resolve the issue — the judge ruled largely in Apple's favor on antitrust but ordered Apple to allow developers to link to external payment methods. The case laid bare what everyone in the industry already knew: **platform holders extract enormous rents from creators.**

Epic launched the Epic Games Store in December 2018 with a 12% cut specifically to undercut Steam, but the store has struggled with discoverability and has yet to become a viable primary platform for most indie studios.

Mobile is even worse. Beyond the 30% platform fee (reduced to 15% for small developers under Apple's Small Business Program), the mobile gaming market is dominated by free-to-play economics where user acquisition costs can reach $3-5 per install or higher. Competing with studios that spend millions on advertising is not a game we want to play.

## The Friction Tax

Revenue splits are painful, but there's an even bigger problem with traditional platforms: **download friction.**

Every step between "I want to play this" and "I'm playing this" loses players. Discovery on a storefront, clicking "buy" or "download," waiting for a multi-gigabyte install, launching the application, sitting through splash screens — each step is a dropout point.

Browser games eliminate almost all of that. **Click a link. You're playing.** The friction is measured in seconds, not minutes. For social games especially — the kind of experiences you share with friends in a Discord call — that instant accessibility is transformative.

Think about the games that proved this model works. **Agar.io** launched in April 2015 and became a phenomenon not because of sophisticated marketing or storefront placement, but because anyone could share a URL and have their friends playing in under ten seconds. **Slither.io** followed the same playbook in 2016, reaching 67 million monthly active users at its peak. These weren't impressive *despite* being browser games — they were successful *because* they were browser games.

Our party game platform **RMHbox** depends on this zero-friction model. When someone is hosting a game night on Discord and wants friends to join, the last thing we want is "okay, everyone go to Steam and download this 2GB client." Instead, it's a link. Click, play, done.

## What We Gain by Owning Our Platform

When you ship on Steam, your relationship with your players is mediated by Valve. Steam owns the storefront, the review system, the community hub, the update pipeline, the wishlisting data, and increasingly, the discovery algorithm that determines whether anyone ever sees your game.

By building for the browser, we own our platform:

- **Direct player relationships.** When someone plays on rmhstudios.com, we know who our players are. We can build community features, send updates, and iterate on the experience without a middleman.
- **Instant updates.** There's no certification process, no build submission, no waiting for storefront approval. We push code, and the next time a player loads the page, they have the latest version.
- **Full creative control.** No content guidelines imposed by a platform holder. No arbitrary rules about what's allowed or how it's presented. Our games, our rules.
- **Zero revenue sharing on the platform itself.** We pay for hosting and infrastructure, but those costs scale predictably and are a fraction of a 30% revenue share.

## The Counterarguments (And Why We Accept Them)

We're not going to pretend the browser is perfect. There are real tradeoffs.

**Discoverability** is the biggest challenge. Steam, whatever its flaws, has 130+ million monthly active users actively browsing for games. Our website has to earn every visitor through content, community, and word of mouth. There's no algorithm surfacing our games to millions of potential players.

**Performance limitations** are real. We covered this in our previous post on the future of browser games — the browser imposes a performance ceiling that native platforms don't. We'll never ship a browser game with the visual fidelity of a PlayStation 5 exclusive.

**Monetization models** are different. The browser gaming audience has different expectations than Steam buyers. Free-to-play, subscription, and cosmetic models all work differently when there's no download commitment.

We accept these tradeoffs because we believe the advantages — instant access, zero friction, platform independence, direct player relationships — outweigh them. And critically, the tradeoffs are shrinking. WebGPU is closing the performance gap. Content marketing and community-driven growth are becoming more effective distribution channels than storefront algorithms. The browser is getting better every year, while the traditional platform tax stays the same.

## The Dream of Instant-Play Gaming

Here's the vision that drives us: **a world where every game is one click away.**

No downloads. No installs. No updates. No "your storage is full" warnings. No platform lock-in. Just games, instantly available, on any device with a browser.

That's not a fantasy — it's the reality we're building right now. Every game in our portfolio, from the competitive chaos of RMHbox to the philosophical depths of Temple of Joy, is playable in a browser tab. We're proving, one game at a time, that the browser isn't a limitation. **It's a liberation.**

The old model of gaming — buy a $500 box, download a $70 game, wait an hour for updates — served its era well. But the next generation of gaming will be defined by accessibility, immediacy, and reach. The browser is the platform that delivers all three.

That's why we chose it. And we're not looking back.
