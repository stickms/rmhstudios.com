## The Camera Is Not Neutral

In most games, the camera is a utility. It shows you the world, it follows your character, and it tries to stay out of the way. In Satan's Library, the camera is an **active participant in the horror**. It has opinions. It has moods. And it reacts to your fear.

The dynamic camera system is one of the features I'm most proud of in the prototype so far. It blends classic fixed-angle survival horror framing with modern over-the-shoulder intimacy, transitioning between them smoothly based on two factors: **room geometry** and **player stress**. The result is a camera that feels alive — one that creates tension through composition, not just content.

## Two Modes, One System

### Fixed-Angle Mode

When you enter a large room — a grand reading hall, a vaulted archive, a mezzanine overlooking the atrium — the camera pulls back to a **fixed cinematic angle**. This is the Resident Evil heritage. The camera positions itself at a predetermined point that maximizes the drama of the space. You see your character small against the vastness. You see the shadows in the corners. You see the door on the other side of the room and the terrifying distance between you and it.

Fixed-angle mode triggers in rooms above a certain size threshold. Each large room has hand-placed camera positions that I've composed like film shots — leading lines, depth staging, negative space that implies threat. The player character becomes part of the mise-en-scène rather than the center of the frame.

### Over-the-Shoulder Mode

In tight corridors, narrow stacks, and cramped reading nooks, the camera pushes in to an **over-the-shoulder perspective**. Now you're intimate with the character. You can see their breathing. You can see the book spines inches from your face. The claustrophobia is mechanical — the camera *forces* you to feel the space the way the character feels it.

This mode also activates when stress is high, regardless of room size. As the Lock In meter drops below 50%, the camera begins creeping closer to the character even in larger spaces. At extreme stress levels, you lose the grand cinematic framing entirely — the whole world collapses to the character's immediate surroundings. You can't see what's behind you. You can barely see what's ahead.

## The Transition System

The magic is in how the camera moves between these modes. There's no cut, no fade, no jarring snap. The camera **smoothly interpolates** between positions using a custom easing system that accounts for:

- **Room geometry**: The transition speed matches the architectural transition. Walking from a corridor into a grand hall triggers a slow, sweeping pull-back. Ducking into a tight passage triggers a quick push-in.
- **Stress level**: Higher stress makes transitions faster and less smooth, adding to the feeling of instability.
- **Player velocity**: If you're running, transitions are more aggressive than when you're walking cautiously.

```
// Pseudocode for camera transition blending
function updateCameraBlend(roomSize, stressLevel, playerSpeed) {
    let targetMode = roomSize > THRESHOLD ? FIXED_ANGLE : OVER_SHOULDER

    // Stress override: high stress forces OTS regardless of room
    if (stressLevel < 50) {
        let stressInfluence = 1.0 - (stressLevel / 50.0)
        targetMode = lerp(targetMode, OVER_SHOULDER, stressInfluence)
    }

    // Calculate transition speed
    let baseSpeed = mapRange(roomSize, SMALL, LARGE, FAST_TRANSITION, SLOW_TRANSITION)
    let stressMultiplier = lerp(1.0, 2.5, 1.0 - (stressLevel / 100.0))
    let speedMultiplier = lerp(1.0, 1.5, playerSpeed / MAX_SPEED)

    currentBlend = moveToward(currentBlend, targetMode, baseSpeed * stressMultiplier * speedMultiplier * deltaTime)
}
```

## Stress-Driven Handheld Shake

The Lock In meter directly controls a **handheld shake effect** layered on top of whatever camera mode is active. At full composure, the camera is steady — clinical, almost detached. As stress increases, the camera begins to exhibit subtle micro-movements that mimic a handheld documentary camera. At extreme stress, it becomes genuinely difficult to keep sight of your surroundings.

The shake isn't random noise. I'm using **layered Perlin noise** at different frequencies to create organic movement that feels human. Low-frequency sway simulates the character's body movement. Higher-frequency tremors simulate trembling hands. The combination creates a shake that feels distressingly real.

I also vary the shake profile based on context. Standing still produces a slow, breathing-like oscillation. Moving adds directional instability. Being chased introduces sharp, jerky corrections as if the character keeps looking over their shoulder.

### The Subliminal Layer

Here's a detail most players will never consciously notice: at very low stress levels (high composure), the camera has an almost imperceptible **drift** — a slow, gentle movement that makes the frame feel subtly alive. It's too small to register consciously, but without it, the camera feels dead. This tiny motion is what sells the feeling of watching through someone's eyes rather than through a game camera.

## Wall Occlusion Handling

Libraries are architecturally complex. Tall shelves, columns, doorframes — there are a thousand things that can get between the camera and the player. The wall occlusion system handles this through a multi-layered approach:

1. **Ray-based detection**: Multiple rays cast from the camera to the player detect obstructions
2. **Material fading**: Obstructing geometry fades to a translucent or X-ray material rather than disappearing entirely, maintaining spatial awareness
3. **Camera repositioning**: If fading isn't sufficient, the camera smoothly repositions to find clear line-of-sight
4. **Emergency snap**: As an absolute last resort, the camera cuts to a fallback position — but this is designed to feel intentional, like a horror film cut

The fading approach is my favorite because it *adds* to the horror. Seeing the world through semi-transparent shelves, watching shapes move on the other side of walls you can partially see through — it's unsettling in a way I didn't anticipate during design.

## The Camera as Horror Tool

What I've learned building this system is that **the camera is the most powerful horror tool available to a game designer**. More than lighting, more than sound, more than enemy design. The camera controls what the player sees, when they see it, and how stable that view is. By making the camera reactive to stress, I've created a system where **the game literally becomes harder to perceive as terror increases**. Your senses are failing you when you need them most.

That's not a punishment — it's an immersive simulation of fear. And when a player reaches a safe room and the camera smoothly pulls back to its composed, steady state, the relief is palpable. Not because anything changed in the game world, but because they can *see* clearly again.

The camera feeds on your fear. And in Satan's Library, that makes all the difference.
