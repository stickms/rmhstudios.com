## More Than Random Choices

When I set out to build the AI for Cursed Logic, I had one guiding principle: **the Protocol should feel like it's thinking.** Not just reacting. Not just rolling dice. *Thinking.* Players needed to feel like they were sitting across from something intelligent — something that could read them, adapt to them, and occasionally surprise them with a move that felt eerily prescient.

Building an AI that achieves this in a four-action tactical game is a fascinating design challenge. Too smart and it feels unfair, like the system is cheating. Too dumb and the game loses all tension. The sweet spot — the place where the Protocol lives — is an AI that plays *plausibly*. It makes smart moves, but also makes mistakes. It has tendencies you can exploit, but those tendencies shift.

Here's how we built it.

## The Three-Mode System

The Protocol operates in one of three behavioral modes at any given time, and understanding these modes is key to understanding why the AI feels alive:

### Pressuring Mode

When the Protocol is in pressuring mode, it favors aggressive actions. It Strikes more often, it takes calculated risks, and it tries to maintain initiative. This mode activates when the Protocol has a health advantage or when it detects that the player has been playing passively (lots of Blocks and Prepares).

The transition logic looks conceptually like this:

```typescript
if (protocol.health > player.health + threshold) {
  mode = 'pressuring';
} else if (recentPlayerActions.filter(a => a === 'block' || a === 'prepare').length > passiveThreshold) {
  mode = 'pressuring';
}
```

The Protocol punishes passivity. If you turtle up, it presses its advantage. This creates a natural tension where defensive play has diminishing returns — exactly the kind of strategic pressure that makes duels feel dynamic.

### Defensive Mode

When the Protocol's health drops below a critical threshold or when it detects the player is being highly aggressive, it shifts to defensive mode. It Blocks more frequently, it takes fewer risks, and it plays conservatively to stabilize.

This mode is intentionally frustrating in small doses. You *know* the Protocol is scared. You can feel it playing safe. But the moment you overcommit — the moment you Strike expecting another Block — it punishes you. Defensive mode isn't about passive play; it's about baiting overconfidence.

### Recovering Mode

This is the most interesting mode. When the Protocol has taken significant damage and needs to rebuild its position, it enters recovering mode. In this mode, it mixes Prepares with defensive actions, trying to build up stance energy and resources while avoiding further damage.

Recovering mode is where the Protocol feels most "human." It's playing from behind, making the best of a bad situation, and looking for an opening. Beating a Protocol in recovering mode feels earned. Losing to one feels like you let it slip through your fingers.

## Variant Behaviors

Beyond the three modes, the Protocol has **variant behaviors** — personality archetypes that fundamentally change how it plays across an entire duel. These aren't just difficulty settings. They're completely different opponents.

**The Watcher** variant is patient and observational. It lets you establish patterns and then ruthlessly exploits them. Playing against The Watcher feels like being studied under a microscope.

**The Hammer** variant is relentlessly aggressive. It pressures constantly and rarely shifts to defensive mode. It doesn't care about its own health — it just wants to break through your guard. Playing against The Hammer is exhausting and exhilarating.

**The Mirror** variant is the most psychologically unsettling. It attempts to match your playstyle, creating an eerie feeling that you're fighting yourself. If you play aggressively, it plays aggressively. If you turtle, it turtles. The Mirror forces you to question every habit.

**The Glitch** variant incorporates controlled randomness. Its mode transitions are unpredictable, and it occasionally makes moves that don't align with any rational strategy. Playing against The Glitch teaches you that pattern recognition has limits — sometimes you just have to react.

Each variant has its own mode transition thresholds, action weight distributions, and behavioral quirks. This means that even experienced players who have "solved" one variant will be caught off guard by another.

## Predictive State Modeling

This is the feature I'm most proud of, and it's what makes the Probe mechanic work. The Protocol doesn't just decide its action for the current round — it **builds future round states** that represent possible outcomes.

When a player uses Probe, they're not just seeing what the Protocol will do *right now*. They're glimpsing a projected state — a model of what the Protocol thinks the next few rounds will look like based on current trajectory. This is computed by running the Protocol's decision logic forward with assumptions about likely player behavior:

```typescript
function buildProjectedState(currentState: GameState, depth: number): ProjectedState {
  const projectedActions = [];
  let simState = clone(currentState);

  for (let i = 0; i < depth; i++) {
    const likelyPlayerAction = predictPlayerAction(simState);
    const protocolAction = selectProtocolAction(simState);
    simState = resolveRound(simState, likelyPlayerAction, protocolAction);
    projectedActions.push({ round: i, action: protocolAction, mode: simState.protocolMode });
  }

  return { projectedActions, confidence: calculateConfidence(simState) };
}
```

The beauty of this system is that it gives Probe real strategic value. You're not just seeing the AI's next move — you're seeing its *plan*. And plans can be disrupted. If the projected state shows the Protocol shifting to pressuring mode in two rounds, you can preemptively adjust your strategy to prevent the conditions that would trigger that shift.

### The Confidence Layer

Each projected state includes a confidence value. This represents how certain the Protocol is about its predictions. High confidence means the Protocol has a strong read on the player. Low confidence means the Protocol is uncertain — and uncertainty makes it more cautious and harder to predict.

This creates an interesting feedback loop: using Probe gives you information, but the Protocol also uses your Probe frequency as a data point. Probing too often signals uncertainty on your part, which the Protocol can exploit.

## Implementation Lessons

Building this AI taught me several important lessons:

**Weighted randomness is your best friend.** The Protocol never uses pure random selection. Every action has a weight based on mode, variant, player history, and current game state. This means the AI is probabilistic — it has tendencies rather than deterministic patterns — but those tendencies are shaped by intelligent analysis.

**History matters more than the current round.** The Protocol tracks the last several player actions and uses that history to detect patterns. A player who has Struck three times in a row is likely to Strike again — or likely to *change* because they realize they've been predictable. The Protocol considers both possibilities and weights its response accordingly.

**Mode transitions need hysteresis.** Early versions of the Protocol switched modes too rapidly, creating an erratic feeling that players found confusing rather than challenging. Adding hysteresis — requiring a mode to be active for a minimum number of rounds before switching — created smoother, more readable AI behavior that players could actually learn to anticipate.

## The Result

The Protocol, in its current form, is about 200 lines of decision logic spread across mode handlers, variant behaviors, and projection systems. It's not a neural network. It's not machine learning. It's carefully crafted heuristic AI — and I think that's exactly right for this kind of game.

Players don't need an opponent that's truly unpredictable. They need an opponent that *feels* unpredictable while actually being learnable. The Protocol walks that line, and watching players develop intuitions about its behavior — learning to read its modes, exploiting its variant weaknesses, using Probe to confirm suspicions — is one of the most satisfying things I've experienced as a game designer.

*The Protocol is watching. Are you ready to duel?*
