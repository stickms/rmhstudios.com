## "Surprise Mechanics"

In June 2019, Kerry Hopkins, VP of Legal and Government Affairs at Electronic Arts, appeared before the UK Parliament's Digital, Culture, Media and Sport Committee. When asked about loot boxes in EA's FIFA games, Hopkins described them as "surprise mechanics" — comparable, she said, to Kinder Eggs.

The room's reaction was palpable. The phrase became an instant meme, but more importantly, it crystallized something players had felt for years: **the gaming industry's relationship with monetization had become fundamentally dishonest**.

EA was spending millions on psychologists, data scientists, and behavioral economists to design spending loops optimized for maximum extraction, then describing the result to regulators as a fun surprise. The gap between what companies *did* and what they *said* had become a chasm.

That chasm is what we call the **trust deficit**, and it's the single biggest threat to the long-term health of the gaming industry.

## The Anatomy of Extraction

To understand how we got here, it helps to trace the evolution:

**2006-2012: The horse armor era.** Bethesda's infamous $2.50 horse armor DLC for Oblivion (2006) was widely mocked, but it proved that players would pay for cosmetic additions. Early microtransactions were simple, transparent, and optional.

**2012-2017: The loot box era.** Games like FIFA Ultimate Team, Overwatch, and Star Wars Battlefront II introduced randomized reward mechanisms. Players didn't buy what they wanted — they bought a *chance* at what they wanted. The mechanics were psychologically identical to slot machines, but because games weren't classified as gambling, they were unregulated.

**2017-present: The whale economy.** Mobile games and live-service titles increasingly optimized for "whales" — the small percentage of players who spend disproportionate amounts. **Diablo Immortal**, released by Blizzard in 2022, became a case study in this approach. Players calculated that fully upgrading a character could cost upwards of **$100,000** in microtransactions. The game generated over $300 million in its first year, but at a devastating cost to Blizzard's reputation among core gamers.

The pattern is clear: each era pushed monetization further from transparency and closer to exploitation.

## The Models That Actually Work

Not every studio has followed this trajectory. Some of the most successful games in history have proven that you can make money without manipulating your players.

**Fortnite** generates billions in revenue through a purely cosmetic Battle Pass and item shop. Nothing you buy gives you a gameplay advantage. The system is transparent: you see exactly what you're buying, you know exactly what you're getting, and the game is entirely free to play. Epic Games proved that if your game is good enough and your cosmetics are desirable enough, players will spend voluntarily and happily.

**Path of Exile**, developed by Grinding Gear Games, has been free-to-play since 2013 with a monetization model built almost entirely on cosmetic microtransactions and stash tabs. The community routinely cites GGG as one of the most player-friendly studios in the action RPG space. When Path of Exile 2 entered early access in late 2024, it carried that trust with it.

**Deep Rock Galactic** by Ghost Ship Games took another approach: a single purchase price with optional cosmetic DLC. No battle passes, no daily login rewards, no FOMO mechanics. The game has sold millions of copies and maintains one of the highest user review scores on Steam.

The common thread: **honesty**. These studios tell you what things cost, what you get, and they never sell power.

## Why Browser Games Face Unique Pressure

Browser games operate in a market segment where free-to-play is the default expectation. Nobody expects to pay $60 upfront for a game they access via URL. This creates intense pressure to monetize through microtransactions, ads, or both.

The mobile gaming industry — which shares many characteristics with browser gaming — has largely surrendered to the extractive model. Forced ad views, energy timers, pay-to-skip mechanics, and gacha systems dominate the top-grossing charts. The result is a market where revenue is high but player trust is in the basement.

We refuse to follow that path.

## The RMH Pledge

We want to be explicit about our monetization philosophy, because we believe players deserve to know where a studio stands *before* they invest their time:

- **We will never sell gameplay advantages.** No pay-to-win. No stat boosts. No premium characters with better abilities. If a game has competitive elements — leaderboards in RMH Type, scores in Synapse Storm, rankings in RMHbox minigames — every player competes on equal footing.

- **We will never use randomized paid mechanics.** No loot boxes, no gacha pulls, no "surprise mechanics." If we sell something, you will see exactly what it is and exactly what it costs before you spend a cent.

- **We will never gate content behind artificial timers.** No energy systems. No "wait 4 hours or pay $1." If a game is available, it's fully available.

- **We will never use dark patterns to pressure spending.** No countdown timers on "limited offers" that aren't actually limited. No notification spam. No social pressure mechanics designed to make non-spenders feel excluded.

- **We will be transparent about how we make money.** If we introduce monetization in a game, we'll tell you about it in advance, explain what it funds, and give you the information you need to make an informed decision.

## How We Actually Sustain the Studio

This raises a fair question: if we're not extracting money through aggressive microtransactions, how do we keep the lights on?

The honest answer is that we're an indie studio, and we optimize for sustainability over growth-at-all-costs. Our costs are low because browser games don't require the infrastructure of AAA development. We don't have 500-person teams. We don't spend $200 million on a single title. We use open-source tools, we leverage the web platform's built-in capabilities, and we build games that are technically lean.

When we do monetize, it's through optional cosmetics, voluntary support options, and — where appropriate — modest subscription tiers that offer convenience features without competitive advantages. We'd rather have a smaller, sustainable revenue stream built on player trust than a larger one built on exploitation.

## Trust Is a Long Game

The gaming industry's trust deficit didn't emerge overnight, and it won't be repaired overnight. Every "surprise mechanics" moment, every $100K whale ceiling, every predatory mobile game targeting children chips away at the goodwill that the medium depends on.

We believe the repair starts with small studios making clear commitments and actually honoring them. It starts with being honest about what your games cost, how they make money, and what you will and won't do.

We're one indie studio making browser games. We can't fix the entire industry's relationship with monetization. But we can control our own behavior, and we can be transparent about it.

That's the pledge. We intend to keep it.
