## The Spark That Started It All

Every game starts with a moment — that fleeting intersection of inspiration and obsession where an idea lodges itself in your brain and refuses to leave. For Signal Forge, that moment happened at 2 AM on a Tuesday while I was staring at an oscilloscope visualization in a music production plugin.

I watched the waveforms dance — **sine waves** rolling smooth and predictable, **saw waves** cutting sharp and aggressive, **pulse waves** snapping with rhythmic precision, and **noise** crackling with beautiful chaos. And a thought hit me: *what if these were cards in a deck?*

I'd been playing a lot of roguelike deckbuilders at the time. Slay the Spire had consumed hundreds of hours of my life. Monster Train, Inscryption, Nowhere Prophet — I loved the genre deeply. But I kept feeling like there was unexplored thematic territory. Most deckbuilders lean into fantasy, sci-fi, or horror. What if the theme itself could *be* the mechanic?

## From Whiteboard to Prototype

The next morning I filled an entire whiteboard with ideas. Signal processing isn't just a visual aesthetic — it's a system with real rules. Signals have amplitude, frequency, phase. They can be filtered, amplified, distorted, combined. These aren't just metaphors for game mechanics; they *are* mechanics waiting to be abstracted into card game form.

I started with the most basic question: **what are the factions?** In any deckbuilder, you need distinct playstyle identities. The four fundamental waveform types gave me exactly that:

- **Pulse** — Binary, on-or-off energy. High burst damage, rigid timing. The aggressive archetype.
- **Sine** — Smooth, flowing, cyclical. Healing, sustain, rhythm-based effects. The control archetype.
- **Saw** — Ascending ramps that reset hard. Scaling damage, buildup mechanics. The combo archetype.
- **Noise** — Random, chaotic, unpredictable. Wildcard effects, high variance. The chaos archetype.

Each waveform naturally suggested a gameplay identity without me having to force it. Pulse *feels* like burst damage. Sine *feels* like sustain. That alignment between theme and mechanic is something I'd chase throughout the entire development process.

## The First Playable Cards

My first prototype was embarrassingly simple — index cards with marker drawings and a Google Sheet for stat tracking. But even at that stage, certain things clicked immediately. A Pulse card that dealt flat damage felt right. A Sine card that healed a small amount each turn felt right. A Noise card that rolled a random effect felt *dangerously* right.

The early design had maybe eight cards total. I'd shuffle them, deal a hand, and play against an imaginary enemy with fixed stats. It was crude, but it told me something important: **the theme carried weight.** When I played a "Sawtooth Ramp" card that gained +1 damage each time it was played, the name and the mechanic aligned perfectly. That's the magic you're looking for in game design — when the player doesn't need a tutorial because the theme *teaches* the mechanic.

## Why Not Just Another Fantasy Deckbuilder?

I asked myself this honestly. Wouldn't it be easier to make a deckbuilder with swords and fireballs? Absolutely. The problem is that easier doesn't mean better, and in the indie space, *differentiation is survival*.

The signal processing theme gives Signal Forge several advantages:

1. **Visual identity** — Neon waveforms, oscilloscope displays, glitch aesthetics. It looks like nothing else in the genre.
2. **Mechanical coherence** — The theme isn't wallpaper. Waveform types inform card behavior, status effects reference real signal phenomena (clipping, distortion, filtering), and even the resource system ties back to signal theory.
3. **Audience crossover** — Musicians, audio engineers, synth enthusiasts, and electronic music fans are a passionate community that rarely gets catered to in games. Signal Forge speaks their language.

### The Dark Aesthetic

From day one, I knew the visual direction: **dark, neon, glitch-wave**. Think a CRT monitor in a dimly lit studio, waveforms pulsing in electric blue and hot pink, with occasional visual corruption when things go wrong. The aesthetic isn't just pretty — it communicates game state. When your deck is corrupted by Glitch cards, the screen literally distorts. When you're low on health, the signal "degrades." The visuals *are* the UI.

## The Tech Stack Decision

I chose **Next.js with TypeScript** for Signal Forge, which might surprise people expecting Unity or Godot. But here's my reasoning: I wanted Signal Forge to be instantly accessible — no download, no install, just open a browser and play. The web is the most democratic platform in existence, and for an indie studio trying to build an audience, removing friction between "I heard about this game" and "I'm playing this game" is everything.

**Zustand** handles state management, which turned out to be a perfect fit for a card game. Each game state is essentially a snapshot — your deck, your hand, the enemy state, the board — and Zustand's simple, immutable-friendly approach makes time-travel debugging and state rollback almost trivial.

```typescript
// Early concept of the game state shape
interface GameState {

  hand: Card[];
  discardPile: Card[];
  player: { hp: number; maxHp: number; shield: number };
  enemy: Enemy;
  resources: { static: number; tempo: number };
  turn: number;
}
```

## Lessons from the Concept Phase

Looking back, the concept phase taught me a few things I want to share with other indie devs:

**Chase thematic coherence.** If your theme and mechanics feel like they belong together, everything else — balance, UI, marketing — becomes easier. Players remember games where the theme *means* something.

**Prototype ugly, prototype fast.** My index-card prototype was hideous, but it answered critical design questions in hours instead of weeks. Don't build systems until you've proven the core loop works on paper.

**Pick your battles with technology.** Using Next.js meant I gave up some game-engine features, but I gained instant web deployment, SEO for discoverability, and a tech stack I already knew deeply. For a solo indie dev, speed of iteration trumps raw engine power.

Signal Forge started as a 2 AM thought experiment. Today it's a fully playable roguelike deckbuilder with over 60 cards, intelligent enemies, and a keyword system that rivals games with ten times the budget. The journey from concept to reality has been one of the most rewarding creative experiences of my life — and we're just getting started.
