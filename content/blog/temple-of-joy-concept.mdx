## The Spark

I've been thinking about idle games wrong for years. We all have.

The genre has a reputation problem. Say "idle game" or "clicker" to most developers and they'll think of Cookie Clicker — a satirical masterpiece that accidentally became the template for thousands of soulless clones. Click the thing. Buy the upgrade. Watch the number go up. Repeat until heat death. And look, I love Cookie Clicker. Orteil is a genius. But somewhere between the grandma apocalypse and the antimatter condenser, the broader genre forgot to ask the most important design question: **what does the number represent?**

In most idle games, the answer is nothing. You're making cookies, or paperclips, or antimatter, or some abstract "resource" that exists purely to be converted into bigger numbers. The mechanics are the entire point. And that's fine — there's genuine satisfaction in watching exponential growth curves unfold. But I kept thinking: what if the number *meant* something? What if the act of watching it grow forced you to confront something real?

That's where Temple of Joy was born. The number is **happiness**. And that changes everything.

## Why Happiness?

Here's the thing about happiness as a game mechanic: it's inherently absurd. The idea that you can quantify joy — that you can earn 3.7 million happiness per second — is ridiculous. And that absurdity is the point. Because we *do* try to quantify happiness in the real world, constantly. We measure GDP as a proxy for national wellbeing. We track steps and sleep scores and meditation minutes. We reduce the most complex human experience to a dashboard of metrics and then wonder why we still feel empty.

Temple of Joy leans into that absurdity with both hands. Your first source of happiness is a **Mood Candle** — *"Smells like 'clean linen.' Definitely vanilla."* — generating 0.1 happiness per second. From there, you buy Nap Pods, Snack Bars, Hot Tubs, and Massage Studios. Simple physical pleasures. The kind of things that actually do make you happy, presented with the full ridiculousness of their commodification. You're not experiencing a nap. You're *purchasing a Nap Pod that generates passive happiness income*. The game knows it's absurd. That's the joke. That's also the thesis.

Because as you progress, the sources of happiness evolve. You move from physical comfort to social validation (Joy Cult: *"'Cult' is a strong word. We prefer 'voluntary bliss congregation.'"*), then to intellectual stimulation (Therapy: *"$250/hr. Worth every penny."*), artistic transcendence (Art Gallery: *"You stood in front of a painting for twelve minutes."*), and eventually to cosmic abstraction (Bliss Singularity: *"Happiness so concentrated it folds space-time."*). The upgrade paths mirror this arc: the Path of the Flesh for sensory pleasures, the Path of the Mind for intellectual satisfaction, the Path of the Spirit for meditative peace, the Path of Indulgence for luxury. You're not just buying things. You're building a *philosophy of the good life*, one upgrade at a time.

## The Philosophers in the Room

This is where I got obsessed. I'd been reading a lot of philosophy of happiness — Epicurus, Bentham, Mill, Aristotle, Nietzsche — and I realized that the arguments these thinkers have been having for two thousand years map *perfectly* onto idle game mechanics.

**Epicurus** said that happiness is the absence of pain and the enjoyment of simple pleasures. In Temple of Joy, the "frugal" choice in philosophical events — choosing contentment over excess — earns his approval. There's an achievement for it: Epicurus Approved. The irony of earning an achievement for *restraint* in a game about maximizing numbers is deliberate. Epicurus would hate this game. He'd also be right.

**Bentham** is the utilitarian — the guy who literally tried to build a "felicific calculus" to measure happiness mathematically. He is, in the most literal sense possible, the patron saint of idle games. He appears in an event offering you the choice to maximize everything or rest. Of course most players choose to maximize. Bentham nods approvingly. Epicurus rolls his eyes.

**Nietzsche's** eternal return — would you live this exact life again, infinitely? — maps directly to the transcendence/prestige mechanic. When you transcend, you lose almost everything and start over with accumulated wisdom. Would you do it again? The game lets you answer that question dozens of times. Most players answer yes before reading the confirmation dialog.

I wanted these philosophical tensions to be more than flavor text. They needed to create genuine mechanical dilemmas. When Nietzsche asks if you'd change anything about your run, and "I would change nothing" gives you +20% permanent HPS and +20 karma while "I would change everything" gives you a flashy ×5 buff for 10 minutes, you're making a real strategic choice that happens to also be a philosophical one. The frugal path is almost always the better long-term play. But the excess path *feels* better in the moment. That's the whole point.

## Idle Games as Meditation

Here's my controversial take: idle games are the closest thing gaming has to meditation.

I don't mean the mindfulness-app version of meditation where a soothing voice tells you to notice your breath. I mean the real thing — the boring, frustrating, revelatory practice of sitting with yourself and watching your mind do its thing. In meditation, you notice that your brain constantly wants to *do* something. Check your phone. Plan dinner. Replay an argument from 2019. The practice is noticing the impulse and choosing not to act on it.

Idle games create the same dynamic. The core tension of the genre is the gap between *action* (clicking, buying) and *waiting* (letting the numbers accumulate). Temple of Joy makes this tension explicit with the **Pilgrimage** mechanic: you start a 120-second timer, and if you don't click *at all* during that time, you receive a massive burst of income. Click once and it resets. It's a meditation on non-action inside a game about action. You're literally rewarded for not playing the game.

The Hedonic Treadmill mechanic reinforces this. In real psychology, the hedonic treadmill is the phenomenon where your baseline happiness adapts to positive changes — a raise feels great for a week, then normal. In Temple of Joy, your baseline happiness slowly drifts upward toward your current level. It doesn't penalize you mechanically (I tried that, it was miserable), but it's tracked and displayed. A gentle reminder that the number always wants to feel normal, no matter how high it gets.

## The Aesthetic of Contentment

Most idle games look like dashboards. Grids of buttons, progress bars, numbers in columns. Functional. Efficient. Soulless.

I wanted Temple of Joy to feel like a *place*. The visual design draws from sacred architecture and manuscript illumination — warm parchment tones, deep walnut browns, serif typography (Cormorant Garamond), ceremonial iconography. Candles, lotus flowers, incense smoke, laurel wreaths. The Smile Button at the center of the screen pulses gently with a golden glow that intensifies during rituals. The overall feeling should be: you've stepped into a temple dedicated to the worship of feeling good, and it takes itself *exactly* seriously enough.

The number formatting carries the theme through to the endgame. When your HPS hits extreme scales, instead of sterile scientific notation, you get named tiers: *Euphoria, Bliss, Rapture, Nirvana, Enlightenment, Transcendence, Awakening, Rapture Cascade,* and — because I couldn't resist — *Nice* at 1e69. At the absolute peak: *Joy Complete*. Because at some point, you have to ask whether it's enough.

## What Comes Next

This post is the origin story. Temple of Joy is playable now, and it's already deeper than I expected. Fifty-six sources, eight upgrade paths, forty relics, twenty synergies, a hundred-plus achievements, and a prestige system (the Wheel of Samsara) that I need an entire devlog to explain properly.

But the thing I'm most proud of isn't any single feature. It's the way the systems talk to each other. The way Epicurus's approval and Bentham's calculus create real strategic tension. The way the Pilgrimage mechanic turns non-action into a verb. The way the number formatting at 1e69 makes you laugh right when you need it.

The question the game keeps asking, the question every philosopher has wrestled with, the question that makes happiness such a loaded thing to quantify: **is this enough?**

The game doesn't answer. It just lets you keep clicking.
