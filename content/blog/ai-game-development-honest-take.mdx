## The Hype Curve Is Steep

According to **Gartner's 2024 Hype Cycle for Emerging Technologies**, generative AI had crested the "Peak of Inflated Expectations" and was beginning its slide into the "Trough of Disillusionment." If you've been following AI discourse in the game industry, that trajectory feels about right.

In 2023, every conference keynote promised that AI would revolutionize game development. Procedural worlds would generate themselves. NPCs would hold meaningful conversations. Asset pipelines would shrink from months to hours. By mid-2024, the conversation had grown considerably more nuanced — not because AI had failed, but because the gap between demos and production-ready tools had become impossible to ignore.

We use AI tools at RMH Studios. We're also deeply skeptical of AI hype. Those two positions are not contradictory. In fact, we think holding both is the only honest posture for a game studio in 2025.

## Where AI Actually Helps Us

### Code Assistance

**GitHub Copilot** has become a genuine productivity tool for our development workflow. We use it for boilerplate generation, test scaffolding, API integration patterns, and the kind of repetitive code that every developer writes but nobody enjoys writing. It's particularly effective for our stack — Next.js, React, TypeScript, WebSocket handlers — because these are well-represented in its training data.

The Game Developers Conference (GDC) 2024 survey found that approximately 31% of game developers reported using AI tools in their workflow, with code assistance being the most common application. That tracks with our experience. Copilot doesn't write our game logic — the creative, architectural decisions that define what a game feels like. But it accelerates the implementation of those decisions.

### Prototyping and Ideation

We use large language models as brainstorming partners. When designing minigames for RMHbox, we'll describe a mechanic and ask for variations, edge cases, or potential balance issues. The output isn't directly usable — it needs heavy filtering by someone who actually understands game design — but it's useful as a creativity prompt. Think of it as rubber-duck debugging for design.

### Documentation and Content

Writing API documentation, help text, patch notes, and internal wikis is work that benefits from AI drafting. We always review and revise, but starting from a draft rather than a blank page saves meaningful time.

## Where We Draw the Line

### We Don't Use AI to Replace Artists

This is the line, and we want to be unambiguous about it.

The past two years have seen a wave of AI image generators — Midjourney, Stable Diffusion, DALL-E — marketed as replacements for concept artists, illustrators, and visual designers. Some studios have quietly replaced art teams with AI pipelines. Others have been less quiet about it, and the backlash has been severe.

The concerns are not abstract. The **SAG-AFTRA strike** of 2023, which lasted 118 days and was the longest actors' strike in SAG-AFTRA's history, centered in part on AI protections — specifically, the right of performers to control how their likeness and voice are used by AI systems. While the strike focused on film and television, the precedent reverberates through every creative industry, including games.

In the visual arts community, concept artists have reported layoffs explicitly tied to studios adopting AI image generation. A widely shared 2024 survey by the Concept Art Association found that a significant majority of respondents had experienced reduced work opportunities that they attributed at least partially to AI tools.

The ethical problems are compounded by the legal ones. Most AI image generators were trained on datasets scraped from the internet without artist consent — a practice that is the subject of multiple ongoing lawsuits, including cases brought by artists against Stability AI and Midjourney.

Our position: **the people who create art deserve to be compensated for their work, credited for their contributions, and in control of how their creations are used.** We use AI for code assistance and text drafting, where the tools are trained on data with clearer licensing (Copilot is trained on public repositories, many with open-source licenses). We do not use AI image generators for game assets.

### We Don't Ship AI-Generated Game Content to Players

Some studios have experimented with AI-generated dialogue, quests, and narrative content. The results, so far, have been mediocre at best. AI-generated text is fluent but shallow — it lacks the intentionality, the surprise, the *craft* that makes great game writing memorable.

When we write dialogue for Echoes of the Spire or flavor text for Signal Forge, a human being writes it. They make choices about tone, rhythm, subtext, and emotional pacing that reflect a creative vision. We're not willing to outsource that to a statistical model, no matter how convincing the output appears.

## The Nuance the Industry Needs

The AI conversation in gaming has become polarized. On one side: breathless techno-optimism, treating every AI capability as an inevitable and unqualified good. On the other: total rejection, treating any use of AI tools as a betrayal of creative values.

We think both positions are wrong.

AI code assistants are **tools**, like IDEs, debuggers, and linters before them. They make developers faster at tasks that were already within their capabilities. That's unambiguously useful.

AI art generators raise **ethical concerns** about consent, compensation, and the displacement of creative workers. Those concerns deserve serious engagement, not dismissal.

AI content generation is **immature** for production game content. It might improve. When it does, the conversation about how to use it responsibly will still matter.

## What We'd Like to See

- **Industry standards for AI disclosure.** Players deserve to know when a game's content — art, writing, music, voice acting — was generated by AI. Not because AI content is inherently bad, but because informed consumers make better choices.

- **Licensing clarity for training data.** The legal ambiguity around AI training datasets needs resolution. Studios should be able to clearly state that their AI tools were trained on properly licensed data.

- **Protection for creative workers.** Game development employs hundreds of thousands of artists, writers, composers, and voice actors. The industry has a responsibility to ensure that productivity gains from AI don't come at the cost of these people's livelihoods.

- **Honest marketing.** When a studio demonstrates an "AI-powered feature," they should be clear about what the AI actually does, what its limitations are, and what human oversight is involved. The demo-to-reality gap in AI marketing is corrosive to trust.

## Our Commitment

We'll keep using AI tools where they make us more productive without compromising our values. We'll keep not using them where the ethical ground is uncertain. And we'll keep being transparent about which is which.

AI is a powerful set of technologies. It's not magic, it's not going to replace game developers, and it's not going to make great games by itself. The studios that navigate this moment well will be the ones that use AI honestly — as a tool in service of human creativity, not a replacement for it.

That's where we stand. We'll update you if our thinking evolves, because honesty means being willing to change your mind when the evidence warrants it.
