## You Can't Outrun the Database

In most games, a reset is a clean slate. Delete your save file, start fresh, pretend the bad ending never happened. It's an unspoken contract between player and game: you get to try again with no baggage.

House Always Wins breaks that contract.

The game runs on **Prisma with a Neon Postgres database**. Your game state doesn't live on your machine — it lives on the server. When you make a choice, when you accumulate Debt, when you betray an NPC's trust, that information is written to a persistent record tied to your authenticated account. And it stays there.

Even if you "reset."

## The Philosophy of Narrative Persistence

Let me be clear about the intent here: this isn't a punishment system. I'm not trying to trap players in consequences they can't escape. The game is always completable, always playable, always fair. What persistence does is make **choices feel real**.

Most narrative games have a dirty secret: experienced players know that choices don't really matter because they can always reload. The "hard choice" between saving two characters loses its weight the moment you realize you can save-scum. The tension evaporates. The emotional investment collapses.

In House Always Wins, that safety net is gone. Not because I want to be cruel, but because *the casino wouldn't let you have one*. The House deals fair games — technically — but the House always remembers what you did at the table last time. And the time before that. And the time before that.

### What Gets Tracked

The persistence system records several categories of player behavior:

- **Debt history** — Not just your current Debt, but the high-water mark. The casino knows the deepest you've ever been.
- **NPC interactions** — Every significant conversation, every betrayal, every act of kindness. NPCs have memory, and it spans sessions.
- **Gambling outcomes** — Your win/loss patterns, which games you gravitate toward, how much you risk.
- **Exploration patterns** — Which areas you visit first, which you avoid, how thoroughly you search.
- **Reset count** — How many times you've tried to start over. The game notices.

## The Fourth Wall Moment

There's a moment in House Always Wins that I've been designing toward since the concept phase. It happens when a player resets for the first time.

You log in. You start the game. The coin flips. And instead of "You've been here before," the text reads: **"You came back."**

The casino is the same, but not exactly. An NPC might reference something you did in your previous run — obliquely, like they're trying to remember a dream. A door you opened before is slightly ajar. A slot machine displays your old Debt total before flickering to zero.

The point isn't to punish the reset. The point is to make the player *feel seen*. The system noticed. The House noticed. And now the question isn't "Can I get a clean start?" It's "How clean can a start really be?"

### Escalating Awareness

Each subsequent reset increases the game's awareness of you. The first reset is subtle — a few altered lines, a shifted detail. By the third reset, NPCs openly acknowledge that something is wrong, that time keeps folding, that they've had this conversation before. By the fifth reset, the casino itself starts breaking. Textures glitch. Music skips. The architecture shifts in ways that shouldn't be possible.

This isn't arbitrary — it's the game's way of saying that the system is straining under the weight of accumulated memory. Every reset adds to the database. Every reset is another layer of history the casino has to hold. And it's starting to show.

## The Technical Challenge

Building narrative persistence at this level is genuinely difficult. It's not just about storing data — it's about making that data *meaningful* in every system.

Consider a single NPC: the Dealer. On a first playthrough, the Dealer is calm and professional. She explains the rules, deals fair hands, wishes you luck. If you accumulate high Debt, she becomes more sympathetic — she slips you hints about reducing Debt, breaks character to warn you about the high-stakes tables.

Now consider: a player who reached maximum Debt, was warned by the Dealer, ignored the warnings, and then reset. On the second playthrough, the Dealer starts calm — but there's a crack. A line of dialogue that doesn't quite fit: *"Be careful with the high tables. I... I have a feeling about you."* She can't fully remember the previous timeline, but something bleeds through.

Implementing this requires a **layered dialogue system** where NPC speech is determined not just by current game state but by historical flags:

```typescript
function getDealerDialogue(
  currentDebt: number,
  resetCount: number,
  previousPeakDebt: number,
  previousRelationship: string
): DialogueLine[] {
  const base = getBaseDialogue(currentDebt);

  if (resetCount > 0 && previousPeakDebt > 80) {
    // Bleed-through: vague memory of past timeline
    base.splice(2, 0, {
      text: "Be careful with the high tables. I... I have a feeling about you.",
      tone: 'uncertain',
      flag: 'dealer_bleedthrough_warning'
    });
  }

  return base;
}
```

Every NPC needs this kind of historical awareness. Multiply that across the full cast and you start to understand why this system keeps me up at night.

## Why Server-Side State Matters

This entire design only works because the game state lives on the server. If state were client-side, a player could simply inspect local storage, clear cookies, or use a different browser. The persistence would be opt-in, not inherent.

By tying state to authenticated accounts via **Better Auth** (email/password + Discord OAuth), the identity is the anchor. You *are* your account. The casino knows you by your login, not your device. Switch computers, switch browsers — the House remembers.

There's a tension here with player expectations. Gamers are used to having control over their save data. It's *their* game, *their* experience, *their* right to restart. I respect that instinct, and I've wrestled with it. The conclusion I've reached is that House Always Wins is, fundamentally, a game about **systems that are not under your control**. The casino was designed to exploit you. Part of that design is that you can't walk away clean.

But — and this is important — you can always walk *forward*. Every run is completable. Every Debt level is survivable. The persistence adds texture and consequence, but it never creates a dead end. You might not get the clean start you wanted, but you'll always get a *start*.

## The Undertale Parallel

Undertale did something similar with its Genocide route — even after a True Reset, echoes of your worst choices lingered. Flowey remembered. The final frames were tinted. It was brilliant because it used the *meta-structure of the game* to deliver consequence.

House Always Wins pushes that idea further by moving the memory off the player's machine entirely. Undertale's persistence could be circumvented by deleting system files. House Always Wins can't. The memory lives in the database, and the database doesn't have a delete button the player can reach.

Is that too far? Maybe. But the casino doesn't care if it's too far. The House always wins.

## Looking Ahead

The persistence system is one of the most ambitious features in House Always Wins, and it's the one I'm most nervous about. Getting it right means every playthrough feels connected, meaningful, and slightly haunted by what came before. Getting it wrong means the game feels like it's cheating — punishing players for engaging with it.

The line between "your choices matter" and "the game is holding your choices hostage" is razor-thin. Walking that line is the challenge. But when a playtester resets, sees that altered opening line, and goes quiet for a moment before whispering "oh no" — that's when I know it's working.

The system remembers everything. And so will you.
