## $46.5 Billion and Counting

Between 2020 and 2023, Meta's Reality Labs division reported cumulative operating losses exceeding **$46.5 billion**. That's not a typo. Forty-six point five billion dollars spent building hardware, software, and infrastructure for a vision of the future that, so far, most people have declined to inhabit.

Meta isn't alone in this bet, but they're the most visible — and the most honest about the financials, thanks to public reporting requirements. Mark Zuckerberg renamed his entire company around the metaverse thesis in October 2021, a move so dramatic that it forced every tech executive, game studio, and investor to take a position on whether persistent, interconnected virtual worlds would become the next computing platform.

We took a position too. We think the metaverse already exists. It's called **the open web**. And it doesn't require a headset.

## What Went Wrong with Web3 Virtual Worlds

The metaverse hype cycle of 2021-2022 produced a wave of projects promising persistent virtual worlds, digital real estate, and interoperable avatars. Two of the most prominent — **Decentraland** and **The Sandbox** — became case studies in the gap between speculative investment and actual user engagement.

Decentraland, despite facilitating millions of dollars in virtual land sales, consistently struggled to maintain concurrent user counts above a few hundred. DappRadar reported that in October 2022, Decentraland had roughly 56 daily active users — on a platform where virtual land parcels had sold for over $2 million. The Sandbox faced similar retention challenges, with most activity concentrated around token speculation rather than actual gameplay or social interaction.

The fundamental problem wasn't the technology. It was the **value proposition**. These platforms asked users to buy virtual land, invest in NFT assets, and spend time in sparsely populated 3D environments with limited gameplay — all while existing social platforms (Discord, Twitch, even Roblox) offered richer social experiences for free.

## Apple Vision Pro: Beautiful Hardware, Uncertain Future

In February 2024, Apple entered the spatial computing market with **Vision Pro**, a $3,499 headset that reviewers praised for its display quality and "passthrough" mixed reality capabilities. Apple, characteristically, avoided the word "metaverse" entirely, positioning the device as a "spatial computer" for productivity and media consumption.

The reception was mixed in a way that's instructive. The hardware was genuinely impressive — the display resolution, eye tracking, and hand tracking set new benchmarks. But the use cases remained narrow. Early adopters reported using it primarily for watching movies and occasionally for productivity tasks that a large monitor could handle equally well. The weight, the price, the social isolation of wearing a headset — these aren't problems that better hardware alone can solve.

Apple reportedly cut production estimates significantly within months of launch. The device found a niche among developers and enthusiasts, but the mass-market adoption that would justify a spatial computing ecosystem hasn't materialized.

## What Players Actually Want

Here's what we've learned from building games that real people actually play:

### Instant access

The single most important feature of a browser game is that **you click a link and you're playing**. No download. No installation. No app store approval. No 50GB update. No hardware requirements beyond "a device with a browser." This is not a minor convenience — it is a fundamental shift in the relationship between a player and a game.

When someone shares an RMHbox room link in a Discord server, everyone in that server is 10 seconds away from playing together. That frictionless entry point is worth more than any amount of graphical fidelity or spatial immersion.

### Social presence without isolation

The metaverse pitch often involves putting on a headset and entering a virtual room where your avatar sits across from another avatar. The reality of social gaming in 2025 is that people want to be present *with each other* while also being present *in their physical space*. They're on a voice call with friends, they've got the game in one tab and a wiki in another, they're eating dinner with their laptop open.

**The browser is the metaverse's natural habitat** precisely because it doesn't demand your entire sensory field. It coexists with the rest of your life.

### Low financial commitment

The metaverse projects that demanded upfront investment — buy this land, buy this avatar, buy this token — selected for speculators, not players. The games and platforms that thrive select for *engagement*. Roblox, Fortnite, and browser games succeed because you can walk in with nothing and start having fun immediately.

Every game in the RMH catalog is free to access. We believe that's not just good ethics — it's good product design.

### Games that respect their time

Players don't want to commute through a virtual world to get to the fun part. They don't want loading screens dressed up as "immersive transitions." They want to click, play, enjoy, and leave when they're done. Respecting a player's time is the most underrated design principle in gaming.

## The Open Web Is the Real Metaverse

We're not being contrarian for the sake of it. We genuinely believe that the characteristics people associate with the metaverse — **persistent identity, social interaction, shared experiences, user-generated content, real-time communication** — are already features of the open web.

- **Persistent identity?** You have accounts, profiles, and login systems across the web.
- **Social interaction?** Discord, forums, chat systems, voice calls, video calls — all browser-accessible.
- **Shared experiences?** RMHTube lets you watch videos together. RMHbox lets you play games together. Figma lets you design together. Google Docs lets you write together. All in a browser.
- **User-generated content?** The web is literally built on it.
- **Real-time communication?** WebSockets, WebRTC, and server-sent events power real-time experiences across millions of applications.

The only thing the "metaverse" adds to this picture is a 3D spatial metaphor — and the evidence so far suggests that most people don't want or need that metaphor for most activities.

When you race down a neon highway in **Neon Driftway**, you're in a virtual world. When you and fifteen friends are screaming over a minigame in **RMHbox**, you're sharing a virtual world.

The difference is that our virtual worlds run in a browser tab. They don't require a $3,500 headset. They don't ask you to buy virtual real estate. They don't promise you'll live your entire life inside them.

We think the future of shared virtual experiences is **accessible, browser-native, and integrated into people's existing digital lives** — not a separate reality you strap to your face. The open web already has the infrastructure: URLs for addressing, browsers for rendering, standards for interoperability. What it needs is better content, better experiences, and better games.

That's what we're building. No headset required.
