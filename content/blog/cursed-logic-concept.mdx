## The Spark That Started It All

Every game begins with a question. For Cursed Logic, the question was deceptively simple: **what if you could duel a mind?**

Not a character. Not a boss with a health bar and attack patterns you memorize after three deaths. A *mind* — something that watches you, adapts to you, and forces you to outthink it every single round. That was the seed. And from that seed, Cursed Logic grew into something I never expected.

I've always been fascinated by the chess-like tension of two opponents staring each other down, each trying to predict what the other will do. Fighting games capture this beautifully — the yomi layers, the reads, the conditioning. But fighting games demand mechanical execution. I wanted to strip that away entirely and leave only the *decision*. The pure, agonizing choice.

## Inspirations and Influences

The concept didn't come from nowhere. A few key inspirations shaped what Cursed Logic would become:

**Rock-Paper-Scissors tournaments** — Yes, seriously. Competitive RPS is a real thing, and it's fascinating because it's entirely about reading your opponent. There's no hidden information about the rules, no complex mechanics. Just prediction. I wanted to capture that purity but add layers of strategic depth on top.

**Inscryption and its meta-game tension** — Daniel Mullins showed that a card game could feel like a *confrontation*. The way Inscryption makes you feel like you're sitting across from something dangerous — that atmospheric pressure was hugely influential. I wanted players to feel that same unease when facing the Protocol.

**The Prisoner's Dilemma and game theory** — Classical game theory problems are endlessly interesting to me. The idea that optimal play depends on what you think your opponent thinks you'll do, which depends on what they think you think... that recursive depth is what makes tactical dueling compelling. Cursed Logic is, at its core, a game theory engine dressed in cyber-noir clothing.

### What Is the Protocol?

The Protocol isn't a character in the traditional sense. It doesn't have a backstory or motivation. It's presented as a system — an adversarial intelligence that exists to test you. Think of it as a firewall with teeth. You're probing it, striking at it, trying to break through its defenses while it actively works to dismantle yours.

This framing was intentional. By making your opponent feel *alien* and systematic rather than human, every interaction carries an undercurrent of paranoia. Is it adapting to my patterns? Did it predict that I'd predict its prediction? The Protocol's nature as an unknowable system amplifies the psychological pressure that makes the game compelling.

## The Core Loop

At its heart, Cursed Logic is brutally simple. Each round, you choose one of four actions: **Strike**, **Block**, **Prepare**, or **Probe**. The Protocol does the same (minus Probe — that's your advantage, your tool for peeling back the curtain). Actions resolve simultaneously, health changes, and you do it again.

That simplicity is the point. When you only have four choices, every choice matters enormously. There's nowhere to hide behind complexity. You can't blame a bad draw or a missed input. It was your read, your decision, your loss — or your victory.

But simplicity doesn't mean shallowness. Layered on top of the four-action core are systems that create extraordinary depth:

- **Stances** that modify how your actions behave
- **Round mutations** that change the rules mid-duel
- **Protocol variants** that completely alter the AI's personality
- **A persistent shop** where fragments earned in duels buy upgrades that carry across runs

### Why Turn-Based?

I get asked this a lot. In a world of flashy real-time combat and twitch shooters, why turn-based? Because I believe the most intense moments in gaming are the ones where time *stops* and you have to commit. That moment before you lock in your action in Cursed Logic — when you're weighing everything you know about the Protocol's tendencies, considering the current mutation, factoring in your remaining health — that moment is electric. Real-time combat distributes tension across time. Turn-based combat *compresses* it into a single instant.

## Building the Foundation

Technically, Cursed Logic is built on Next.js with TypeScript and uses Zustand for state management. The game component itself grew to over 750 lines — which sounds like a lot, but every line serves the duel. The state machine that governs round flow, action resolution, mutation application, and Protocol decision-making is intricate but deliberately contained. I didn't want a sprawling codebase. I wanted something *tight*, like the game itself.

The early prototype was just two health bars and four buttons. No animations, no mutations, no shop. Just you versus a random AI making random choices. And even then — even with a *random* opponent — the four-action system had something. Blocking a strike felt satisfying. Getting struck while preparing felt like a deserved punishment. The emotional feedback loop was already there.

## What Makes Tactical Dueling Compelling

I've spent a lot of time thinking about why this format works, and I think it comes down to three things:

**Ownership of outcomes.** In Cursed Logic, you can't blame anyone but yourself. The game gives you all the tools — four actions, information via Probe, upgrades via the shop — and says "figure it out." That ownership creates genuine emotional stakes.

**Pattern recognition as gameplay.** Humans are pattern-matching machines. We can't help it. The Protocol exploits this by having patterns that shift and evolve. You *think* you've figured it out, you lean into your read, and then it switches modes. That constant cycle of confidence and doubt is addictive.

**Brevity with depth.** A single duel takes minutes, not hours. But within those minutes, you experience a complete arc of tension, adaptation, and resolution. It's the game design equivalent of a short story — no wasted words, every beat matters.

## Looking Forward

This is just the beginning. Cursed Logic has grown far beyond that initial question of "what if you could duel a mind?" The Protocol has become something more sophisticated than I originally envisioned, the mutation system adds replayability I didn't know was possible, and the shop creates a metagame that keeps players coming back.

But the core remains unchanged: you, four choices, and an opponent that refuses to be predictable. That's Cursed Logic. And I can't wait to show you more.

*Duel the Protocol.*
