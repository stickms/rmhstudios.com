## The Default Matters

When you open any RMH Studios product — a game, an app, the website itself — the first thing you see is a dark interface. Dark backgrounds, light text, colored accents that glow against the void. This isn't an afterthought or a toggle buried in settings. **Dark mode is our default**, and it's a deliberate design philosophy rooted in science, aesthetics, and respect for our players.

This post explains why we chose dark-first design, what the research says about dark interfaces, and how we built a theming system flexible enough to power it across every product we ship.

## The Dark Mode Movement

Dark mode went mainstream between 2018 and 2020. Apple introduced system-wide dark mode in **iOS 13** (September 2019) and **macOS Mojave** (September 2018). Google followed with **Android 10** (September 2019). By 2020, virtually every major app — Twitter, YouTube, Slack, WhatsApp, Reddit — offered a dark mode option.

This wasn't a fad. The adoption was driven by genuine user preference. A 2020 survey by Android Authority found that **81.9% of respondents** used dark mode on their phones. Whether driven by battery savings on OLED screens, aesthetic preference, or comfort during nighttime use, the demand was overwhelming.

For RMH Studios, the question wasn't whether to support dark mode — it was whether to make it the default. We chose yes, for three reasons: science, aesthetics, and audience.

## The Science: What Research Actually Says

The relationship between dark mode and eye health is more nuanced than marketing copy suggests. We want to be honest about what the research shows.

**What's supported:** Studies on screen use in low-light environments consistently show that high-contrast bright screens in dark rooms cause more subjective discomfort. Research published in the journal *Applied Ergonomics* (Taptagaporn & Saito, 1990) found that dark-on-light displays in dim environments produced more visual fatigue than light-on-dark displays. The American Academy of Ophthalmology notes that while screens don't cause permanent eye damage, reducing brightness in dark environments can decrease discomfort.

**What's debated:** Whether dark mode reduces eye strain in well-lit environments is less clear. A 2013 study by Piepenbrock et al. in *Ergonomics* found that positive polarity (dark text on light background) actually produced better visual acuity for reading tasks. However, this study focused on sustained reading, not the mixed interface of gaming and app usage.

**Our position:** Most gaming happens in **reduced lighting conditions** — evening hours, dimmed rooms, late-night sessions. For this use case, dark interfaces produce less screen glare, less light pollution into the environment, and less subjective discomfort. Since the majority of our users interact with our products in these conditions, dark mode as default makes practical sense.

We're not claiming dark mode is universally superior. We're saying it's the right default for our audience and their usage context.

## The Aesthetic Alignment

Beyond the science, dark mode aligns with **gaming culture's visual identity**. Gaming has always had an affinity for dark interfaces:

- Console dashboards (PlayStation, Xbox) default to dark themes
- Gaming hardware uses dark colorways with accent lighting
- Streaming platforms for gaming (Twitch, Discord) are dark by default
- Competitive gaming overlays and HUDs are almost universally dark

This isn't coincidental. Dark interfaces create a **cinema-like viewing experience** — they recede, letting the content (the game, the stream, the conversation) take center stage. A bright white interface around a game window is like watching a movie with the lights on. The frame competes with the content.

For RMH Studios, where the products ARE the content — games, watch parties, typing races — a dark interface ensures the product is the most visually prominent thing on screen.

Dark interfaces also enable a design technique we use extensively: **luminous accents**. On a dark background, a splash of cyan or magenta or electric blue doesn't just look colored — it looks like it's **emitting light**. This is the same principle that makes neon signs striking at night but unremarkable during the day. Our UI elements glow against the dark, creating an energy and vibrancy that would be impossible on a white background.

## The CSS Variable Architecture

Implementing dark mode in a single app is straightforward. Implementing it across an entire portfolio of games and apps — each with its own visual identity — required a systematic approach. We built a **CSS custom property (variable) system** that powers theming across every RMH Studios product.

### The Token Hierarchy

Our theming system uses a three-level token hierarchy:

**Primitive tokens** define raw color values:

```css
--color-gray-900: #111111;
--color-gray-800: #1a1a1a;
--color-cyan-500: #06b6d4;
--color-magenta-500: #d946ef;
```

**Semantic tokens** assign meaning to primitive tokens:

```css
--surface-primary: var(--color-gray-900);
--surface-secondary: var(--color-gray-800);
--text-primary: var(--color-gray-100);
--accent-primary: var(--color-cyan-500);
```

**Component tokens** apply semantic tokens to specific UI elements:

```css
--button-bg: var(--surface-secondary);
--button-text: var(--text-primary);
--button-border: var(--accent-primary);
```

This hierarchy means switching themes requires changing only the semantic layer. The primitive tokens and component tokens stay the same. A light mode override simply reassigns semantic tokens:

```css
[data-theme="light"] {
  --surface-primary: var(--color-gray-50);
  --surface-secondary: var(--color-gray-100);
  --text-primary: var(--color-gray-900);
}
```

### Per-Product Accent Colors

Each RMH Studios product has its own accent color palette:

- **RMHbox**: Electric blue — energetic, party-like
- **Signal Forge**: Amber — mysterious, strategic
- **Neon Driftway**: Magenta — fast, retro
- **Echoes of the Spire**: Teal — eerie, cerebral
- **RMH Type**: Green — competitive, precise

These accents are applied at the semantic token level, meaning every product uses the same component structure but with different visual emphasis. The dark background is consistent; the accent personality is unique.

### Respecting System Preferences

While dark mode is our default, we respect user choice. Our theming system checks `prefers-color-scheme` on first visit and allows manual override. The preference is persisted in localStorage and synced across products through our shared authentication system.

```css
@media (prefers-color-scheme: light) {
  :root:not([data-theme="dark"]) {
    /* light mode overrides */
  }
}
```

Users who prefer light mode get light mode. We default to dark, but we don't insist.

## Accessibility Considerations

Dark mode introduces specific accessibility challenges that we take seriously:

**Contrast ratios.** WCAG 2.1 requires a minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text. On dark backgrounds, it's tempting to use dim text for aesthetic reasons, but we enforce minimum contrast ratios across all text elements. Our design system includes automated contrast checking that flags violations during development.

**Color distinction.** Some color combinations that are distinguishable on light backgrounds become harder to differentiate on dark backgrounds, particularly for users with color vision deficiency. We test all color combinations using tools that simulate deuteranopia, protanopia, and tritanopia.

**Focus indicators.** Focus rings and selection indicators must be visible against dark backgrounds. We use a combination of bright outline colors and subtle glow effects to ensure keyboard-navigable elements are always identifiable.

**Halation.** Some users, particularly those with astigmatism, experience "halation" — a blooming effect around white text on dark backgrounds that reduces readability. We mitigate this by using slightly off-white text (`#e4e4e7` rather than `#ffffff`) and ensuring text sizes are adequate for comfortable reading.

## Dark Mode and Performance

There's a tangible performance benefit to dark mode on devices with **OLED or AMOLED screens**. These displays turn off individual pixels to display true black, which means dark interfaces consume measurably less power. Google confirmed in a 2018 Android Dev Summit presentation that dark mode on OLED screens can reduce display power consumption by up to 63% at full brightness.

Given that a significant portion of our mobile users are on OLED devices, dark mode as default provides a measurable battery life improvement. For a gaming session that might last 30-60 minutes, this matters.

## The Cultural Statement

Defaulting to dark mode is, in a small way, a **cultural statement**. It says: we built this for people who live online, who game at night, who prefer their screens to complement a dim room rather than illuminate it. It says we understand our audience.

Gaming culture is nocturnal. Discord is dark. Twitch is dark. The games themselves are usually dark. An indie game studio that defaults to a bright white interface is slightly out of step with its community. We'd rather be in sync.

At RMH Studios, dark mode isn't a feature — it's a philosophy. It's a commitment to comfort, to aesthetics, and to building products that feel right from the first pixel. The void is our canvas. The glow is our paint. And the darkness is where our games look their best.
