## Where We Are

It's been almost a year since I first wrote about Satan's Library, and the game has grown from a concept and a feeling into something tangible. The core mechanics are prototyped and validated. The Aura system works. The dynamic camera transitions smoothly between fixed-angle and over-the-shoulder. The movement feels weighted and deliberate. The Succubi AI creates genuine tension in greybox environments. We've proven the foundation — now it's time to build the house.

This post is the roadmap. Not a marketing promise, not a feature list designed to generate hype — an honest look at where Satan's Library is headed, what's planned, and the order in which it's coming together. Plans change, especially for indie studios, but this is the current vision.

## The Chapter Structure

Satan's Library is structured as a **five-chapter experience**, each chapter corresponding to a deeper section of the library and an escalation in both narrative and mechanical intensity.

### Chapter 1: The Reading Room

The player's introduction to the library. Relatively well-lit. The architecture is grand but not yet threatening. The Aura system is gentle — stress builds slowly, relief is accessible. One Succubus patrols a limited territory. The chapter teaches the player the core mechanics through environmental design rather than tutorials.

**Status: In production.** Layout finalized. Camera positions placed. First art pass in progress.

### Chapter 2: The Archives

The library's organizational backbone. Denser shelving. More corridors. The over-the-shoulder camera mode becomes more prominent. A second Succubus introduces the concept of overlapping territories. The catalog system becomes the primary navigation tool, and the first forbidden texts appear.

**Status: Greybox complete.** Gameplay flow tested. Art production not yet started.

### Chapter 3: The Rare Collections

Access-restricted areas of the library housing dangerous and valuable texts. The architecture becomes less conventional — rooms that don't follow expected geometry, corridors that seem longer than they should be. The Aura system becomes significantly more aggressive. Environmental storytelling reveals the library's true nature.

**Status: Design phase.** Room layouts sketched. Mechanical escalation planned.

### Chapter 4: The Basement Stacks

Compact shelving on mechanical rails that can move, reshaping corridors in real-time. Near-total darkness. The camera system is primarily OTS with rare, dramatic fixed-angle shots. Multiple Succubi with varied personalities. The player's knowledge of the library's patterns is actively subverted.

**Status: Concept phase.** Core mechanical concept (moving shelves) prototyped in isolation.

### Chapter 5: The Forbidden Collection

The final chapter. I won't say more than this: everything the player has learned about the game's systems will be challenged. The library reveals itself fully, and the cost of knowledge becomes the central question.

**Status: Written outline only.** This chapter will be built last, informed by everything learned from the previous four.

## Planned Features

### Near-Term (Next 3 Months)

- **Art production for Chapter 1**: Replacing greybox geometry with finished assets. Bookshelves, reading desks, architecture, lighting fixtures. The visual identity of the library.
- **Sound design implementation**: Replacing placeholder audio with final ambient layers, footstep variations, and environmental sounds. The four-layer ambient system described in previous devlogs.
- **Succubus visual design**: Moving from red spheres to actual character models. The "almost-human" visual language I've been developing in concept art.
- **UI/UX design**: The Lock In meter's visual presentation. Environmental interaction prompts. The catalog interface.

### Mid-Term (3-6 Months)

- **Chapter 2 production**: Full art, sound, and gameplay implementation for the Archives.
- **Save system**: Safe rooms will serve as save points, tying saving to the game's risk-reward loop — reaching a save point is an achievement, not a menu option.
- **Expanded Succubus AI**: The full personality system (Stalker, Rusher, Trapper, Lurker) with memory and adaptation.
- **Catalog puzzle system**: The in-game catalog as both navigation tool and puzzle mechanism. Finding entries. Cross-referencing. Decoding.

### Long-Term (6-12 Months)

- **Chapters 3-5 production**: The remaining chapters, built sequentially, each informed by playtesting of the previous.
- **Accessibility options**: Stress sensitivity sliders, visual effect intensity controls, subtitle and audio description support.
- **Performance optimization**: The dynamic camera and particle systems need careful optimization for target hardware.
- **Narrative integration**: Voice acting, environmental narrative finalization, the full story arc connecting all five chapters.

## Technical Milestones

### Optimization Targets

The dynamic camera system, real-time shadow calculations, and particle systems are demanding. Current performance targets:

- **60 FPS on target hardware** with full visual effects
- **Sub-frame camera transitions** — no perceptible hitching during mode switches
- **AI processing budget** of under 2ms per frame for up to four simultaneous Succubi
- **Audio system** supporting 32+ simultaneous sound sources for the layered ambient system

### Tools Development

I'm also investing time in in-house tools:

- **Camera composition tool**: A visual editor for placing and previewing fixed-angle camera positions within rooms, with stress-level simulation
- **Aura event editor**: A timeline tool for designing stress event sequences and testing their cumulative impact
- **AI territory visualizer**: A debug overlay that shows Succubus territories, curiosity targets, and behavior states in real-time

These tools aren't player-facing, but they're critical for maintaining development velocity as the project grows in complexity.

## The Full Vision

When I close my eyes and imagine the finished Satan's Library, I see a game that does something no other survival horror game has done: **makes the player's own psychology the primary threat**. Not monsters (though they're there). Not puzzles (though they're there too). Not resource scarcity (though that's present). The primary threat is your own composure — your ability to stay calm, think clearly, and make good decisions while the game systematically undermines your capacity to do all three.

I want players to finish Satan's Library and feel like they've survived something. Not beaten something — *survived* it. The distinction matters. Beating a game implies mastery. Surviving implies endurance. And endurance, in the face of sustained psychological pressure, is its own kind of victory.
