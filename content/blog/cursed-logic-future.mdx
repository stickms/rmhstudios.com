## Looking Ahead

Ten months ago, Cursed Logic was a concept sketch — four buttons, two health bars, and a dream about dueling an AI. Today it's a living game with Protocol variants, mutations, minigame interludes, a persistent shop, and a growing community of players who've discovered the strange joy of tactical mind games against a digital opponent.

But I'm just getting started. The foundation we've built supports far more than what's currently in the game, and the roadmap ahead is electrifying. Today I want to share where Cursed Logic is going — the features, modes, and dreams that keep me up at night.

## New Protocol Variants

The four current variants — The Watcher, The Hammer, The Mirror, and The Glitch — each offer a distinct dueling experience. But the variant system is designed to be extensible, and I have several new Protocols in development:

### The Prophet

The Prophet is a variant built around **predictive play**. Where other variants react to what you've done, The Prophet attempts to predict what you're *about to* do based on longer-term pattern analysis. It track sequences of actions across multiple rounds and looks for habitual chains.

The Prophet punishes players who play on autopilot. If you always Prepare after a successful Block, The Prophet will learn that chain and Strike on your predicted Prepare. Playing against The Prophet requires deliberate pattern-breaking — consciously varying your behavior to prevent the AI from locking onto your habits.

This variant is in active development and is already producing fascinating playtest sessions. Players who thought they were unpredictable discover just how many unconscious patterns they actually have.

### The Phantom

The Phantom is a stealth variant. It **hides its mode** from the player entirely. Where other Protocol variants display their current mode (pressuring, defensive, recovering), The Phantom shows nothing. You can still Probe for information, but you're operating blind in the absence of Probe data.

The Phantom creates an experience that's closer to the original design vision — facing a truly unknown opponent. It's intended for experienced players who've mastered mode-reading and want a greater challenge. Removing the mode display strips away the safety net and forces you to build your mental model entirely from observed behavior.

### The Hydra

The Hydra is the most ambitious variant concept. It's a Protocol that **splits into two sub-Protocols** at a certain health threshold. When the Hydra's health drops below 50%, both sub-Protocols act simultaneously — you face two actions per round instead of one. You still choose a single action, but it's resolved against both Protocol actions, meaning you can win one interaction and lose the other in the same round.

The Hydra fundamentally changes the mathematical structure of the duel. Instead of optimizing against a single action, you're trying to find the action that performs best against two *simultaneous* actions. This creates entirely new strategic calculus and makes the Hydra's second phase feel like a genuinely different game.

## Expanded Mutation Pool

The current mutation roster has served well, but I want to double it. New mutations in development include:

**Echo Round** — Your action from the previous round automatically repeats. You choose an action as normal, but the previous round's action also fires. This creates two-action rounds that interact with the Protocol's single action in complex ways.

**Countdown Round** — A timer ticks down during your decision phase. If you don't choose before the timer expires, a random action is selected. This mutation adds time pressure to a normally untimed game, testing decision speed alongside decision quality.

**Inverse Health** — Damage heals and healing damages for one round. This mind-bending mutation reverses your strategic priorities. Striking becomes dangerous (you're "healing" the Protocol), and getting Struck becomes beneficial (you're being "healed"). It's delightfully counterintuitive.

**Linked Round** — Your action and the Protocol's action are linked. Whatever you choose, the Protocol is forced to choose the same action. This creates guaranteed Clashes (mutual Strikes), mutual Blocks, or mutual Prepares — and makes the normally impossible mutual-Probe scenario a reality for one strange round.

Each new mutation is tested extensively to ensure it meets our design criteria: it must change optimal strategy, be instantly understandable, affect both sides, and create interesting decisions.

## Multiplayer PvP

This is the dream. The big one. The feature that would transform Cursed Logic from a solo mind game into a competitive arena.

**Player vs. Player dueling.**

The four-action system was always designed with symmetric play in mind. Two humans, each with access to Strike, Block, Prepare, and Probe (yes — in PvP, both players can Probe), locked in a pure battle of prediction and adaptation. No AI tendencies to exploit. No behavioral modes to read. Just two minds trying to outmaneuver each other.

The technical infrastructure is partially in place. The game already uses a state machine architecture that could support networked play with relatively modest modifications. The round-based, simultaneous-action structure is ideal for netcode — there's no need for frame-perfect synchronization because actions are submitted discretely.

The key design challenges for PvP are:

**Symmetric Probe.** If both players can Probe, what does Probe reveal? In PvE, Probe shows the Protocol's projected state. In PvP, it would need to show... what? The opponent's recent action history? Their current health trend? A probability distribution of likely actions based on their behavioral patterns? This is an open design question that requires careful thought.

**Anti-stalling.** In PvE, the Protocol always acts decisively. In PvP, a player might deliberately slow-play, taking maximum time for every decision to frustrate their opponent. We'd need a decision timer — and we'd need to make that timer feel tense rather than punitive.

**Matchmaking.** With a small player base (let's be realistic), matchmaking needs to be fast and reasonably fair. An Elo-style rating system would work mechanically, but we'd need enough players to create meaningful skill brackets. Early PvP would likely need to accept wider skill gaps in matchmaking.

### The Social Layer

PvP isn't just about the duel — it's about the community around the duel. I envision:

- **Duel replays** that can be shared and analyzed
- **Player profiles** showing win rates, favorite actions, and variant performance
- **Season rankings** that reset periodically to keep competition fresh
- **Custom lobbies** where friends can duel with modified rules (specific mutations, variant restrictions, etc.)

The social infrastructure is as important as the mechanical implementation. PvP games live or die on their communities, and building those community tools alongside the gameplay is essential.

## Tournament Mode

Even before PvP launches, I want to build a **Tournament Mode** for single-player. The concept: a bracket of Protocol variants, each with increasingly challenging behaviors and modifier sets. You start at the bottom of the bracket, duel through progressively harder opponents, and try to reach the top.

Tournament Mode would introduce several mechanics unique to the format:

**Health persistence** — Your health carries between duels in the bracket. If you win a duel with only 30% health, you start the next with 30% health. This creates resource management across the tournament — do you play conservatively to preserve health, or aggressively to end duels quickly?

**Draft mutations** — Before each tournament duel, you draft from a selection of mutations that will appear during the duel. This adds a pre-duel strategic layer where you choose mutations that favor your playstyle while potentially disadvantaging the opponent's variant.

**Fragment multipliers** — Tournament victories award significantly more fragments than standard duels, making tournaments the most efficient path to shop progression for skilled players.

**Leaderboards** — Tournament scores (combined across time, efficiency, and remaining health) would be ranked globally. This creates a competitive framework even without live PvP.

## Technical Evolution

Beyond features, Cursed Logic needs technical improvements:

**Mobile optimization.** The current UI works on mobile, but it's designed for desktop. A proper mobile experience would need larger touch targets, simplified animations, and potentially a portrait-oriented layout. Mobile is where most casual gaming happens, and Cursed Logic's session length (a duel takes 5-10 minutes) is perfect for mobile play.

**Accessibility features.** Screen reader support for action selection and round outcomes. High-contrast mode options beyond the default cyber-noir palette. Reduced motion settings for players sensitive to glitch animations. Accessibility isn't optional — it's a priority.

**Performance optimization.** The 756-line game component has served well but needs refactoring as new features arrive. Breaking it into smaller, more focused components will improve maintainability and performance. The Protocol AI logic, in particular, should be extracted into a dedicated module that can be tested and iterated independently.

## The Bigger Vision

Cursed Logic started as an experiment: can a game about four actions and an AI opponent be compelling? The answer, I'm thrilled to say, is yes. But the experiment has grown into something larger — a *platform* for tactical mind games.

I see a future where Cursed Logic is not just one game but a framework. Different Protocols, different rule sets, different worlds, all built on the same core engine of simultaneous-action prediction and adaptation. The four-action system is a *grammar* — and grammar can express infinite ideas.

Maybe someday there's a Cursed Logic set in a medieval fantasy world where you duel sorcerers. Or a sci-fi variant where you're negotiating with alien intelligences through action-based diplomacy. Or an educational version where the "actions" map to study strategies and the "Protocol" simulates test-taking challenges.

These are distant dreams. Right now, the priority is making the core game as good as it can be, building PvP, launching Tournament Mode, and growing the community. But dreams matter. They're the fuel that keeps an indie dev pushing through the hard days and late nights.

Cursed Logic has a bright, strange, neon-lit future. And I can't wait to share it with you.

*The Protocol evolves. So must we. See you in the arena.*
